## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues that the popular fear of AI becoming self-aware and dominating humanity is vastly overblown, as true self-awareness requires a level of embodied, sensory interaction with the world that current AI architectures cannot replicate. He contends that the genuine and immediate danger of AI is not its potential sentience, but rather humanity's tendency to blindly trust AI outputs as infallible — a failure mode already demonstrated by the UK Post Office Horizon scandal. The real risk is human belief in AI correctness, not AI itself.

## Key Topics
- The nature of thinking as an *action* (analogous to walking) rather than a product secreted by the brain
- Why current AI cannot become self-aware: it lacks the embodied, multi-sensory interaction with the world that human consciousness requires
- The distinction between a *model* of the world and the real world, and how models inevitably oversimplify
- The danger of humans over-trusting AI systems and treating their outputs as ground truth
- The UK Post Office Horizon scandal as a real-world case study of uncritical faith in computer systems causing mass harm
