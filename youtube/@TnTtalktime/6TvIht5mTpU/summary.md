## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues against rigid, binary thinking in science — particularly the habit of asking "what's the best X?" without context. Using passive electronic components and energy storage devices as examples, he demonstrates that real-world devices always contain a mixture of effects (capacitive, resistive, inductive, battery-like), and that what we *call* something is simply determined by which effect predominates. The core takeaway is that context determines the correct answer, and that demanding a single "truth" leads to meaningless conclusions.

## Key Topics
- The problem with asking "what is the best?" without defining context
- Lithium's limitations as a universal battery material due to scarcity
- The difference between ideal and real electronic components
- How real resistors, capacitors, and inductors all contain elements of all three passive component types
- The structural similarity of batteries, supercapacitors, and solar cells
- Why labelling a device is about the *predominant* effect, not exclusive identity
- Responding to viewers who argued his solar cell experiments were "really" a battery
- The importance of contextual proportion (1/9000th contribution is negligible)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Lithium** — high energy density battery material, but too scarce for global EV adoption
- **Carbon, iron, aluminium, magnesium** — more abundant alternative battery electrode materials
- **Electrolyte** — present in batteries; can also be present in capacitors
- **Insulating membrane/separator** — microporous or nanoporous, used in both batteries and capacitors
- **Metal plates** — standard electrode material in capacitors, batteries, and solar cells
- **Tap water** — used by a viewer as an improvised electrolyte in a stripped-down version of Robert's design

## Techniques and Methods
- **Equivalent circuit modelling** — representing real passive components as combinations of ideal R, L, and C elements to capture parasitic effects
- **Structural comparison** — analysing batteries, supercapacitors, and solar cells as variants of the same two-plate/separator architecture
- **Power output measurement** — comparing microwatt outputs (4 µW vs 32 µW) to quantify the relative battery contribution to a solar cell device
- **Contextual scaling** — using proportional reasoning (1/9000th) to assess whether a secondary effect is significant
