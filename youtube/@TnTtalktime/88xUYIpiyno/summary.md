## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith gives an informal, free-ranging talk on philosophy, exploring the two fundamental branches — idealism and materialism — and why neither can be conclusively proven or disproven. He argues that the drive to *prove* a philosophical position is ultimately about control over others, and concludes that personal belief, used as an ethical guide for behaviour, is both sufficient and honest.

## Key Topics
- The nature and purpose of philosophy, historically and academically
- Idealism vs. materialism as the two foundational philosophical positions
- Solipsism and why it cannot be disproved
- Determinism/mechanism and its practical absurdity
- Dualism (Descartes) and the difficulty of bridging idealist and materialist frameworks
- Dialectic idealism and dialectic materialism
- The limits of proof: axioms, definitions, and the problem of universal categories
- Science as a self-referential (tautological) system with no claim to ultimate truth
- Gödel's incompleteness / Heisenberg uncertainty as limits on formal proof
- Philosophy, ethics, and moral guidance
- The distinction between proving something to others (control) vs. proving it to yourself (self-understanding)
- Rousseau-esque belief in the essential goodness of people
