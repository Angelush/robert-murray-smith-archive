## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith makes the case that Stirling engines are a seriously underrated technology with a long history of development. Contrary to their reputation as low-power, inefficient, and outdated, modern Stirling engines have demonstrated 45% greater efficiency than conventional petrol engines, quiet operation, and cost-effectiveness up to 100 kW — making them a compelling choice for combined heat and power (CHP) applications. The video concludes by explaining why Robert has chosen the Stirling engine route over alternatives like Peltier devices for his own CHP project.

## Key Topics
- History of the Stirling engine from precursors (Amonton 1699, George Cayley 1807) through Robert Stirling's 1816 closed-cycle design with regenerator
- Why Stirling engines lost ground to steam engines in the 19th century
- Philips' 1951 revival of Stirling engine development for portable radio generators
- NASA's 1986 automotive Stirling engine test showing 45% efficiency improvement over a conventional engine
- Swedish and Japanese Navy use of Stirling engines in submarines (1996) for quiet, efficient battery recharging
- Cost-effectiveness of Stirling engines vs. petrol generators up to ~100 kW
- NASA's Stirling-based radioisotope power systems (CASTIS/Christie experiments) for space habitats and vehicles
- Combined Heat and Power (CHP) applications using a rocket stove paired with a Stirling engine
- Comparison of Stirling engines vs. Peltier devices for small-scale power generation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Twigs, wood, coal, waste oil** — example fuel sources usable in a Stirling engine's external combustion system
- **Radioactive decay material** — referenced as a thermal source for NASA's Stirling-based space power systems

## Techniques and Methods
- **External combustion** — the operating principle of Stirling engines, allowing use of any heat source
- **Closed-cycle regenerative heat exchange** — the key innovation Sterling added in 1816, enabling higher efficiency
- **Rocket stove + Stirling engine integration** — proposed approach for domestic CHP generation
- **Radioisotope thermal generation** — NASA's use of Stirling engines driven by radioactive decay heat
