## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reflects on the concept of "purpose" as a social construct imposed from birth through education and career structures. He argues that asking "what is the purpose of life?" is the wrong question entirely, and proposes instead that life — like nature, music, or dancing — is best understood as something that simply *is*, lived for its own sake rather than toward any external goal.

## Key Topics
- How Western education and career systems instil a purpose-driven mindset from infancy
- The work/leisure divide as a symptom of purpose-oriented thinking
- Colin Wilson's idea of "opening the windows of the mind" through travel and new experience
- Nature (trees, cats, butterflies) as a model of purposeless, non-goal-directed existence
- Music and dance as analogies for activity that has value in itself, not in reaching an endpoint
- The Gandhian idea of embodying the change you want to see in the world
- "The life that is" as an alternative framing to the purpose-driven life
