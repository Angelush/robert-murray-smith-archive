## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reflects on the phenomenon of "users" — people who shamelessly try to take advantage of others' generosity, particularly in the context of open-source work and collaboration. Using historical parallels (the Tragedy of the Commons, the Enclosure Acts) and everyday examples (motorway behaviour), he argues that selfishness at the individual level degrades outcomes for everyone. His main takeaway is that users are not hard to identify and should be firmly dealt with.

## Key Topics
- The concept of "users" — people who exploit the goodwill of others
- Historical parallel: the Tragedy of the Commons and the English Enclosure Acts
- Modern parallel: selfish motorway driving and its ripple effect on traffic
- Open-source work as a target for exploitation
- "Falling in love with the actor" — projecting a false image onto someone based on their public persona
- Red flags for identifying users: credential-dropping, vague business opportunities, work-dumping
- Suspicious NDAs — one-sided, perpetual, with no two-way protections
- Students asking others to effectively write their dissertations
- Robert's personal approach to handling users (giving them the runaround)
- Two contrasting views of humanity: "penguins huddling" vs. "chimpanzees throwing feces"
