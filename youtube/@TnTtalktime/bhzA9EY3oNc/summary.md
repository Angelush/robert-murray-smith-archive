## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the potential of water-based systems as a practical home energy storage solution, combining historical hydraulic technology (accumulators) with water motors. He argues that pressurized water accumulators — which can last over a century — offer a compelling alternative to short-lived battery banks, and that even partially efficient DIY implementations are worthwhile. The main takeaway is that water can function simultaneously as a battery, store, generator, and renewable medium accessible to anyone, even in urban environments without natural water sources.

## Key Topics
- Triboelectric and capacitance-based water electricity generation (prior work on the main channel)
- Rainwater wheel development as modern water power
- Historical hydraulic technology as a valuable reference alongside cutting-edge research
- UK mains water pressure and why extracting power from it directly has been illegal since 1999
- Daily household water usage statistics (150 L/person/day in UK) as an energy scavenging opportunity
- Toilet and bath/shower water flow as charging opportunities for a motor (~25+ minutes of charge time per day)
- Hydraulic accumulators as long-lived, low-maintenance "water batteries"
- Comparing accumulators (120+ year lifespan) to lithium battery banks (~3–5 years)
- Off-peak electricity use to recharge accumulators cheaply
- Pelton wheel construction using accessible hand tools and magnetic bearings
- The philosophy of practical, imperfect implementation vs. waiting for perfection

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Water (as working fluid and energy medium)
- Teaspoons (mentioned as raw material for fabricating Pelton wheel buckets)

## Techniques and Methods
- Scavenging energy from household mains water flow (toilet fill, bath/shower) via an inline water motor
- Using a hydraulic accumulator (weighted plunger tank) to store pressurized water for later discharge
- Recharging the accumulator with off-peak (cheap) grid electricity or manual pumping
- Discharging the accumulator through a water motor to generate ~1 kW
- Fabricating a Pelton wheel using hand tools (accessible low-efficiency approach)
- Mounting a Pelton wheel on magnetic bearings (higher-efficiency approach)
- Closed-loop water cycling: recapturing discharged water and re-pressurizing it, avoiding mains dependency
