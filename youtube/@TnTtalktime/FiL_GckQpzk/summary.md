## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reflects on the importance of context when interpreting statistics and scientific claims. Using examples from taxation figures and secondhand smoking cancer rates (drawn from Bill Bryson), he argues that numbers presented without context can be deeply misleading. He applies this lesson directly to graphene, cautioning viewers to look beyond headline claims about its properties.

## Key Topics
- The danger of decontextualised statistics
- Tax "freedom day" figures and how working days vs. calendar days change the interpretation
- Secondhand smoking cancer statistics and how they compare to eating carrots
- Bill Bryson's writing on statistical context
- Misleading graphene headlines and the importance of reading behind the claims

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Graphene — discussed in the context of overstated headline claims (strength vs. steel, electricity from saltwater, speed-of-light conductivity)

## Techniques and Methods
- Critical analysis of statistics — examining whether figures use calendar days vs. working days
- Comparative contextualisation — benchmarking a statistic against a familiar baseline (e.g., cancer risk from carrots vs. secondhand smoke)
