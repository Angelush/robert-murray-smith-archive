## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith debunks the "self-charging car" marketing term used by manufacturers like Lexus, exposing it as simply a rebranded hybrid (equivalent to a 20-year-old Toyota Prius). He argues that neither clever battery branding nor improved battery technology alone can solve the energy transition challenge — the energy must come from somewhere — and that the real solution requires addressing all three sides of the problem: batteries, generation, and usage.

## Key Topics
- The misleading "self-charging car" marketing term and its reality as a standard hybrid
- The UK national grid's capacity concerns in the face of mass EV adoption
- Energy consumption implications of widespread electric vehicle uptake
- The three-part energy problem: storage (batteries), generation, and usage
- Decentralised microgeneration as a potential way forward
- The 2030/2035 UK deadlines for phasing out petrol and diesel vehicles

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Petrol/diesel (as the fuel source powering hybrid "self-charging" car batteries)

## Techniques and Methods
- Back-of-envelope energy calculation (kilowatt-hours per mile, weekly household EV energy demand)
- Comparison of UK electricity consumption vs. production statistics to illustrate grid strain
