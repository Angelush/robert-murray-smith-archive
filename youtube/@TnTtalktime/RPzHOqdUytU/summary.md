## Overview
Robert reflects on the rising cost of petrol in the UK and Europe, using it as a springboard to revisit the Sinclair C5 — the infamous 1985 electric recumbent tricycle designed by Sir Clive Sinclair. He argues that while the C5 was a commercial disaster at launch, it was simply ahead of its time, and that today's market conditions and cultural acceptance of electric micro-vehicles make it ripe for a comeback.

## Key Topics
- Rising fuel prices in the UK (£2/litre) and Europe, including the cooking oil as diesel trend
- Brief history of Sir Clive Sinclair and Sinclair Research (ZX Spectrum, home computing)
- Development history of the C5, from the C1 prototype through the 1980s
- The disastrous January 1985 launch at Alexandra Palace
- Technical specifications and limitations of the original C5
- The 1983 UK legislation governing electric-assisted vehicles (250W motor, 60kg max weight, 15mph)
- Why the C5 failed: poor market positioning, mail-order sales model, no weatherproofing, road safety concerns
- The case for a modern C5 revival in today's e-vehicle landscape

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Lead acid battery (original C5 power source)
- Leaded petrol (mentioned in context of exhaust fume hazard to C5 riders in 1985)
- Cooking oil (discussed as a short-term diesel substitute)

## Techniques and Methods
