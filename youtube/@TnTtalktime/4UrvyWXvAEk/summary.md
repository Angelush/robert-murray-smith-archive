## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the physical origin of counter torque in electrical generators, tracing it back to the fundamental electromagnetic relationship between moving magnets and conductors. Using simple demonstrations (a shake torch and a magnet dropped through a copper pipe), he shows that counter torque is an unavoidable consequence of Faraday's law, and that it acts as a practical limiting factor on how many coils a generator can usefully have.

## Key Topics
- The basic electromagnetic principle: a moving magnet induces current in a conductor, and current in a conductor creates a magnetic field
- Lenz's law in action: the induced magnetic field always opposes the motion that created it
- Definition of torque as a rotational force
- Counter torque as the rotational expression of this opposing electromagnetic force
- How counter torque increases with generator speed and number of coils
- Practical limits on coil count in generator design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper pipe (used in the falling-magnet demonstration)
- Permanent magnet
- Coil of wire

## Techniques and Methods
- Magnet-through-copper-pipe demonstration to illustrate eddy current braking
- Empirical approach to finding the counter torque limit: adding coils until the generator can no longer be turned by hand, then removing a few
