## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demystifies solid state batteries, explaining that they differ from conventional batteries only in having a solid rather than liquid electrolyte. He argues that the real barrier is not technological — solid state batteries have existed since the 1970s — but economic: the question the public is actually asking is when affordable solid state batteries will arrive, not merely when they will exist.

## Key Topics
- What a solid state battery actually is (same five components as a normal battery, only the electrolyte differs)
- The history of solid state batteries, dating back to Faraday's experiments in 1831–1834
- Commercial solid state batteries already in use (e.g., in pacemakers since the 1970s)
- Why large-scale affordable solid state batteries remain elusive — manufacturing cost, not fundamental science
- The Murata battery (2021) as a notable recent example of small-scale solid state technology
- Pseudo-solid state (gel electrolyte) batteries as an intermediate approach
- Technical challenges: interface void formation, dendrite propagation through ceramics, cost of deposition techniques
- Comparison to the "fusion is always 30 years away" problem — the moving goalposts of affordability
- Snake oil and hype in the solid state battery marketplace

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Silver sulfide** — investigated by Faraday as a solid electrolyte; conductive when heated
- **Lead fluoride** — also investigated by Faraday as a solid electrolyte
- **Silver iodide** — formed by layering silver on iodine; used as a solid state electrolyte in pacemaker batteries since the 1970s
- **Fumed silica** — added to lead-acid batteries ~100 years ago to gel the sulfuric acid electrolyte, producing a pseudo-solid state battery
- **Sulfuric acid** — the liquid electrolyte in lead-acid batteries, gelled by fumed silica
- **Plaster of Paris** — used in early dry cells as a gelling/solidifying agent, qualifying them as pseudo-solid state

## Techniques and Methods
- **Atomic layer deposition (ALD)** — used to fabricate solid state batteries; currently limited to small-scale devices
- **Sputtering** — thin-film deposition technique used in solid state battery manufacture
- **Chemical bath deposition** — another fabrication route for solid state batteries; also small-scale only
- **Gel electrolyte / pseudo-solid state approach** — adding gelling agents (fumed silica, plaster of Paris) to liquid electrolytes as a cost-effective intermediate strategy
