## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores unconventional geoengineering proposals for addressing climate change, focusing on two radical "Plan B" ideas: using small nuclear bombs to throw cooling dust into the atmosphere, and using nuclear explosions to pulverise olivine — a CO₂-absorbing mineral — and distribute it globally. He presents these ideas seriously but with personal reservations, ultimately inviting viewers to form their own opinions.

## Key Topics
- Geoengineering as a climate change intervention strategy
- NASA's 2011 study on the climate effects of a limited nuclear exchange (50–100 Hiroshima-scale bombs)
- Historical precedent: Project Argus (1958) and above-ground nuclear tests through 1962
- Olivine as a natural carbon sequestration material
- The proposal to use a hydrogen bomb to pulverise an olivine mountain and disperse the dust globally
- Criticisms and risks of geoengineering (ozone depletion, UV increase, food production loss, political problems)
- James Fleming's critique of geoengineering as ungovernable and transformative of humanity's relationship with nature

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Olivine** (magnesium iron silicate) — a naturally occurring mineral with strong CO₂ absorption properties; one litre absorbs the CO₂ produced by burning one litre of petrol
- **Boron, oxygen, water** — mentioned as materials that can absorb neutrons and reduce radioactive contamination from a nuclear device by a factor of ~100
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — the greenhouse gas olivine absorbs
- **Soot/dust** — the atmospheric particulate that would cause cooling in the nuclear detonation scenario

## Techniques and Methods
- **Atmospheric nuclear detonation** (historical) — detonating hydrogen bombs at altitude (~300 miles) to disrupt communications and ballistic missiles (Project Argus)
- **Nuclear geoengineering via soot injection** — detonating 50–100 small nuclear bombs to throw enough particulate into the atmosphere to reduce global temperature by ~2.25°C
- **Nuclear mining/pulverisation** — detonating a hydrogen bomb inside an olivine mountain to simultaneously mine, crush, and distribute the mineral as fine dust
- **Olivine enhanced weathering** — using powdered olivine spread through the atmosphere to sequester atmospheric CO₂
- **Thermal energy generation** — using the heat produced by olivine's CO₂ absorption reaction (when kept in large mass) as an electricity source
