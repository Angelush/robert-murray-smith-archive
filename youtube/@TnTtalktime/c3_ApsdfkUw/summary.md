## Overview
Robert demonstrates how particle size — specifically the surface area to volume ratio — dramatically affects the speed and intensity of oxidation reactions. Using steel wool, iron powder, and thermite, he shows that the same material (iron) can rust imperceptibly slowly, burn visibly, or react with explosive violence depending purely on how finely divided it is. The key takeaway is that smaller particles expose more surface for reactions, which is also why nanomaterials are so significant.

## Key Topics
- Surface area to volume ratio as a controlling principle in chemical reactions
- Redox (reduction-oxidation) chemistry, with oxidation reframed as "burning"
- Rusting as slow oxidation of bulk steel
- Steel wool as a high-surface-area form of iron, igniteable with a 9V battery
- Iron powder blown through a flame producing a dramatic pyrotechnic effect
- Thermite as an extreme demonstration of small-particle, high-energy redox reactions
- Relevance of these principles to nanotechnology

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bulk steel** — used as the low-surface-area baseline; rusts slowly
- **Steel wool** — same iron material with vastly greater surface area; ignites easily
- **9V battery** — used to ignite the steel wool
- **Iron powder** — blown through a candle flame to produce a sparking reaction
- **Thermite** — iron oxide/aluminium mixture (implied); produces molten iron at ~2,500 °C
- **Chemical fuse** — used to ignite the thermite from a safe distance
- **Candle** — introductory example of visible oxidation
- **Straw** — used to propel iron powder through the flame
- **Steel pot** — container for the thermite reaction; visibly melted by the reaction

## Techniques and Methods
- Comparing oxidation rate by varying physical form of the same material (bulk vs. wool vs. powder)
- Igniting steel wool by shorting a 9V battery across it
- Accelerating steel wool combustion by blowing (adding oxygen/reactant)
- Projecting iron powder through an open flame via a straw to demonstrate flash combustion
- Setting up a thermite burn with a chemical fuse for a timed, remote ignition
