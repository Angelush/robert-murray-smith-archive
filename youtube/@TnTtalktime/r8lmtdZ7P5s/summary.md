## Overview
Robert reacts with amused disbelief to two viral videos promoting a graphene-infused sanitary pad product from India called "Colen." He debunks the marketing claims as pure buzzword exploitation, and exposes a demonstration video — in which the pad appears to light a bulb when wet — as a simple magician's trick light bulb.

## Key Topics
- Graphene as a marketing buzzword exploited to sell consumer products
- The "Colen" graphene chip sanitary pad, marketed in India with health and absorption claims
- A viral video purporting to demonstrate graphene generating electricity when wet
- The magician's trick light bulb as the actual mechanism behind the demonstration
- Historical parallel to radium being added to toothpaste as a selling gimmick
- Commentary on public credulity and viral misinformation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — claimed ingredient in the sanitary pad; Robert argues it is merely a buzzword with no meaningful functional role here
- **Radium** — mentioned as a historical analogy for dangerous/absurd ingredient marketing (radium toothpaste)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pencil drawing** — Robert observes the "graphene chip" pattern on the pad appears to be drawn on with a pencil
- **Magician's trick light bulb** — a battery-concealed novelty bulb (~$14 on Amazon) used to fake a graphene-powered electricity demonstration
