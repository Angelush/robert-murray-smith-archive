## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reflects on the nature of creativity, arguing that it is not a rare gift but a skill that can be developed like a muscle. He critiques the "sledgehammer" approach to idea generation popularised by Edison and Linus Pauling, favouring instead a mindset of active observation and noticing the world around you. His central takeaway is that creativity flourishes through self-belief, a supportive community, and the habit of truly paying attention.

## Key Topics
- The "sledgehammer" approach to creativity (Edison, Linus Pauling) and its limitations
- Creativity as a muscle that must be exercised, not a fixed trait
- How Western education suppresses creative thinking through rote repetition
- The importance of noticing and observing the world as the foundation of invention
- How social environment and others' opinions shape one's creative self-image (Colin Wilson)
- Reframing "good" vs "bad" ideas as "appropriate" vs "inappropriate"
- Community and mutual respect as enablers of creativity

## Techniques and Methods
