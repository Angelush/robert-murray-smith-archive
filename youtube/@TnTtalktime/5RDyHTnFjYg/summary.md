## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the concept of "pathological science," a term introduced by Irving Langmuir in 1953 to describe genuine scientists who are deceived by subjective effects, wishful thinking, and barely-detectable phenomena into building elaborate but unfounded theories. Unlike fraud or pseudoscience, pathological scientists sincerely believe they are doing real science. Robert argues this phenomenon is not a relic of the past — he sees its markers in modern research areas like dark energy and cold fusion.

## Key Topics
- Definition and origin of "pathological science" (Langmuir, 1953)
- Distinction between pathological science, fraudulent science, and pseudoscience
- Three historical case studies: Martian canals, N-rays, and polywater
- Diagnostic markers of pathological science
- Whether science has truly learned from past pathological episodes
- Speculation that dark energy research may exhibit pathological science characteristics

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Quartz tubes — used in the polywater experiments to squeeze water and allegedly induce polymerisation
- Water (polywater) — claimed to be a novel polymerised phase of water, later explained by glass contamination

## Techniques and Methods
- Telescopic observation of Mars (19th century, ~6-inch diameter optics) — prolonged staring to catch brief moments of atmospheric clarity, followed by rapid sketching
- Dark-room observation — used by Blondlot to detect alleged N-ray effects on dim sparks
- Prism-based N-wave generation — the central apparatus of Blondlot's experiments, shown to be irrelevant when secretly removed by physicist Wood
- Controlled repetition / blind removal — Wood's informal debunking method: repeating the experiment after secretly pocketing the prism
- Squeezing water through quartz tubes — the preparation method claimed to produce polywater
