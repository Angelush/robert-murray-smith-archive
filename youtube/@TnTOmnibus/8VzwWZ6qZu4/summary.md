## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a series of 3D-printed mechanisms he calls "impossible gears" — gear arrangements that produce a non-1:1 gear reduction ratio even when all gears involved are exactly the same size, contradicting a long-held assumption in mechanical engineering. He builds and tests five distinct mechanisms across the video (a week-long series), achieving ratios of 2:1 and 3:1 from same-sized gears, and closes with a mathematical explanation rooted in epicycloids and epicyclic geometry.

## Key Topics
- Gear ratio fundamentals: pitch circle, tooth count as shorthand, drive vs. driven relationships
- The "impossible" premise: why same-sized gears were assumed to always yield a 1:1 ratio
- Mechanism 1: Spur gears with a lever/bar arm producing a 2:1 ratio and simultaneous power split across two axles
- Mechanism 2: Three identical bevel gears in a fixed/rotating arrangement producing a 2:1 ratio
- Mechanism 3: Three bevel gears with an additional modular stage producing a 3:1 ratio (and back-drivability)
- Mechanism 4: Same-sized spur gears in a rotating cage with a fixed gear producing a 2:1 ratio
- Mechanism 5: Trammel of Archimedes (ellipsograph / "do nothing machine") incorporated into a gear mechanism for a 2:1 ratio
- Mathematical explanation: epicycloids, rolling circles, and why the path length constraint is satisfied
- Connection to planetary gear systems and the general formula for epicycloid lobes: **n + 1** (where n = ratio of fixed to rolling circle radii)
- Leibniz's philosophical concept of the monad as an aside on infinitesimal points

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed PLA parts (designed in Tinkercad, files released on Thingiverse)
- Small clips and pegs (press-fit fasteners for assembly)
- Adhesive/glue (used to fix certain gears rigidly to their axles)
- Silicone oil (applied to the Trammel of Archimedes mechanism to reduce friction and allow smooth operation)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD modelling in Tinkercad
- FDM 3D printing of all mechanism components
- Press-fit and clip-based assembly (tight-fit axles and snap clips)
- Fixed vs. free-spinning axle differentiation to create the gear reduction effect
- Modular design: adding stages to increment the gear ratio (2:1 → 3:1)
- Use of a rotating cage around a fixed gear (epicyclic/planetary-style arrangement) with spur gears
- Integration of the Trammel of Archimedes slider mechanism into a gear train
- Registration dot marking for visual demonstration of rotation counts
