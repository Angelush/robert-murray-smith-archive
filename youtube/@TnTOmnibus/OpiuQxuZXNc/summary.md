## Overview
This video explores Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWTs) as an alternative to conventional Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWTs), and investigates how biomimicry — specifically humpback whale tubercles and owl wing trailing-edge serrations — can improve their performance. Robert 3D prints prototype VAWT blades with and without these biological features and measures the RPM difference using a digital tachometer, demonstrating meaningful gains including self-starting capability.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWTs): lightning strikes, wildlife hazards, fire risk, structural failure
- Introduction to VAWTs: omnidirectional wind capture, simpler design, lower noise, scalability
- VAWT challenges: higher cut-in wind speed, lower efficiency at low wind speeds, public perception
- Biomimicry as an engineering approach
- Humpback whale tubercles: leading-edge bumps that reduce drag and increase lift, applied to VAWT blades
- Owl wing serrations: Siemens "Dino Tails" and their noise-reduction effect on turbine blades
- 3D printed VAWT prototype construction and comparative RPM testing
- Self-starting behaviour achieved with the owl-tail addition

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Cyanoacrylate glue (used to assemble 3D printed blade components)
- Butyl rubber tape (used to attach owl-style trailing edge tails, allows angle adjustment)
- Reflective aluminium strip (applied to rotor for tachometer measurement)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of VAWT blade prototypes (with and without tubercles; with and without owl-style tails), files shared on Thingiverse
- Assembly of rotor from printed blades, rod connectors, couplers, washers, and skate bearings (22mm × 8mm × 7mm)
- Construction of a cradle/test stand from printed base and uprights
- Fan-driven wind simulation for bench testing
- RPM measurement using a digital laser tachometer with reflective strip target
- Comparative testing across three configurations: no tubercles → tubercles → tubercles + owl tails
