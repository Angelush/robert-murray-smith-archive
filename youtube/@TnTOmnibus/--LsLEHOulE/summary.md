## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores biomimicry in wind turbine design, focusing on the Samara seed (sycamore/maple winged seed) as a blade shape for a small wind turbine. He scans a real sycamore seed, 3D prints it as a three-blade turbine, then refines the blade profile using the free sculpting tool SculptGL. He also investigates whether adding whale-fin-inspired tubercles (nodules along the leading edge) to the blade improves efficiency, drawing on research by marine biologist Frank Fish.

## Key Topics
- Biomimicry in wind turbine design: birds, whale fins, and trees as inspiration
- The Samara seed (sycamore) as a wind turbine blade shape
- History of the three-bladed "Dutch concept" turbine (credited to Hinrich Styal, 1980s)
- Building and testing a simple two-blade pivoting turbine prototype (inspired by Finnish artist Simon Gripenberg)
- 3D scanning a sycamore seed and modeling it in TinkerCAD and 3D Builder
- Using SculptGL to clean up and refine scanned 3D models
- Humpback whale tubercles (nodules/cubicles on the leading edge) and their aerodynamic benefits
- Research by Frank Fish and Philip Watts on tubercle-induced lift increase (~15–20%) and drag reduction
- Comparative outdoor test of plain vs. tubercle-equipped Samara blades
- Sharing designs on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Fiberglass rod (used as blade pivot axle in prototype)
- Aluminium sheet (bent to form prototype blades)
- 8 mm steel bar, 200 mm long (turbine shaft)
- 22 mm × 8 mm × 7 mm skate bearings (two per handle assembly)
- 8 mm washers
- 3D-printed PLA (implied, for Samara blade prints)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D scanning a physical sycamore seed
- Mesh cleanup in 3D Builder
- Parametric modeling and peg addition in TinkerCAD
- Organic mesh sculpting in SculptGL (drag, smooth, inflate, flatten, pinch tools)
- Exporting/importing STL/OBJ files between tools
- FDM 3D printing of blade sets and nose cone
- Assembly with skate bearings and steel shaft
- Qualitative side-by-side wind testing of two blade variants on a hilltop
- Adding tubercle nodules to the leading edge of printed blades (biomimetic modification)
