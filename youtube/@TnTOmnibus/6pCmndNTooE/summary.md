## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds an unusual Wimshurst electrostatic machine using large circular glass display shelves as the rotating discs instead of the typical plastic. The key innovation is a hubless, axle-free cradle design that uses rim-mounted pulley wheels and motors to drive the discs, allowing the machine to be scaled to virtually any disc size. The completed machine successfully generates high-voltage sparks.

## Key Topics
- History and operating principle of the Wimshurst machine (invented by James Wimshurst, early 1880s)
- Electrostatic induction as the underlying mechanism
- The electrophorus as a precursor device, including Lichtenberg's 1777 giant version and Lichtenberg figures
- Design considerations for metal sector placement and quantity (18–40 sectors depending on disc size)
- Hubless/axle-free cradle construction as the novel design approach
- Assembly of capacitors, current collectors (Combs), and neutralizer bars
- Motor-driven rim friction drive spinning discs in opposite directions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Large circular glass display shelves** — used as the rotating discs (unusual choice; normally plastic)
- **Copper foil tape** — used for the metal sectors; noted for durability but subject to oxidation
- **Aluminium HVAC tape** — alternative sector material; pre-glued but less durable than copper
- **Builder's board (fascia board / crаo in the USA)** — used to construct the cradle frame; bonds well with super glue
- **75 mm downpipe (PVC)** — used to make the Leyden jar–style capacitors
- **Aluminium foil (HVAC grade)** — wrapped inside and outside the pipe capacitors
- **30 mm pulley wheels** — rim support and drive components
- **8 mm plumbing olives and washers** — used as spacers to align pulley wheels
- **36 mm waste pipe** — used as a top stabiliser to prevent disc wobble
- **Old hard drive platters (aluminium)** — repurposed as current collector (Comb) discs
- **Carbon fibre tow (unravelled carbon fibre cloth)** — used as brush material for neutralizer bars; resin must be burned off before use
- **Brass strip** — used for neutralizer bars
- **Terminal block clips** — used to mount neutralizer bars on steel posts
- **Super glue** — bonding agent for builder's board construction

## Techniques and Methods
- **Induction-based charge multiplication** — the core operating principle of the Wimshurst machine
- **Sector template fabrication** — drawing a scaled circle divided by sector count (e.g. 24 sectors = 15° spacing), using a washer to round corners
- **Metal foil sector application** — cutting petal shapes from foil tape, aligning with card template, pressing down and burnishing to remove air bubbles
- **Hubless cradle construction** — rim-resting design using pulley wheels in a builder's board frame, eliminating the need to locate the disc centre
- **Rim friction drive** — two small motors with rubber-coated pulleys pressed directly against the glass rim, wired to rotate discs in opposite directions
- **DIY Leyden jar capacitors** — pipe sections wrapped with aluminium foil inside and out, with isolated bolts connecting to inner and outer foil layers separately
- **Comb current collectors** — hard drive platters with jagged teeth filed in, connected to capacitor inner terminals
- **Neutralizer bar assembly** — brass strip mounted on a swivelling steel post with carbon fibre brush tow inserted at the tips
