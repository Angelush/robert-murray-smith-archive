## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the surprisingly broad range of practical applications for mushrooms and fungi beyond their traditional uses in food, medicine, and recreation. The video covers mycelium-based composite materials, supercapacitors, solar steam generation, and leather alternatives, demonstrating hands-on experiments throughout. The central takeaway is that fungi — particularly their mycelium networks — are emerging as a versatile, sustainable material platform with applications ranging from packaging to space habitats.

## Key Topics
- The scale and structure of fungal mycelium networks (density, node count, electrochemical communication)
- Mycelium as a composite binder material for packaging, furniture, and construction
- Fungal building blocks for NASA lunar/Martian habitat research
- Space debris and the Kessler syndrome as motivation for biodegradable satellites
- Mushroom carbonization to make supercapacitor electrodes (hands-on experiment)
- Solar steam generation / desalination using mushroom structure (hands-on experiment)
- Kombucha SCOBY as a leather replacement material
- Traditional leather production and its environmental/labour costs

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Mycelium** — the underground root network of fungi, used as a structural binder in composites
- **Straw / rice husk / sawdust / corn shells / wood** — organic substrates for mycelium growth
- **Potato starch-based plastic** — used to 3D-print molds for mycelium furniture
- **Activated carbon** — used as a protective burial medium during carbonization and as a reference electrode material
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — mentioned as a chemical activation agent for carbon (4:1 wet ratio at 700 °C); not used in the filmed experiment
- **Acrylic binder** — used as a 10–20 wt% binder in the supercapacitor paste
- **Deionised (DI) water** — used to make the supercapacitor paste and as electrolyte medium
- **Graphite foil** — used as the current collector for the supercapacitor
- **Kitchen towel** — used as separator between supercapacitor electrodes
- **Oasis (floral foam)** — used as a water-wicking substrate for the artificial solar evaporator
- **Kombucha SCOBY (cellulose mat)** — grown as a leather substitute; described as long-chain cellulose
- **Lime (calcium hydroxide solution)** — mentioned in the historical leather-tanning process
- **Algae** — mentioned as a food source / off-gassing agent for inflating fungal space habitat molds

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mycelium composite moulding** — packing organic substrate into a mold with fungal spores, allowing growth for several days, then heat-killing at ~60–70 °C to fix the structure
- **Carbonization in a home kiln** — burying organic material in activated carbon inside a steel container, ramping to 700 °C at ~1–2 °C/min, holding for one hour; preserves original shape
- **Grinding carbonized material** — using a kitchen blender to produce fine carbon powder
- **Supercapacitor fabrication** — mixing carbon powder with acrylic binder and DI water into a paste, coating onto graphite foil, drying, assembling a symmetric two-electrode cell with a paper separator and electrolyte
- **Capacitor charging and discharge test** — charging to 1.6 V and discharging through a small motor to confirm energy storage
- **Chemical activation (described, not performed)** — KOH activation at 700 °C for ~1 hour to increase surface area; yields ~20% of already-carbonized mass
- **Artificial mushroom solar evaporator** — cutting Oasis foam into a cap-and-stem form, painting the cap with mushroom carbon paste, placing in water, and weighing over ~4 hours against a flat-water control
- **SCOBY leather production** — growing kombucha SCOBY, drying on a frame until brittle, then oiling to produce a pliable leather-like sheet that can be stitched
