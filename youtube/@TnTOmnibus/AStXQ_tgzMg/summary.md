## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build the "Thunderbolt" (wooden) and "Mighty Mite" (metal) axial-flux generators entirely from hardware store components — bolts, plywood or mild steel discs, neodymium magnets, and enamel wire. He explains the physics behind why the design works (Lorentz law, 90° field angle) and integrates the final generator into a Darrieus/Grinus-type wind turbine made from drain pipe, achieving roughly 50–120 V AC output. The key takeaway is that this generator is not more efficient than any other — its real value is that anyone with a basic drill can build it cheaply from scavenged parts.

## Key Topics
- Building an axial-flux generator from M10 bolts, plywood/steel discs, and enamel wire
- Winding and matching coils by weight or resistance (instead of counting turns)
- Connecting coils in series (input-to-output chaining)
- Lorentz law: EMF = BLV sin θ — why wire length and 90° field angle dominate output
- Why this bolt-core design bends the magnetic field to 90° constantly, unlike a conventional rotating generator
- Surprising result: a single continuous coil wound around all bolts still generates voltage
- Metal "Tiny/Mighty Mite" variant using 3 mm mild steel discs and M5 coach bolts
- Offsetting dual stator plates by 7.5° to reduce cogging
- Integrating the generator into a pipe-housed Grinus (Darrieus) wind turbine
- Realistic power output analysis: high voltage but milliamp current, bound by input mechanical power
- Wind energy as a renewable — proportional to wind speed, comparable cost to solar

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **M10 bolts** (70 mm) — stator poles for wooden version
- **M10 nuts and washers** — bolt assembly
- **Heat shrink tubing** (4 cm lengths) / electrician's tape — insulating bolt threads before winding
- **Enamelled copper wire (~0.35 mm)** — coil winding material; matched by weight or resistance
- **Plywood** — stator disc plates for wooden Thunderbolt version
- **3 mm mild steel plate** — stator discs for metal Tiny/Mighty Mite version
- **M5 coach bolts** (smooth shank) — stator poles for metal version
- **N35 neodymium magnets** — rotor magnets; two sizes used (0.5 cm × 1 cm and 3 mm thick × 0.5 cm across)
- **8 mm steel bar** — rotor axle
- **Aluminium spacer tube** (10 mm OD, 8 mm ID) — rotor gap spacer
- **Bearings** — centre bearing in stator disc
- **Plastic disc** — rotor base plate for Mighty Mite
- **Drain/gutter PVC pipe** — housing for pipe-integrated wind turbine
- **Resin** (mentioned) — optional permanent potting compound for weatherproofing

## Techniques and Methods
- Wrapping bolts in heat shrink / electrician's tape to protect enamel wire from sharp threads
- Winding coils by hand, drill, or lathe — all in the same direction, same start/end orientation
- Matching coil length by **weighing copper** or **measuring resistance** per bolt (avoiding turn-counting)
- Connecting individual coils **in series** (output of one to input of next, ×8)
- Drilling plywood/steel discs with hole saw; spacing 8 bolt holes at 45° (wooden) or 12 holes for metal version
- Threading M5 holes in steel discs for coach bolts
- Arranging magnets alternating N–S at 22.5° spacing (16 magnets, wooden rotor) or 24 magnets at 15° spacing (Mighty Mite rotor)
- **Offsetting two stator plates by 7.5°** relative to each other to reduce magnetic cogging
- Inserting generator into drain pipe with tape wrapping for snug fit; wires routed through a small gap
- Attaching a 3D-printed Grinus serpentine wind turbine rotor to the shaft
- Using a drill as a test prime mover; reading output on a voltmeter or LED panel
