## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith gives a brief history of fire extinguishers before demonstrating how to safely decommission and repurpose expired dry powder extinguishers as steel pipe for a vortex rocket stove. The video covers safe discharge, powder disposal, and practical metalworking techniques including a clever jig for making straight cuts on cylindrical objects and a method for notching pipe for 90° joins.

## Key Topics
- History of fire extinguisher development from ancient Greece to modern smart extinguishers
- Safe discharge of pressurised dry powder extinguishers and containment of powder
- Removal of the valve and disposal of dry powder chemical contents
- Building a simple pipe-cutting jig using caster wheels and an angle grinder
- Notching pipe for 90° joins using a paper template method
- MIG welding pipes end-to-end using the caster jig as a rotary positioner
- Goal: building a vortex rocket stove chimney from repurposed extinguisher bodies

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ammonium phosphate** — primary component of UK dry powder extinguisher fill (90%)
- **Ammonium sulfate** — secondary component of UK dry powder fill (10%); both are fertilisers
- **Sodium bicarbonate** — historical extinguisher agent; also recommended for neutralising residual powder during cleanup
- **Potassium carbonate** — used in early extinguisher designs (Ambrose Godfrey, Captain Manby)
- **Sulfuric acid** — used in 19th-century soda-acid extinguishers
- **Carbon tetrachloride** — 20th-century extinguisher agent, later phased out due to toxicity
- **Nitrogen** — pressurising gas used in modern dry powder extinguishers
- **Halon alternatives / clean agents** — mentioned as modern eco-friendly suppressants
- **Inert gases and foam-based agents** — mentioned as non-ozone-depleting alternatives
- **Silicon coating** — used on the sphericised dry powder particles

## Techniques and Methods
- **Controlled discharge into a containment box** — discharging extinguisher into a plastic box with a cloth-filtered vent hole to minimise powder spread
- **Valve removal by percussive loosening** — striking the valve flat with a hammer to loosen it for hand removal after pressure is gone
- **Caster-wheel pipe-cutting jig** — four casters screwed to a bench with an L-bracket stop, allowing cylindrical pipe to rotate; angle grinder clamped via hose clips and a hinge to cut a straight circumferential line
- **Paper circle template method for pipe notching** — tracing the pipe on paper, cutting the circle, folding to find centre, dividing diameter into thirds, transferring marks to pipe, then cutting the saddle profile
- **Spot welding then continuous MIG welding using the jig as a rotary positioner** — holding the MIG torch stationary while rotating the pipe on the casters for an even circumferential weld
