## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds and demonstrates a "Strange Engine" — a thermoacoustic heat engine constructed from three empty bean cans, a balloon diaphragm, fish tank gravel as a regenerator, and a small amount of water. He then shows two significant improvements: replacing the steel middle can with a ceramic cup to act as a thermal break (greatly boosting efficiency), and substituting methanol for water so the engine runs from heat sources as low as ~60°C. The video concludes with the suggestion to convert this into a simple linear generator for phone charging.

## Key Topics
- Building a thermoacoustic engine from three bean cans
- Role of each section: boiler (bottom can), regenerator (middle can with gravel), and diaphragm chamber (top can)
- Pressure equalization technique using a small drilled hole and tape
- Thermoacoustic standing wave explanation (attributed to Blader Tiller)
- Improvement 1: ceramic cup as thermal break to reduce conductive heat loss
- Improvement 2: methanol as a lower-boiling-point working fluid
- Heat source options and the importance of temperature difference
- Potential linear generator application
- Comparison of this engine's efficiency (~Carnot limit) vs. Peltier devices (~3–5%)
- Credit to original designers Blader Tiller and Timsin

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Empty bean cans (×3)** — main structural components; each serves a distinct functional role
- **Water (2–20 mL)** — working fluid in the bottom (boiler) can
- **Fish tank gravel (2–4 mm)** — regenerator material filling the middle can
- **JB Weld (two-part epoxy)** — heat-resistant adhesive used to bond cans together
- **Balloon** — cut and stretched over the top can as the diaphragm
- **Electrical/electrician's tape** — used to secure balloon and seal can joints
- **M12 nut** — mass glued to the center of the diaphragm as a reaction weight
- **Methanol** — alternative working fluid; boiling point 64.7°C, allows operation from ~60°C heat sources
- **Ceramic cup** — substituted for the steel middle can to create a thermal break
- **Rice** — mentioned as historical regenerator material ("rice drum," attributed to ancient China)
- **Glass beads (small bracelet/necklace beads)** — mentioned as alternative regenerator material
- **Stainless steel scourer / steel wool** — mentioned as layer used by Blader Tiller in single-can version

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling many small holes in the bottom of the middle can (to retain gravel while allowing steam/air flow)
- Using a hole saw or can opener to cut a large hole in the bottom of the top can
- Mixing and applying JB Weld (equal parts A and B, ~8-hour cure) for heat-resistant bonding
- Stretching a cut balloon section over the top can opening as a diaphragm, secured with electrical tape
- Gluing an M12 nut to the diaphragm center as an oscillating mass
- Drilling a 1–2 mm pressure equalization hole in the top can, covered with tape
- Tuning internal pressure by peeling and re-applying tape until the engine self-starts
- Replacing steel middle can with a drilled ceramic cup to reduce conductive heat loss
- Testing with methanol in place of water for low-temperature operation
- Running on varied heat sources: tealight candles, hot plate (250°C), boiled kettle water, rocket stove
