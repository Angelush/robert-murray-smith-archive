## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the "Dumas Effect," a claimed super-efficient water heating device invented by Jean-Christophe Dumas, consisting of two electrodes submerged in water and connected to mains AC supply. He traces the same invention through multiple independent discoverers, replicates it using graphite plates and then carbon rods from a zinc-carbon battery, and concludes that the effect is simply ohmic (resistive) heating — not resonance — and is essentially a modern rediscovery of the old "stinger" immersion heater.

## Key Topics
- History of the Dumas Effect and its independent rediscoveries (Dumas, Peter Davey, George Wiseman)
- Open-source vs. patent-locked approaches to invention
- Commercial development by Heatworks and their "ohmic array" graphite plate heater
- Why ohmic/direct water heating is more efficient than conventional resistance-wire immersion heaters (no limescale, no thermal lag)
- Debunking the resonance explanation (mains frequency 50–60 Hz vs. water's resonant frequency ~22+ GHz)
- Explanation of why AC cannot produce meaningful electrolysis (contrast with DC/HHO cells)
- Practical replication using graphite plates and carbon rods from batteries
- The "legacy effect" as a barrier to adoption of superior technologies
- Electrical safety precautions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite plates** — primary electrode material used by Heatworks and replicated by Robert; inert in water
- **Carbon rods (from zinc-carbon batteries)** — used as an alternative electrode material in the second experiment
- **Deionised / distilled water** — used as the working fluid in both experiments
- **Magnesium oxide** — insulating fill inside conventional resistance-wire heating elements
- **Copper** — noted by George Wiseman to contaminate water when used as electrodes
- **Hot glue** — used temporarily to space the graphite plates; melted during the experiment, causing a short
- **Tile (ceramic)** — drilled and used as a spacer/holder for the carbon rod electrodes

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ohmic (direct) water heating** — passing AC through the water itself so it acts as the resistive element
- **Graphite plate electrode fabrication** — drilling plates to accept lugs, trimming to prevent contact, spacing ~4 mm apart
- **Carbon rod extraction** — removing rods from zinc-carbon batteries using pliers and tin snips, soldering wire leads to the metal caps
- **Ceramic tile spacer** — drilling 8 mm holes in a tile to hold carbon rods at fixed separation without touching
- **Variac (variable autotransformer)** — used for voltage control and mains isolation/protection during experiments, run at ~100 V
- **External thermistor / thermocouple** — taped to the outside of the glass vessel to monitor temperature rise without submerging the sensor
- **Electrolysis explanation** — theoretical comparison of DC vs. AC current behaviour at electrode surfaces to rule out gas evolution
