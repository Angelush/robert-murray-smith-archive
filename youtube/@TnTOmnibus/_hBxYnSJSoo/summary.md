## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses the design and build of a folding portable wind turbine as a practical framework for teaching how all electrical power generation systems work. He breaks down any generation system into three universal components — input, link (gearing), and output (generator) — and walks through building each element: a serpentine-coil axial-flux generator, a 3-stage compound planetary gearbox, and folding turbine blades cut from PVC drain pipe. The key takeaway is that "best" design choices are always contextual; understanding the three-component framework lets you reason through any power generation scenario.

## Key Topics
- The three-component framework for all power generation systems: input, link (gearing), output (generator)
- How electric motors and generators are electrically identical
- Fleming's right-hand rule explained via a physical vector demonstration with strings
- Wire angle relative to magnetic field and its effect on generator output (BLV sinθ formula)
- Serpentine coil design vs. standard wound coils — efficiency and ease of construction
- Gear theory: transmission, force, and rotational direction; historical context (Antikythera mechanism, Crabble Corn Mill, Dover)
- Planetary (epicyclic) gear systems — stackable 3:1 ratio stages yielding 3×, 9×, 27× multiplication
- Magnetic gearing as an alternative to mechanical gears
- Folding wind turbine blade design using PVC soil/drainage pipe
- Bike dynamo integration as a generator option
- Rectification strategies: per-coil rectification vs. three-phase rectification; series vs. parallel coil connection

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVC soil/drainage pipe** (100 mm / 4-inch diameter, ~5 mm wall thickness) — cut into blade blanks
- **N35 neodymium magnets** (10 mm × 2 mm) — used in the axial-flux rotor
- **N35 neodymium magnets** (65 mm × 10 mm × 3 mm) — used in the larger generator rotor
- **N35–N52 neodymium magnets** (15 mm diameter circular, 4 mm thick) — used in the magnetic gearbox
- **M8 steel nuts** — embedded in the magnetic gearbox housing (8 nuts as the outer magnetic ring)
- **8 mm steel bar** — rotor axle
- **Thrust bearing** (35 mm OD × 22 mm ID × 10 mm thick) — supports the small generator rotor
- **Skate bearings** (22 mm × 7 mm × 8 mm centre) — used in planetary gear stages and blade hub
- **Hair-thin magnet wire (copper winding wire)** — wound into serpentine coils (~200 turns per coil)
- **PC fan motor** — repurposed as a demonstration generator
- **Bike dynamo** — used as the generator in the final folding turbine assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- **Serpentine coil winding** — winding a continuous coil around two bolts on a board, then bending it into a twisted serpentine shape to maximise wire alignment with the magnetic field
- **Axial-flux generator assembly** — stacking a 3D-printed stator (holding coils) and rotor (holding magnets in N-S-N-S alternating pattern) around a thrust bearing
- **Per-coil rectification** — each coil rectified individually, then outputs connected in series (to sum voltages) or parallel (to sum current)
- **Compound planetary gearbox construction** — 3D-printing stackable ring gear + sun/planet carrier stages, each providing 3:1 ratio, stacked to 27:1
- **Magnetic gearbox fabrication** — using embedded M8 nuts and circular neodymium magnets in a printed housing to achieve contactless gear coupling
- **Turbine blade fabrication from pipe** — marking PVC pipe in quadrants, cutting longitudinally and to length to produce four curved aerofoil-section blades
- **Folding blade hub design** — 3D-printed three-arm pivot assembly with steel pin hinges, nose cone to lock blades open, clips as temporary blade retention
- **Hand-crank and pull-cord load testing** — validating generator and gearbox output voltage (up to 104 V measured by hand-cranking through the 27:1 gearbox)
- **Tinkercad CAD design** — all printed parts designed in Tinkercad; STL files provided free in the video description
