## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith dismantles an expired CO2 fire extinguisher to explore its reusable components, primarily the thick aluminum tube that forms the body. He demonstrates three distinct projects using salvaged parts: building a resonant bell-speaker using a piezo element, turning the aluminum ring into DIY headphones, and constructing a simple beta/gamma-type Stirling engine using the tube as the cylinder.

## Key Topics
- Disassembly and internal anatomy of a CO2 fire extinguisher
- Repurposing the aluminum tube body as a resonant bell/speaker
- Making headphones and a flat-panel ceiling speaker from piezo buzzers
- Removing the fire extinguisher's resin paint coating
- Making a decorative column vase from the cleaned aluminum tube
- Building a low-temperature-differential Stirling engine using the tube as a cylinder

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Aluminum** — the main body tube of the CO2 extinguisher; noted as surprisingly thick-walled
- **Brass** — the valve assembly inside the extinguisher
- **CO2 (carbon dioxide)** — the pressurized gas stored inside
- **Piezo element (buzzer)** — sourced from gift cards or circuit boards; used as a speaker driver
- **Latex rubber / exercise resistance bands** — used as the working diaphragm and displacer membrane for the Stirling engine
- **750 ml paint can** — used as the displacer piston inside the Stirling engine cylinder
- **Resin coating** — the red paint on the extinguisher body, noted as difficult to remove
- **Magnets and coil** — mentioned speculatively for electricity generation from the Stirling engine output

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a CO2 fire extinguisher and identifying salvageable components
- Cutting the aluminum tube to length with a saw
- Scraping the resin paint coating off using a box/trimming knife blade (scoring lengthwise, then peeling)
- Gluing a piezo element onto the aluminum ring to create a resonant bell-speaker
- Connecting the piezo speaker to an amplifier and also directly to an unamplified headphone output
- Attaching a piezo transducer to a ceiling tile for a hidden flat-panel speaker (technique credited to the YouTube channel *Tech Ingredients*)
- Constructing a beta/gamma-type Stirling engine: aluminum tube as cylinder, paint can with pinhole as displacer, latex diaphragm as working piston, with a small stirrup weight (~half a kilogram) on top
