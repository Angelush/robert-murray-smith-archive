## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores building-integrated solar energy, focusing on light guides and fluorescent dyes as low-cost alternatives to conventional solar panel installations. He demonstrates that a fluorescent dye-infused jelly (made from highlighter ink and gelatin) placed over a solar cell can significantly boost output by converting invisible UV light into visible light, and proposes using fluorescent prism shapes as a passive solar tracking mechanism.

## Key Topics
- Building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) vs. centralised solar farms
- Light guides (from LCD screens and LED lighting panels) as concentrating PV (CPV) systems
- Fluorescent dyes as spectrum converters for solar cells
- Passive solar tracking using prism/triangle shapes coated in fluorescent material
- University of Rochester research on light guide CPV (reporting ~50% efficiency improvement)
- Cost breakdown of solar installations (panels ~20%, installation ~35% of total cost)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic light guide panels** — extracted from LCD screens and LED office lighting panels; used to concentrate light onto solar cells
- **Fluorescent highlighter ink** — water-soluble dye extracted from highlighter pens; used as the fluorescent absorber
- **Gelatin** — used to suspend the fluorescent dye in a jelly/gel medium
- **Deionised water** — solvent for extracting highlighter dye
- **Cellulose acetate** — the absorbent felt material inside highlighter pens
- **Fluorescent acrylic sheets** — commercial alternative to DIY jelly (~£10 each)
- **Fluorescent safety shirt (yellow dye)** — used as an initial test of fluorescence boosting solar output
- **Perovskites** — mentioned as thin-film solar material for building integration (not used experimentally)
- **Multi-junction solar cells / GaAs solar cells** — mentioned as high-efficiency (~40–45%) options

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling LCD screens and LED lighting panels to extract acrylic light guides
- Measuring solar cell output (voltage and current) with and without a light guide to quantify concentration gain
- Extracting fluorescent dye by soaking highlighter felt in deionised water
- Casting fluorescent gelatin jelly in an oiled plastic tray mold
- UV lamp illumination to verify fluorescence of the gel
- Comparative solar cell measurements with and without fluorescent overlays
- Cutting fluorescent material into triangular/prism shapes and testing as passive angular concentrators
- Repurposing an LED lighting panel as a solar generator by removing the frosted diffuser panel
