## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the history of thermoelectric power generation from the Seebeck effect (1829) through to the WWII-era Soviet "Partisan Battery" — a water-jacketed thermoelectric generator used to charge radios in the field. He builds a modern interpretation using off-the-shelf Peltier modules sandwiched between two aluminium pans, demonstrates it successfully lighting an LED strip, then pivots to a more promising approach using a hand-made copper/copper-oxide thermocouple that can withstand far higher temperatures.

## Key Topics
- History of the Seebeck effect and the Seebeck coefficient
- Development of metal-junction and semiconductor thermocouples and thermopiles
- The 1929 semiconductor thermoelectric breakthrough (attributed to a Soviet researcher)
- The Soviet TG1 thermoelectric generator and the "Russian lamp"
- WWII Partisan Battery: design rationale and water-jacket heat-control principle
- 1959 semiconductor junction improvements and the Minnesota Mining Company AZC-12
- Nuclear RTG applications (Cassini probe, strontium-90, plutonium)
- Modern commercial Peltier/Seebeck devices: capabilities and temperature limitations
- Building a modern partisan battery from aluminium pans and Peltier modules
- Copper/copper-oxide thermocouple as a high-temperature alternative
- Series and parallel connection strategies for building usable voltage/current

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper wire and copper plate** — used for Seebeck-effect junctions and the copper-oxide thermocouple
- **Nichrome wire** — paired with copper to demonstrate the Seebeck effect
- **Platinum** — Seebeck's reference material for measuring thermoelectric coefficients
- **Zinc and nichrome** — used in early Soviet thermoelectric generators (pre-WWII)
- **Constantan (Constantin)** — metal used in original partisan battery thermocouple junctions
- **Nickel alloy semiconductor** — mentioned as the other element in original partisan battery junctions (~300 junctions total)
- **Semiconductor materials (P-type and N-type blocks)** — inside modern Peltier/Seebeck modules
- **Copper oxide (CuO / Cu₂O)** — black and red layers formed by flame-oxidising copper; forms the thermoelectric junction with bare copper
- **Graphite foil** — used as thermal interface material between module faces and pans
- **Thermal paste** — mentioned as an alternative to graphite foil for heat transfer
- **High-temperature fibre sleeving** — rated to ~1200 °C; sourced from toasters; used to insulate wiring from the metal pans
- **Aluminium** — material of the two cooking pans used in the build
- **Strontium-90 and plutonium** — fuels for nuclear RTGs mentioned in context of space applications
- **Kerosene** — fuel for the commercial "Russian lamp" (~$56 unit sold in remote Siberia/China)
- **Graphene** — noted as a future material under investigation for higher-efficiency thermoelectrics

## Techniques and Methods
- Twisting two dissimilar metal wires and heating the junction to demonstrate the Seebeck effect
- Water-jacket heat control: sandwiching thermoelectric modules between an inner water-filled pan (cold side capped at 100 °C) and an outer pan exposed to flame
- Bare crimping (instead of soldering) for electrical connections at high temperature
- Sleeving wire in high-temperature fibre insulation to prevent short-circuits against metal pans
- Drilling and bolting two aluminium pans together around the module stack
- Flame-oxidising copper wire or plate to grow a copper-oxide thermoelectric layer
- Point-contact thermocouple: a bent copper wire loop rested gently on an oxidised copper plate
- Capacitor charging to store and measure output from low-current thermoelectric sources
- Series/parallel connection strategies: paralleling junctions for current, series-connecting for voltage; or charging capacitors in parallel then discharging in series
