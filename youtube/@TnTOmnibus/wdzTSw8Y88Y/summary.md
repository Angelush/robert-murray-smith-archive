## Overview
"Man In A Shed - Episode 5" is a wide-ranging episode covering five distinct topics: solar energy and Prussian Blue photo-rechargeable batteries, solar desalination, electricity generation from plants, aluminium as a hydrogen fuel source, and unusual gear and power transmission mechanisms. Robert demonstrates several experiments himself — making Prussian Blue from scratch and building a working solar battery — while reviewing recent research papers across all topics. The overarching theme is simple, low-cost, and elegant engineering solutions to major energy and resource challenges.

## Key Topics
- Solar cell efficiency history: copper oxide (~4%), silicon (18–22%), perovskites (~25%), tandem cells (~30%), and the Shockley–Queisser theoretical limit (~33.7%)
- Prussian Blue as a self-recharging solar battery: bleaches white when discharging, returns to blue when exposed to sunlight
- Solar water desalination: simple solar stills, evaporation enhancement using Oasis foam and black carbon felt, and MIT's thermohaline circulation method to prevent salt clogging
- Three methods for extracting electricity from plants: microbial fuel cells at roots, direct photosynthesis from leaf cells, and transpiration-driven ion flow
- Aluminium's reactivity: stripping its oxide layer to react with water and generate hydrogen on demand
- Hydrogen-on-demand systems for vehicles: gallium–aluminium alloy method and the François Cornish patent (tested by BMW in 1981)
- Gear types: spur, bevel, worm, mangle, oval, cube, heart, brain, Borromean ring, and the Venix/ABM spherical gear
- Power transmission mechanisms: shaft couplings, chain/gear/belt drives, lead screws, the Coulisse (slide) mechanism, Hobson coupler, and spherical Coulisse drive

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium ferricyanide (K3)** — used with iron(II) sulfate to synthesise Prussian Blue
- **Potassium ferrocyanide (K4)** — alternative route to Prussian Blue, used with iron(III) chloride
- **Iron(II) sulfate** — iron salt / fertiliser used in Prussian Blue synthesis
- **Iron(III) chloride** — alternative iron salt for Prussian Blue synthesis
- **Potassium chloride (3 M solution)** — electrolyte in the Prussian Blue battery
- **Prussian Blue** (iron cyanide pigment) — photorechargeable battery active material; the basis of blueprints
- **Aluminium** — reactive metal used for hydrogen generation and as electrode in Prussian Blue cell
- **Potassium hydroxide / sodium hydroxide** — used to strip aluminium oxide coating and drive the aluminium–water reaction
- **Gallium** — mixed with aluminium to create an alloy that continuously exposes bare aluminium to water; reclaimed and reused
- **Mercury** — mentioned historically (used by Ørsted to separate aluminium; can strip oxide layer, but not recommended)
- **Indium, tin, bismuth** — mentioned as candidate alloying elements for aluminium hydrogen-on-demand systems
- **Nitrogen** — recent (as of 2024) additive to activate aluminium, improving hydrogen output
- **Platinum and iron electrodes** — used in leaf-based living solar cells
- **Activated carbon felt** — black material used to absorb solar energy and accelerate evaporation
- **Oasis foam** — floral foam used to wick water to the evaporating surface
- **Stainless steel mesh** — used as top electrode (light-transparent) in the Prussian Blue cell
- **Supercapacitor** — used to measure and store energy output from the Prussian Blue cell

## Techniques and Methods
- **Prussian Blue synthesis on filter paper**: dip coffee filter strip in potassium ferricyanide solution, dry, then dip in excess iron(II) sulfate solution — instant deep blue colour indicating insoluble Prussian Blue formation
- **Prussian Blue solar battery construction**: aluminium foil base → separator paper soaked in KCl electrolyte → Prussian Blue filter paper → stainless steel mesh top electrode; weighted for good contact
- **Solar recharging cycle**: battery discharges (Prussian Blue → Prussian White), then placed in sunlight to recharge (White → Blue)
- **Evaporation rate comparison**: two bowls of equal water (2 L each) placed in sun — one plain, one with Oasis foam + carbon felt — weighed hourly; carbon felt setup evaporated ~50% faster
- **MIT thermohaline solar still design**: exploits density difference between hot fresh water (rises) and cool brine (sinks) to continuously flush salt away from the evaporating surface, preventing clogging; projected yield ~4–6 L/hr per 0.5 m²
- **Aluminium–water hydrogen generation demonstration**: aluminium foil clipped to a motor, submerged in potassium hydroxide solution in a stainless steel pot; KOH strips oxide layer, aluminium reacts with water producing hydrogen, heat, and measurable electrical current driving the motor
- **3D-printed spherical Coulisse drive**: redesigned in Tinkercad, printed and assembled from 7 parts + 10 mm ball bearing; 2:1 torque ratio, motion transmitted at 90°
