## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the global fresh water scarcity problem and demonstrates that the atmosphere contains six times more water than all the world's rivers combined. He surveys both historical and modern methods of atmospheric water generation — from ancient Incan fog catchers to cutting-edge absorbent gels — and builds a simple working proof-of-concept device using a salvaged Peltier mini-fridge to condense water directly from the air.

## Key Topics
- Global fresh water scarcity (only ~3% of Earth's water is drinkable)
- Atmospheric water content (6× more water in the air than in all rivers)
- Historical fog-catching techniques (Incan civilisation, Peru/Lima)
- The Stenocara (Namib desert) beetle as inspiration for surface research
- Superhydrophobic surface design for water collection
- Dew point physics and condensation principles
- Air wells and passive condensation structures
- Active condensation (dehumidifier/refrigeration-style devices)
- Absorption-based atmospheric water generation
- DARPA-funded research for military field water supply
- Beth Koigi's "Majik Water" project in Kenya
- DIY Peltier-based water-from-air device build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Calcium chloride** — passive desiccant/absorbent sold commercially in the UK
- **Zeolite** — solid absorbent material used in atmospheric water generation
- **Silica gel** — desiccant commonly found in electronics packaging
- **Cellulose** — component of the DARPA-researched absorbent gel
- **Konjac gum (conjac gum)** — combined with cellulose to form the high-performance military water-harvesting gel; 15 L/kg/day at 30% humidity, 6 L/kg/day at 15% humidity
- **Brine** — liquid absorbent used in tower-based atmospheric water systems; related to desalination processes
- **Aluminium** — used as the cold-side block/heat-spreader on the Peltier device
- **Acrylic** — used for legs on the DIY device

## Techniques and Methods
- **Fog net / fog catching** — passive mesh nets that intercept fog droplets; used historically by the Incas and currently in Lima, Peru
- **Condensation / dew point harvesting** — cooling a surface below the local dew point to cause water to condense out of the air
- **Air wells** — passive structures designed to maximise dew-point condensation; modern examples in India paired with solar condensers
- **Dehumidifier / reverse air-conditioner condensation** — forcing air over a chilled surface to collect liquid water (used by Beth Koigi's Majik Water in Kenyan schools)
- **Peltier (thermoelectric) cooling** — solid-state temperature differential device used to chill a surface; demonstration device built from a salvaged Peltier mini-fridge with a CPU cooler heatsink attached to the cold side
- **Solar-driven condensers** — using photovoltaic power to run condensation systems off-grid
- **Solid desiccent absorption** — passive or forced-air passage through absorbent solids (zeolite, silica gel, calcium chloride, cellulose/konjac gel)
- **Liquid absorbent towers (brine drip towers)** — brine trickled down a tower absorbs atmospheric moisture; pure water then extracted by concentration
- **Sun/plastic-sheet solar regeneration** — drying a saturated absorbent material using sunlight under a plastic sheet to recover collected water
