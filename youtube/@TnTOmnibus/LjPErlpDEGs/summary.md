## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith designs and builds a DIY micro hydroelectric generator based on the Archimedes screw principle, inspired by a Japanese inventor's low-head water turbine. Using recycled CDs as screw flights and expanding foam for the housing, he constructs a working prototype that successfully generates 5.1 volts in a shallow stream. The video also includes a theoretical discussion on torque, generator matching, and energy scavenging philosophy.

## Key Topics
- Hydroelectric power generation from low-head, slow-flowing water
- The Archimedes screw as a reverse-operation generator (hydrodynamic turbine)
- A Japanese-designed micro turbine using the Archimedes screw for drainage ditches and emergency lighting
- Fabricating screw flights from recycled CDs (polycarbonate)
- Heat welding and epoxy-lapping techniques for joining CD flights
- Building a housing/channel from expanding polyurethane foam
- Testing in a garden hose, a small stream (5.1 V output), and ocean waves
- Torque vs. speed tradeoff in generators (high-torque slow vs. low-torque fast)
- Generator matching and using capacitors as a dissociation buffer
- Energy scavenging philosophy — harvesting ambient flows vs. centralised power

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polycarbonate CDs** — used as screw flight material; softens at ~140°C for forming
- **ABS pipe (40 mm diameter)** — central spindle/core for the screw
- **Aluminium rod** — used as the spindle/axle
- **Epoxy glue** — used to lap and bond CD flights together
- **Expanding polyurethane foam** — sprayed over a former to create the rigid channel housing
- **Polyethylene sheet (PETG/polyethylene terephthalate)** — used as a release liner to prevent foam adhesion to the paper former
- **Builder board / Cra-board** — cardboard-like former material for shaping the foam housing
- **Cellotape** — used to hold CD discs flat during heat welding
- **NEMA motor** — used as the generator (noted as a mismatch: low-torque, fast-spinning device on a high-torque slow machine)
- **Rectification circuit and capacitors** — used to condition the AC output and store charge

## Techniques and Methods
- **Flight calculator** — using online Archimedes screw flight calculators to determine the flat-cut profile
- **Enlarging and slotting CDs** — drilling the central hole from 15 mm to 40/48 mm and cutting a radial slot to increase flexibility
- **Heat welding with a soldering iron** — fusing adjacent CD disc edges together using a sacrificial piece of polycarbonate as filler material
- **Epoxy lap jointing** — overlapping CD edges and bonding with epoxy for cleaner, stronger joints (per community suggestion "Demon's 1988")
- **Paper former technique** — wrapping paper around the screw to define clearance before casting foam housing around it
- **Expanding foam casting** — spraying polyurethane foam over a formed assembly, then trimming after cure
- **Inclined channel construction** — building one leg longer than the other to create a natural slope without precise angle cutting
- **Capacitor buffering** — decoupling the generator load from the application load using a capacitor bank
- **Field testing** — testing in a garden stream (5.1 V), and exploratory wave testing at the seaside
