## Overview
"Man In A Shed - Episode 8" is a wide-ranging episode in which Robert covers six distinct science and engineering topics, each centred on energy generation or conversion. The through-line is his enthusiasm for open-access research and counter-intuitive engineering solutions — from soil-powered sensors to nuclear fusion milestones. His central takeaway is that simpler approaches (vertical panels, Fibonacci blade shapes, switched reluctance) often outperform complex ones.

## Key Topics
- **Microbial fuel cells** — how they work, and Northwestern McCormick School's new perpendicular 3D-printed design that outperforms competing designs by 120%
- **Fluorescent acrylic and 3D-printed fluorescent filament** as light guides/concentrators for solar cells
- **Concentrated solar photovoltaics (CSPv)** — HyperStealth's lenticular lens + mirror combination for boosting solar panel output
- **Vertical bifacial solar panels** — Dutch research showing temperature-driven efficiency gains from hanging panels vertically
- **Nuclear fusion breakthrough** — NIF's inertial confinement fusion experiment (confirming 2022 results) achieving net energy gain (~2× energy out vs. in)
- **Switched reluctance / flux-switching alternators** — the Alexanderson generator principle, guitar pickups, and missile power supplies
- **Spiral wind turbines** — Fibonacci/conical spiral Savonius-type blades, including the Liam F1 commercial turbine

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon felt** — used as electrodes in the Northwestern microbial fuel cell (cheap, abundant, inert)
- **Water-jetted stainless steel** — used for some parts of the Northwestern MFC prototype (noted as reproducible by hand with a hacksaw)
- **Fluorescent acrylic sheet** — acts as a luminescent solar concentrator / light guide
- **Fluorescent yellow PLA filament** (from 123-3D) — found to be transparent and act as an optical fibre / light guide when 3D printed
- **Lenticular / prism film** (salvaged from an LCD screen) — used to concentrate light onto a solar cell
- **Aluminium foil** — tested as a mirror substitute in the CSPv experiment
- **Deuterium and tritium** — hydrogen isotopes used as fusion fuel at NIF (220 micrograms per capsule)
- **High-density carbon capsule** (4.25 mg) — the fuel pellet housing in the NIF experiment
- **Steel / iron** — used in the switched reluctance demonstration to redirect magnetic flux

## Techniques and Methods
- **Microbial fuel cell construction** — perpendicular anode/cathode geometry (vs. traditional parallel), fully buried with a 3D-printed cap and air chamber for cathode ventilation
- **Luminescent solar concentrator** — fluorescent acrylic panel placed over a silicon solar cell to redirect and amplify incident light
- **3D-printed fluorescent prism** — right-angle prism printed at 100% infill in fluorescent filament to exploit total internal reflection over a solar cell (~10% power improvement demonstrated)
- **Lenticular lens + mirror stack** — prism film from an LCD combined with a bathroom mirror placed over a small solar panel (12% current increase measured)
- **Vertical bifacial solar panel installation** — East–West orientation, no tracking, passive cooling via temperature differential (3–5% efficiency gain)
- **Inertial confinement fusion (ICF)** — 192 high-power lasers fired at a hohlraum cylinder, compressing a DT fuel capsule to ~600 billion atmospheres and 151 million °C
- **Switched reluctance / flux-switching alternator demonstration** — moving a steel bar across a magnet-and-coil assembly to redirect magnetic flux and generate electricity without moving the magnet or coil
- **Fibonacci/conical spiral wind turbine fabrication** — cutting a flat Fibonacci spiral from sheet material, rolling it into a cone section, assembling three blades at 120° (or 3D printing); tested in outdoor wind conditions
