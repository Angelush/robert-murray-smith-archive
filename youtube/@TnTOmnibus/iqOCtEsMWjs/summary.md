## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates two accessible methods for making graphene at home or in small workshops: laser scribing onto kapton tape or borax-treated paper, and blending graphite with whey protein in water. The video argues that graphene's reputation as difficult and dangerous to produce is now outdated, and showcases impressive real-world applications including graphene-reinforced casein plastic and concrete that tested 58% stronger than conventional mixes.

## Key Topics
- History of graphene discovery (Geim & Novoselov, 2004 Nobel Prize 2010) and why it failed to achieve widespread adoption
- Traditional dangerous synthesis methods and why they held back the field
- Flash graphene via capacitor discharge (Rice University method) — powerful but expensive (~£3–4k capacitor bank)
- Laser scribing graphene onto kapton tape and borax-treated paper using a desktop diode laser
- Blending graphite with whey protein (bovine serum albumin + lactoglobulins) in water — the "kitchen chemistry" wet method
- Graphene-reinforced casein bioplastic and its stab/bullet resistance
- Graphene-enhanced concrete: 58% stronger, 7-day cure outperforming competitors' 28-day cure
- Dispersion challenges and master batching for plastic composites
- Environmental case for graphene concrete (using one-third less concrete)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** (150-micron flake, ~45–250 micron range acceptable) — feedstock for both methods
- **Kapton tape** — primary substrate for laser scribing graphene
- **Borax (sodium tetraborate)** — fire retardant soak for paper before laser scribing
- **Paper** — alternative laser scribing substrate after borax treatment
- **Whey protein powder** (chocolate-flavoured used; 71% protein by mass) — surfactant/stabiliser for wet blending method
- **Bovine serum albumin** — key active protein in the wet method
- **Lactoglobulins** — key active protein found in milk/whey
- **Casein** — used to make the bio-based plastic matrix; also present in milk but removed in whey
- **Deionised water** — solvent for wet blending (500 ml used)
- **Blood, eggs** — tested as protein sources; work but less effective than whey
- **Hemoglobin** — protein in blood tested as surfactant
- **Lysozyme / albumin** — proteins in egg white tested
- **Epoxy resin** — mentioned as a matrix material (used by Tech Ingredients)
- **98% sulfuric acid** — cited as the dangerous chemical in traditional Hummers-type graphene synthesis (not used here)
- **Potassium permanganate** — cited as explosive hazard in traditional synthesis (not used here)
- **Cement/concrete** — matrix for graphene dispersion application

## Techniques and Methods
- **Laser scribing (flash graphene via light):** Desktop diode laser (405–450 nm, ≥3.6 W optical power); multiple passes over substrate — first pass carbonises, second forms graphene, third promotes growth; focused beam gives spongy graphene, defocused gives needle-like growth; interdigitated electrode pattern drawn in software
- **Capacitor-discharge flash graphene:** Brass rods in glass tube, ~0.22 F at 400 V capacitor bank; heats sample to ~3000 K in ~10 ms (Rice University method — noted as impractical at home)
- **Wet kitchen blending:** Graphite + deionised water + whey protein blended ~30 minutes; foam/graphite allowed to settle; graphene layer collected as suspension (~7–8 mg/mL); stable ~2 months
- **Centrifugation:** 1500 RPM for 45 minutes to separate and dry graphene powder
- **Casein plastic fabrication:** Milk heated to precipitate casein, dried to powder, 0.5% graphene added, pressed in steel mould at 135°C under ~20 tonnes
- **Master batching:** Graphene compounded into virgin plastic pellets via extruder for uniform dispersion before dog-bone test samples produced
- **Concrete testing:** Standard 4×4×4 inch / 6×6×6 inch steel-mould cubes; 7-day and 28-day compression tests at a certified test house
- **Capacitance measurement:** Rigol DM3058 meter with aluminium contact strips; dry and electrolyte-wetted tissue paper separator tested
