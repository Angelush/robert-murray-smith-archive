## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the centuries-long history of solar thermal energy, arguing it has repeatedly been adopted during fuel crises only to be abandoned when easier options returned. Using this historical context as motivation, he demonstrates how to build practical solar thermal devices — a solar kettle and a solar barbecue — from cheap evacuated tubes and 3D-printed parts, and explores concentrated solar power techniques including parabolic reflectors and a DIY Fresnel lens made from water-filled plastic tubing.

## Key Topics
- Historical cycles of solar thermal adoption from ancient times through the Industrial Revolution to the present
- Why solar thermal is repeatedly overlooked in favour of solar PV despite its maturity and efficiency
- Evacuated solar thermal tubes: how they work, their efficiency (94–96%), and cost (~£10)
- Heat pipe principles and construction
- Building a 3D-printed solar kettle using an evacuated tube and a parabolic reflector
- Building a 3D-printed solar barbecue (same concept oriented horizontally)
- Large-scale concentrated solar power: heliostats, parabolic troughs, and solar furnaces
- Rice University nanoparticle steam generation research
- Stirling engines as an alternative to steam for solar thermal power
- Red River College flat-panel Stirling engine design
- Parabolic reflector construction methods (mylar vacuum forming, petal/flower fold-up, repurposed satellite dishes, woks, fire pits)
- Reflective materials for parabolic surfaces
- Fresnel lenses: history, principles, and a DIY version made from coiled polypropylene tube filled with water

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Evacuated solar thermal glass tubes** — vacuum-insulated tubes with a selective black coating for heat absorption
- **Barium (and alternatives: aluminium, sodium, potassium)** — used as the getter inside the vacuum tube to absorb residual oxygen
- **Copper pipe** — used for the heat pipe construction
- **Acetone (and isopropanol/alcohol)** — working fluid inside DIY heat pipes; evaporates at low temperature
- **Aluminium** — fin material inside tubes; also used as reflective sheeting (sticky-back HVAC tape, foil)
- **Transition metals (tungsten, cobalt, nickel) in a ceramic matrix** — the magnetron-sputtered selective absorber coating on evacuated tubes
- **Cadmium selenide** — mentioned in the history of the photovoltaic effect (1932)
- **Silicon** — first silicon solar cells (1954)
- **Metal and carbon nanoparticles (including graphene)** — Rice University research; absorb sunlight and transfer heat to water to generate steam at sub-zero ambient temperatures
- **Mylar sheet** — vacuum/pressure-formed parabolic reflectors
- **Polypropylene clear tube (5 mm, 7 m)** — coiled and water-filled to act as a DIY Fresnel lens
- **Rust-Oleum Mirror Effect spray paint** — used to create mirror finish on reflector guttering
- **Aluminium sticky-back tape (HVAC type)** — reflective surface for parabolic reflectors
- **Decorative metal foil (self-adhesive)** — alternative reflective material
- **Kitchen foil (extra strong)** — used with spray adhesive as a reflective surface
- **Stainless steel sheet** — bent into a food shelf for the solar barbecue
- **110 mm PVC guttering** — used as the curved reflector wings on the solar kettle/barbecue

## Techniques and Methods
- **Magnetron sputtering** — industrial process for depositing the multi-layer selective absorber coating on evacuated tube glass
- **Heat pipe fabrication** — capping copper pipe, adding acetone, heating to steam, crimping, and cooling to create a sealed two-phase heat transfer device
- **3D printing (FDM/filament)** — printing structural parts for the solar kettle and solar barbecue (files available on Tinkercad)
- **Solar kettle assembly** — mounting an evacuated tube in a 3D-printed holder with hinged parabolic reflector wings, 8 mm threaded rod, and a thermometer lid
- **Solar barbecue assembly** — same components as the kettle but oriented horizontally; includes a stainless steel food shelf
- **Parabolic reflector construction (petal/flower method)** — dividing a ~1 m square sheet into 16 sectors, drawing four circles (r = 75, 254, 400, 528 mm), cutting trapezoid petals with defined cut-outs, and folding up to approximate a parabola
- **Repurposing a fire pit as a parabolic dish** — applying reflective sticky-back material to a commercially available bowl-shaped fire pit
- **DIY Fresnel lens** — coiling 7 m of 5 mm clear polypropylene tube using double-sided tape, filling with water via syringe to create a refracting lens; demonstrated burning paper in sunlight
- **Finding focal point** — holding paper above a reflector/lens and moving it until the light spot is smallest/hottest
- **Rice University nanoparticle solar steam generation** — injecting nanoparticles into water and placing in focused sunlight to boil water even at ambient temperatures near 0 °C
- **Heliostat arrays** — mirrors that track the sun and focus energy onto a central boiler for large-scale steam/electricity generation
