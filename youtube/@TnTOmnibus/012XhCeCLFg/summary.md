## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the origins of mathematics from early nomadic human needs — counting time, animals, and resources — through the development of number systems, the abacus, and measurement, up to the Greeks' pivotal introduction of abstraction and formal proof. He argues that mathematics did not begin as abstract theory but emerged directly from practical survival needs, only becoming abstract when Greek culture demanded logical justification over priestly rules of thumb.

## Key Topics
- The distinction between **number** (discrete, unambiguous) and **measurement** (context-dependent, error-prone)
- The origin of number from the need to track time in nomadic life
- Tally sticks as the earliest form of recorded number
- The development of **bases** (groups of 5, 10, 20) from finger counting
- The independent invention of the **abacus** across cultures
- The **Indus Valley** origin of zero as a positional placeholder
- The transition from nomadic to settled town life (~5000–3000 BC) and the social classes that emerged
- **Taxation** as a driver of formal measurement and geometry
- The role of the **Priestly class** in keeping mathematical knowledge secret and as rules of thumb
- Egyptian and Babylonian practical geometry (triangles, area, volume)
- The approximation of **Pi as 22/7**, derived by trial and error over centuries
- The Greek demand for **proof** and the shift to abstraction
- Euclid's framework: defined terms, postulates, and justified methods
- The breakdown of flat-plane geometry upon discovering the Earth is a sphere

## Techniques and Methods
- **Tally sticks** — scratches on sticks or stones to record numbers
- **Pebble counting with positional placement** — precursor to the abacus
- **Abacus** — pebbles on sticks for calculation, invented independently worldwide
- **Stretched rope between pegs** — practical field technique for measuring triangular areas
- **Trial-and-error approximation of Pi** — iterative refinement over generations to reach ~22/7
- **Priestly rule-of-thumb scrolls** — recorded working formulas without derivation (examples held in the British Museum and Moscow museum)
- **Greek axiomatic proof method** — define terms, state postulates, justify methods
