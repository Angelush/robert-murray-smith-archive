## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith, filming during Storm Unice, explores generating electricity directly from falling rain using the triboelectric (static) charge naturally carried by raindrops. He replicates and extends a University of Hong Kong study showing that a simple capacitor made from aluminium foil and PTFE tape can produce surprisingly large voltages when water rolls across it, ultimately building a rotating wheel version capable of harvesting energy from flowing water.

## Key Topics
- Triboelectric electricity and how raindrops carry natural charge
- University of Hong Kong research achieving ~50 W/m² from rain (vs ~150 W/m² for solar)
- DIY replication of a rain energy harvester using kitchen foil and plumber's PTFE tape
- Improved version using conductive ink and polyurethane varnish as the insulating layer
- Scaling to a rotating water wheel with slip rings and brush contacts
- Parallel vs series connection of cells

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Kitchen aluminium foil** — used as the conductor layer
- **PTFE tape (plumber's tape)** — used as the insulating/dielectric layer; same material as non-stick frying pan coating
- **Indium tin oxide (ITO)** — conductor used in the University of Hong Kong device
- **Polyethylene/PTFE** — insulator layer in the original research device
- **Conductive ink** — painted onto aluminium to improve surface conductivity (see his video #1381)
- **Polyurethane varnish (outdoor grade)** — dip-coated over conductive ink as a waterproof insulating layer, replacing the need for PTFE tape
- **Aluminium ducting tape** — used for electrodes on the wheel version
- **8 mm copper pipe** — used as slip rings on the wheel axle
- **Tap water / rainwater / seawater** — tested; tap and rainwater outperformed seawater

## Techniques and Methods
- Layered capacitor construction: plastic substrate → aluminium foil (conductor) → PTFE tape (insulator) → small electrode contact
- Double-sided tape lamination to assemble layers
- Conductive ink painting onto aluminium as an alternative conductor surface
- Dip-coating in polyurethane varnish to waterproof and simultaneously provide the insulating layer (replacing PTFE tape)
- Voltage measurement via multimeter with phone camera for recording
- Wheel construction: 8 cells mounted on a plastic disc, wired in parallel, with copper-pipe slip rings and wire-brush contacts for rotary electrical pick-off
- Water wheel rotation test in a still-water bath to confirm capacitive triboelectric generation from relative water/surface motion

## Robert's Own Replies
- Robert clarifies that charge transfer from the raindrop to the capacitor **does not require direct contact** — it works by **induction**. This is an important correction to a common misconception and explains why the mechanism differs from simple conductive charge transfer.
