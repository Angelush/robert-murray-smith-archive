## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores sand batteries as a practical and cheap method of thermal energy storage, demonstrating a DIY version using a kettle heating element buried in sand, and building a rocket stove with a sand-filled gas cylinder as a mass heater. He argues that storing energy as heat — rather than in lithium batteries — is underutilised despite being one of the oldest and most cost-effective solutions, noting the concept predates the Finnish commercialisation by at least 40 years.

## Key Topics
- How sand batteries work as thermal energy stores (analogous to a hot water bottle)
- Ohm's law and resistive heating elements: voltage, current, resistance, and power calculations
- DIY sand battery using a kettle element and cake tin
- Retrieving stored energy via Peltier devices, Stirling engines, or copper coil heat exchangers
- Building a rocket stove from square steel tube using a 1:2:4 golden ratio
- Converting a gas cylinder into a sand-filled mass heater
- Heat capacity comparison: sand/clay/concrete (~850 J/kg/°C) vs. water (~4,000 J/kg/°C)
- Historical context: NASA research from 1979 on sand thermal storage
- Finnish commercial sand battery: first to market, not first to invent
- Ground-scale thermal storage (buried sand/gravel beds) as a home-scale heat pump alternative
- Sand scarcity concerns and desert sand (UAE) as a viable, abundant alternative
- Zeolite/calcium chloride moisture-absorption systems as another thermal storage option

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sand** — primary thermal storage medium; can withstand 500–1200°C
- **Resistance wire (Nichrome/kanthal-type)** — heating element wire from toasters, ovens, kilns, vapes
- **Magnesium oxide** — insulating filler inside the kettle heating element sheath
- **Stainless steel** — outer sheath of the kettle heating element
- **Copper** — coil used to extract heat from a sand battery via water circulation
- **Zeolite / calcium chloride** — mentioned as an alternative thermochemical storage system
- **Clay and concrete** — noted as comparable thermal storage materials (~850 J/kg/°C)
- **Pine wood** — fuel used in the rocket stove burn test (~3–4 kg used)
- **4×4 (100×100 mm), 3 mm thick square steel tube** — rocket stove construction material
- **Propane/gas cylinder** — repurposed as the outer shell of the mass heater

## Techniques and Methods
- **Resistive heating** — passing current through resistance wire to generate heat; Ohm's law used to calculate operating parameters at different voltages
- **DIY sand battery assembly** — embedding a kettle heating element in sand inside a cake tin, powered at 12V DC
- **Peltier device heat-to-electricity conversion** — placing a Peltier module between the hot sand battery and a cold aluminium block to drive a small motor
- **Rocket stove fabrication** — cutting and welding square steel tube to the 1:2:4 combustion chamber ratio; adding an air intake damper flap
- **Gas cylinder preparation** — emptying, filling with water, draining before cutting or welding
- **Sand-filling a gas cylinder** — pouring ~40 kg of kiln-dried sand into a modified gas cylinder rocket stove to create a mass heater
- **Thermal cutoff switches** — wiring a bimetallic thermal cutoff (e.g., 200°C) or microcontroller temperature controller in series with the heating supply
- **Wind turbine dump load integration** — replacing a resistor dump load with a sand-embedded heating element to capture otherwise-wasted energy
- **Polystyrene wire cutting** — demonstrating resistance wire heat by cutting polystyrene at 12V
