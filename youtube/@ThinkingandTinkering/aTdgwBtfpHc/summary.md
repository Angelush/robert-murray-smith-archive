## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed nutation drive — a compact reduction gear based on the phenomenon of nutation (simultaneous wobbling and rotation). He explains the mechanism's history, traces it to Peter Davidson's 1973 patent, walks through the assembly of a four-bevel-gear system he designed in Tinkercad, and reflects on why a demonstrably superior mechanism never displaced the planetary gear systems found in servo motors.

## Key Topics
- The difference between pure wobble (used in wobble motors/pumps) and nutation (wobble + rotation)
- Peter Davidson's 1973 patent for the nutation drive as a reduction gear
- How gear ratio is determined by both tooth count *and* mutation angle (unlike spur gears)
- Four-bevel-gear design: two fixed outer gears differing by one tooth (41 vs 42), an offset axle barrel angled at 10°
- Assembly walkthrough of the 3D-printed model — spacers, pins, super glue
- Demonstration achieving approximately 40:1 gear reduction
- Why better technology doesn't always win: incumbent manufacturing lines, risk aversion, established history of planetary gears
- Robert's broader theme of revisiting obscure, forgotten inventions that may solve modern problems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Super glue (cyanoacrylate) — used to fix gear components during assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D modelling in Tinkercad; files shared on Thingiverse
- FDM 3D printing of bevel gears, spacers, axle, and handle components
- Manual assembly: press-fit spacers, pin-through holes for gear alignment, super glue for fixed joints
- Offsetting the axle barrel by 10° to create the mutation angle (typically 1–5°; increased here for camera visibility)
- Gear ratio verification by marking a dot and counting output rotation per input revolution

## Robert's Own Replies
Most of Robert's replies are brief acknowledgements ("cheers mate", "awesome", etc.), but a few add useful context:

- **Camera visibility choice:** He confirms that using a 10° mutation angle (rather than the typical 1–5°) was a deliberate decision purely to make the motion easier to see on camera, acknowledging a commenter was correct to flag it.
- **Related videos:** He points viewers to his own video **2390 "The Compact Gear"** and a video on the **"perpetual wedge"** as related approaches to the same gear-reduction problem — "unsurprisingly, lots of ways to solve this problem."
- **Nomenclature:** He notes that which term you use (e.g. "nutation" vs alternatives) likely depends on which engineering field you come from.
- **Motorising the model:** He confirms it would be possible to motorise the printed model if a commenter wanted to do so.
