## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make homemade soldering flux after failing to find any in his lab. Using just three common ingredients mixed with deionized water and heated to consistency, he produces a flux suitable for use on a range of metals. The video serves as a quick practical guide ahead of a follow-up video where he attempts to solder copper wire to stainless steel.

## Key Topics
- Why homemade flux is a practical alternative when commercial flux is unavailable
- The three-ingredient recipe for a general-purpose soldering flux
- Suitable metals for the resulting flux (silver, copper, bronze, brass, nickel)
- Brief mention of the follow-up stainless steel soldering project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Boric acid (H3BO3)** — 3 teaspoons; primary flux ingredient
- **Trisodium phosphate / TSP** — 2 teaspoons; flux ingredient; available at Home Depot or eBay
- **Borax (sodium borate, Na2B4O7)** — 2 teaspoons; flux ingredient
- **Deionized water** — ~100 ml; used to dissolve the mixture

## Techniques and Methods
- Combining dry ingredients (boric acid, TSP, borax) by teaspoon measure
- Dissolving in ~100 ml deionized water and mixing
- Heating the mixture on a heat source, boiling until the desired thickness/consistency is reached
- Allowing to cool before potting/storing

## Robert's Own Replies
- Clarifies the chemistry: borax is sodium borate (Na2B4O7) and boric acid is H3BO3 — they are distinct compounds, not the same thing.
- TSP is readily available from Home Depot (sold in bags) or eBay.
- Encourages making/synthesising your own reagents, noting you learn a lot from the process.
- Confirms rosin (available as a powder) can also be used as a flux alternative.
- Points viewers to the companion video on soldering copper to stainless steel for a practical demonstration of this flux in use.
- Advises building up a chemical stock gradually by buying what you need as you need it, rather than all at once.
