## Overview
Robert demonstrates a new physical implementation of his "power amplifier" concept, built while waiting for additional magnets to arrive. The setup uses a coil wound around a laminate steel core with opposing-polarity magnets, showing that a pulse of DC current produces significant mechanical deflection only when the magnets are present — which he presents as evidence that the magnetic field is amplifying the output energy relative to the electrical input.

## Key Topics
- Construction of a simple electromagnetic actuator using salvaged materials
- Demonstration that the magnets are essential to producing mechanical output (deflection)
- Comparison of output with and without magnets in place
- Plans to test the device with a sine wave input instead of pulse DC
- Framing the device as a possible "power amplifier" — generating useful mechanical work from battery input

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Spring metal strip (salvaged from a bandsaw blade)
- Laminate steel block (salvaged from a microwave oven transformer core)
- Wound coil (wrapped around the laminate block)
- N42 magnets — 1 cm × 2.2 cm, arranged in opposing polarities (north–south)
- 6-volt battery
- Plastic block (used as a mounting chassis)

## Techniques and Methods
- Pulse DC applied to a coil to induce deflection in a spring-metal reed
- Controlled comparison: device tested with magnets present vs. magnets removed
- Visual/mechanical deflection used as a proxy for output power
- Planned extension: substituting a sine wave signal to observe bidirectional deflection
