## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the construction and testing of a compact, floating solar still designed to produce fresh water from contaminated sources such as muddy ponds or seawater. The device uses oasis (floral foam) to wick water upward through carbon felt, which promotes evaporation; condensation then collects on a transparent cover and drains into a pipe. Testing on a partly sunny English day yielded approximately 100 ml/hour from an A4-sized footprint — which Robert notes is a strong result for such simple, cheap materials.

## Key Topics
- Follow-up to a previous experiment using oasis and carbon felt to enhance evaporation
- Design and construction of a portable, floating solar still
- Role of the Fresnel lens (and its limitations) as a condensing cover
- Wicking action of oasis (floral foam) and evaporation via carbon felt
- Experimental results: ~100 ml/hour under partly cloudy English conditions
- Potential use cases: survival, boating, field water purification
- Identified improvements: angle optimisation, use of glass, adding a reflector

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oasis (floral foam)** — used as a wicking medium to draw contaminated water upward
- **Carbon felt** — placed on top of the oasis to promote evaporation
- **Fresnel lens (spot type)** — initially tested as the condensing cover; found ineffective once fogged with condensation
- **Lenticular Fresnel lens** — also tested; performed similarly to plain plastic
- **Plain plastic sheet** — ultimately found to work as well as the lenses for condensation collection
- **Builders board (foamex/PVC board)** — used as the still body; acts as an insulator and is white for partial reflection
- **Plastic pipe** — used as the collection/drainage channel for condensed water
- **Rubber pipe + glass jar** — proposed adaptation for floating deployment at sea
- **Sealant** — used to seal the Fresnel lens into the frame

## Techniques and Methods
- Wicking evaporation: oasis draws water up, carbon felt accelerates surface evaporation
- Passive solar condensation: water vapour rises and condenses on the cooler cover, then drips into a collection pipe
- Shading one side of the still to maintain a temperature differential between the evaporation and condensation zones
- Comparative lens testing: spot Fresnel vs. lenticular Fresnel vs. bare plastic sheet
- Angle optimisation of the condensing surface to ensure droplets run down rather than pooling and dripping back
- Floating deployment concept: device placed directly on a contaminated water body with no preparation
