## Overview
Robert salvages a broken rotary fan heater from dumpster diving and uses its well-balanced rotor to experiment with three different small wind turbine generator designs. He tests a pancake (Tesla) coil, a standard coil-and-magnet arrangement, and a switch reluctance approach using steel laminates. The standard magnet-and-coil setup performs best for lighting LEDs, while switch reluctance is highlighted as the most promising direction for future development.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging components from discarded appliances (dumpster diving)
- Three generator configurations compared: pancake coil, standard coil with magnets, switch reluctance
- Pancake coil winding technique and its disappointing performance
- Standard rotor-mounted magnets with coil producing usable voltage (~22V)
- Switch reluctance generators: redirecting magnetic flux using steel laminates instead of permanent magnets
- Advantages of switch reluctance motors/generators (lightweight, fast-spinning, cheap, durable)
- Teaser for an upcoming switch reluctance motor project (the "mini")

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium or similar magnets (north-south arrangement, 8 magnets on rotor)
- Enamelled copper wire (described as fairly thick gauge)
- Super glue (used to hold the pancake coil winding)
- Steel laminates from a microwave oven transformer (thin single-laminate steel strips)
- Plastic board (used as cradle for the magnet arrangement)
- Wood block and bolt (coil winding former)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of consumer electronics for component harvesting
- Winding a single-layer pancake (Tesla) coil using a wooden former with bolt and plastic guide
- Securing coil windings with super glue and releasing from former
- Mounting magnets in alternating north-south polarity on a rotor disc
- Testing output with a multimeter (voltage and current modes)
- Using a hair dryer as a controlled wind substitute for bench testing
- Driving an LED panel (~36 LEDs) as a load test
- Switch reluctance demonstration: steel laminate strips in a crisscross-of-eight pattern passing through a stationary magnetic field to redirect flux
