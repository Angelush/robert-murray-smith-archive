## Overview
Robert tests a Chinese diesel heater running on a homemade fuel blend — used cooking oil clarified with gelatin and mixed with 20% paraffin — as a cheap alternative to diesel. He runs the heater for approximately half an hour, demonstrating a clean, odour-free burn with good heat output. The main takeaway is that this DIY fuel mix (costing roughly 30–50p per litre vs. £1.80/L for diesel) works well in these simple heaters, making them a viable and affordable heating solution.

## Key Topics
- Overview of how Chinese diesel heaters work, and their similarity to the Bulerjahn (Buljan) stove principle — isolated combustion chamber with separate room-air heat exchanger
- Safety advantages of the heater design: combustion gases are fully separated from room air
- Previous video series context: teardown of the diesel heater (video 1682) and preparation of the cooking oil fuel (video 1784)
- Sourcing used cooking oil cheaply from a local pop-up restaurant at 10p/litre
- Cost comparison: homemade fuel at ~30–50p/L vs. diesel at ~£1.80/L
- Test run results: clean exhaust, no smell during operation, sustained heat output
- Practical considerations: viscosity, pump compatibility, coking risk, ease of cleaning the burn chamber
- UK legal classification of the heater as a portable heater

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Used cooking oil** — sourced from a neighbour's pop-up restaurant at 10p/litre; used as the primary fuel base
- **Paraffin (kerosene)** — added at 20% by volume to reduce viscosity and improve pumpability; brings fuel cost to ~40p/litre
- **Gelatin** — used in the prior video (1784) to clarify/filter the used cooking oil
- **Diesel** — the standard fuel the heater is designed for, priced at ~£1.80/litre in the UK at time of filming
- **Stainless steel flexible hose** — supplied with the heater for routing exhaust and intake

## Techniques and Methods
- **Clarifying used cooking oil with gelatin** — to remove suspended solids and food residue (detailed in video 1784)
- **Blending oil with paraffin** — 80% used cooking oil / 20% paraffin to lower viscosity for reliable pump operation
- **Visual exhaust assessment** — checking for invisible exhaust as a sign of complete combustion
- **Outdoor test run on car battery** — running the heater on 12V DC from a car battery with exhaust unpiped to inspect combustion quality
- **Disassembly and reassembly** — full teardown for inspection described in prior video 1682; noted as easy to clean if coking occurs

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Paraffin = kerosene**: Clarifies these are the same substance with different names (paraffin in Europe, kerosene in the USA) — mentioned multiple times in replies.
- **Cold weather performance**: States the fuel mix is rated down to **−8°C**.
- **Fuel nozzle size**: Replies **2–5mm** when asked.
- **Methanol incompatibility**: Does not think methanol and cooking oil mix.
- **Pump type**: His unit has a **diaphragm pump** (notes this may vary by model).
- **Manufacturer specs**: Repeatedly directs people to check the manufacturer's data sheet for their specific model regarding heat output, fuel consumption, and power draw.
- **Oil cleaning important**: When someone reports problems, asks *"what kind of used oil and did you clean the oil first?"* — implying cleaning is critical.
- **Long-term follow-up**: Acknowledges he hasn't tested long-term coking but intends to; notes that series follow-up videos tend to get low views.
- **Not his original idea**: Credits the cooking oil clarification method to existing cooking/food-processing resources; he simply applied it to heating fuel.
- **Used motor oil**: Expresses uncertainty — "used motor oil is pretty dirty."
- **Waste heat recovery**: Agrees with commenters who suggest capturing waste heat from the exhaust, calling it an interesting idea worth exploring.
