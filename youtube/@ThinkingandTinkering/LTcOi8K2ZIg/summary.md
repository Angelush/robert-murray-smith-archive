## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history and practicality of compressed air engines, tracing their origins from 17th-century demonstrations to 19th-century street transport. He builds and demonstrates a simple 3D-printed single-acting compressed air engine remixed from an existing design, showing it can be powered by breath alone. The video highlights how steam and internal combustion engines can be converted to run on compressed air with minimal modifications.

## Key Topics
- History of alternative engines (steam, compressed air, gunpowder, liquid air, ammonia)
- Advantages and disadvantages of compressed air as a power source
- 3D-printed compressed air engine build (remixed from a design by "newly")
- Assembly walkthrough of the single-acting engine
- Converting steam and internal combustion engines to run on compressed air
- Off-the-shelf air engine demonstration (eBay, ~£20)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Compressed air (working fluid)
- M8 nuts (used as flywheel ballast weights)
- Glue (for assembly of piston hook and feed spigot)
- Teac cut / cutting compound (mentioned as an option to ease tight-fitting parts)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing engine components from STL files (remixed design)
- Sanding pistons and rotary valves for smooth, tight fit
- Assembly of single-acting piston engine (piston, barrel, flywheel, rotary valve, feed spigot)
- Breath-powered demonstration of the engine
- Converting IC engines to air engines via slide valve substitution
- Hand-pump pressurisation of a commercial air engine toy
