## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith replicates an axial magnetic gear design originally demonstrated by William Fraser, building it from simple materials (builders board, bolts, and permanent magnets). He demonstrates that two magnetic discs with differing pole pairs create a contactless gear reduction, and has a key insight: by fixing one disc and using electromagnets to create a rotating magnetic field, the design could form a novel hub motor.

## Key Topics
- Axial magnetic gear replication (credit to William Fraser)
- Pole pair arithmetic: adding pole pairs produces opposite rotation; subtracting produces same-direction rotation
- 8:1 gear reduction ratio demonstrated physically
- Hub motor concept — using electromagnetic coils to replace the manually rotated disc
- Potential applications: hub motors for wheels, wind turbine generators, electronic transmissions with variable gear ratios

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Builders board (identified in comments as uPVC-coated foam board used for roofline fascia and soffit)
- Steel (glued onto the discs as pole pieces)
- Permanent magnets (neodymium implied; 16 total on the 8-pole-pair disc, 2 on the 1-pole-pair disc)
- Bolts (9 bolts to match 9 pole pairs in the rotor disc)
- Bearings and axle hardware

## Techniques and Methods
- Low-fidelity physical mockup / rapid prototyping with builders board
- Pole pair counting and arithmetic to determine gear ratio and rotation direction
- Manual demonstration of contactless magnetic torque transmission
- Conceptual substitution: replacing manual rotation with electromagnetic coil-driven rotating field to realise a motor
- FEMM (Finite Element Method Magnetics) modelling mentioned in comments as the intended next step for field analysis

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Pole pair clarification:** "7 and 9 mate — 8 and 1 pole pairs — so add pole pairs together for one direction and subtract for the other direction."
- **Motor/transmission potential:** Confirmed the design could serve as both a hub motor and an electronic transmission; notes that using electromagnets as the pole pairs would allow active, variable gear ratio changes.
- **Material identification:** The board is "uPVC coated foam board used for roofline, things like fascia and soffit — cuts easily, is rigid and great for making mock-ups."
- **Next steps — FEMM modelling:** "I tend to use FEMM to model the fields" and "you can FEMM model it mate"; also considered salvaging silicone steel from a MOT (microwave oven transformer) for a more serious build.
- **Honest assessment of efficiency:** "To be honest mate the real question is will it work at all — lol" and "I don't know — it is just a model of an axial magnetic gear mate."
- **Comparison to existing designs:** Distinguished the concept from axial flux induction motors, noting it is "similar but not quite."
- **Encouragement to community:** Repeatedly encouraged viewers to try the build themselves and take the idea further.
