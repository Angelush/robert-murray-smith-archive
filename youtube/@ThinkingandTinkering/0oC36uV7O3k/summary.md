## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple adsorption chiller built from two paint cans connected by a tube, filled with water and zeolite respectively. By pulling a vacuum, water evaporates at ambient temperature in one can (cooling it) while the vapor is absorbed by the zeolite in the other (heating it). He explains how this same principle underpins desalination, solid-state Stirling engines, and thermal batteries.

## Key Topics
- How adsorption/evaporation chillers work (vacuum-driven phase change)
- Why water boils at lower temperatures under reduced pressure
- Using the system as a passive, electricity-free chiller
- Thermal regeneration and use as a thermal battery
- Desalination via low-pressure evaporation of salt water
- Solid-state Stirling engines as a related concept
- Alternative working pairs for adsorption systems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — the evaporating refrigerant in the cold can
- **Zeolite** — the adsorbent in the hot can; described as a clay, available online
- **Silica gel** — mentioned as an alternative adsorbent paired with water
- **Lithium chloride** — alternative adsorbent/working fluid
- **Lithium bromide** — alternative adsorbent/working fluid
- **Calcium chloride** — alternative adsorbent/working fluid
- **Activated carbon** — alternative adsorbent, paired with methanol
- **Methanol** — alternative refrigerant paired with activated carbon
- **Metal hydrides / hydrogen** — mentioned as another working pair
- **Silicone sealant** — used to make tube-to-can joints airtight
- **Fiberglass** — used by Robert as his preferred sealant alternative
- **Rubber balloons** — placed over paint can lids to help maintain vacuum seal

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling stepped pilot holes (2 mm → 6 mm → 10 mm) in thin tin to avoid deforming the material
- Filing drilled holes smooth with a needle file for a tight tube fit
- Sealing tube joints with silicone sealant or fiberglass resin
- Pulling a vacuum with a low-cost vacuum pump (~18 mmHg / ~30 mbar) to initiate evaporation
- Thermal regeneration: applying heat to the zeolite can drives water vapor back to the water can, resetting the cycle
- Using the device for desalination by evaporating salt water and collecting condensed pure water vapor
- Demonstrating temperature differential with conical flasks for clearer visual observation

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief acknowledgements, but a few contain substantive points:
- **Cycling for continuous cooling**: He notes you never need to replace the working materials — just keep heating and cooling in a cycle. Using three units in rotation would provide cold continuously.
- **Material choice guidance**: He declines to design a specific system for users but advises that material selection depends on local availability, ease of working, and how "green" you want to be — all materials have drawbacks and you work within those limits.
- **Framing as a battery**: He explicitly describes the device as "a kind of battery… stored energy."
- **Acknowledges the visual dullness**: He jokes it's a pity the device looks so unimpressive given how interesting the underlying concepts are.
