## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make any pair of gloves compatible with capacitive touchscreens — motivated by the inconvenience of removing motorcycle gloves to answer his phone in cold weather. By mixing conductive ink with a latex-based adhesive, painting the fingertips, letting it dry, then peeling off the latex layer and washing the gloves, a thin invisible conductive film is left behind that enables touchscreen interaction.

## Key Topics
- Problem of non-touchscreen-compatible gloves in cold weather and while motorcycling
- Making gloves touchscreen-compatible using conductive ink and a peelable adhesive
- Demonstrating the before/after on a smartphone touchscreen
- Comparing treated vs. untreated fingers on the same glove

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — the primary active ingredient; available from Robert's store (ecko.com implied)
- **Copydex adhesive** — a latex-based adhesive used as the binder/transfer medium
- **Soapy water** — used in the final step to remove latex residue from the glove surface
- **Graphene** — Robert's hypothesis for what the conductive ink leaves behind on the glove after the latex is peeled off

## Techniques and Methods
- Mixing conductive ink and latex adhesive in approximately a 1:1 ratio (slightly more ink than adhesive)
- Painting the mixture onto fingertips of gloves with a paintbrush
- Allowing the mixture to dry fully before proceeding
- Peeling off the dried latex layer to leave a conductive residue bonded to the glove fabric
- Washing fingertips with soapy water to remove remaining latex residue
