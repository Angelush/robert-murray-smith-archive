## Overview
Robert takes the "Perpetual Wedge" mechanism from video 2310 — an external gear system achieving a 100:1 gear ratio from just three parts — and adapts it into an internal ring-gear configuration suitable for clockmaking. By converting the external spur-gear design into a pair of internal ring gears (60 and 61 teeth), he eliminates the elliptical motion problem and produces a compact, concentric 60:1 reduction mechanism. The video illustrates a general principle: understanding what a mechanism *does* allows you to adapt it into the specific form your application requires.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the Perpetual Wedge mechanism from video 2310
- How a one-tooth difference between two gears produces a large gear ratio (100:1 example)
- Why clock mechanisms need 60:1 and 3600:1 reductions (60 seconds/minute, 60 minutes/hour)
- The principle that motion is relative — an external mechanism can be "turned inside out"
- Conversion from external spur gears to internal ring gears to achieve concentric rotation
- Using a cam to drive the intermediate gear in an elliptical path inside the ring gears
- 3D printing and assembly of the final mechanism
- Planned application of this mechanism in a simple clock build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed PLA or similar filament (implied by printing process)
- Thermoplastic copolymer (mentioned in comments as a material Robert is about to experiment with)

## Techniques and Methods
- Designing ring gears and cam in **Tinkercad** (60-tooth and 61-tooth ring gears)
- 3D printing all components (ring gears with base plates, cam, drive shaft, handle, clip)
- Cam-driven mechanism: a 40 mm central hole in the intermediate gear, with an off-centre cam forcing elliptical travel around the ring gears
- Gear tooth profile: involute profile, module 2, pitch angle adjusted from the standard 20° to 30°
- Tooth profile modification on the smaller ring gear: topmost 1 mm removed from teeth to prevent jamming
- Clip-based assembly to hold the stack together

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Clock gearing clarification**: Confirmed that traditional clocks do *not* use this system — they use a chain of spur gears for reduction. This mechanism is his own minimalist alternative.
- **Tooth profile detail**: The gears use an involute tooth profile, module 2. He deliberately moved the pitch angle from 20° (standard) to 30° to eliminate jamming in the internal configuration, and removed the topmost 1 mm from the teeth on the smaller ring gear — changes he had to make to get it working reliably for a clock.
- **Hand-setting mechanism**: He sees two options for moving clock hands — friction fit or dog clutches — and is considering selector forks on a single arm as a flip-switch to disengage gears.
- **Next steps**: The next iteration will be motor-driven (several viewers requested this), which he'll use to demonstrate motor drives and control before returning to weights and springs.
- **Materials**: He hasn't tried it yet but is about to experiment with thermoplastic copolymer.
- **Personal note**: Several replies suggest Robert is going through a period of personal loss ("throwing myself into the work so I don't spend too much time thinking about other things"; "she is always with me x"), indicating the work itself is carrying emotional significance.
