## Overview
Robert demonstrates wand (brush) electroplating as a simpler alternative to bath plating, using zinc as the plating metal on conductive HDPE plastic. The video is a preparatory step for a separate project building a "sunflower motor," showing how wand plating can metallize plastic surfaces with minimal equipment and cost.

## Key Topics
- What wand/brush electroplating is and how it differs from bath plating
- Why wand plating is more accessible for small-scale or one-off projects
- Common applications: gold-plating phone cases, battery terminals, small decorative items
- Using conductive HDPE as the substrate
- Importance of surface preparation and even current distribution
- Safety considerations when handling plating solutions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — filled with conductive material, ~100 ohms/square surface resistance; used as the substrate
- **Zinc** — the anode metal, connected to the positive terminal; also available as sacrificial anodes from boatyards
- **Bright zinc wand plating solution** — proprietary electrolyte solution sold in Robert's shop; described as producing a bright, shiny finish
- **Isopropyl alcohol** — used to degrease the surface before plating
- **Kitchen foil (aluminium foil)** — used as a ground plane to ensure even current density across the substrate
- **Cloth/fabric strip** — used as the wand wrap to hold electrolyte and prevent shorting

## Techniques and Methods
- **Wand/brush electroplating** — rubbing a cloth-wrapped metal anode soaked in electrolyte over the workpiece (cathode) connected to the negative terminal
- **Ground plane preparation** — wrapping the substrate in kitchen foil connected to the negative to ensure even current distribution
- **Surface degreasing** — wiping with isopropyl alcohol before plating to avoid adhesion defects
- **Voltage control** — starting at 5V, increasing to 10V as needed; monitoring wand temperature to avoid overheating
- **Metallizing plastic** — applying a conductive zinc film to non-metallic substrates as an alternative to vacuum deposition
