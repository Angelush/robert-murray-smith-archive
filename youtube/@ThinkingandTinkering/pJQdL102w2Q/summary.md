## Overview
In Part 3 of his Wimshurst machine build series, Robert Murray-Smith constructs the body and drive mechanism of his electrostatic generator. He assembles the frame using repurposed mahogany coffee table components, fabricates a twisted belt pulley drive system from leather boot laces, and brings the machine to a near-complete state — with only the collector combs and neutralizing bars remaining for the next video.

## Key Topics
- Designing and building the central drive pulley axle from stacked mahogany discs
- Assembling the uprights from repurposed coffee table legs
- Choosing a traditional twisted belt (figure-of-eight) drive over a gear/cog system for aesthetic reasons
- Fabricating drive belts from leather boot laces, including joining technique
- Mounting the Leyden jar capacitors and connecting them with a brass bar
- Overview of remaining steps (collector combs, neutralizing bars)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Mahogany** — sourced from a repurposed coffee table; used for pulley discs, uprights, and support pieces
- **Leather boot laces** — used as drive belts; noted as traditionally appropriate material
- **Superglue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to hold belt joints in registration before sewing
- **Cotton thread** — used to sew and whip the drive belt joints
- **Brass bar** — used to connect the two Leyden jar capacitors at the base
- **Rubber** — applied to feet for grip; mentioned as an alternative belt material
- **Plastic drive belt / heat-weld strip** — mentioned as a vegan-friendly alternative to leather

## Techniques and Methods
- Stacking and gluing mahogany discs to build a correctly spaced central pulley axle
- Gluing and dowelling coffee table legs together to form uprights, with one left non-permanent for disassembly
- Measuring drive belt length by looping around pulleys and accounting for stretch
- Configuring one belt as a straight loop and one as a figure-of-eight to achieve contra-rotation
- Pre-drilling 1 mm holes through leather before sewing belt joints
- Sewing 4–5 stitches through the belt join, then whipping with cotton and finishing with superglue on the knot
- Sourcing aesthetically suitable parts opportunistically (e.g., angled cutting board found on sale)

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief social exchanges. A few notable points:
- He confirms he plans to **run a motor** with the finished machine.
- He emphasizes the machine is **"made half for the look of it and half for the use of it"** — aesthetics are a deliberate priority alongside function.
- He describes his design sensibility with: **"I like to think I have a bit of an eye for design."**
- He jokes that **"no self-respecting mad scientist should be without one"** — referencing the Wimshurst machine.
