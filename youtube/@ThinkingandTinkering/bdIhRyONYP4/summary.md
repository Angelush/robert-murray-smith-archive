## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make waterproof cloth using casein (milk protein) dissolved with borax and plasticized with glycerin, then fixed onto cotton fabric using tannic acid from tea. The casein coating, once treated with tannin, becomes rubbery and water-repellent, causing water to bead off rather than soak in. Robert frames this as part of a broader argument for using dairy biopolymers as sustainable alternatives to conventional plastics.

## Key Topics
- Extracting vs. buying casein as a starting material
- Formulating a casein/borax/glycerin solution for fabric coating
- Using tannic acid (from tea or pure tannic acid) to fix and waterproof the coating
- Demonstrating water-beading behaviour on treated cloth and paper
- The case for repurposing dairy waste streams into biopolymers

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Dried skimmed milk / liquid milk** — source for extracting casein at home
- **Calcium caseinate (food-grade, 2 kg bag)** — preferred purchased form of casein; dissolves easily in water
- **Borax (sodium tetraborate)** — 5 g used to dissolve casein; common cleaning agent
- **Glycerin** — 1 teaspoon used as plasticizer to prevent the dried film from becoming too brittle
- **Hydrochloric acid (1% HCl)** — one option for precipitating casein from milk
- **Sulfuric acid (1%)** — alternative acid for casein precipitation
- **Acetic acid / household vinegar** — alternative acid for casein precipitation
- **Tannic acid** — the active waterproofing agent; pure form gives best results
- **Tea (strong brew, ~20 min steep)** — practical substitute source of tannins
- **Methylene blue** — used in demo to make water visible on treated paper
- **Deionized water** — used in the waterproofing demonstration
- **Cotton decorators rag (close-woven cloth)** — substrate for the waterproof coating

## Techniques and Methods
- **Acid precipitation of casein** — adding acid (HCl, H₂SO₄, or vinegar) to milk to precipitate casein as a ropey mass, then washing and drying
- **Casein dissolution** — swelling and dissolving casein powder in water with borax for ~1 hour
- **Plasticization** — adding glycerin to the casein/borax solution to produce a flexible rather than brittle dried film
- **Dip-coating** — immersing cotton cloth in the casein solution, wringing out, and drying (repeated 2–3 times for better results); drying with a hair dryer or air drying
- **Tannin cross-linking / fixing** — soaking the dried casein-coated cloth in strong tea (or tannic acid solution) for ~20 minutes to fix the coating and impart rubbery, water-repellent properties
- **Water-beading demonstration** — dripping tap water and dyed deionized water onto treated cloth and paper to show hydrophobicity

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Protein structure clarification:** Casein and whey are two distinct protein structures, both present in milk.
- **Cross-linking mechanism:** He believes the tannic acid works by cross-linking the casein (not just coating it).
- **Cracking troubleshooting:** If the coating cracks, there is likely too much solid casein in the mix; experimentation is needed since natural casein varies in quality.
- **Preservatives:** Suggests adding a preservative (e.g., Euoxyl 90 or similar, available from soap-making suppliers) if making a more durable product.
- **Food-grade casein note:** Food-grade casein still contains fat, which may need to be removed for some applications.
- **Safety disclaimer:** Any repetition is done at the experimenter's own risk; if making a product or giving it to children, independent safety testing is the maker's responsibility.
- **Broader vision:** Passionate about the potential of dairy biopolymers as sustainable plastic alternatives — notes that British dairy farmers are struggling while the industry discards casein that could replace conventional plastics, calling the situation "madness."
- **Oak galls:** Mentions oak galls as another good source of tannins (also useful for making ink).
- **Edibility/safety curiosity:** When asked if the treated cloth could be eaten (being made from tea and milk), he says he has no idea but it won't hurt — and he would be fascinated to hear results.
