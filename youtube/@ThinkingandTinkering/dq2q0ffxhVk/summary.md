## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a homopolar generator built from microwave parts, then investigates a counterintuitive result: when the magnets and conducting disc are rotated together — with no relative motion between them — the device still generates measurable current (~4 microamps). This challenges the conventional expectation that current requires relative motion between a conductor and a magnetic field, and turns out to be an example of the Faraday paradox.

## Key Topics
- Basic principle of electromagnetic induction and the requirement for relative motion
- Homopolar (unipolar) generator construction and operation
- The unusual case where magnet and disc co-rotate with no relative motion
- Measurement of DC current output via carbon brush and axle contacts
- Encouragement for independent replication
- The Faraday paradox as the underlying explanation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium disc/wheel — acts as the rotating conductor
- Permanent magnets (salvaged from microwave oven parts) — provide the magnetic field
- Plastic plates — used to mount the magnets on either side of the disc
- Carbon brush — used as the sliding contact at the rim of the wheel
- 100-ohm resistor — placed in series as a load
- Rubber insulating glove — worn to avoid interfering with measurements

## Techniques and Methods
- Homopolar generator construction using salvaged microwave magnets mounted on plastic plates
- Axle-and-rim contact method: one electrical contact at the axle, one carbon brush pressed against the outer rim
- DC current measurement with a multimeter (microamp range)
- Drill used as a drive mechanism to spin the disc
- Control test: repeated experiment without magnets to rule out triboelectric/contact electrification artefacts (result: zero current)

## Robert's Own Replies
- Identifies the phenomenon as the **Faraday paradox** — he read up on it after filming and found it to be well-studied.
- Quotes the Wikipedia explanation: EMF is produced whenever the disc moves relative to the return path; it is not possible to tell whether the magnetic field rotates with the magnet, and the result depends on the Lorentz formula applied to the velocity of the charge carrier relative to the measurement frame.
- Confirms the disc is **aluminium** and that he also tried **copper**.
- Confirms a **100-ohm resistor** was used as the load, giving ~4 microamps output (V = IR).
- States he tested without the magnets and got **zero current**, ruling out triboelectric effects as the source.
- Suggests **angular momentum** may have a role to play.
- Directs several commenters to **Lindsay Butler's posts** in the comments for the best and most reasoned explanation.
- Pushed back against dismissive comments, noting the Faraday paradox is a legitimate and well-studied phenomenon.
- Mentions that large homopolar generators have been built for high-current applications.
