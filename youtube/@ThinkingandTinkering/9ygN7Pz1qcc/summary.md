## Overview
In this live-stream workshop, Robert Murray-Smith guides a group of attendees through the hands-on synthesis and assembly of a zinc hexacyanoferrate (Prussian blue analogue) battery from a kit. He explains the chemistry of nanoparticle formation via double-displacement precipitation, the importance of electrode structure over chemistry alone, and demonstrates that the finished cell can discharge at ~6 mA and spin a small motor — impressive for a first-ever live build event.

## Key Topics
- Zinc hexacyanoferrate as a Prussian blue analogue cathode material
- Nanoparticle synthesis through concentration-controlled co-precipitation
- Importance of battery structure (thin films, electrode geometry) vs. chemistry
- Zinc-ion insertion mechanism and why zinc sulfate is the preferred electrolyte
- Comparison of Prussian blue analogues vs. activated carbon supercapacitors (energy density)
- Dendrite suppression strategies in zinc batteries
- Battery assembly: cathode, separator, anode, electrolyte
- Charging/discharging with bench-top equipment; discharge curve showing two redox potentials
- The "WISE electrolyte" concept — highly concentrated salt solutions raising the water electrolysis over-potential

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium hexacyanoferrate** (yellow powder) — soluble precursor for active material synthesis
- **Zinc sulfate** (white powder) — second precursor; also used as the battery electrolyte (1–2 molar solution)
- **Zinc hexacyanoferrate** — the insoluble active cathode material formed by double displacement
- **Graphite / graphfoil** — current collector for the cathode
- **Zinc metal** — anode material; source of zinc ions during cycling
- **Celgard separator** — polyethylene/polypropylene tri-layer coated with zirconia (ZrO₂) to suppress zinc ion migration
- **Deionized water** — preferred over tap water to avoid impurities
- **Polyethylene glycol 200 (PEG-200)** — mentioned as a dendrite suppressant additive (~3%)
- **Sodium hydroxide** — discussed but rejected as electrolyte due to dendrite formation
- **Activated charcoal from coconut husks** — compared unfavourably (~3 Wh/kg vs ~114 Wh/kg for zinc hexacyanoferrate)
- **Carbon / graphene oxide** — discussed as potential support materials; mixing with graphene oxide before precipitation suggested as an experiment
- **Potassium acetate** (17 molar, "WISE electrolyte") — mentioned as enabling charging up to ~3.7 V without off-gassing
- **Nickel chloride / cobalt salts** — mentioned as higher-energy alternatives to zinc in Prussian blue analogues (not used due to toxicity concerns)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Double-displacement co-precipitation** — mixing dilute potassium hexacyanoferrate and zinc sulfate solutions drop-wise to precipitate zinc hexacyanoferrate nanoparticles
- **Concentration control for nanoparticle sizing** — weak solutions added slowly to limit crystal growth ("weak and slow")
- **Sedimentation and filtration** — two strategies for collecting the precipitate; sedimentation rate used as a proxy for particle size
- **Electrode ink preparation** — mixing active material with a binder and applying it to graphfoil current collectors
- **Hair-dryer drying** of cathodes (~5–10 minutes)
- **Battery stack assembly** — cathode / separator / anode sandwich with electrolyte
- **Galvanostatic discharge testing** — using a BK Precision electronic load at 6 mA, computer-logged
- **Bench-top power supply charging** — 2 V applied; also demonstrated with series-connected C-cells and neodymium magnets as a clip
- **Microbalance weighing** — tare/zero method for measuring small masses (~0.5 g)
- **Dropping funnel technique** — laboratory method for controlled drop-wise addition; improvised at home with a pipette
