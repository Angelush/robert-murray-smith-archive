## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to build a friction (frictional) heater in under an hour using two stainless steel food canisters that happen to fit together with close tolerance. He fills the inner canister with oil and spins it with a drill, showing that at 2,600 RPM the device raises the oil temperature to 46 °C in just 3 minutes. The video reinforces his earlier observation that squat, broad heater geometries are more thermally efficient than long, thin ones.

## Key Topics
- Friction heater design and construction using off-the-shelf components
- Importance of close-tolerance fit between rotating and static bodies
- Effect of RPM on heating rate (580 RPM vs 2,600 RPM comparison)
- Geometry efficiency: squat/broad shapes vs long/thin shapes
- Practical use of common workshop tools (pillar drill, lathe optional)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel food canisters** — used as the two mating bodies of the heater
- **Oil** — working fluid filling the canister to generate heat through viscous friction
- **Silicon sealant** — used to seal around the 6 mm bottom hole
- **Acrylic** — lid material bonded to the steel rim
- **Bearing** — fitted in the lid to allow the shaft to spin freely

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling a 6 mm centre hole through both canister body and lid
- Using a dumb nut and silicon sealant to seal the bottom shaft hole
- Turning down a bolt on a lathe to fit the canister (lathe noted as optional — a drill works too)
- Attaching a thermocouple to monitor temperature in real time
- Driving the inner body with a Dremel (too weak), then a pillar drill at 580 RPM and 2,600 RPM
- Timed temperature-rise tests to compare RPM settings
