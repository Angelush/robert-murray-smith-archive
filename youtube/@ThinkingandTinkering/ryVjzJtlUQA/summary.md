## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how a freewheel mechanism works by 3D printing one from scratch and explaining its underlying ratchet-and-pawl principle. He connects the concept to everyday objects like bicycle freewheels and ratchet wrenches, then applies a custom-built freewheel to a small flywheel generator to show how momentum can continue driving a load after the input stops.

## Key Topics
- How a bicycle freewheel works (pedalling vs. coasting)
- The ratchet-and-pawl mechanism as the core principle
- Historical/conceptual link to the Archimedes catapult
- 3D-printed freewheel design: drive wheel, driven wheel, pawls, and springs
- Application of the freewheel to a flywheel generator
- Gear ratios in the finished assembly
- Broader applications: ratchet wrenches

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed plastic parts (drive wheel, driven wheel, top and lower sections)
- Small springs sourced from a ballpoint pen (used to return the pawls to engaged position)
- Magnets (mentioned in passing during final assembly of the flywheel generator)
- LED (used to demonstrate electrical generation)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of freewheel components (drive wheel, driven wheel, pawl housings)
- Ratchet-and-pawl assembly: fitting pawls into slots with spring-loaded return mechanism
- Integration of freewheel into a flywheel generator with a 2:1 gear ratio
- Demonstration of momentum run-on by spinning the assembly and observing continued rotation after the drive input stops
