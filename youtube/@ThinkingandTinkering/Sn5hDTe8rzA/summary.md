## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the "aeromine" — a bladeless wind energy device based on NACA F1 airfoils positioned to create a low-pressure zone that draws air upward through pipes. He builds a prototype using 3D-printed airfoils from an Elegoo Neptune 3D Plus printer and tests it with a fan, concluding that while the concept works, it performs best at high wind speeds and is likely not worth the significant fabrication effort for typical wind conditions.

## Key Topics
- The history and shifting terminology around wind turbines and "aeromines"
- How the original aeromine design works (NACA F1 airfoils creating low pressure to induce airflow)
- 3D printing large airfoil components using the Elegoo Neptune 3D Plus
- Prototype construction and bench testing
- Performance assessment: speed dependency, low-wind limitations, and comparison to solar and other wind designs
- Skepticism about wind power fashion trends and marketing claims

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA/FDM filament (~2.5 kg used for the two airfoil prints)
- 32 mm pipe (used as the central draw tube)
- Plywood (for the support structure)
- Wooden dowels (for the frame)
- Yellow cotton thread (used as a flow indicator during testing)

## Techniques and Methods
- FDM 3D printing of large NACA F1 airfoil shapes (4–5 day print run)
- Manual frame construction: cutting plywood squares, drilling holes, assembling with dowels
- Low-speed wind tunnel testing using a household fan (~3.5 m/s airflow)
- Flow visualisation using a lightweight thread to detect draft at the pipe inlet
