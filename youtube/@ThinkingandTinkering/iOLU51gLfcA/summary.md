## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the RidgeBlade wind turbine by first giving a thorough grounding in crossflow wind turbine technology — its history, design parameters, and trade-offs. He concludes that while crossflow turbines are not the most efficient, the RidgeBlade is a sensible choice for urban/domestic use because it mounts on an existing rooftop ridge, eliminating tower costs, and operates quietly at low wind speeds.

## Key Topics
- Classification of wind turbines: horizontal axis (HAWT) vs. vertical axis (VAWT)
- Savonius and Darrieus VAWT subtypes
- Crossflow wind turbines: orientation, history, and how they work
- Historical inventors: Paul Moutier (1891), Anthony Mitchell (1903), Donat Banki (1922), Fritz Ossberger
- The Banki-Mitchell and Ossberger turbine lineage
- Key design parameters for crossflow turbines (blade profile, blade number, blade angle, blade thickness ratio)
- Power coefficient and limitations of crossflow turbines
- Augmentation devices (guide nozzles, vanes, deflectors, cowlings)
- The RidgeBlade as a crossflow turbine: advantages, cost structure, and urban suitability

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Lightweight materials (referenced generally as the construction material that keeps costs down and limits maximum wind speed tolerance)

## Techniques and Methods
- Differential drag principle (Savonius-type operation)
- Wind augmentation via guide nozzles, guide vanes, casings, windshields, deflectors, and cowlings
- Mechanical and electromagnetic braking for overspeed protection
- Rooftop ridge mounting as a passive wind deflector
