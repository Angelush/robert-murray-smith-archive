## Overview
Scientists at the University of Nottingham have developed a photocatalytic method to convert atmospheric CO₂ and water into methanol using single copper atoms dispersed in a graphitic carbon nitride matrix, activated by sunlight. The catalyst is 44 times more effective than conventional carbon nitride and, critically, produces methanol rather than the greenhouse gas methane. Robert highlights this as a significant step toward a circular carbon economy using Earth-abundant, scalable materials.

- **Methanol (CH₃OH)** — the desired liquid fuel product

- **Melamine** — used in the Nottingham process to control material structure at the nanoscale
- **Palladium / Platinum** — rare-earth catalysts noted as expensive alternatives this work supersedes
- **Hydrogen (H₂)** — briefly mentioned as an example of a fuel currently made by hydrocarbon cracking with CO₂ emissions

## Techniques and Methods
- **Photocatalysis** — shining sunlight onto a semiconductor material to excite electrons, which then drive the reduction of CO₂ to methanol
- **Magnetron sputtering** — used to deposit single copper atoms onto the carbon nitride surface
- **Melamine-controlled synthesis** — using melamine to tune crystal structure for optimal light interaction and charge separation
- **Photocurrent measurement** — used to quantify and compare catalyst efficiency (44× improvement over standard carbon nitride)
- **Graphitic carbon nitride synthesis from urea** — referenced as a method Robert has demonstrated on his channel
- **Copper nanoparticle synthesis** — referenced as another technique demonstrated on the channel, with potential to combine both for replicating the experiment
