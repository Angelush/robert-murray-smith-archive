## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests "the Daisy," a turbine made from spoons, as a dual-function device capable of generating electricity from both wind and water. The design originated from a viewer suggestion to combine two previous experiments. The test yields 21V and 21mA at just 2 litres per minute flow, leading Robert to tentatively claim this may be a world-first combined wind-and-water turbine.

## Key Topics
- Evolution from water wheels → Pelton wheels → turbo wheels → spoon-based "Daisy" turbine
- Viewer-inspired idea to create a dual wind/water turbine
- Live water-jet test of the Daisy and electrical output measurement
- Potential real-world application in the UK (frequent rain, occasional wind)
- Possibility of a world-first dual-medium turbine design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Spoons — used as turbine blades to construct the Daisy rotor
- Water — delivered via a water jet at 2 litres per minute as the driving fluid
- 12V light bulb — used as a load during measurement (confirmed in comments)

## Techniques and Methods
- Water-jet impingement on a spoon-blade rotor
- Open-circuit voltage and current measurement (voltmeter/ammeter on table, kept away from water)
- Load testing with a 12V lamp to verify real output under electrical load
- Direct comparison of wind and water performance using the same rotor geometry

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Measurement was under load:** Robert confirms the electrical readings were taken with a 12V light bulb connected, not open-circuit — making the output more meaningful.
- **Flow rate clarification:** He jokes that 2 litres per minute is "quite real" in terms of energy density, gently correcting a commenter who apparently cited 2 litres per hour.
- **"Swiss army knife of generation":** Robert embraces a commenter's phrase, agreeing the dual-function concept is a versatile approach to small-scale generation.
- **On creativity and invention:** He reflects that the value of an idea lies in its creativity, not just its technical polish — crediting "zero views" for the spark.
- **Drag concern:** He raises a practical engineering question in reply to a suggestion: adding extra elements might create drag on whichever half of the turbine isn't doing useful work at a given moment.
