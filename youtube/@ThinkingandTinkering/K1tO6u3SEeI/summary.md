## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates running a Stirling engine using the heat of hydration from dissolving sodium hydroxide in water, inspired by the historical "soda train" — a real steam locomotive that used the same chemical principle to operate in hazardous environments like mines. He shows first that the engine runs from a hot coffee cup, then successfully powers it from caustic soda dissolved in water, presenting the concept as a rechargeable "heat battery" where the sodium hydroxide can be dried out in the sun and reused.

## Key Topics
- The historical "soda train" — a steam engine powered by dissolving sodium hydroxide or similar chemicals in water
- Why the soda train was used in dangerous environments (mines) where open flames were prohibited
- Demonstration of a low-temperature differential (LTD) Stirling engine running on coffee cup heat
- Running the same Stirling engine inverted on heat generated by dissolving sodium hydroxide in water
- The concept of a chemical "heat battery" with solar recharging (evaporation in sunlight restores the dry salt)
- Brief comparison with calcium chloride as a safer alternative heat source

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH / caustic soda)** — primary heat source; dissolving it in water produces significant exothermic heat
- **Calcium chloride** — mentioned as a safer alternative that produces the same exothermic dissolution reaction
- **Sodium carbonate (washing soda)** — mentioned historically; the "soda train" name was marketing to avoid alarming the public about caustic soda
- **Water** — added to sodium hydroxide to trigger the heat of hydration reaction
- **Stirling engine** — cheap LTD Stirling engine purchased from Amazon, used as the heat engine
- **Coffee** — used in initial demonstration as a low-grade heat source for the Stirling engine

## Techniques and Methods
- **Heat of hydration** — exploiting the strongly exothermic dissolution of sodium hydroxide (or calcium chloride) in water to generate a high-temperature heat source without combustion
- **Inverted Stirling engine setup** — mounting the engine upside down in a custom cradle so the hot plate faces downward into a beaker of reacting solution
- **Heat battery / solar recharge cycle** — allowing the spent solution to evaporate outdoors in sunlight to recover the dry salt for reuse, creating a closed energy cycle
- **LTD (low temperature differential) Stirling engine operation** — running the engine from a modest temperature difference between a hot end (chemical reaction) and an ambient cold end

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Self-limiting temperature**: Robert clarifies that in open atmosphere the reaction is self-limiting — once the temperature exceeds 100°C the water boils off, stopping hydration. It could exceed 100°C only in a pressurised vessel.
- **Safer alternatives**: He recommends looking at hand warmers and self-heating cans as related applications, and mentions the company **Sunamp** as a commercial player in chemical heat storage.
- **Peltier devices**: He notes you wouldn't really run a Stirling from a Peltier, but you could run a fan directly from a Peltier.
- **Solar Stirling suggestion**: He describes a simple solar build — buy a small LTD Stirling, a Fresnel lens, invert the engine in a cradle, and focus the lens on the bottom plate.
