## Overview
Robert demonstrates a flame test on a graphene-reinforced plastic developed by his colleague Steve, comparing it against standard polypropylene. The 7mm-thick material withstands a butane blow torch tip of approximately 700–800°C, recording only ~260°C on the surface and just 16°C on the reverse side, with minimal charring. The result highlights the material's exceptional thermal insulation properties and hints at potential applications in body armor and automotive industries.

## Key Topics
- Ongoing testing programme for graphene-reinforced plastic (strength, durability, service life)
- Thermal/heat resistance via flame test
- Comparison against polypropylene
- Thermal insulation behavior (non-reticulated structure)
- Potential commercial applications: body armor, electric vehicles, injection molding
- Proprietary nature of the material formulation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene-reinforced plastic** — the primary test subject; a composite developed by Steve; proprietary formulation
- **Polypropylene (PP)** — used as a standard comparison material (sourced from a nail box)
- **Butane** — fuel for the blow torch used in the heat test
- **ABS and PCL** — mentioned in comments as other plastics Robert has experimented with graphene additions
- **Graphene platelets** — responsible for the shimmering appearance of the material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Butane blow torch flame test** — applied directly to the surface of the 7mm plastic sample
- **Surface temperature measurement** — infrared/temperature gauge used to measure front face (~260°C) and rear face (~16°C) during torch application
- **Visual inspection** — checking for charring, deformation, or combustion
- **Comparative testing** — polypropylene tested alongside as a baseline
- **Raman spectroscopy** — mentioned in comments as the preferred method to verify graphene content

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Non-reticulated structure is key**: The material is non-reticulated, which makes it a thermal insulator — explaining why one side reaches ~700°C while the other stays at ambient temperature across just 7mm.
- **Carbonisation threshold**: The top layer carbonises at around 200°C, which is where it loses conductivity; this loss appears permanent, but repeated tests show the same resistance values.
- **Re-pliability**: At around 100°C the material becomes pliable again (thermoplastic behaviour).
- **Biodegradable**: Confirmed the material is completely biodegradable and made from renewable sources.
- **See-through version in development**: Steve is working on a transparent variant.
- **Automotive interest**: Robert was in talks with a car manufacturer (an EV company) in early 2016, though he was limited in what he could disclose.
- **Graphene shimmer**: The sparkle visible in the material is simply light catching the graphene platelets.
- **Not a rigorous scientific test**: Robert explicitly noted the video is a YouTube demo, not a controlled scientific test — "the happy music is a dead giveaway."
- **Four years of prior work**: He had been researching this material for approximately four years before this video.
- **Spray application possible**: Confirmed the material can be sprayed.
- **Injection molding preferred route to market**: He saw injection molding as the most viable commercial production method at the time, given the still-small 3D printing industry.
