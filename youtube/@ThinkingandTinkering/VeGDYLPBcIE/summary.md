## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith begins a multi-part series on building a Wimshurst electrostatic generator almost entirely from scrap materials. He constructs the two counter-rotating acrylic disks (salvaged from broken LCD displays) and their wooden drive bosses (cut from an old coffee table), and explains how conductive ink ("version 2 ink") will be used to paint the 18 conductive sectors onto each disk — an untested approach he is openly curious about.

## Key Topics
- What a Wimshurst machine is and how it differs from other electrostatic machines (e.g., Bonetti/Bonnetti machine)
- Why the Wimshurst is self-starting (due to its conductive sector arrangement)
- Sourcing and cutting acrylic disks from salvaged LCD displays
- Fabricating wooden drive bosses from scrap mahogany
- Using a paper template to lay out 18 sectors at equal angular intervals
- Using conductive ink instead of aluminium foil for the sectors
- Adding brass pinheads at sector points (inspired by Poggendorff's design) to protect the ink from brush wear
- Plan to lacquer over the sectors for additional protection and higher voltage output

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic disks** — salvaged from broken LCD displays; used as the main rotating disks
- **Mahogany (scrap coffee table)** — used to fabricate drive bosses
- **Conductive ink ("version 2 ink")** — painted onto disks to form the conductive sectors
- **Aluminium foil** — mentioned as the traditional alternative for sectors
- **Brass drawing pins** — hammered flat to create pinheads placed at sector contact points
- **Super glue** — used to fix brass pinheads to the disk surface
- **Lacquer/resin** — mentioned as a protective coating over sectors (referencing Poggendorff's method)
- **Skater bearings** — press-fitted into bosses to support the axle
- **Dowels** — six used to reinforce the glued boss assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting large acrylic disks from LCD displays (references a separate video)
- Turning/filing wooden disks on a lathe (or drill with file) to create grooved pulley bosses
- Gluing and pinning the stacked boss components with dowels
- Drilling and press-fitting skater bearings into the boss
- Creating a paper protractor template by folding and marking degrees for sector layout
- Bending and snapping drawing pins with a steel block and hammer to produce flat brass pinheads
- Super-gluing brass pinheads at sector intersection points using a pencil tip (graphite prevents sticking)
- Painting conductive ink over the sector outlines and brass pinheads

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief social responses ("cheers mate", "lol"), but a few contain useful clarifications:
- **Bonnetti machine**: Confirmed it is "a sector-less Wimshurst really."
- **Acrylic surface prep**: Noted that the salvaged acrylic plates have a smooth side and a rough side, and they used the rough side. Recommends sanding if both sides are smooth. Importantly warns **not** to push pins through the acrylic material, as that would short-circuit the charge build-up on the disks.
- **Energy output**: Expressed doubt that the joules produced would be sufficient to power certain applications "unless you have quite some caps on it or a large disk radius."
- **Leyden jar**: Confirmed a Leyden jar would be "very doable" and mentions building one in the next video.
- **Electrostatic machine history**: Noted development "kind of stalled after Pidgeon machines until the late 60's when folks like Jefimenko and Moore got involved."
- **Steampunk aesthetic**: Acknowledged the build will likely look "a bit steampunk."
