## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines a cheap portable fan (sold across Asia and the US for a few dollars) that people are converting into wind turbines, and explains why the fan motor works so well as a generator. He disassembles the fan to reveal it uses a crab-claw alternator design — identical in principle to a car alternator — and explains how the same modification (replacing the electromagnet coil with a permanent magnet) can be applied to ordinary car alternators to make efficient, low-cost generators.

## Key Topics
- Cheap portable fans being repurposed as wind turbine generators
- Disassembly and analysis of the fan motor's internal construction
- Crab-claw (Lundell) alternator design and how it works
- How permanent magnets can replace the field coil in a car alternator rotor
- In-runner vs. out-runner motor/generator configurations
- The economics and simplicity advantages of single-coil crab-claw designs
- Previous related projects on the channel (speaker magnet generator, bicycle wheel generator)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ring magnet** — used in the fan motor rotor (similar to PC fan magnets)
- **Ceramic magnets** — mentioned as an option some people use in car alternator conversions
- **Neodymium magnet (N52)** — the recommended replacement for the field coil in a car alternator rotor; described as extraordinarily powerful and requiring caution
- **Steel / ferromagnetic material** — used for flux path bending (the crab-claw fingers and end plates)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of a portable fan to identify rotor and stator components
- Using a **mechanical puller** to separate pressed-together crab-claw rotor halves (hammering is possible but risks mushrooming the shaft ends)
- Replacing the field coil in a car alternator rotor with a permanent magnet (neodymium N52)
- Bending magnetic flux paths using ferromagnetic finger/disc structures to reorient the field to the face of the crab-claw fingers
- Using the motor as a generator by rotating an external magnetic field rather than driving a current through the coil
