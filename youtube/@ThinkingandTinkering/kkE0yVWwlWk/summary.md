## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the principles of gear mechanics and directional motion, then demonstrates how toothbrush bristles can serve as a low-cost ratchet mechanism. Combining bevel gears with this "bristle ratchet," he builds a mechanical motion rectifier — a device that converts oscillating (bidirectional) input into unidirectional rotation — and proposes it as a cheap, rust-proof solution for harvesting wave energy from the sea.

## Key Topics
- Drive and driven gear mechanics; gears turning in opposite directions when meshed
- Bevel gears: redirecting rotational motion through 90 degrees
- The relativity of clockwise/anticlockwise direction depending on viewpoint
- Ratchet and pawl mechanism: allowing motion in only one direction
- Sprag clutch as an alternative to a ratchet (more expensive and temperamental)
- Bristle ratchet: using nylon toothbrush bristles as a self-springing ratchet/pawl
- Mechanical motion rectifier: converting oscillating input into unidirectional output
- Application to wave energy harvesting (sea power)
- Nylon's durability and corrosion resistance in marine environments
- Tinkercad 3D-printable files for the designs

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Nylon bristles** — used as the ratchet/pawl element; noted as mechanically tough and corrosion-resistant
- **Toothbrushes** — used as a ready-made, cheap source of nylon bristle bundles (~12p / ~20 cents each)
- **Steel** — mentioned as a contrast; susceptible to rusting in seawater, unlike nylon
- **Bevel gears** — the mechanical component combined with bristle ratchets to form the rectifier (3D-printable via Tinkercad)

## Techniques and Methods
- Physical demonstration of intermeshed spur gears and bevel gears to illustrate directional motion
- Gluing toothbrush heads onto bevel gear hubs to create bristle ratchet clutches
- Assembling a two-bevel-gear + bristle-ratchet framework to produce a working mechanical motion rectifier
- Using Tinkercad for designing and sharing 3D-printable gear components

## Robert's Own Replies
- The bristle ratchet mechanism also functions as a kind of **differential** when the two ratchets interact.
- The device works equally well in the **anticlockwise** direction — the same principle applies, just reversed.
- A **flywheel version** has been built and works well, though a flywheel is not strictly necessary.
- In response to questions about wear, Robert implies the applications are varied and wear may not be a significant issue in many use cases.
- Light-hearted deflections on time estimates and on the choice of new vs. used toothbrushes, consistent with his informal style.
