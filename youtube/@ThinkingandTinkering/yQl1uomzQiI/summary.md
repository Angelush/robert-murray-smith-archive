## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a small wind turbine rotor mounted on the low-friction magnetic bearing developed in video 1428, using a coil salvaged from a microwave turntable motor. By rebuilding the rotor in lightweight plastic (40 g vs. 150 g copper), he achieves enough spin from just three puffs of breath to blink an LED multiple times, proving the concept as an effective low-power energy scavenger.

## Key Topics
- Continuation of the magnetic bearing series (videos 1428 and 1429)
- Design rationale for minimising rotor weight to suit the magnetic bearing
- Assembly of a modular, magnet-coupled rotor from off-the-shelf plastic parts
- Energy harvesting / scavenging from very low input power (breath, wind)
- Flywheel-like behaviour of a disc rotor with edge mass
- Potential for scaling and attaching other power-take-off devices

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper** — original heavy rotor prototype (~150 g); discarded in favour of plastic
- **Plastic builders/centring board** (~200 mm disc) — rotor disc
- **Plastic squirrel pipe** — rotor shaft/hub
- **Neodymium (permanent) magnets** — 8 on the rotor disc; also used in the magnetic bearing base for lift and coupling
- **Coil from a microwave turntable motor** — cordless coil embedded in the cradle as the generator stator
- **LED** — output indicator for generated electricity
- **Radially magnetised magnets** (discussed in comments, not used) — noted as rare and expensive (~£245 each from specialist suppliers; Eclipse Magnets can custom-make them)
- **Pyrolytic carbon** (mentioned in comments) — noted as a diamagnetic levitation material but too weak to support rotor weight

## Techniques and Methods
- Magnetic bearing construction (passive levitation via opposing magnet fields)
- Lightweight rotor fabrication by cutting and gluing plastic sheet and pipe
- Modular magnet-coupled assembly (sections held together magnetically, allowing easy magnet swap to tune lift)
- Salvaging a generator coil from a consumer appliance (microwave turntable motor)
- Proof-of-concept energy generation by breath/blowing as a proxy for wind input
- LED as a simple electrical output monitor

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Radially magnetised magnets** are the ideal choice for the bearing ring but are rare and costly (~£245 each; two needed). Eclipse Magnets can custom-make them but at high cost. He suggests approximating a ring with bar magnets arranged with alternating north/south faces — a larger ring with smaller bar magnets gives a better field approximation, though with some "bounce."
- **Pyrolytic carbon / diamagnetic levitation** was considered but he doubts it could support the rotor's weight, as the diamagnetic effect is quite weak.
- **Earnshaw's theorem** is acknowledged as the theoretical barrier to pure static magnetic levitation; he notes he hasn't tried circumventing it directly.
- **Needle-tip bearings** don't outperform the magnetic bearing — he tested them.
- **Scaling up** — he sees no reason it wouldn't work at larger scale, but emphasises that building it is the only reliable way to find out.
- **Magenn** (airborne wind energy company) is referenced: raised US$5 M, had unresolved stability issues, and quietly closed.
- He confirmed he is **still using the bearing** and still enjoys it.
- General encouragement throughout for viewers to build their own versions.
