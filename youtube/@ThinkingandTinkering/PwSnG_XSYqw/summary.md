## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a PC fan into a simple electricity generator by coupling it with a cheap clockwork mechanism. The fan's motor coils are rewired as a generator, driven by the wound clockwork spring, and connected to a bridge rectifier and LED to prove the concept. The result is a novelty "clockwork battery" producing 2.5–3 volts — not a serious power source, but an entertaining and educational build.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a PC fan motor as an AC generator
- Coupling a clockwork mechanism to the fan's axle
- Identifying the correct coil output pins on the fan's Hall-effect PCB
- Rectifying AC output with a bridge rectifier to drive a DC load
- The steampunk aesthetic and educational appeal of the project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PC cooling fan (brushless DC fan, used as generator)
- Clockwork mechanism (purchased on eBay for £1, or salvaged from children's toys)
- Builders board (PU foam coated in PVC) — used to make an axle adapter disc
- Superglue — to bond the adapter disc to the clockwork spindle
- 1N34 diodes — used to build the bridge rectifier
- LED — used as a proof-of-concept load

## Techniques and Methods
- Fan disassembly: removing the label, prising out the retaining washer and o-ring to extract the rotor
- Trimming/sawing off the fan blade mounting arms from the rotor hub
- Drilling a 2 mm centre hole in a 20 mm disc to create a friction/glue axle adapter
- Tracing PCB pins to identify the two relevant coil output lines (V1 and V2), ignoring V0
- Soldering wires to the identified coil pins
- Building a bridge rectifier from 1N34 diodes to convert AC to DC
- Winding the clockwork spring by hand and using a finger to hold the cog while winding
