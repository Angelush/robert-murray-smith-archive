## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the fundamentals of wind turbine design after encountering an overpriced commercial folding turbine that claimed a 40W output. He walks through the physics of wind energy capture, showing why you must start with the blade/rotor section — not the motor — when designing a turbine. The key takeaway is that power output is determined by blade swept area and the cube of wind speed, and any motor mismatch can be resolved with gearing.

## Key Topics
- Why the advertised 40W output of cheap folding turbines is misleading at typical wind speeds
- Wind power fundamentals: swept area and the cube of wind speed
- Correct design order: start with the wind capture device, then choose the motor
- Betz limit and ~30% efficiency as a realistic design target
- Matching motor characteristics (speed/torque) to the rotor output
- Using gears to resolve speed/torque mismatches between rotor and generator
- Online calculators (e.g., Omni Calculator) as practical design tools
- Horizontal vs. vertical axis turbine designs (briefly)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PVC pipe (mentioned as a material blades can be cut from)
- 3D-printed blade designs (referenced as a printable option)

## Techniques and Methods
- Using online wind power calculators (Omni Calculator) to estimate output from swept area and wind speed
- Consulting Met Office average wind speed data (UK) to set design wind speed targets
- Gear reduction/multiplication to match rotor torque/speed to generator requirements
- Selecting brushless or stepper motors for high-speed, low-torque direct-drive applications
- Axial flux motor designs as an option for wind generation
