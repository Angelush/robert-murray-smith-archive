## Overview
This is a quick response video addressed to viewer Terry Hull, demonstrating how to cast thin, rigid bioplastic sheets using casein (a milk protein). Robert explains the full process from dissolving casein in an alkaline solution to hardening it with formaldehyde, and shows a ~25 micron transparent sheet as a proof of concept. The video highlights casein's versatility as a substrate for functional materials, including conductive films.

## Key Topics
- Casein as a bioplastic material (historically known as Galalith/Galile)
- Dissolving casein in alkaline solution for casting
- Formaldehyde cross-linking to harden and waterproof cast films
- Additives for improving rigidity and toughness
- Conductive casein films incorporating conductive ink

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Casein** — protein extracted from milk or purchased as sodium caseinate; the primary casting material
- **Alkaline solution** — used to dissolve casein into a liquid casting solution (sodium carbonate / washing soda noted in comments as a good option)
- **Formaldehyde (methylene glycol)** — cross-linking agent used to harden and waterproof dried casein films
- **Marble dust** — additive for increased rigidity
- **Chalk** — additive for increased rigidity
- **Clay** — additive for increased rigidity
- **Kevlar fibers** — additive for toughness
- **Conductive ink** — blended into casein to produce thin conductive sheets
- **Ammonium ferric citrate** — mentioned in comments as a material Robert was beginning to experiment with alongside gelatin
- **Whey protein** — noted in comments as a related but distinct protein requiring separate development

## Techniques and Methods
- Extracting casein from milk (referenced from an earlier video)
- Dissolving casein in alkaline solution to produce a liquid casting slurry
- Mold casting to target film thickness (with allowance for shrinkage)
- Air drying cast films
- Fume cupboard vapour exposure to formaldehyde for cross-linking/hardening
- Blending additives (fillers, fibres, conductive inks) directly into the casting slurry
- Solvent casting (mentioned briefly in comments as an alternative approach for certain applications)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Film thickness prediction:** There is a linear relationship between solids content and final film thickness — a 100 micron wet film at 35% solids will dry to 35 microns, which can be used to dial in target thickness precisely.
- **Formaldehyde and "bio" status:** Formaldehyde does not negate the bioplastic designation, as "bio" refers to the monomer source; formaldehyde is naturally occurring (e.g., in apples) and fully biodegradable. It can also be called *methylene glycol* when in aqueous solution.
- **Formaldehyde alternatives:** For acid electrolyte applications, formaldehyde may be unnecessary since casein is insoluble in weak acid anyway.
- **Conductivity measurement:** The conductive casein sheet (50:50 binder-to-ink, ~10 microns thick) measured **250 ohms per square**, equivalent to 100 ohms/square/mil — with no optimisation effort.
- **Sodium carbonate** (washing soda) is preferred over other alkalis for dissolving casein; the resulting colour is clear with a very slight amber tint.
- **Whey protein** will behave differently from casein; Robert advises mastering casein first before moving to other proteins.
- **Optical curing** of casein is possible but requires harsh photo-initiators and cross-linkers, making it a significant challenge.
- **Future work:** Robert notes he is beginning experiments with **ammonium ferric citrate and gelatin**, and plans to do more videos on casein bioplastics.
- **Formaldehyde is a protein cross-linker** and therefore not suitable for starch-based (polysaccharide) materials.
