## Overview
In this video, Robert tests a Mark II venturi turbine by combining the cone/pipe arrangement developed in video 1382 with an impeller salvaged from an exercise bike (previously used in his "ros turbine" from video 1126). Testing is done indoors using a fan due to no wind that day. The result demonstrates the concept works, generating measurable voltage from a microwave oven coil, and Robert outlines plans for a tighter-tolerance larger version.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the venturi cone/pipe arrangement from video 1382
- Reuse of the exercise bike impeller from the "ros turbine" (video 1126)
- How an orifice acts as an inefficient venturi, and how the cone improves on this
- Addressing the key weakness of the original ros turbine: unidirectional wind requirement and the rear column
- Indoor fan substitution for wind during testing
- Voltage generation via a multi-turn coil from a microwave oven
- Discussion of scale — why small prototypes are still meaningful proofs of concept
- Plans for a rebuilt, higher-tolerance larger version

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Exercise bike impeller** — salvaged component, used as the turbine rotor
- **Venturi cone and pipe** — from video 1382, used to concentrate and direct airflow
- **Multi-turn coil from a microwave oven** — used as the generator coil; produces measurable high voltage even when spun by hand
- **Magnet** — paired with the coil; approximately 1 cm gap noted
- **Electric fan/blower** — used as a substitute wind source for indoor testing

## Techniques and Methods
- **Venturi effect** — cone collects and accelerates wind into the impeller's central orifice
- **Orifice-as-venturi** — Robert explains that a hole/orifice inherently acts like an inefficient venturi, and the cone formalises this effect
- **Electromagnetic induction** — spinning impeller drives a coil through a magnetic field to produce voltage
- **Indoor fan testing** — fan used to simulate wind when outdoor conditions were calm
- **Iterative prototype development** — small rough prototype used to validate concept before committing to a larger, precision-built version

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Machinery-on-the-ground philosophy**: Robert clarifies that the core design premise is to keep all machinery at ground level to reduce installation and maintenance costs. He acknowledges this comes at an efficiency cost but argues the relevant metric is **cost per watt generated**, not peak efficiency.
- **Cone-high, machinery-low constraint**: In response to a suggestion about placing the cone up high, Robert explains this would defeat the purpose — the design specifically requires the cone to be elevated while the machinery stays on the ground, and that constraint shapes the whole architecture.
