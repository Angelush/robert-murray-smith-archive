## Overview
Robert demonstrates a proof-of-concept device he calls the "Tesla Torch," which repurposes a Slayer Exciter circuit to wirelessly power a ring of compact fluorescent lamp tubes. By winding a small coil and placing it at the center of a CFL ring (stripped from its ballast circuitry), nearly all the radiated electromagnetic energy is captured by the surrounding tubes as light. The result is a low-power, wireless lamp running on just 5V and ~8mA, with planned improvements for brightness and efficiency.

## Key Topics
- The inefficiency of the standard Slayer Exciter circuit (energy radiated in all directions)
- Using a compact fluorescent lamp's tube ring as a 360° energy-capturing light fitting
- Winding a miniature Slayer-style secondary coil to fit inside the CFL ring
- Proof-of-concept demonstration and future improvement plans

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Compact fluorescent lamp (CFL)** — base cap sawn off, ballast circuit removed, leaving only the tube ring
- **40H (very fine, hair-like) magnet wire** — used to wind the secondary coil (~1000 turns)
- **Electrician's tape** — used to create a secure pin connection on the fine wire
- **Breadboard** — used for the test circuit assembly
- **Transistor** — part of the Slayer Exciter circuit
- **LED** — used as a power-on indicator

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a CFL to isolate the tube ring from its ballast circuit
- Winding a miniature secondary coil (same topology as a Slayer Exciter coil, ~1000 turns on a small former)
- Mechanical wire termination: wrapping hair-fine wire around a sturdier wire stub, secured with electrical tape under tension to form a reliable pin connection
- Wireless power transfer via the Slayer Exciter's radiated electromagnetic field
- Voltage scaling to increase brightness (5V → 12V planned)
