## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues that the global energy crisis — driven by exponential growth in demand, the shift to electric vehicles, and the slow pace of power station construction — forces us to choose between centralized and distributed (micro) generation. He makes the case that centralized generation is increasingly fragile, expensive, and unable to scale, while microgeneration offers resilience and individual empowerment, though it requires a strategic, multi-solution mindset rather than a single fix.

## Key Topics
- Exponential growth in energy demand vs. lagging supply capacity
- Critique of centralized energy generation (vulnerability to storms, price hikes, long build times)
- Nuclear power: advantages, risks, and notable disasters (Chernobyl, Fukushima, Three Mile Island)
- Japan as a post-Fukushima model for transitioning to microgeneration
- The parallel between distributed manufacturing (3D printing) and distributed energy
- Microgeneration: definition, benefits, and limitations
- Household energy breakdown: HVAC (~75%) vs. other uses (~25%)
- The psychological resistance to changing energy habits and accepting complexity
- Energy reduction as a strategy alongside generation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Thorium, uranium, plutonium — nuclear fuels used in fission reactors
- Zeolite — mentioned in the context of zeolite thermal storage for heating/cooling

## Techniques and Methods
- Central/grid-scale electricity generation (coal, gas, oil, nuclear — all combustion-based)
- Hydroelectric generation — noted as the only major centralized method that doesn't burn fuel
- Solar photovoltaic (PV) panels
- Solar thermal
- Air source heat pumps
- Ground source heat pumps
- Wind generation
- Zeolite thermal energy storage
- Microgeneration / distributed energy networks
