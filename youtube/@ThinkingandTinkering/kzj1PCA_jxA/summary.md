## Overview
In this video, Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a DIY sand battery by filling a homemade wood-burning boiler (built in a previous video) with kiln-dried sand instead of water. He burns it for one hour using a few kilos of pine wood, then monitors how slowly the sand retains and releases heat — concluding that even this rudimentary build performs impressively as a thermal energy storage device. The video serves as a practical follow-up to his earlier theoretical discussion of sand batteries (video 1604), showing the concept works at a small DIY scale.

## Key Topics
- Sand vs. other thermal storage materials (water, concrete, clay) and their relative heat capacities
- Why sand is advantageous: cheap, plentiful, stable at very high temperatures (up to 1200°C industrially)
- The environmental cost of concrete as a competing material (CO2 emissions)
- Repurposing a DIY wood-burning boiler as a sand battery
- Real-world heat retention test: 1 hour burn, 3.5 hours cool-down
- Comparison to Scandinavian masonry/soapstone stoves as an existing analog
- Scarcity of recoverable sand as a nuanced consideration

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Kiln-dried sand** — primary thermal storage medium; chosen over builder's sand because it flows freely and has no moisture
- **Pine wood** (3–4 kg) — fuel used to heat the battery during the test
- **Concrete** — mentioned as a comparable thermal storage material but with high CO2 production cost
- **Clay** — mentioned alongside sand and concrete as having similar heat capacity (~850 J/kg/°C)
- **Water** — discussed as a high heat-capacity fluid (~4000 J/kg/°C) but limited to below 100°C
- **Desert sand (UAE)** — referenced from a report claiming superior thermal efficiency
- **Molten salt** — briefly mentioned by commenters and Robert as an alternative, dismissed as expensive and hazardous

## Techniques and Methods
- Filling an enclosed metal boiler vessel with ~40 kg of kiln-dried sand, leaving the top cap open to allow steam escape
- Open-air wood fire (pine) burned inside the vessel for approximately one hour
- Temperature measurement at multiple points on the vessel using a thermal/IR method
- Post-burn passive cool-down monitoring over 3.5 hours to assess heat retention
- Rough energy calculation: mass × specific heat capacity × temperature rise to estimate stored megajoules/kilowatt-hours

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Heat loss via exhaust**: Robert agreed with commenter Tommi that a significant portion of potential thermal energy was lost, and identified the exhaust as the primary culprit — visible as heat haze in the footage. He noted the sand itself was storing well, which was his primary interest.
- **Extracting heat from the sand**: Suggested pipes in the sand or blowing air over/through a box as viable heat-extraction methods.
- **Water as an alternative**: Acknowledged water is good for lower-temperature applications (below 100°C), but sand's advantage is operating at much higher temperatures.
- **Using a resistive heater**: Recommended a resistive heating element as the clean/renewable way to charge the battery (e.g., from wind or solar), rather than burning fuel.
- **Sand scarcity**: Noted that sand is surprisingly a limited resource — extraction is regulated, and in the UK much is dredged from seabeds (e.g., Bristol Channel area).
- **Molten salt comparison**: Dismissed molten salt as expensive, scarce relative to sand, and dangerous if spilled — sand wins on practicality.
- **Scandinavian stoves as inspiration**: Confirmed he sees traditional Scandinavian heat-retention stoves as the clear precedent and inspiration for this concept.
- **"Sand battery" terminology**: Clarified he didn't coin the term — it was marketing language used to make an old idea sound new, and he was simply testing the underlying principle.
- **Math video**: Hinted at potentially doing a dedicated video on the thermal energy calculations.
