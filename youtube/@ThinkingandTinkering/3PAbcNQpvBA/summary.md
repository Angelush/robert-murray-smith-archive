## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith compares a passive magnetic (maglev) bearing against a conventional mechanical thrust bearing by spinning two similar rotors and observing which stops first. The magnetic bearing rotor spins dramatically longer, illustrating how repelling ring magnets can near-eliminate axial friction. He explains the physics behind passive magnetic bearings, including Earnshaw's theorem and its practical workaround, and makes the case for their use in low-wind-speed wind turbines.

## Key Topics
- Direct comparison of a mechanical thrust bearing vs. a passive magnetic bearing
- How repelling ring magnets create a levitating (frictionless) axial bearing
- Earnshaw's theorem: why passive magnets cannot balance statically without a physical restraint
- Active vs. passive magnetic bearings
- Inverse-square law governing magnetic repulsion force with distance
- Practical application in wind turbines, especially in low-average-wind environments (UK ~4.5 m/s)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ceramic ring magnets** — used as the two repelling magnets forming the passive magnetic bearing; sourced from speakers or Amazon
- **Three-part mechanical thrust bearing** — two metal rings with ball bearings between them, used as the comparison bearing
- **Ceramic (roller blade / skater) bearings** — used to provide lateral restraint and counteract the sideways thrust predicted by Earnshaw's theorem (22/8/7 mm skate bearings)
- **Rotors / flat discs** — the spinning loads placed on each bearing for the demonstration

## Techniques and Methods
- **Passive magnetic bearing construction**: two same-pole ring magnets stacked so they repel, with the rotor's weight compressing them to a non-contact gap; a central spike/bar provides lateral restraint to satisfy Earnshaw's theorem
- **Spin-down comparison test**: both rotors hand-spun simultaneously; elapsed spin time used as a proxy for bearing friction
- **Blow-spin demonstration**: blowing gently on the side of a flat disc to show how little torque is needed to keep the magnetically-levitated rotor turning
- **Active magnetic bearing (described, not built)**: feedback-controlled electromagnets that switch on/off to eliminate the need for a physical lateral restraint and can compensate for rotor imbalance/wobble

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Bearing interference is negligible**: Robert repeatedly reassured commenters that the magnetic field from the ring magnets has negligible interference with the generator coils, and that the inverse-square drop-off means the field is effectively zero even a short distance away.
- **Eddy currents non-issue**: He clarified multiple times that the generator coils in the demo were *not connected*, so there is no current flow and therefore no eddy current losses.
- **Lift capacity = pull rating**: "The lift will be the same as the pull — in this case 1.5 kg." Magnets are rated by kg of pull, which equals their lifting capacity in this orientation.
- **Longevity over efficiency**: "It will increase efficiency a little for sure — but the main improvement is going to be longevity which will decrease the cost per kW generated — and that is much more important."
- **Bearing spec he uses**: "I always use the same bearings — 22/8/7 mm skater bearings — they are cheap and easy to get hold of."
- **Magnetic flux routing**: "You can't shield per se but you can route flux from a magnet using a magnetic circuit."
- **Magnetic gears**: He mentioned having built a series of magnetic gears, noting their main problem is slip under high torque.
- **Vacuum chamber**: Jokingly noted that eliminating air friction entirely would just require "a big enough vacuum chamber."
- **Accuracy of the spin test**: Acknowledged the test was not rigorous but defended it as a demonstration of a well-known principle where the magnitude of the difference makes fine accuracy unnecessary.
