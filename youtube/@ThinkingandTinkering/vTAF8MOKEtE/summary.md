## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple, flexible infrared heating element using uncoated carbon fiber tape and ribbon crimps (ribbon clamps). By connecting the carbon fiber tape to a power supply via crimped wire connections, he shows it can produce gentle to moderate heat (26–41°C) at low voltages. The video emphasizes the versatility of this approach for wearable heating applications like heated clothing, gloves, and pocket warmers.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to ribbon crimps/clamps as electrical connectors for carbon fiber tape
- Carbon fiber tape as a flexible, foldable heating element
- Powering the element at 9V and 18V, with measured current draw and temperatures
- Insulating the element with sellotape or Kapton tape to prevent shorting
- Practical applications: heated clothing, gloves, socks, pocket warmers, cushions
- References to companion videos on resistance/voltage/amperage relationships for heater design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon fiber tape** — uncoated, 25mm wide; used as the heating element; available in various widths and forms (thread, rope, tape)
- **Ribbon crimps / ribbon clamps** — 25mm metal clamps with serrated teeth; used to make electrical connections to the carbon fiber tape
- **Sellotape (scotch tape)** — used as insulation; rated to ~120°C
- **Kapton tape** — higher-temperature insulation option; rated to ~400°C
- **18V battery pack** — used as a portable power source (~1.5 Ah)

## Techniques and Methods
- Folding carbon fiber tape over a ribbon crimp and pressing with pliers to create a secure electrical terminal
- Attaching wires to crimps by twisting, clipping, or soldering
- Powering the element from a lab bench supply to measure voltage, current, and temperature at different voltages
- Applying sellotape or Kapton tape to both faces of the element to prevent accidental shorting when used standalone
- Sewing carbon fiber tape directly into clothing (no tape insulation needed when fabric holds it in place)
- Wrapping carbon fiber tape around pipes with a small gap between windings (for pipe heating)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **IR clarification:** Confirmed the energy emitted is infrared radiation — invisible to the naked eye but perceptible as heat; this is why the device qualifies as an "IR heater."
- **Tape vs. no tape:** Repeatedly clarified that insulating tape is only needed for standalone use; when sewn into clothing the fabric itself prevents the element from crossing/shorting.
- **Machine washable:** Confirmed the carbon fiber element is machine washable when sewn into garments.
- **Battery tip:** Suggested using recycled/used batteries, which still retain ~80% capacity.
- **Coated vs. uncoated fiber:** Advised that coated carbon fiber can be used but should be burned off (plugged in outdoors briefly) before use; uncoated fiber avoids this step entirely.
- **Crimp connections:** Noted that crimps count as permanent connections under 18th Edition wiring regulations; suggested fixing the crimp to a base cloth and sewing the tape to the same cloth if reliability is a concern.
- **Pipe heating:** Advised wrapping carbon tape around a pipe with a small gap between each winding, and applying Kapton tape to the pipe first.
- **Carbon fiber vs. nichrome:** Expressed the view that carbon fiber tape is better suited to personal/wearable heaters, while nichrome is better for fixed appliances.
- **High-voltage warning:** Cautioned that high voltages would produce dangerous heat levels for wearables; warm (not hot) is the target for clothing applications.
- **Suggested uses endorsed:** Seat warmers, hand grip heaters, heat packs, belly warmers, pipe heaters, and a fan/vent combination for a simple space heater.
