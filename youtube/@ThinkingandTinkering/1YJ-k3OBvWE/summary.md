## Overview
This video presents computational fluid dynamics (CFD) modeling work done by community member Trey Rousey using the open-source tool FreeFLOW, analyzing the airflow behavior of the "Darwin Waters Wind Turbine" — a squirrel-cage-style wind turbine with a passive wind-capture sock. The modeling confirms that baffle plates and a hyperbolic cone are effective at directing airflow through the turbine's vanes, validating the design direction. Robert shares this as an example of community collaboration driving the project forward.

## Key Topics
- The four essential components of any wind turbine: capture, generation, conversion, and storage
- The Darwin wind turbine concept — a passive wind-capture device (described as "a big sock in the air")
- CFD modeling of airflow with and without baffle plates
- The effect of a hyperbolic cone on directing airflow downward through the squirrel cage
- The Waters turbine design and its hyperbolic cone vane-splitting mechanism
- Wing profile geometry and its impact on turbine efficiency
- 3D printing of turbine components

## Techniques and Methods
- CFD (computational fluid dynamics) modeling using FreeFLOW (open-source)
- Airflow visualization using color-coded velocity maps (red = fast, blue = slow)
- Baffle plate integration to redirect airflow through the squirrel cage
- Hyperbolic cone geometry for passive wind direction
- NACA 8410 blade profile with 75° angle of attack and a Gurney flap set at 45° to the main flap
- C-wing profiles for squirrel cage blade design
- FDM 3D printing of turbine components (print time ~2 days 18 hours for the Waters turbine)
