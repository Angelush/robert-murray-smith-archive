## Overview
Robert demonstrates a bellows-based air engine capable of running on nothing more than a human breath. The central insight is that handmade bellows function simultaneously as a piston and a spring — a combination that enables sustained oscillation with minimal energy input, referencing Tesla's "earthquake machine" as a conceptual parallel. Robert argues this makes ultra-cheap, low-power engines viable from scavenged or ambient energy sources.

## Key Topics
- Bellows as a combined piston-and-spring mechanism
- Low-power air engine design running on breath or vacuum
- Tesla's piston-spring oscillating system as conceptual inspiration
- Energy scavenging from cheap, low-power sources
- Oscillating valve design and construction
- Crankshaft configuration (cranks set 180° apart, valve crank offset 90°)
- Ultra-low-cost fabrication using paper, tape, and timber
- Potential applications: Stirling engines, wind-powered variants

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Concertina flexible ducting** — commercial dryer/AC duct used in the earlier bellows engine attempt
- **22 mm (¾ inch) copper pipe** — main valve body
- **Brass bar** — valve mounting, with rings soldered on
- **12 mm aluminium pipe** — inlet/outlet pipes attached to the valve
- **Aluminium brazing rod** — used to join aluminium pipes to copper
- **20 mm plastic pipe** — plunger sleeves inside the 22 mm copper (close sliding fit)
- **Tape and paper** — primary bellows construction material (from previous video)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Bellows fabrication** — tape-and-pattern method (detailed in a prior video), producing a spring-return folded bellows
- **Oscillating slide valve construction** — two plungers spaced to match port centres, travel calibrated to pass top dead centre
- **Aluminium brazing** — joining dissimilar metals (Al pipe to Cu valve body)
- **Soldering** — attaching brass rings to the brass mounting bar
- **Crankshaft assembly** — two bellows cranks at 180°, valve crank at 90° phase offset
- **Vacuum cleaner as test pressure source** — used to demonstrate full-speed running
