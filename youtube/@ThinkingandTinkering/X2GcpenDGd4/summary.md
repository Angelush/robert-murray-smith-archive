## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a synchronous motor from a microwave oven turntable to illustrate how its unique coil-and-bent-metal flux-directing arrangement generates electricity. Building on this principle, he constructs a prototype generator stator from a steel bicycle wheel rim — cutting interleaved teeth, winding a single large coil around it, and demonstrating ~100 mV of output by moving ceramic magnets along the rim. The key takeaway is that this design achieves 36 poles with only one coil to wind, making it far simpler and cheaper to build than conventional multi-coil alternators.

## Key Topics
- Anatomy of a synchronous microwave turntable motor (magnet, coil, gear train, flux-directing bent metal tabs)
- How the bent metal "pole pieces" redirect magnetic flux to enable generation and rotation
- Contrast with conventional coil-wound motors/dynamos (coil around the outside vs. coil wound around the magnet)
- Scaling the microwave motor principle up to a bicycle wheel rim stator
- Fabrication of an interleaved-tooth stator ring from a steel bike rim
- Single-coil alternator concept: 36 poles, one winding
- Proof-of-concept voltage generation (~98–100 mV) with ceramic magnets
- Plans to combine the stator with a flywheel rotor fitted with permanent magnets

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Synchronous microwave oven motor** — used as the reference design and disassembled for analysis
- **Ceramic (ferrite) magnets** — used as the moving field source in the proof-of-concept test
- **Steel bicycle wheel rim** — repurposed as the stator body
- **18 SWG enamelled copper wire** — used to wind the single stator coil
- **Fiberglass / fibreglass** (resin/cloth implied) — used to bond and reinforce the assembled stator ring
- **Steel flywheel** — planned rotor component to carry permanent magnets

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and reverse-engineering of a synchronous AC motor
- Cutting interleaved slots into a bicycle wheel rim to create alternating north/south pole teeth
- Gluing and sanding the two-ring stator assembly with fiberglass for structural integrity
- Single-coil toroidal winding around the outer rim of the stator
- Proof-of-concept measurement with a multimeter and "captain tape" camera filter trick to improve meter readability
- Flux-path analysis using bent metal pole pieces (flux steering/shaping)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Wire gauge trade-off**: On the topic of using a different wire size, Robert notes it would be half the thickness (to fit the same space), carry only half the amps, and have twice the resistance — a significant electrical compromise.
- **Flywheel freewheel**: He confirms he plans to use a freewheel mechanism on the flywheel rotor.
- **Clarification on the microwave motor**: He reminds a commenter that the motors are only a guide to construction — the actual generator device uses bicycle wheels, not the motors themselves.
- **Visualisation plans**: He mentions he is thinking of adding close-up shots and a drawing to help explain the flux-path concept more clearly.
- **Cutting vs. welding**: He notes that cutting (rather than welding) the stator teeth is the better approach for most builders since more people can cut than weld.
- **Motor potential**: He agrees the design would also make a great motor, not just a generator.
- **Caption tape trick**: He acknowledges positive feedback on the camera filter/caption tape trick for reading the multimeter and says he will use it going forward.
