## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a DIY UV curing box from inexpensive fluorescent light fittings purchased at a local hardware store, replacing the standard tubes with UV fluorescent equivalents. The project is motivated by his work developing a conductive flexographic printing ink that requires UV curing. He demonstrates that a functional UV cure box can be built for around £20, compared to the hundreds of pounds a commercial unit would cost.

## Key Topics
- Why UV curing is needed for flexographic (flexo) printing inks
- How standard fluorescent lamp fittings work and how to identify compatible UV replacement tubes
- The difference between UVA, UVB, and UVC light, and which is needed for curing
- Step-by-step construction of the box housing using builder's board (UPVC soffit/fascia) and MDF
- Wiring the four lamp units in series and adding a light switch
- Adding aluminium foil reflectors to improve efficiency
- Expected cure time vs. industrial machines (~5 minutes vs. a few seconds)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink / flexo printing base (Seworks transparent white base)** — used as the vehicle for his conductive ink formulation
- **UV fluorescent tubes (UVA, G5 fitting, T5 ballast, 300 mm, 8 W)** — direct replacements for standard fluorescent tubes
- **Standard fluorescent light fittings** — £2.50 each from a DIY store, provide the ballast and socket
- **MDF (medium-density fibreboard)** — used as the backboard for mounting the lamp fittings
- **Builder's board / UPVC soffit and fascia** — PVC outer skin with polyurethane foam core, used for the box sides
- **Cyanoacrylate (superglue)** — used to bond the builder's board panels
- **Aluminium foil** — used as a reflective lining inside the box
- **1 mm twin core and earth lighting cable** — used to wire the lamps in series to mains
- **Kitchen cabinet cable clamps** — used as strain relief on the mains cable

## Techniques and Methods
- Identifying lamp compatibility from box labelling and ballast markings (ballast type, fitting type, wattage, tube length)
- Replacing standard phosphor-coated fluorescent tubes with bare UV (UVA) tubes of identical form factor
- Routing wire channels in MDF backboard for clean cable management
- Series wiring of multiple fluorescent fittings with a single switched mains feed
- Cutting and gluing UPVC builder's board to construct the enclosure
- Applying aluminium foil as a reflective interior coating before fitting the lamps

## Robert's Own Replies
- **365 nm is the curing wavelength** for most UV printing inks; the homemade lamp is not highly powerful but will cure in time without being an eye hazard — those wanting faster cures should use higher-wattage tubes.
- **Lamp source**: he bought his UV tubes from [lampsdirect.co.uk](https://lampsdirect.co.uk/).
- **Higher UV intensity = shorter cure time**, though he hadn't tested the specific scenario a commenter asked about.
- **Builder's board** is a PVC-coated foam-core material widely used by window fitters in the UK.
- He acknowledges that **nail polish UV setters don't work** for this purpose and that commercial face-tanning UV units were still ~£50 — making the DIY route both cheaper and more scalable (the same construction approach works for any wattage or tube size).
- Emphasises the build's **accessibility**: it requires no more electrical skill than wiring a standard plug, making it suitable for non-engineers.
