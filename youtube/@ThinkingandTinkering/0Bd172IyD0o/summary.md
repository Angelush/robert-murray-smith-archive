## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces three common 3D printing filaments — PLA/PLA+, ABS, and TPU — drawing on roughly 10 months of hands-on printing experience. He explains the practical strengths and weaknesses of each material and shares his first successful attempt at printing flexible bellows using TPU. His main takeaway is that TPU is far easier to work with than its reputation suggests, and starting with generic slicer settings is usually enough to get good results.

## Key Topics
- Why PLA/PLA+ is his default filament (low fumes, minimal warpage, ease of use)
- Limitations of PLA for mechanical/impact applications
- ABS as a historically popular but smelly alternative
- Introduction to TPU as a flexible filament for bellows and other elastic parts
- Generic slicer settings as a practical starting point for new materials
- Philosophy of learning by doing and iterating from mistakes

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA (Polylactic Acid) / PLA+** — go-to filament; fume-free, low shrinkage/warpage, ostensibly biodegradable but requires industrial composting facilities; poor mechanical/impact properties
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — widely used industrially (e.g., Lego bricks); emits fumes during printing, limiting its appeal for home use
- **TPU (Thermoplastic Polyurethane)** — a flexible filament in the TPE (thermoplastic elastomer) family; high flexibility, high durability, insoluble; used here to print bellows

## Techniques and Methods
- Printing with generic slicer profiles (LottiCura / Cura generic PLA and generic TPU settings) without manual tuning
- Reducing print speed for TPU (50 mm/s) to avoid print failures
- Moisture management: storing filament in sealed bags with desiccant sachets
- Iterative/exploratory approach: printing test objects (bellows) to evaluate a new material before committing to full models
