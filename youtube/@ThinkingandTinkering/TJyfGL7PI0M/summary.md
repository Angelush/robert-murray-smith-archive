## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a DIY solar thermal kettle using an evacuated solar thermal tube, 3D-printed parts, and curved reflectors made from household guttering. He explains how evacuated tubes work — including their getter, vacuum insulation, and magnetron-sputtered black absorber coating — and demonstrates the finished kettle successfully heating water to make coffee, though it takes around 80 minutes. The video argues that solar thermal is a highly efficient, underutilised technology that makes a practical alternative to a 3 kW electric kettle.

## Key Topics
- Solar thermal vs. solar photovoltaics (and why solar thermal is often overlooked)
- How evacuated solar thermal tubes work (getter, vacuum layer, black absorber coating)
- Heat pipes: principle, construction, and use within solar tubes
- Building a solar kettle using 3D-printed parts (available on Tinkercad)
- Curved reflectors using 110 mm plastic guttering and mirror-effect spray paint
- Optimal tilt angles for solar collectors in the UK by season
- Cost comparison: DIY build vs. commercial solar kettles (~£100–120)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Evacuated solar thermal tube** (58 mm × 500 mm, ~£10) — core light-to-heat converter
- **Barium** (typically used as getter material) — absorbs residual oxygen to maintain vacuum; aluminium, sodium, and potassium mentioned as alternatives
- **Tungsten, cobalt, nickel** (in ceramic matrix) — transition metals used in the magnetron-sputtered black absorber coating
- **Copper pipe** — used to make heat pipes
- **Acetone** — low-boiling working fluid inside DIY heat pipes
- **Isopropanol/alcohol** — alternative heat pipe working fluid
- **110 mm plastic guttering** — used as the reflector housing/parabolic trough
- **Rust-Oleum Mirror Effect spray paint** — applied inside guttering to create a reflective mirror surface
- **8 mm threaded bar** — structural element for the hinge assembly
- **Aluminium bar** — stabilising foot to prevent the kettle tilting
- **ABS filament** — recommended material for 3D-printed parts (per author comments)
- **Water** — working fluid for the kettle

## Techniques and Methods
- **Magnetron sputtering** — industrial process used to deposit the multi-layer black absorber coating onto the tube glass
- **DIY heat pipe fabrication** — cap one end of copper pipe, add acetone, heat gently until steaming, then crimp the open end to seal
- **3D printing** — all structural parts (base, hinge, lid, foot, spacers) designed in Tinkercad and printed in ABS
- **Mirror-effect spray painting** — Rust-Oleum Mirror Effect applied to the interior of curved guttering sections to produce curved reflectors
- **Solar angle optimisation** — positioning the tube at 20° in summer, 45° in spring/autumn, 60° in winter (northern hemisphere, UK)
- **Hinge assembly** — 3D-printed wing pieces and 8 mm threaded bar used to create a fold-out reflector system

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Build difficulty**: The assembly is straightforward — essentially just 4 long bolts — but the 3D-printed parts are the main challenge.
- **Tube pricing**: He found evacuated tubes for as little as US$5 each via a quick internet search (eBay, Amazon widely available); links a supplier (Dakota Lithium) as an example.
- **Print material**: Recommends printing in **ABS** (implied to be for heat resistance).
- **Technology history**: Notes that solar thermal is not new — Frank Schuman was using it around 1910 — and finds it strange it isn't more widely adopted.
- **Parabolic reflectors**: When asked about parabolic mirrors, he notes he has made them before and finds it easier to use a washing-up bowl as a former.
- **Heating rate**: Clarifies it depends on the mass being heated, the intensity of sunlight, and the conversion efficiency of the tube.
- **Commercial units**: Confirms commercial solar thermal systems exist and are available.
- **Legal note**: Making solar-cooked food on a UK beach is apparently against the law.
- **Ferrimagnetic coating**: Confirms the coating is ferrimagnetic, noting it has many uses beyond solar.
