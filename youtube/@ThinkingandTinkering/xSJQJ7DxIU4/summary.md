## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to solder a copper wire onto stainless steel — normally considered nearly impossible — by first electrochemically depositing a copper layer onto the steel surface using his homemade coppering gel and ferric chloride. He then solders directly to that copper pad, achieving a surprisingly strong bond. The video proves that copper-plating stainless steel creates a viable soldering surface.

## Key Topics
- The challenge of soldering onto stainless steel
- Using a coppering gel (copper nanoparticles) with ferric chloride to plate stainless steel
- Mechano-chemical application technique (rubbing with wire wool)
- Tinning wires and copper-plated surfaces before soldering
- Managing heat carefully to avoid burning off the thin copper coating
- Using homemade flux to improve solder adhesion
- Demonstrating bond strength by attempting to pull the wire free

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Coppering gel** — homemade gel containing copper nanoparticles in fumed silica, used to deposit copper onto stainless steel
- **Ferric chloride gel** — mixed 50/50 with coppering gel to activate the plating reaction
- **Solder** — used to tin both the wire and the copper-plated surface
- **Homemade flux** — applied to the copper pad prior to soldering (made in a previous video)
- **Wire wool** — used to mechanically activate the surface during copper plating
- **Stainless steel** — the substrate (demonstrated on a spoon)
- **Copper wire** — the component being soldered on

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mechano-chemical copper plating** — applying coppering gel and rubbing vigorously with wire wool to deposit copper onto stainless steel
- **Tinning** — pre-coating both the wire and the copper pad with solder before joining them
- **Indirect/offset torch heating** — heating the spoon at a distance from the copper pad (or from underneath) to avoid burning off the thin copper layer
- **Flux application** — applying homemade flux to the copper pad immediately before soldering
