## Overview
Robert demonstrates a mold-free method for fabricating fiberglass body panels, adapted from boat-building techniques, as part of his lawnmower-to-electric-vehicle conversion project. He uses recycled sheet PVC as a structural core, fiberglass over it, and finishes with filler and paint. The key insight is that a foam or sheet core can substitute for a traditional mold, enabling one-off bodywork without the expense of mold-making.

## Key Topics
- Mold-free fiberglass panel fabrication for a one-off vehicle body
- Lawnmower-to-EV conversion project (body construction phase)
- C-flex boat-building technique as the conceptual basis
- Sheet PVC as a lightweight, easy-to-cut core material
- Foam core alternatives and their suitability with resin
- Stitch-and-glue boat-building technique (Mirror dinghy reference)
- Panel template-making and assembly workflow
- Finishing with filler and paint

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sheet PVC** — recycled from neighbours' redecoration; used as the structural core/former for panels
- **Fiberglass (glass fibre cloth/mat)** — laminated over the PVC core on the exterior; inside to be glassed as well for a sandwich structure
- **Resin** (implied, unspecified type) — used to wet out the fiberglass
- **Super glue** — used to join PVC panels together when assembling the form
- **PIR foam** — mentioned as an excellent alternative core material resistant to resin
- **Kingspan** — a brand of PIR board, available 25–100 mm thick; noted as carvable into complex curves
- **Styrene foam** — explicitly flagged as *unsuitable* because resin dissolves it
- **Foam board** — used for making cut-out templates
- **Filler** — applied after fiberglassing for surface finishing
- **Paint (bright yellow)** — finish coat; colour chosen as a Doctor Who reference
- **Copper wire** — mentioned historically in the stitch-and-glue Mirror dinghy technique

## Techniques and Methods
- **C-flex / foam-core sandwich construction** — frame covered with core material, fiberglassed on the outside, then inside; no mold required
- **Panel template-making** — drawing and cutting templates from foam board to define panel shapes
- **Box-knife cutting** — quick and clean method for cutting sheet PVC panels
- **Super-glue panel assembly** — panels glued together into a former/core shape
- **External fiberglassing** — laminating fiberglass over the assembled core
- **Internal fiberglassing** — planned second step to complete the sandwich
- **Foam carving** — noted for Kingspan/PIR foam to achieve compound curves
- **Stitch-and-glue** — referenced as an analogous boat-building method (copper wire stitching, fiberglass fillets at joints)
- **Sanding and filler application** — surface prep before painting
- **Painting** — final finish coat
