## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple, homemade magnetic switching element — a device that can turn a permanent magnet's external field on and off using electromagnetically reversed flux. By combining a neodymium magnet core with a wound copper coil and an H-bridge relay, the assembly can be switched from zero attraction to strong attraction, or vice versa. The device is presented as a building block for a larger axial flux magnetic-assisted motor project.

## Key Topics
- Concept of a magnetically-assisted electromagnet as a switchable permanent magnet
- Construction of a simple coil-and-washer bobbin from common hardware components
- Magnetic flux path control: containing flux within a steel jacket vs. directing it to the ends
- H-bridge relay circuit for reversing current polarity
- Magnetic assistance increasing strength while reducing current draw (referencing video 1036)
- Controlling direction of rotation in an axial flux motor via a slight twist of the magnet assembly
- Overview of axial flux reluctant motor research

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **M10 (10mm) steel washers** — stacked and super-glued to form the coil bobbin body
- **Repair washers** — used as end caps on the bobbin
- **Neodymium magnets** — 10mm diameter, ~2.5cm long, five used as the permanent magnet core
- **Copper wire** — 0.35mm diameter, wound around the bobbin to form the coil
- **Super glue** — used to bond the washer stack
- **Plastic sheet / tape** — insulating layer to prevent wire chafing on metal edges
- **Transformer laminations (or mild steel bar)** — forms the external magnetic circuit / flux path bars
- **H-bridge relay** — electronic component for current reversal

## Techniques and Methods
- Stacking and bonding M10 washers with super glue to form a cylindrical bobbin
- Hand-winding copper coil around the washer bobbin
- Inserting neodymium magnets axially into the centre of the bobbin
- Magnetic flux path engineering: using a steel jacket to redirect or contain permanent magnet flux
- H-bridge current reversal to switch between "off" (flux contained) and "on" (flux directed outward) states
- Magnetic assistance: using a permanent magnet to augment an electromagnet's field, achieving greater force at lower current
- Controlling motor rotation direction by applying a slight angular twist to the magnet/steel arm relative to the rotor

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Alnico comparison:** An earlier version using Alnico (soft magnetic material) had too-slow switching speed and too high a realignment energy cost, though the long-lasting effect was a positive.
- **Eclipse Magnetics:** Notes that Eclipse Magnetics already produces something similar commercially, used for magnetic vices.
- **Rotor clarification:** Clarifies that the rotor is made of isolated steel points — there are no spinning permanent magnets.
- **Amp vs. Farad explanation:** Offers a clear analogy — an amp is a coulomb per second (rate of flow), while a farad is one coulomb at one volt (an amount of charge); likens it to water flow vs. water in a bucket.
- **Design philosophy:** States he is "always aiming at KISS" (Keep It Simple, Stupid).
- **Geometry:** Affirms that geometry is critically important in this type of magnetic work.
- **Motor direction / further experiments:** Indicates he intends to try additional configurations once the motor aspect is resolved, and welcomes others to experiment and build on the design.
