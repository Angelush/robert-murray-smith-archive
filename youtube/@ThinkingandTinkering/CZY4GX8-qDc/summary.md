## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a "Bellows Drive" — a composite machine that combines his previously designed hyper cycloid drive with a dual-bellows system and check valves to produce a directed airstream. The airstream is intended to spin a blisk turbine and generate electricity. Along the way, he discusses why bellows fell out of favour compared to centrifugal fans and makes the case for creative, exploratory machine-building.

## Key Topics
- Efficiency comparison between bellows and centrifugal pumps/fans
- Pulsed vs. continuous airstream characteristics
- Construction of a dual hyper cycloid drive with modified flywheel/cog
- Assembly of bellows into the drive using a base plate and air-director block
- Check valve integration for directional (one-way) airflow
- Directing the bellows output at a blisk turbine to generate electricity
- The reversibility of the design: bellows-as-engine vs. bellows-as-pump
- Philosophy of exploratory, "Heath Robinson" / Rube Goldberg–style making

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **TPU (thermoplastic polyurethane)** — 3D-printed flexible bellows material
- **PLA (polylactic acid)** — 3D-printed rigid structural components (end caps, plinth, block, gears, bars)
- **Gaffer tape** — used in hand-made bellows construction (referenced from earlier video)
- **Card strips** — used with gaffer tape for hand-made bellows (earlier video reference)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing with a multi-material printer (TPU + PLA combined print for bellows)
- Hand-fabrication of bellows using gaffer tape and card strips (prior method, referenced)
- Hyper cycloid drive mechanism — converts rotation to linear reciprocating motion
- Gear coupling of two mirror-image drives via a joining/idler gear to prevent locking
- Check valve arrangement — four valves in two pairs to enforce one-way airflow in and out
- Air-director block with square pin/hole registration to align bellows output
- Blisk turbine as the downstream load for air-driven electricity generation
