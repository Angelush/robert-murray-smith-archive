## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a casein-based bioplastic that behaves like rubber, using milk protein (casein), potassium hydroxide, and sulfur. The resulting material is flexible, waterproof, and can be used to coat fabrics — similar to a Macintosh raincoat. He extends the experiment by adding carbon to create a flexible conductive ink with potential uses in wearable heating elements and electronics.

## Key Topics
- Extracting casein from milk via acid precipitation, or buying it as a bodybuilding supplement
- Dissolving casein in an alkaline potassium hydroxide solution
- Adding sulfur to achieve rubber-like elasticity
- Coating cloth to produce a flexible, waterproof material
- Extending the material with carbon to create a flexible conductive ink
- Potential applications: flexible heaters, heated clothing (shirts, jackets, gloves), EMI attenuation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Casein (calcium caseinate)** — milk protein, either extracted from milk or bought as a bodybuilder supplement (2 kg for £16); the primary matrix material
- **Deionized water** — 200 mL used as the solvent
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — 10 g; highly caustic alkali used to dissolve the casein; sodium hydroxide or washing soda (sodium carbonate) are noted alternatives
- **Sulfur (yellow)** — 10–30 g; sourced from a DIY store; controls the degree of elasticity
- **Carbon** — added in a follow-up experiment to create a conductive ink
- **Acetic acid / weak hydrochloric acid / weak sulfuric acid** — for precipitating casein from raw milk (if not using powder)
- **Glycerine** — mentioned in comments as an additive to improve the material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Acid precipitation** — adding acid to milk to clump out casein, followed by alkali dissolution and re-precipitation for purification
- **Alkaline dissolution** — dissolving casein in a KOH solution (target pH ~11) to form a thick, glutinous mass
- **Sulfur vulcanization analogue** — incorporating 10–30 g sulfur into the casein gel to impart rubber-like elasticity
- **Cloth coating / impregnation** — applying the latex-like mixture to fabric and allowing it to dry to produce waterproof, flexible material
- **Conductive ink formulation** — mixing carbon into the casein rubber base to produce a flexible, crack-resistant ink (~2 kΩ/sq at ~10 µm thickness)
- **Cross-linking** — mentioned as the mechanism controlling biodegradation rate

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Glycerine as additive:** Suggests adding glycerine to improve the material's properties.
- **Alternative alkalis:** Sodium hydroxide (drain cleaner) and washing soda (sodium carbonate) both work; KOH is preferred. The key parameter is pH — only needs to reach ~11.
- **Casein sourcing:** Rennet casein works well; whey protein cannot be substituted. Soaking casein in water overnight before adding alkali helps dissolution.
- **Alternative acids:** Just about any acid will work for precipitation; deviating from the recipe simply requires more experimentation.
- **Deionized water:** Specifically recommended over tap water.
- **Decomposition / durability:** Cross-linking controls biodegradation — the material can decompose in months or be fixed to last hundreds of years depending on treatment.
- **General philosophy:** Repeatedly encourages experimentation as the best way to learn the material's behaviour, noting that results vary because casein is a natural material.
