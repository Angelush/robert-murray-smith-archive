## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reflects on what he sees as misplaced global priorities — vast quantities of usable goods like shopping trolleys and bicycles are discarded or abandoned while millions of disabled people worldwide lack access to basic mobility aids. Inspired by a Thai designer who converted abandoned shopping trolleys into wheelchairs, he builds his own version by modifying a trolley and combining it with a cheap Chinese tricycle to create a functional, low-cost wheelchair.

## Key Topics
- The economics of shopping trolley abandonment and why supermarkets don't bother retrieving them
- China's bike-share oversupply problem and mountains of wasted bicycles
- The global mobility access gap (estimated 80,000 disabled people without mobility aids in Thailand alone)
- A Thai designer's upcycled shopping trolley wheelchair as inspiration
- Step-by-step conversion of a shopping trolley into a chair/wheelchair
- Integration of a tricycle rear-end to create a complete human-powered wheelchair

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Shopping trolley (£100–£125 retail)
- Chinese tricycle (~£180 new)
- Brake handles (salvaged from tricycle)

## Techniques and Methods
- Measuring and marking a cut line (~50 cm seat depth)
- Angle grinding / cutting the trolley frame to open the front for easy entry/exit
- Bending cut frame sections to form armrests or seat sides
- Notching a half-shape into metal for a friction/slide fit joint
- MIG/arc welding the tricycle rear axle assembly onto the trolley frame
- Fitting brake handles to retain braking function
