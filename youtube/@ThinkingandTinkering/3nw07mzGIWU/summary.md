## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple resistive heat pad using activated carbon felt and copper tape, inspired by a viewer suggestion for heated gloves. The pad can be powered by a standard battery and optionally fitted with a thermal breaker switch for safety. He runs multiple voltage tests showing the pad can reach 27–39°C depending on power input, making it practical for a wide range of warming applications.

## Key Topics
- Inspiration from a viewer comment suggesting heated gloves
- Materials needed for the bare-minimum build
- Step-by-step construction: cutting carbon felt, applying copper tape, sealing in plastic
- Optional safety upgrade using a thermal breaker switch (cuts out before 40°C)
- Voltage and amperage tests at 5V, 9V, 15V, and 18V
- Practical applications beyond gloves

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **5mm activated carbon felt** — the resistive heating element (preferred over 3mm for better resistance)
- **Copper tape** — used as electrodes on either side of the carbon felt
- **Plastic heat-seal bags** — used to encase and waterproof the pad
- **Thermal breaker switch** — low-cost safety component that cuts the circuit before 40°C
- **Wires and battery connector** — for connecting to a power supply or battery

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting carbon felt to a specific size (12cm × 13cm)
- Applying copper tape strips along both long edges of the felt with slight overhang
- Sealing the assembly inside a plastic bag using either a lighter or an industrial/catering vacuum sealer
- Wiring a thermal breaker switch in series with the positive supply line
- Testing with a bench power supply across multiple voltages, measuring surface temperature with a heat gun (thermal camera implied)
