## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history of the kaleidoscope — from its 16th-century precursors to David Brewster's 1815 invention and the Victorian craze it sparked — before building a working "collidoscope" from wooden breadboards, acrylic mirror tiles, and a marble. The video argues that the kaleidoscope is far more than a children's toy, representing the fusion of art, science, and mathematics, and drawing a parallel to fractal geometry.

## Key Topics
- History of mirror-reflection devices from Giambattista della Porta (1500s) through Athanasius Kircher (1646) to David Brewster (1815)
- Brewster's invention, patent, and partnership with manufacturer Philip Carpenter
- The Victorian kaleidoscope craze (~200,000 units sold almost immediately)
- Connection between kaleidoscope symmetry and fractal self-similarity
- Variations of the kaleidoscope: telescopic, microscopic, reflective, projecting, sculptural, and architectural
- Step-by-step DIY "collidoscope" build from breadboards

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Breadboard (wood)** — two boards (one dark, one light) used as the main structural material
- **Acrylic mirror tiles** — cut into strips to form the internal mirror triangle; noted as easier to cut than glass
- **Marbles (25 mm)** — placed at the bottom of the scope to create the kaleidoscopic object

## Techniques and Methods
- Laminating two thin breadboards with glue to create a thicker stock
- Cutting wood at 60° angles to assemble a hexagonal block
- Boring/turning the hexagonal block into a cylindrical tube
- Scoring and snapping acrylic mirror tiles with a box knife
- Assembling three mirror strips on tape and folding into an equilateral triangle insert
- Cutting and stacking wooden discs to form the eyepiece (22 mm aperture in a 32 mm hole)
- Drilling a stepped hole (25 mm outer, 22 mm inner) in the bottom cap to retain the marble
