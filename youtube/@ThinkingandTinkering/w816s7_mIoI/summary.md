## Overview
Robert demonstrates a surprisingly simple and free method for creating time-lapse videos of 3D prints using only a USB webcam, the built-in Windows Camera app, and a free Microsoft Store app called "Timelapse Creator." He walks through the full workflow — from capturing ~3,800 photos over a 12-hour Triceratops skull print to stitching them into a finished video — concluding that when technology just works this effortlessly, it's worth sharing.

## Key Topics
- The analogy of technology being like ill-fitting mass-produced shoes — functional but imperfect
- Using a USB webcam plugged into a PC as a time-lapse capture device
- Using the Windows Camera app's built-in photo timer (10-second interval) to automate image capture
- Managing ~3,869 sequential photos without worrying about power or storage
- Stitching photos into a time-lapse using the free "Timelapse Creator" app (by Kyle Wy, Microsoft Store)
- 3D printing a Triceratops skull as the example project (STL from Thingiverse)
- Gifting the finished print to his five-year-old grandson

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- **Interval photography / time-lapse capture**: Using Windows Camera app's 10-second photo timer to take sequential, auto-numbered photos throughout a long 3D print
- **Image-to-video stitching**: Importing the photo folder into "Timelapse Creator" at 24 fps to produce a finished time-lapse video
- **3D printing**: Printing a Triceratops skull model over ~12 hours (STL sourced from Thingiverse)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Autofocus issue**: Acknowledged that the bed movement caused autofocus to hunt during capture — he forgot to turn off autofocus, and noted he has a vague memory of how to cure it.
- **OctoPrint / limit switch tip**: Responded to a suggestion about triggering the camera via OctoPrint, noting it is possible but requires linking it to an actuator — a limit switch and a 220-ohm resistor will do it if using a mobile phone as the camera.
- **Video length**: Jokingly acknowledged that 12 hours of printing makes for a long raw video.
