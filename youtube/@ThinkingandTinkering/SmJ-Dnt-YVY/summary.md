## Overview
In this second installment of his rotating ventilator wind generator series, Robert Murray-Smith explains how he plans to turn a salvaged roof ventilator into a low-speed axial generator by winding his own coils from recycled materials. He demonstrates that a single homemade coreless coil — built from scrap ABS bobbins wound on a lathe — can produce roughly 2 watts in a test rig, leading him to project approximately 64 watts from a full set of 32 coils arranged around the ventilator's circumference.

## Key Topics
- Design rationale for using edge-mounted coils rather than a shaft-coupled motor
- Repurposing microwave oven synchronous motor internals as a coil reference
- Fabricating bobbins from ABS light fittings using a simple jig and lathe
- Live bench test of a single homemade coil on the exercise bike rig (~2 W output)
- Magnet selection: ferrite vs neodymium, cost vs performance trade-offs
- Coreless coil design to eliminate magnetic cogging and preserve flywheel effect
- Fan blade modification to the ventilator for an estimated 40% efficiency gain
- Projected total output (~64 W) and overall project cost assessment

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **ABS light fittings** — impact-resistant ABS, drilled into discs to form bobbin flanges
- **8 mm perspex rod** — used as the bobbin axle in the winding jig
- **6 mm hardboard** — spacer in the jig to set coil width
- **Hair-thin magnet wire** — sourced from recycled microwave oven fan motors
- **Ceramic / ferrite magnets** — what he had on hand; 100 ordered for ~£25
- **Neodymium magnets** — considered but rejected due to cost (~£5 each, ~£500 for a full set)
- **Super glue** — bonding bobbin discs during assembly
- **Synchronous motor from microwave oven turntable** — used as the coil design reference

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hole-saw disc cutting** — cutting bobbin flanges from flat ABS sheet with a hole cutter, then cleaning up the 8 mm centre hole
- **Jig-assisted bobbin assembly** — perspex rod + hardboard spacer + super glue to produce consistent bobbins
- **Lathe coil winding** — chucking the bobbin in a lathe to wind the wire coil uniformly
- **Resistance / fill-factor matching** — using winding depth from edge and coil resistance as proxies for coil-to-coil consistency (rather than counting turns)
- **Exercise bike test rig** — spinning a single coil past ceramic magnets to measure open-circuit voltage and short-circuit current with a multimeter
- **Coreless (air-core) axial generator configuration** — deliberately omitting iron cores to avoid cogging and exploit the ventilator's flywheel mass
- **Fan blade reversal** — fitting an inverted fan blade to create a slight negative pressure, intended to improve ventilator throughput

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Wire sourcing:** All wire comes from recycled sources at no cost — specifically from microwave oven fan motors. He doesn't know the exact turn count or wire gauge ("hair thin") and isn't counting; he judges coil equality by fill factor and resistance.
- **Coil winding direction:** Single winding, clockwise.
- **Component placement:** Plans to put coils and magnets inside the rim for weather protection.
- **Iron core question:** Acknowledges the debate but sticks with his coreless choice; thinks it will likely be "six and two threes" either way.
- **Aluminium cans:** Collecting Coke cans — suggesting he's considering them for a future casting or fabrication step.
- **Faraday's & Ampere's laws:** Offers a clear plain-English explanation: EMF depends on the rate of change of magnetic flux (speed matters), and field strength depends on magnet strength (stronger magnet = more current).
- **Cost philosophy:** Explicitly frames the whole project as a low-budget cobble-together; wants to prove the concept works before spending more.
- **Roof installation:** Humorously acknowledges he'll need to climb on the roof to fit it and jokes about the prospect.
- **Project status (at time of replies):** Ongoing — he couldn't share details but expected to have news soon.
