## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a true helical Archimedes screw — capable of both pumping water and generating electricity — using recycled CDs as the screw flights, mounted on standard 15mm plastic pipe. He walks through the full fabrication process, from heating and shaping CDs into helical flights to gluing them onto pipe, presenting an accessible and low-cost approach. The video sets up a follow-up project to turn the finished screw into a hydroelectric generator.

## Key Topics
- Distinction between a simple pipe-helix screw (pump only) and a true helical/auger-style screw (pump and generator)
- History of the Archimedes screw (attributed to Archimedes of Syracuse, originally for pumping ship bilges)
- Modern use of Archimedes screws in hydroelectric generation
- Flight parameters: pitch, external diameter, internal diameter
- Using online flight calculators to determine flat-cut profiles
- CDs as a cheap, readily available polycarbonate disc source
- The 15mm CD centre hole matching standard UK water pipe sizing
- Making a flight former jig for consistent shaping
- Attachment methods and joint sealing options
- Plans for a follow-up hydroelectric generator build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **CDs (CDRs)** — polycarbonate discs used as flight blanks; aluminium coating on one side can be scraped off for better adhesion
- **15mm plastic pipe (PEX/HDPE)** — used as the central shaft; fits CD centre hole directly
- **Hot glue** — used to attach flights to pipe (demonstration-grade fix)
- **Super glue** — used to close the overlap joint between adjacent flights
- **Solvent weld / polycarbonate cement** — recommended for a stronger, more permanent bond when pipe and flights are the same polymer
- **Polycarbonate (general)** — the material of CDs; softens at approximately 140°C

## Techniques and Methods
- **Heat-gun shaping** — heating the CD from the label side (not the metallic side, which bubbles) at ~140°C until pliable, then stretching it into a helical flight and holding until cool
- **Line/wedge cutting** — cutting a radial line (or wedge) into the CD before heating to allow it to open into a helix
- **Flight former jig** — a custom male/female profile tool pressed onto the heated CD to produce consistent, repeatable flight shapes; can also be used as a casting mould
- **Hot gluing to pipe** — threading flights onto pipe and fixing with hot glue, maintaining consistent spacing by hand
- **Heat sealing** — running a soldering iron along overlapping flight edges to fuse polycarbonate joints
- **Aluminium coating removal** — scraping the metallic layer off CDRs with a knife to expose clear polycarbonate for better glue adhesion

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Generator intent confirmed**: Robert states he is remaking the screw using polycarbonate cement (stronger than hot glue) with a polycarbonate centre pipe, specifically for a hydro generator build.
- **Simple pump alternative**: For those only needing a water pump, he suggests wrapping garden hose around a pipe — the hose naturally forms a helix and works as a basic Archimedes screw with no fabrication.
- **Wedge vs. line cut**: Acknowledges a wedge cut is technically better but says with practice the line-cut method works adequately by eye.
- **Variable pitch interest**: Engages positively with a commenter's idea of variable-pitch screws for generation, noting fixed-pitch tapered screws are already in commercial use.
- **Hot glue formulations**: Notes there are many hot glue formulations with different properties — a point he learned from bookbinding — so strength varies significantly by product.
- **Unstated future plans**: Cryptically references things he "can't speak about right now," hinting at projects in development beyond this video.
