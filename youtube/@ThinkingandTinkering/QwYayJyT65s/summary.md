## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores viewer suggestions for his wind turbine project by building and testing a blade-only variant of his Mark III design — removing the backplate to see if the blades alone could function as a wind turbine. The test reveals the backplate is essential, as the blades without it fail to spin at low wind speeds, confirming his theory that the design acts as an impeller rather than a Savonius-style rotor.

## Key Topics
- Progression from Mark I to Mark III wind turbine design
- Testing a viewer-suggested blade-only configuration (no backplate)
- Comparison of backplate vs. no-backplate turbine performance in low wind
- Robert's theory on how the turbine works: wind entering and being forced out impeller-style, not Savonius drag-cup style
- Value of failed experiments as learning experiences
- Future plan: mounting the Mark III on a wall to test airflow behaviour

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium sheet (0.05 mm, used for blades)
- Plywood (backboard for rim-magnet design)
- Rim magnets
- Pop rivets
- AB cut screws

## Techniques and Methods
- Two-roll mill used to curve flat aluminium sheet into a cylinder
- Paper folding method to locate the centre of a circular piece
- Bending 2 cm tabs on blade edges for attachment to central cylinder
- Fitting an outer hoop/ring to stiffen the thin blade assembly
- Adding a 45-degree twist to the blades
- Side-by-side outdoor wind testing of two turbine variants simultaneously
