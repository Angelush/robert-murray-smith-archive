## Overview
This video, presented by Luke (a collaborator on the channel), explores London's largely forgotten high-pressure hydraulic water network, which operated from 1883 to 1976. It explains how high-pressure water — rather than electricity — was used to distribute power across London for nearly a century, and reflects on what might have been had the network survived into the energy crises of the late 1970s.

## Key Topics
- Distinction between potential energy and kinetic energy in water systems
- History of the London High Pressure Water Network (1883–1976) and the London Hydraulic Power Company
- Technical specifications: 800 psi (55 bar) operating pressure, five pumping stations across London
- Water treatment process: river intake → settlement tanks → accumulators
- Evolution of pumping station power sources from coal/steam to electric motors
- Other nations with similar hydraulic networks (Belgium, Switzerland, Argentina, Australia, Italy)
- Decline of hydraulic power networks in favour of electrical grids
- UK's Winter of Discontent (1978–1979) and the irony of the network's closure two years prior

## Techniques and Methods
- Hydraulic accumulator storage — pressurised water stored in large accumulators for later use
- Sedimentation/settlement tanks — river water allowed to settle so impurities and mud separate before use
