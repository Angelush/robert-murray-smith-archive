## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores a 1978 German patent for an unusual vertical-axis wind turbine rotor — the "C Rotor" — which was directly inspired by the Pelton wheel used in hydropower. He traces the concept from its historical roots in fluid mechanics through to modern research validating its efficiency gains, and concludes by proposing a next step: using 3D printing to fabricate a double-cup version more faithful to the original Pelton wheel design.

## Key Topics
- The fluid mechanics connection between wind and water turbines
- History of the Pelton wheel (Lester Allen Pelton, 1870s) and its bucket-cup efficiency mechanism
- German patent DE4120908 by Von Kanstein: the C Rotor concept inspired by the Pelton wheel
- Two C Rotor design variants: Savonius-type (fin leading into a cup) and Darrieus-type (curved wing)
- Academic research confirming the C Rotor increases lift, torque, and efficiency
- The Filippini rotor (1970s) as a related design with a noted ~36% efficiency improvement
- Robert's design thinking process: research → history → validation → improvement
- Proposed next step: 3D-printing a double-cup C Rotor blade to more closely replicate the Pelton wheel geometry

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Metal (used in higher-quality commercial C Rotor examples)
- Plastic (used in basic/low-cost commercial C Rotor examples)
- 3D printing materials (proposed for fabricating the double-cup version)

## Techniques and Methods
- Literature review via Google Scholar to evaluate the concept's viability
- Analysis of velocity field and pressure field data from published research papers
- Patent analysis (German patent DE4120908)
- Conceptual design iteration: identifying gap between single-cup C Rotor implementations and the full double-cup Pelton wheel geometry
- 3D printing proposed as the fabrication method for the improved double-cup blade design
