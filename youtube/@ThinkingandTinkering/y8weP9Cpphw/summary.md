## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the transverse flux machine — a generator/motor topology combining radial and axial magnetic flux paths — which has been around since 1968 but remains largely unknown. He explains how it works using C-shaped silicon steel stator pieces and a toroidal coil, then builds a working prototype from salvaged motor laminations and cheap neodymium magnets, demonstrating it generating enough power to light an LED headlight. The key takeaway is that transverse flux machines are more compact, efficient, and gearless than conventional designs, making them highly attractive for wind turbines and electric vehicles.

## Key Topics
- Difference between radial flux and axial flux motors/generators
- How transverse flux machines combine both flux paths to reduce back-EMF competition
- Construction principles: C-shaped stator laminations, toroidal coil, alternating-polarity rotor magnets
- DIY build using salvaged silicon steel laminations and acrylic magnet discs
- Advantages of transverse flux machines: single coil, compact, energy-dense, no gearbox needed
- Drawback: high static torque (cogging) at startup
- Applications: direct-drive wind turbines, direct-drive electric vehicle wheels

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Silicon steel laminations** — salvaged from a radial flux motor outer casing; used for C-shaped stator pieces
- **Neodymium magnets (6 mm × 3 mm disc)** — ~120 for ~£10; inserted into acrylic rotor disc at 15° intervals
- **Acrylic disc** — used as the rotor body with drilled holes for magnets
- **Copper wire (~0.1 mm gauge)** — wound 200 turns to form the toroidal stator coil
- **Bearings** — glued into acrylic to support the rotor shaft

## Techniques and Methods
- Peeling and stacking silicon steel laminations to a defined thickness (~2 mm / 4 layers)
- Cutting alternating fingers from stator plates to create the C-shaped pole geometry
- Drilling 24 evenly spaced holes (15° apart) in acrylic disc for magnet insertion with alternating polarity
- Winding a toroidal coil (200 turns, ~0.1 mm wire) around the stator assembly
- Press-fitting and gluing a bearing into acrylic for the rotor mount
- Hand-spinning the rotor against an LED headlight to verify generator output
