## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to repurpose leftover primary coils from microwave oven transformers (which happen to measure ~8 ohms) as speaker voice coils. Rather than the conventional approach of a fixed magnet with a moving coil, he inverts the design — fixing the coil and attaching neodymium magnets to a tapered mahogany reed, which vibrates to produce sound. The result is a functional, aesthetically striking wooden speaker made almost entirely from salvaged materials.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing microwave oven transformer (MOT) primary coils as speaker voice coils
- Inverted speaker design: fixed coil, vibrating magnets
- Using a tapered mahogany reed as the acoustic radiator
- Testing the speaker with a small amplifier
- Brief mention of giving the finished speaker away to a channel member

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Microwave oven transformer (MOT) primary coils** — ~8 ohm resistance, used as the voice coil
- **MOT steel laminations** — steel pieces from the transformer top, used as a magnet backing plate
- **Neodymium magnets (×6)** — powerful permanent magnets attached to the steel plate on the reed
- **Mahogany** — reclaimed from an abandoned painted door; thinned to form a tapered reed panel
- **Glue** — used to affix the steel plate to the mahogany and to mount the coil at a fixed distance from the magnets

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of microwave oven transformers to extract primary coils and steel laminations
- Measuring DC resistance of coils (~8 ohms) to identify speaker-compatible impedance
- Thinning/tapering a mahogany panel to create a reed with a frequency gradient (thinner end = higher frequencies, thicker end = lower frequencies)
- Gluing a steel backing plate to the reed and attaching neodymium magnets to it
- Mounting the fixed coil at a small gap from the magnets (analogous to a voice-coil gap)
- Connecting the assembly to a conventional audio amplifier for testing

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Reed geometry matters most:** Robert acknowledges he had to experiment to get the magnet placement right and notes the even taper on his reed is "probably not the best design" — he believes reed shape and material geometry would have a large impact on acoustic performance.
- **Material choice:** He chose raw wood deliberately but concedes "style over substance" — output quality has suffered, and he treats this as a prototype requiring further development.
- **Steel variant:** He mentions he was also thinking about making a steel-reed version.
- **Sourcing MOTs:** He suggests scrap yards or local dumps for those who don't want to sacrifice a kitchen microwave.
- **Flat panel speakers:** After a commenter mention, he expressed interest in looking at flat-panel speaker designs.
- **Frequency response / "exercising":** On the question of whether speakers improve with use, he agrees they likely do — "it's a question of exercising them."
- **Reed design advice for future builds:** He would focus on optimising reed taper geometry if building again, confident an even taper is suboptimal.
