## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the fluid dynamics parallel between air and water, tracing the historical divergence of water turbine and wind turbine engineering. He revisits a wind turbine design he created in 1989 that applies the Pelton wheel principle — turning fluid flow 180° to dramatically boost efficiency — to wind energy. He announces the design is now publicly available on Thingiverse and discusses practical considerations for improving it further.

## Key Topics
- Air and water as equivalent fluids (both governed by the same flow dynamics)
- Early water wheel and windmill history — simple paddles with ~30% efficiency
- The Pelton wheel: achieving ~98% efficiency by redirecting flow 180° through shaped buckets
- Divergence of water turbine and wind turbine engineering around the mid-1800s
- Robert's 1989 Pelton-inspired wind turbine design combining NACA wing blades with Pelton buckets
- Design limitations: pressure buildup without a top, counter-force on circular turbines (similar to Savonius turbine issues)
- Proposed improvements: guide cone with exhaust hole, wind guide/block to direct airflow to one side only
- Release of the design on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Applying Pelton wheel bucket geometry (180° flow reversal) to wind turbine blade design
- NACA aerofoil wing profile integrated into turbine blades to shape airflow into the Pelton bucket
- Guide cone with hole to allow pressure exhaust from enclosed turbine volume
- Wind guide or block to eliminate counter-force on the returning blade face (addressing the core inefficiency of circular/Savonius-style turbines)
