## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make iron gall ink using acorns as the tannin source, inspired by the traditional use of acorns as food by Native American peoples. He walks through extracting tannin via blitzing and boiling acorns, preparing an iron(II) chloride solution from hydrochloric acid and steel wool, and combining the two to produce a blue-black permanent ink. He concludes that while chemistry can speed up the process, the simplest approach — soaking chopped acorns in water for a week or two — works just as well.

## Key Topics
- Background on iron gall (iron tannate) ink and a previous video on oak apple ink
- Native American use of acorns as a food source (leaching tannins to make acorn flour)
- Extracting tannin from whole blitzed acorns via simmering
- Separating fats/oils from the tannin solution using alcohol (de-emulsification)
- Preparing iron(II) chloride from hydrochloric acid and steel wool
- Combining tannin extract with iron salt to form blue-black ink
- Simple low-tech alternative: long soak with natural layer separation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acorns** — primary tannin source, blitzed whole (including shell) then simmered
- **Water** — extraction solvent (~500 mL per 50 g acorns)
- **Hydrochloric acid (37%, sold as brick cleaner)** — used to dissolve steel wool and produce iron salt
- **Steel wool / iron wool** — reacted with HCl to form iron(II) chloride
- **Iron(II) chloride** — the iron salt that reacts with tannins to form the ink pigment
- **Methanol (or ethanol)** — used to de-emulsify the acorn extract and agglomerate fats
- **Acorn fats and oils** — by-product of extraction; noted as edible if acorn is de-shelled

## Techniques and Methods
- **Blitzing/milling** — whole acorns processed in a food processor to a fine flour
- **Simmering extraction** — acorn flour simmered in water at ~100°C for one hour
- **Decanting** — separating liquid extract from solid acorn material
- **Alcohol de-emulsification** — adding methanol/ethanol to destabilise the fat-tannin emulsion and accelerate fat separation
- **Natural gravity separation** — leaving the emulsion undisturbed for days/weeks to allow layer separation without alcohol
- **Iron salt preparation** — dissolving steel wool in concentrated HCl until reaction stops, then filtering to yield green iron(II) chloride solution
- **Tannin–iron reaction** — combining tannin extract with iron(II) chloride solution to form blue-black iron tannate ink
- **Writing test** — ink applied with a homemade pen (from a prior video); observed light application darkening to blue-black on drying

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Acorn flour as food:** Robert is enthusiastic about acorn flour as an underutilised foodstuff, noting that the fat/flour layer from de-shelled acorns is edible and joking that it could sustain people in an economic collapse.
- **Any tannin source works:** He clarifies that the same iron-tannate process applies to any tannin source — it produces a ferrogallotannic complex regardless of origin.
- **Phosphoric acid vs. HCl:** He notes that phosphoric acid would form iron phosphates, which he believes are harder than tannates.
- **Particle size matters:** For faster extraction, smaller particle size is better; larger pieces take longer to release tannins.
- **Leather tanning:** He expresses personal interest in leather tanning as a related topic he may explore.
- **Acid storage:** He mentions keeping acids in accordance with COSHH and REACH regulations in response to a safety tip from a commenter.
