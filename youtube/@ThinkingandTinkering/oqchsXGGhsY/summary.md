## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith performs his first actual cut on the Carvera Air desktop CNC machine, following earlier videos covering unboxing and software setup. He walks through the full workflow from powering on the machine, mounting and probing a workpiece, uploading a G-code file, and running the cut — using acrylic as a safer practice material before attempting aluminium. The cut quality impresses him, and he concludes that CNC, while messier than 3D printing, meaningfully expands a maker's capabilities.

## Key Topics
- Powering on the Carvera Air and initial setup (voltage selector, dust cover, air hose, emergency stop)
- Tablet requirement: connecting a Samsung Galaxy A9 via the Carvera Air app over Wi-Fi
- Criticism of the machine requiring an external tablet/app rather than having an onboard display
- Workpiece preparation: placing acrylic on a sacrificial bed, securing with clamps and double-sided tape
- Probing workflow: margin scan, auto Z-probe, and optional full-surface auto-levelling
- Tool change procedure: swapping the probe for a 3.175 mm × 25 mm single-flute bit
- Running the first cut and assessing cut quality on acrylic
- Observations on noise, debris/chip management, and the need for vacuum and air-blow attachments
- Plans to repeat the cut in aluminium for a proper machine review
- Comparison of CNC (subtractive) vs. 3D printing (additive) as complementary tools

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic** — used as practice/sacrificial workpiece material for the first cut
- **Aluminium** (specifically grades 2024, 6061, 7070, 7075 mentioned in comments) — intended for the next cut; noted as the real test material
- **Double-sided tape** — alternative workholding for lighter materials

## Techniques and Methods
- **Margin scan** — machine traces the outline of the programmed cut to confirm it falls within the stock
- **Auto Z-probe** — probes a single corner to establish Z zero on the workpiece surface
- **Full-surface auto-levelling** — grid probe of the entire workpiece to compensate for warped or thin stock
- **Contour cutting** — used to cut the outer profile of the part
- **Pocket milling** — used to cut recessed features (pocket depth ~2 mm)
- **Tool change mid-job** — machine pauses, prompts operator to swap probe for cutting tool, then re-measures tool height

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed the machine **came with the laser probe** and considers it a solid, well-built machine, though he has no other CNC for comparison.
- Agrees that **CNC complements rather than replaces 3D printing** — FDM is unsuitable for high-heat applications (e.g., engines), and CNC opens additional possibilities.
- On aluminium grades: agrees **not all aluminium is equal** — rates 6061 as a good all-rounder for machinability, and mentions 2024, 7070, and 7075 as also being well-regarded.
- Acknowledges the machine is at **an impressive price point** for what it offers.
- Characteristically pragmatic about the learning curve: *"like anything mate — using it is going to be an adventure."*
