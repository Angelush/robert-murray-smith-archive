## Overview
This video demonstrates a simple home method for testing whether a car thermostat is functioning correctly before spending money on a replacement. The presenter submerges an old thermostat in near-boiling water and observes whether it opens as designed; the tested unit fails to retract, confirming it is faulty. The key takeaway is that a working thermostat should open at its rated temperature (in this case ~92°C), and failure to do so explains symptoms like engine overheating or coolant leaks.

## Key Topics
- Symptoms of a bad car thermostat (overheating, failure to reach temperature, coolant puddles)
- Two failure modes: stuck open (constant coolant flow, engine runs cold) vs. stuck closed (no coolant flow, engine overheats)
- At-home hot-water test procedure to diagnose thermostat condition
- Interpreting OEM part specifications (opening temperature)
- When cleaning may restore function vs. when replacement is necessary

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Engine coolant (referenced functionally, not tested directly)
- Water (used as test medium in saucepan/kettle)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Boiling-water immersion test:** Thermostat is submerged in near-boiling water (~85–100°C) and observed for the valve opening/retracting to its rated temperature
- **Hot plate escalation:** If kettle water cools below rating, a hot plate is used to maintain a rolling boil and ensure the thermostat reaches specification temperature
- **OEM spec cross-reference:** Part number checked to confirm the opening temperature (92°C) before drawing conclusions
