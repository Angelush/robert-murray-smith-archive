## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates an early-stage concept for 3D printing using a liquid plastic made by dissolving polycaprolactone (sold as "Polymorph") in dichloromethane. The idea arose as a by-product of solvent casting experiments: he noticed the partially-dried solution reaches a viscous, sticky consistency that can be extruded from a syringe to form self-supporting 3D structures as the solvent evaporates. He presents this as a back-of-the-envelope proof of concept with acknowledged limitations, chiefly shrinkage, but notes its potential for extremely fine print resolution down to the submicron level.

## Key Topics
- Solvent casting of polycaprolactone as the originating experiment
- Concept of using a partially-evaporated polymer solution as a printable liquid plastic
- Extrusion of the viscous solution via syringe to build 3D structures
- Shrinkage as the primary technical challenge
- Nozzle width flexibility, including potential for submicron-scale printing
- Setting time as a function of solvent content and nozzle width

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polycaprolactone (PCL)** — sold commercially as "Polymorph"; the base polymer used
- **Dichloromethane (DCM / "Dorome")** — solvent used to dissolve the PCL into a printable viscous liquid
- **HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — material of the syringe, chosen because DCM does not attack it
- **ABS** — mentioned in comments as the next plastic Robert intended to test the method with
- **Xanthan gum** — mentioned in comments as a potential shear-addition agent (0.5% addition)
- **Inert filler** — discussed in comments as a strategy to reduce shrinkage and cost
- **Microwave susceptor material** — mentioned in comments (~1% addition) as a possible route to microwave-cured high-viscosity paste printing

## Techniques and Methods
- **Solvent casting** — dissolving PCL in DCM to produce a liquid/paste form of the polymer
- **Partial evaporation control** — allowing the solvent to evaporate to a target viscosity before use
- **Syringe extrusion** — manually squeezing the viscous solution through a syringe to deposit material layer by layer
- **Ambient solvent-evaporation curing** — the extruded bead solidifies as DCM evaporates in air
- **Filler addition** (proposed) — incorporating inert filler to reduce shrinkage and lower material cost
- **Microwave curing with susceptors** (proposed in comments) — adding ~1% susceptor to enable microwave-triggered cure of the paste

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Viscosity target:** He likens the ideal printing consistency to melted chocolate, which is what inspired the idea.
- **Shrinkage as the core problem:** Robert identifies shrinkage (caused by solvent evaporation pulling the material inward) as the primary challenge, one he is familiar with from his paints and inks background.
- **Inert filler as the likely solution:** He proposes adding inert filler to reduce shrinkage, lower material cost, and enable functional tuning of the plastic — standard practice in the plastics industry.
- **Testing ABS next:** He plans to test the method with ABS before conducting formal shrinkage measurements.
- **Microwave curing route:** He floats the idea of a high-viscosity paste with ~1% microwave susceptor material, cured in a domestic microwave, as an alternative processing route.
- **Xanthan gum for rheology:** He suggests xanthan gum (~0.5% addition) as a possible shear-thinning additive.
- **Cost is a hard constraint:** He notes that most plastic solvents are either highly volatile or extremely expensive (hundreds of pounds per litre), and the method will only be adopted if it matches or undercuts existing 3D printing costs.
- **Philosophy on invention:** He cautions against over-engineering — emphasising simplicity and focus as what separates an inventor from a dreamer, and stresses the core concept is making solvent casting a viable print method.
- **Collaboration:** He mentions a collaborator ("Steve") working on the project alongside his Indiegogo work.
