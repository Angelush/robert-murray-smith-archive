## Overview
In this short video, Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates an improved method for electrically connecting carbon fiber to copper conductors for use in nanocarbon fiber heating elements. Building on a previous video where solder was used (limited to ~150°C), he shows how crimping provides a more durable, high-temperature connection good up to ~400°C — the point at which carbon itself begins to burn.

## Key Topics
- Connecting carbon fiber to copper for heating elements
- Limitations of soldered joints (max ~150°C)
- Crimping as a higher-temperature alternative (up to ~400°C)
- Standard crimp sizes and tooling

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Carbon fiber — used as the heating element material
- Copper — the conductor being joined to the carbon fiber
- Solder — previous method, limited to ~150°C before melting
- Crimp connectors ("crepes") — come in three standard sizes: red, blue, and yellow; yellow used in this demo

## Techniques and Methods
- **Wrapping** — aligning and wrapping the carbon fiber and copper before joining
- **Soldering** — prior method shown in an earlier video; suitable only to ~150°C
- **Crimping** — wrapping the joint and feeding it into a crimp connector, then using a crimping tool to form a permanent mechanical and electrical connection; rated to ~400°C
