## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith designs and builds a DIY folding portable wind turbine, motivated by the high cost and surprisingly low performance of commercial options like the Shine ($360) and Rutland 504 (£425), which produce only 3–5 watts in typical UK wind conditions. The turbine uses 3D-printed hinged hubs combined with blades cut from standard soil pipe, and incorporates a magnetic gearbox from a previous video. After a successful outdoor test at just 1.8 m/s wind speed with helpers Ginger Jack and Jacob, Robert concludes the folding blade and gearbox sections are working well and announces a custom generator will be built in a follow-up video.

## Key Topics
- Critique of commercial portable wind turbine pricing and real-world performance
- Design philosophy: combining 3D-printed parts with off-the-shelf materials
- Fabricating turbine blades from 100mm PVC soil/drainage pipe
- Folding blade mechanism using printed hinged hubs and steel pivot pins
- Magnetic gearbox (developed in video 1922) for RPM multiplication
- Integration with a bicycle dynamo as a placeholder generator
- Outdoor field test with observers Ginger Jack and Jacob
- Plans for a future custom-built generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **100mm diameter soil/drainage pipe (PVC)** — cut into quarters lengthwise to form turbine blades (~53 cm each)
- **3D-printed parts (PLA, via Tinkercad)** — hub sections, nose cone, gearbox housing
- **Steel bar, 100mm** — main axle/pin
- **Steel bars, 35mm (×3)** — blade pivot pins
- **M8 nuts (×8)** — embedded in the magnetic gearbox rotor
- **Neodymium magnets, N35–N52, 15mm diameter × 4mm thick** — 12 for outer ring (alternating N/S), 4 for inner final-drive section
- **Skater bearings (×2)** — used in the hub assembly
- **Washers** — used with bearings on the axle
- **Bicycle dynamo** — used as temporary generator (~3–5 W output)
- **Broom handle** — used as a test mast
- **Thin cyanoacrylic (superglue)** — mentioned in comments as a surface coating to strengthen PLA prints

## Techniques and Methods
- **Blade fabrication from pipe**: cutting a 60 cm section of 100mm PVC pipe, marking into four quadrants, and sawing to produce four curved blades
- **3D printing**: Tinkercad-designed hub, hinge, nose cone, and gearbox parts printed in PLA
- **Folding hinge assembly**: three printed arm sections pinned with steel bars to allow blades to fold flat and open to 1-metre sweep
- **Magnetic gearbox construction**: embedding M8 nuts as inner rotor poles, pressing neodymium magnets alternating N/S into outer ring and final-drive ring to create contactless gear ratio multiplication
- **Nose cone retention**: removable printed nose cone pressed onto projecting axle to hold blades open during operation
- **PLA hardening**: surface coating of thin cyanoacrylic glue to significantly increase print strength (mentioned in comments)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **The key innovation is just the hinge**: Robert clarifies that the only novel element in this design is the folding hinge on the propeller — everything else is standard wind turbine construction.
- **Blade orientation matters**: convex side facing the wind produces a lift effect; concave side facing the wind produces a drag effect — depending on what the builder wants.
- **Nose cone is essential**: Robert believes the blades will fold shut in gusty conditions without it, throwing the turbine out of balance and risking breakage — he recommends testing rather than assuming it can be omitted.
- **Magnetic gear prevents over-speeding**: the magnetic gearbox acts as a natural limiter, which Robert highlights as a safety advantage.
- **Budget is UK£50**: Robert has set himself a £50 budget and plans to calculate and publish the final cost.
- **Open-source ethos**: he has no interest in commercialising the design and explicitly wants people to use the files, experiment, and share their improvements.
- **Files are fully exportable**: Tinkercad files can be exported as OBJ, STL, GLV, or SVG and imported into slicers like Cura or CAD tools like Fusion 360, or sent to a print service.
- **The handle also collapses** (not shown in the video) — an additional portability feature.
- **Work in progress**: Robert repeatedly emphasises this is an ongoing project and actively encourages community contributions and shared results.
