## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a rubber band-powered tractor toy using 3D-printed parts, building on his earlier rubber band motor video. He uses the project to explain the fundamental trade-off between torque and speed in geared systems. The video doubles as a practical lesson in prototyping philosophy — rubber bands are cheap, fast, and ideal for testing mechanical designs before committing to more expensive materials.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the rubber band motor from video 2297
- The value of rubber band motors as low-cost prototyping tools
- Torque vs. speed trade-off in gear systems (4:1 ratio example with 42-tooth and 10-tooth gears)
- Step-by-step assembly of a rubber band-powered tractor chassis
- 3D-printed tractor body in JCB yellow as a gift for a child

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Rubber bands (used as both the power source and wheel traction aids on slippery floors; Robert notes he used 3)
- 3D-printed parts: axle blocks (front and rear), small cog, large wheels, small wheels, axles, spacers (thick and thin), tractor body shell
- Glue (for attaching cog and tractor body)

## Techniques and Methods
- Gear ratio calculation (tooth count ratio to determine speed and torque change)
- Assembly of a wheeled chassis from 3D-printed components
- Mounting a drive cog onto the rubber band motor output shaft
- Using spacers to prevent wheel/gear fouling on the axle
- Gluing the tractor body shell onto the axle support frame
