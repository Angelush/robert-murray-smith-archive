## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith surveys the landscape of solar energy storage technologies — from molten salt and sand batteries to mechanical gravity storage — before introducing the MOST (Molecular Solar Thermal) project, which stores solar energy as a chemical transformation rather than heat or electricity. He concludes that while MOST is scientifically intriguing, its ~3% efficiency and lack of cost data mean it faces a tough road in a highly competitive field where economic and practical factors will ultimately decide the winner.

## Key Topics
- Overview of solar energy storage approaches: thermal, electrical, and mechanical
- The sand battery and concrete/rock thermal storage concepts
- Molten salt storage systems
- Gravity-based mechanical energy storage (concrete weights in mine shafts)
- Introduction to the MOST (Molecular Solar Thermal) project
- The norbornadiene-to-quadricyclane photochemical conversion
- Prussian blue/Prussian white photochemical reactions as historical analogy
- Self-recharging solar battery concept based on photochemical reactions
- Practical and economic criteria for viable energy storage technologies

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sand** — used in sand batteries to store wind/solar energy as heat
- **Concrete and rock** — used in Texas thermal storage facility
- **Molten salt** — heated to 500–600°C for thermal energy storage and turbine driving
- **Norbornadiene** — the input molecule in the MOST photochemical conversion
- **Quadricyclane** — the output molecule produced from norbornadiene under UV light
- **Prussian white** — photosensitive coating used in blueprint-making, converts to Prussian blue on sun exposure
- **Prussian blue** — product of the sun-driven reaction; used in a prototype solar cell/battery

## Techniques and Methods
- Photovoltaic (PV) electricity generation and battery storage
- Direct thermal collection (e.g., roof heat tubes, domestic hot water)
- Thermal storage in sand, rock, and concrete
- Molten salt heat exchange and turbine driving
- Gas shuttling between hot and cold containers for thermal cycling
- Gravitational/mechanical energy storage (concrete weights in shafts)
- Molecular solar thermal (MOST) photochemical energy storage — UV-driven isomerisation of norbornadiene to quadricyclane
- Liquid-phase pumping of the MOST working fluid
- Peltier-element-based thermoelectric generation coupled to the MOST system (developed in association with a Chinese team)
