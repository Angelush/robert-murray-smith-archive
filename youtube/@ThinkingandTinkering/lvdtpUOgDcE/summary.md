## Overview
Robert demonstrates the history, construction, and practical applications of peg gears, showing how they can be built with minimal tools (hole saw, chop saw, sander, drill). The central build features two cage gears and one partial peg gear arranged to convert continuous rotary motion into reciprocating (back-and-forth) motion, which he argues has a wide range of useful applications.

## Key Topics
- History of peg gears as the foundation of windmills and watermills
- Cage gears: construction and their ability to redirect rotation by 90 degrees
- Why peg gears are "forgiving" and easy to make compared to precision-cut gears
- A jig-based method for cutting large circular discs using a chop saw
- Edge-finishing circles using a belt sander with a pivot jig
- Creating reciprocating motion by filling only slightly less than half a peg wheel
- Applications of the reciprocating mechanism
- Philosophical argument for retaining hands-on making skills alongside modern tools (3D printers, laser cutters, CNC)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Hardboard (used for gear discs)
- Dowel (used for pegs)
- Timber/wood (general construction, jig base)
- Plastic strip (used as a compass to mark circles)
- Steel peg (pivot point in the circle-cutting jig)
- Brass sheet and brass bar (mentioned as an alternative construction material)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Circle marking with a plastic-strip compass** — drilling two holes 15 cm apart, pinning one end and scribing with a pencil
- **Chop-saw circle cutting** — pivoting a square blank on a steel peg at a fixed distance from the blade and rotating through successive cuts
- **Belt-sander edge finishing** — clamping a belt sander to a bench and spinning the disc on a pivot pin to smooth the circumference
- **Peg spacing layout** — spacing pegs at 30° intervals (yielding 12 pegs) to ensure the driving peg can enter and exit the cage without fouling
- **Reciprocation via partial peg wheel** — loading pegs on just under half of the wheel circumference so continuous rotation of the driver produces oscillating output

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Ratchet and pawl for unidirectional motion:** When asked about getting output in only one direction, Robert suggests a ratchet and pawl would be needed.
- **Cages are optional:** He notes you could skip cages entirely and use three plain peg gears, though cages add strength; making pegs from softer/hollow material would weaken them.
- **Peg gears as the basis for magnetic gears:** He confirms a commenter's point that peg gears are the conceptual foundation of magnetic gear designs.
- **Bennett's Doubler / high-voltage application:** He twice mentions that the reciprocating mechanism could drive a Bennett's Doubler to generate serious high voltage — flagged as a fascinating potential build.
- **Leonardo da Vinci connection:** He muses whether Leonardo used this reciprocating mechanism in his ornithopter design.
- **Polhem's Mechanical Alphabet:** He expresses interest in Polhem's alphabetic mechanisms and mentions he used to visit Stockholm frequently, hinting at a possible future visit.
- **Philosophy on modern tools:** Repeatedly agrees with commenters that 3D printers, laser cutters, etc. are valuable tools for specific jobs, but pushes back on the idea they are "the answer to everything" — maintaining hands-on skills is essential for understanding, building, and repairing technology.
- **Water wheel application:** Confirms the same peg gear principles apply to water wheel construction, just at a different scale.
- **Safety note:** Jokingly summarises his safety advice as "don't cut your fingers off."
