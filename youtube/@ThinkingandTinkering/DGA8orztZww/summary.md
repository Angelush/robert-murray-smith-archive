## Overview
Luke rebuilds a DIY push scooter from scratch, motivated by dissatisfaction with his previous version and improved skill level. He strips down a donor bicycle, fabricates a custom steel frame using welding and cutting techniques, and assembles a finished scooter with suspension forks, a repurposed footplate, and a traditional rear brake. The final result is a functional, blue-and-red painted scooter he rides in a car park to test the braking.

## Key Topics
- Reasons for remaking the original DIY scooter (improved skills, disliked previous design)
- Selecting and stripping a donor bicycle frame with suspension forks
- Designing the frame via 1:1 scale drawing
- Fabricating and welding the head unit and rear wheel mounts
- Designing and cutting an H-frame base plate with angled cuts for ground clearance
- Refurbishing and attaching a repurposed footplate from a mower
- Extending the handlebars using a larger tube sleeve
- Painting the frame in a blue and red colour scheme
- Fitting a traditional rear friction brake
- Test ride and brake evaluation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel angle iron/tubing — used for the frame structure
- Body filler — applied over welds to create a seamless appearance
- Paint (blue and red) — finish coat on the frame
- Non-slip mat — applied to the footplate surface
- Contact adhesive — used to bond the non-slip mat to the footplate
- M8 bolt — used to attach handlebars/forks
- Washer and cap nut — fasteners for handlebar assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- Stripping a donor bicycle down to bare frame components
- 1:1 scale drawing/layout of parts for design visualisation
- MIG/arc welding of steel frame components
- Angle cutting of tubing to create angled joins for ground clearance
- Tube-over-tube handlebar extension (sliding a larger-diameter tube over the original and welding)
- Body filler application over welds for aesthetic finishing
- Painting the fabricated frame
- Bolting down the footplate
- Fitting a traditional rear foot brake
