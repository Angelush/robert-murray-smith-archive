## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple "kitchen chemistry" method for producing graphene using a blender, water, graphite powder, and protein-rich biological fluids — specifically blood, eggs, or milk (whey protein). Based on a published research paper, he shows that milk-derived whey powder (containing bovine serum albumin and lactoglobulins) works best as a surfactant to prevent graphene platelets from restacking after being sheared off graphite. The takeaway is that high-quality graphene can be produced at home in about 30 minutes with no dangerous chemicals.

## Key Topics
- Current graphene pricing (~$200,000/ton) vs. graphite (~$150–350/ton) and the economics of home production
- Overview of prior blender-based graphene methods (Trinity College Dublin / washing-up liquid technique)
- The "kitchen chemistry 101" research paper and its use of biological proteins as surfactants
- Comparison of blood, egg white, and milk as protein sources
- Why whey powder (casein-removed milk) is the optimal choice
- Practical demonstration of the blending process
- Post-blending separation: settling, de-gassing, and optional centrifugation
- Potential applications: graphene-enhanced concrete, epoxy resins, plastics

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite powder** (~150 micron platelets, 45–250 micron range acceptable) — the graphene precursor
- **Deionised water** — blending medium (500 mL used)
- **Whey powder (chocolate-flavoured)** — protein source; 71 g protein per 100 g, used as surfactant
- **Bovine serum albumin (BSA)** — key protein in milk/whey that prevents graphene restacking
- **Lactoglobulins** — second key protein found in milk/whey
- **Casein** — milk protein *not* wanted; removed to produce whey
- **Hemoglobin** — the active protein in blood (tested, works but not optimal)
- **Lysozyme / albumin** — proteins found in egg white (tested, works but not optimal)
- **Washing-up liquid** — prior surfactant method (Trinity College Dublin), mentioned for comparison

## Techniques and Methods
- **Liquid-phase exfoliation via kitchen blender** — shearing graphene platelets off graphite using high-speed blending for ~30 minutes
- **Protein surfactant stabilisation** — biological proteins coat exfoliated graphene sheets, preventing restacking
- **Gravity sedimentation** — leaving blended mixture to settle so heavy unexfoliated graphite sinks and graphene remains in suspension
- **De-gassing** — allowing foam to dissipate after blending before collecting the graphene layer
- **Centrifugation** — optional step at 1500 RPM for 45 minutes to isolate graphene as a dry powder
- **Raman spectroscopy** — mentioned as the proper verification method (G and D bands), though Robert relies on the paper's characterisation

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief and conversational, but a few contain useful clarifications:
- **Torching the graphene is a bad idea** — burning it off destroys it, since carbon burns at ~400 °C.
- **To obtain a dry powder**, centrifuge at 1500 RPM for 45 minutes, then dry — confirms the video's method.
- **Using full milk (with casein)** requires extracting the casein first before it will work as a surfactant.
- **Graphene is bio-compatible**, confirming its suitability for biological or body-adjacent applications.
- **Graphite sourcing**: he buys his from a specialist importer (also available on Amazon for smaller quantities).
- **Graphene concrete**: directed commenters to Google Scholar for the extensive published literature on this application.
- **On other protein sources** (beyond blood/eggs/milk): he suggests others would likely work too, but declines to list them all — "give it a go."
