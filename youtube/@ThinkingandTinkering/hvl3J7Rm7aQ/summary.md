## Overview
In this video, Robert Murray-Smith explores the potato battery, explaining that the potato itself acts only as a salt bridge rather than contributing to the electrochemical reaction. The key insight — drawn from research by the Hebrew University of Jerusalem — is that boiling a potato for 8 minutes dramatically reduces its internal resistance, yielding up to 24 times more power output compared to a raw potato. He connects this discovery to the similarly high internal resistance seen in Crystal cells.

## Key Topics
- How potato batteries actually work (salt bridge, not energy source)
- The role of copper and magnesium electrodes in the reaction
- Hebrew University of Jerusalem research on boiled potato batteries
- Comparison between raw and boiled potato performance (voltage and current)
- Relationship between potato batteries and Crystal cells
- Effect of cutting boiled potato into slices on current output
- Cost comparison of potato-generated electricity vs. alkaline batteries

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potato (raw and boiled)** — used as a salt bridge electrolyte medium
- **Copper** — electrode (cathode)
- **Magnesium** — electrode (anode); the consumable that determines battery life
- **Zinc** — mentioned as an alternative anode used in the Hebrew University study
- **Plaster of Paris** — referenced from a prior video (Video 122a) on Crystal cells
- **Artificial urine** — referenced from prior Crystal cell video as electrolyte
- **Epsom salt** — referenced in a comment exchange as a potential electrolyte

## Techniques and Methods
- Boiling potato for 8 minutes to break down cell walls and reduce internal resistance
- Slicing boiled potato into thin slices/cubes to increase effective surface area
- Connecting potato cells in series to drive an LED
- Measuring open-circuit voltage and current draw (using a multimeter) on raw vs. boiled potato cells
- Comparing internal resistance characteristics between Crystal cells and potato/lemon cells
