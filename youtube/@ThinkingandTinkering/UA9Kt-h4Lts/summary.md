## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates diode-like behavior in a graphene sheet made with a blender and soap, dried on a plastic substrate with copper strips. By applying a DC bias and swapping polarity, he shows a dramatic asymmetry in resistance (~20+ MΩ in one direction, ~8 MΩ in the other), consistent with rectifying behavior. He concludes this suggests a simple, printable graphene diode is achievable, and notes the effect is stronger when graphene lines run parallel to the conductive strips.

## Key Topics
- Graphene dispersion prepared using a blender and soap
- Diode behavior in dried graphene sheets under DC bias
- Effect of graphene line orientation (parallel vs. perpendicular to copper strips) on diode performance
- Comparison of graphene ink vs. plain graphene solution
- Potential for a printable graphene diode

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — homemade dispersion made with a blender and soap, approximately 2 months old
- **Graphene ink** — used in a comparison sheet; parallel orientation tested
- **Soap** — used as a dispersant in graphene preparation
- **Copper strips** — deposited on plastic substrate as electrodes
- **Plastic** — used as the substrate for the graphene sheet

## Techniques and Methods
- Graphene dispersion prepared by blending graphite with soapy water
- Graphene drop-cast and spread onto a plastic substrate with copper strip electrodes, then air-dried overnight (~16 hours)
- Drying under a magnetic field to explore alignment effects on conductivity
- DC bias (6 V) applied across the graphene sheet
- Resistance measured with a multimeter (megaohm range) in both forward and reverse polarity
- Comparison of two sheet configurations: graphene lines running perpendicular vs. parallel to copper strips

## Robert's Own Replies
- **MIM diode identification**: Robert repeatedly clarifies that the device behaves like a **MIM (Metal-Insulator-Metal) diode**, linking to the Wikipedia article on MIM diodes for further reading.
- **Measurement methodology**: He references **Texas Instruments technical note EET-110** (measuring diodes) as the basis for his test setup, and notes that a bias voltage larger than the meter's internal voltage is required to properly characterise this type of diode.
- **High resistance context**: He acknowledges that operating in the megaohm range with a small voltage means errors are significant, but argues the asymmetry is large enough to be meaningful.
- **Forward voltage**: He is uncertain of the exact forward voltage drop, but expects it to be high for this type of device.
- **Replication encouraged**: When asked about repeatability, he admits he put the experiment aside and invites others to replicate it.
- **Patent note**: Jokingly suggests that coining a new name for the material could help with patents.
