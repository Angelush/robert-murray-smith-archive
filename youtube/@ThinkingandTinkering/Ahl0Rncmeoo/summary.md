## Overview
In this concluding video of his Wimshurst machine series, Robert Murray-Smith finishes the build by constructing and fitting the neutralizer brushes and current collectors, then successfully tests the machine. The video opens with a clear explanation of electrostatic induction — the physical principle behind how the machine generates high voltage — before walking through the final fabrication steps. The machine works as intended, producing visible sparks, and Robert notes the output voltage surprised him given the modest disk size.

## Key Topics
- Explanation of electrostatic induction as the operating principle of the Wimshurst machine
- Construction of neutralizer brush arms from brass bar stock
- Making the current collectors from salvaged and repurposed parts
- Fabricating carbon fiber brushes and burning off resin to soften them
- High-voltage safety considerations when operating the machine
- Relationship between spark gap distance and discharge voltage
- Plans to use the machine to drive an electrostatic motor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Brass bar (6 mm)** — used for neutralizer brush arms
- **Terminal block end pieces** — cut out and repurposed as brush arm end fittings
- **6 mm electrical lugs (13 mm diameter hole)** — sourced from electrical factors, soldered onto brush arms
- **Plastic (salvaged from printer)** — used as insulating plug on the axle; turned down to fit
- **Rubber** — mentioned as an alternative insulating material
- **Carbon fiber tow (resin-coated)** — used as brush material for the neutralizer brushes and current collectors
- **Cotton thread** — used to tie off and form the carbon fiber brush bundles
- **Drawing pins** — soldered onto the current collector to form contact points
- **Brass tube** — used to form the current collector body
- **Scrap timber and recycled LCD screens** — primary structural materials for the machine overall
- **Aluminium welding rods** (zinc-coated, sold on eBay) — mentioned in comments as the rod material used

## Techniques and Methods
- **Electrostatic induction** — principle explained: a charged plate induces charge separation in a nearby neutral plate; grounding then removing the ground leaves the plate with net charge
- **Annealing brass** — heating brass bar before bending to prevent it from snapping
- **Soldering** — joining lugs to brass arms, sweating the C-clamp onto the collector body, attaching drawing pins
- **Forming carbon fiber brushes** — folding resin-coated carbon fiber tow, binding with a cotton slip-knot, then burning in a flame to remove resin and produce a soft brush
- **Salvaging parts** — extracting C-clamp components from broken electrical connectors by sawing
- **Lathe turning** — turning down a salvaged printer roller to fit the axle insulator

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Voltage is limited by the machine's geometry**, not just the capacitors: voltage builds until *some* part of the machine reaches its breakdown voltage — whether the glass caps, the gap between sectors, the plates, or the spark balls. Separating the balls raises the limiting voltage, but other breakdown points set an upper ceiling.
- **The capacitors store energy; they don't govern voltage** — he clarifies this to a viewer who thanked him.
- **Current output is extremely low** — nano- to microamps. The danger is the high voltage, not the current.
- **Measuring the output voltage** would require a high-voltage probe attachment he doesn't own, though he floated the idea of making one.
- **Maximum voltage is constrained by disk size** — the disks in this build are relatively small; larger disks would allow higher voltages ("maybe a coffee table next").
- **Preferred electrostatic machine is the Dirod**, though he also likes Bonetti machines.
- **Next build is an electrostatic motor** powered by this Wimshurst machine — confirmed in multiple replies.
- **Motorised Wimshurst** is a possibility he's thinking about but not imminent.
- On terminology: he notes that "electrostatic influence machine" is an older term and "electrostatic induction" became more common around the turn of the century — both are understood and neither is strictly incorrect.
- The machine is predominantly built from scrap and salvaged materials, and he intends to keep it rather than sell it.
