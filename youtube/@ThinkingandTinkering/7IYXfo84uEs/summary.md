## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the concept of replacing gas as the working fluid in a Stirling engine with rubber (an elastomeric solid), arguing that rubber's gas-like thermodynamic behavior makes it a surprisingly practical and underexplored alternative. He demonstrates a simple pendulum-based rubber heat engine inspired by early 1900s chemist William Wand, and makes the case that rubber heat engines — especially when built with materials like spandex/Lycra — offer meaningful advantages over conventional gas-based engines. His key conclusion is that anyone considering building a Stirling engine should seriously investigate rubber-based engines instead.

## Key Topics
- How a conventional Stirling engine works (hot/cold sides, power piston, displacer piston, crankshaft)
- Rubber as a thermodynamic working fluid and its gas-like behavior
- Why ordinary rubber bands perform poorly in heat engines (rubber is a heat insulator)
- Why spandex/Lycra is a superior material for rubber heat engines (fine strands, better heat transfer)
- Richard J. Farris's 1977 research achieving ~1 kW/kg power density with only a 30°C temperature difference
- William Wand's early pendulum-based rubber heat engine design
- Practical advantages of rubber engines over gas-based engines
- Rubber's suitability for heat pump applications (reversible — can act as a refrigerator)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Rubber / elastomeric solids** — proposed working fluid replacing gas in heat engines
- **Spandex / Lycra** — fine-strand elastomeric fiber recommended as a superior working fluid due to better heat transfer and thermal properties
- **Rubber bands** — used in the demonstration engine; noted as performing poorly due to bulk thermal insulation
- **Wood (8 mm dowel)** — used for constructing the pendulum
- **Atmospheric steam** — used as heat source in Farris's 1977 experiments
- **Air** — noted as the working fluid in conventional Stirling engines

## Techniques and Methods
- **Knife-blade bearing construction** — scoring a knife blade into a wooden dowel to create a low-friction pivot point for the pendulum
- **Pendulum fabrication** — assembling a bob, counterweight, and rod balanced on a knife-blade bearing
- **Radiant heat cycling** — using a heat lamp shaded by the pendulum pole to alternately heat and cool the rubber band, driving oscillation
- **Heat engine cycle analysis** — comparing isothermal and adiabatic cycles for gas vs. rubber to demonstrate thermodynamic equivalence
