## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith converts a salvaged river bike's rear wheel into a hubless, spokeless wheel driven by a custom cog system made from a gear-making material introduced in video 1084. The cog is glued inside the wheel rim so that caster-wheel drive units engage the rim directly, eliminating the need for a centre axle. By the end of the video the prototype supports his weight and rolls freely, with only the chain drive still to be added.

## Key Topics
- Concept and motivation: extending the river-bike repair (video 1086) into a hubless wheel experiment
- Using a flexible gear-making strip (from video 1084) glued inside the wheel rim as a drive cog
- Designing a two-part support structure (wishbone + sledge) to hold the hubless wheel on the bike frame
- Three-point contact rule for stabilising a free-rolling wheel rim
- Stopping precession (side-to-side wobble) of the spinning wheel
- Drive options: which external cog to chain-drive, with direct motor drive mentioned as a future direction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Flexible gear-making strip/material (introduced in video 1084) — used to form the inner cog on the rim and wrap caster wheels
- Caster wheels (rated 100 kg) — used as the load-bearing and drive rollers inside the wishbone and sledge
- Super glue — used to bond the gear strip to the rim (test piece)
- Contact adhesive — mentioned as an alternative bonding agent
- Acrylic epoxy — mentioned as an alternative bonding agent
- Steel tube / paint metal from a salvaged exercise bike — fabricated into the wishbone
- Flat plate from an old winch — used as the rear frame-mounting plate
- Nylon-sleeved roller bearings — fitted to guide and constrain the wheel against precession

## Techniques and Methods
- Splitting the gear strip lengthwise and gluing it inside the wheel rim to form a large internal cog
- Lathe-sanding the gear strip ends to achieve a flush, gap-free join around the rim
- Welding caster wheels onto steel tube ends to create the wishbone support arm
- Welding around swivel castors to lock them rigid before incorporating them into the wishbone
- Bolting a flat-plate mount to the existing rear dropout to adapt the original frame
- Sliding a third cog unit into the front of the frame to achieve the required three-point rim contact and add chain-drive capability

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Sada Bike reference:** Robert twice points viewers to the *Sada Bike* as a real-world example of a hubless wheel design, suggesting it validates the concept he is exploring.
- **Rim thickness:** He acknowledges using extra-thick rims but concedes long-term durability is uncertain; he frames the project as exploring the *drive system concept* rather than producing a finished product.
- **Future drive idea:** He mentions he is thinking about **direct drive with a motor** as the next step for this wheel.
- **Prototype philosophy:** He explicitly notes this is him "working ideas out" rather than presenting a polished build, and that some mess/imperfection is acceptable at this stage.
- **Cleaning tip:** In response to a question about rust/dirt, he recommends a **vinegar and salt cleaning fluid** (which worked well for him on the river bike).
