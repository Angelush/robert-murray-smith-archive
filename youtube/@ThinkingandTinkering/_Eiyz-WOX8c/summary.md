## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains how the anisotropic electrical properties of graphene ink can be exploited to build a thermoelectric generator. He describes how layering sheets of unidirectionally-painted plastic or paper at an angle, with electrodes at each end and a heat differential applied across the stack, forces electrons along a differential path and generates current. The video is a conceptual explanation prompted by a viewer question, rather than a hands-on demonstration.

## Key Topics
- Anisotropic properties of graphene ink
- Difference between in-plane (ballistic) and perpendicular (near-zero) electrical conductivity of graphene sheets
- Operating principle of anisotropic thermoelectric generators
- Construction approach: layered, angled sheets with end electrodes and an applied heat differential

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene ink** — the core material; its anisotropic conductivity is the basis for the generator
- **Plastic or paper substrate** — suggested base for painting the ink in a unidirectional manner

## Techniques and Methods
- Unidirectional painting of graphene ink onto a flat substrate (plastic or paper)
- Stacking multiple painted sheets and offsetting them at an angle to exploit anisotropic conductivity
- Collecting current from end electrodes while applying a thermal gradient across the stack
