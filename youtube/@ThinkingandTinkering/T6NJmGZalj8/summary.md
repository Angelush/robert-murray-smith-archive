## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the construction of the body/enclosure for an Archimedes screw water generator, building on a previous video where the screw itself was made from repurposed CDs. He shows a clever method for creating a perfectly fitted U-channel enclosure using paper as a removable former, polyethylene terephthalate (PET) angle supports, and expanding polyurethane foam, resulting in a rigid, inclined housing that holds the screw with just the right clearance.

## Key Topics
- Building on a previous Archimedes screw project made from CDs
- Improving CD joint quality by lapping and epoxy-gluing (inspired by viewer suggestion)
- Design rationale for open vs. enclosed Archimedes screw generators
- Alternative generator design (pipe rotates around fixed screw) briefly mentioned
- Using paper as a sacrificial/removable forming mandrel
- Creating a tilted channel enclosure without complex angle calculations
- Curing properties of expanding polyurethane foam

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **CDs** — repurposed and shaped into the helical screw blades
- **Epoxy glue** — used to bond the lapped CD joints for a cleaner finish
- **40 mm pipe** — central shaft/body of the Archimedes screw
- **Aluminium rod** — used as the spindle inserted into centred plugs at pipe ends
- **Paper / sellotape** — used as a sacrificial former/mandrel to create clearance around the screw
- **Builder's board / Citra board** — rigid former board used to shape the channel (UK name: builder's board; US equivalent: Citra board)
- **Polyethylene terephthalate (PET)** — angle pieces used to line the channel and prevent foam adhesion to the paper former
- **Expanding polyurethane foam (expanding filler/expanding foam)** — sprayed over the former to create the rigid enclosure body; doubles in volume on curing

## Techniques and Methods
- **Lapping and epoxy bonding** — overlapping CD joints slightly before gluing for a stronger, neater join
- **Sacrificial paper mandrel** — wrapping paper tightly around the screw and securing with sellotape to create a removable inner form with built-in clearance
- **PET angle lining** — placing PET sheet at a 60° angle inside the channel to prevent foam from bonding to the paper
- **Foam application ("icing a cake" method)** — spraying expanding foam in thin layers, allowing it to expand to ~2× volume before trimming/sanding
- **Leg-length differential for tilt** — making one support leg longer than the other to achieve an inclined plane without cutting angles
- **Air-cure management** — leaving the foam structure partially open to allow air exposure needed for curing

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief social exchanges ("cheers mate", "lol"), but a few contain useful content:

- **On power generation options:** Recommends building a large Archimedes screw generator if you have flowing water, a large wind turbine if you don't, or investing in solar panels — and suggests beginners start small to gain experience first.
- **On a conical variant:** Responds to a viewer's suggestion about a cone-shaped central core, saying it is "very doable" with his technique, though the pitch would likely need to change.
- **On multiple flights:** Confirms that multiple screw flights could be used.
- **On foam type for enclosed curing:** Notes that the expanding foam he uses requires air exposure to cure, so for enclosed applications a two-part foam (which doesn't need air) would be needed — and confirms such products are available.
- **On the foam's name:** Clarifies that in the UK the product is simply called "expanding filler" or "expanding foam."
- **On scaling up:** Hints he hopes a viewer will take on the challenge of scaling the design up.
