## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the recently discovered giant pliosaur skull found on the Jurassic Coast in Dorset, examining how this apex predator's four-flipper locomotion system achieved remarkable efficiency. He then draws a direct parallel to modern dual-rotor wind turbine design, arguing that nature has already solved many engineering problems — we just need to look.

## Key Topics
- The pliosaur discovery at Kimmeridge Bay, Dorset (Jurassic Coast)
- Physical characteristics of the pliosaur as an apex predator
- How pliosaurs used four flippers in a figure-of-8 motion for propulsion
- Vortex dynamics in bird flocking (geese) as an analogy for flipper interaction
- Dr. Luke Muscutt's research on four-flipper swimming efficiency
- The Betz limit and conventional wind turbine efficiency constraints
- Ducted wind turbines and dual-rotor wind turbines as ways to exceed the Betz limit
- Biomimicry as a general principle for engineering innovation
