## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple technique for bending copper pipe into a coil suitable for an induction heater, without resorting to the traditional sand- or salt-filling method. By using a steel pipe as a former and applying slow, incremental bending, he produces a clean 5-turn, 70 mm diameter coil in minutes. The hollow tube ends are intentionally left open to allow water cooling if the coil overheats during operation.

## Key Topics
- Why coil bending is a critical step in building an induction heater
- Problems with the sand/salt-fill bending method (messy, time-consuming)
- Using a pipe former to shape copper tubing without kinking
- Adjusting coil turn spacing like a spring
- Water cooling as an option for managing coil temperature

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 8 mm copper pipe (flexible, used for the coil itself)
- 70 mm diameter steel pipe (used as the bending former)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Former-based pipe bending**: inserting a rigid steel pipe into a workbench fixture and slowly wrapping the copper tube around it with incremental pressure ("bounce and pull through")
- **Waste-end technique**: leaving a short straight lead-in section at the start of the bend, then cutting it off after forming
- **Spring-adjustment of turn spacing**: the finished coil acts as a spring, allowing turns to be pushed together or pulled apart for even spacing
- **Water cooling provision**: leaving tube ends open so water can be pumped through the coil if needed

## Robert's Own Replies
Several commenters suggested using the **frozen water method** (filling the pipe with water and freezing it before bending) as an alternative. Robert repeatedly declined, noting he does not have a freezer in his lab — making the former-based technique his practical go-to.

He confirms the induction heater will use **n-channel MOSFETs** in its circuit, responding to a question about components. He also notes this video is intentionally kept short as it is **one part of a longer prototype build**, which he plans to continue in follow-up videos.
