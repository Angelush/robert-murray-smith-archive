## Overview
This short follow-up video revisits Robert's earlier peen plating video, expanding on the possibilities beyond what was originally covered. Robert emphasizes that low-cost equipment (a tumbler bought for ~£145) opens up a wide field of experimentation in metal plating and mechanochemical nanoparticle synthesis. His central message is that scientific investigation doesn't require large budgets — just curiosity, a small amount of equipment, and use of resources like Google Scholar.

## Key Topics
- Follow-up on peen plating of copper onto aluminium
- Low-cost DIY approach to scientific experimentation
- Extending peen plating to other materials (plastics, silver, gold, titanium, rare earth metals)
- Mechanochemical synthesis of nanoparticles using a tumbler/ball mill
- Using Google Scholar as a research springboard

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper — used in the original peen plating experiment on aluminium
- Aluminium — substrate used in prior video; traditionally difficult to plate
- Silver — suggested as a candidate for peen plating experiments
- Gold — suggested as a candidate for peen plating experiments
- Titanium — mentioned as a possible coating material
- Rare earth metals — mentioned as an esoteric possibility for plating
- Copper nanoparticles — produced via peen plating process
- Acetone — suggested for pre-treating 3D-printed plastic parts to seal them before plating

## Techniques and Methods
- Peen plating — mechanical deposition of metal onto a substrate using tumbling with metal particles (e.g., marbles/balls)
- Mechanochemical synthesis — using mechanical energy (tumbling) to drive chemical reactions and produce nanoparticles
- Acetone vapour smoothing of 3D-printed parts — suggested as a preparation step before attempting to plate plastics
- Google Scholar literature review — recommended as a method for guiding experimental design
