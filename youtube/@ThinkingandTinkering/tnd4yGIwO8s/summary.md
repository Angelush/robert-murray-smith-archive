## Overview
Robert travels 186 miles to Skegness on the east coast of England to visit a Ventom Dynamics wind turbine installation on the pier, tracing its design lineage back to a 1790s wind capture device conceived by Erasmus Darwin (Charles Darwin's grandfather). He explains how the Ventom turbine's nested hyperboloid funnel structure accelerates airflow through a shrouded Venturi system without needing yaw control, and argues that for distributed energy generation the principle of "nearer is better" outweighs the conventional wisdom that "bigger is better."

## Key Topics
- History of Erasmus Darwin's wind capture device (c. 1797), developed in collaboration with Josiah Wedgwood
- The Ventom Dynamics wind turbine as a modern descendant of Darwin's design
- How the nested hyperbolic funnel structure concentrates and accelerates wind (Venturi effect)
- Omnidirectional wind intake — no yaw control required
- Comparison of tower-mounted large turbines vs. compact wind capture devices
- "Nearer is better" vs. "bigger is better" as competing paradigms for wind energy projects
- Suitability for urban/residential use and planning permission constraints
- The Skegness pier installation as a real-world trial (three-month paid test)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Venturi/shrouded duct acceleration — funnelling wind through converging hyperbolic passages to increase flow speed across the turbine
- Omnidirectional passive intake — capturing wind from all directions without mechanical yaw tracking
- Horizontal-axis turbine rotated 90° (vertical shaft) to fit inside the capture structure
- Distributed/near-load generation as an efficiency strategy (reducing transmission losses)
