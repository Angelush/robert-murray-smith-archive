## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build large, low-cost planetary gear systems by repurposing car timing belts glued to bicycle wheels and caster wheels. He explains the three main configurations of planetary gears (fixed ring, fixed sun, fixed planets) and presents compelling applications for DIY electric vehicle hub motors and vertical-axis wind turbines. The key insight is that industrial-scale ring gears are prohibitively expensive, but a bicycle wheel lined with timing belt material provides a functional equivalent for a fraction of the cost.

## Key Topics
- Recap of previous timing belt gear experiments (videos 1084 and 1089)
- Planetary gear system components: sun gear, planet gears, ring gear, and carrier
- The three operational configurations of planetary gears and how to switch input/output/fixed elements
- Gear ratio calculation using tooth counts
- Application as a hub motor gear reduction for electric vehicles
- Application in the rim of a vertical-axis wind turbine to extract energy at low wind speeds
- Comparison to planetary gears found in power drills, winches, window wiper motors, and mobility scooters
- Durability of plastic gears in commercial applications
- Using finished gears as casting patterns for more permanent versions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Car timing belt** — cut into strips and wrapped around wheel rims to form gear teeth; plastic and cloth construction, sometimes steel-reinforced, rated for ~5 years of continuous engine use
- **Bicycle wheel** — used as the ring gear body; thick aluminium rim provides a flat inner surface
- **Fixed caster wheel (plastic)** — trimmed and wrapped with timing belt to form the sun gear
- **Swivel caster wheel (with internal bearings)** — wrapped with timing belt to form the planet gears; bearings allow free spinning
- **Evo-Stik contact adhesive** — primary adhesive for bonding timing belt to gear bodies
- **Super glue** — used on some components; noted as a potential weak point
- **PTFE spray** — suggested lubricant to reduce friction if needed
- **Moly (molybdenum disulfide)** — mentioned as an alternative lubricant
- **Oil** — mentioned as a further lubrication option

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting timing belt into strips and inverting/wrapping them around wheel rims to expose teeth outward
- Bonding timing belt strips to aluminium and plastic surfaces using contact adhesive
- Trimming the diameter of caster wheels by trial and error to achieve the correct tooth count for mesh
- Assembling a three-planet gear train at 120° spacing inside the ring gear
- Demonstrating all three planetary configurations by hand to illustrate input/output relationships
- Proposing use of finished timing-belt gears as lost-pattern molds for metal casting

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed he **stopped pursuing this project** because viewer interest dropped off ("no one was watching it so I stopped making videos on it").
- Agreed that **rivets** are a good fastening idea (likely as an alternative to adhesive bonding, which he flagged as the main weak point in the video).
- Provided a detailed clarification on **friction and power transmission**: responding to a comment about a frictionless CVT design, he explained that frictionless transmission is physically impossible — friction is the mechanism of power transfer, and eliminating it entirely would cause slippage with no torque delivered. He noted that systems using high-pressure fluid at a sphere-disk interface (similar in principle to ferrofluid transmissions) increase friction locally to mimic gear tooth engagement.
- Called the sphere-disk CVT design "very clever" and "a genuine innovation," while maintaining his technical critique about the frictionless claim.
