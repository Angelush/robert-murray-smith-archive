## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make carbon cloth by catalytically graphitizing fabric (specifically J cloth) using a kiln and activated carbon as an oxygen barrier. The process converts ordinary cloth into a conductive, graphitic carbon material useful as wicks, activated carbon for odor absorption, and electrode materials for supercapacitors and batteries. The J cloth experiment at 900°C proved too aggressive, prompting him to recommend experimenting at lower temperatures to preserve the cloth's structure.

## Key Topics
- Uses of carbon cloth: wicks, odor absorption (shoes, boxes), supercapacitor and battery electrodes
- Catalytic graphitization vs. simple charcoal-making
- Role of iron (from stainless steel pot and wire) as a graphitization catalyst
- Oxygen exclusion using activated carbon as a burial medium
- Temperature and soak-time experimentation strategy
- Results comparison: J cloth vs. denim vs. wood

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **J cloth** — the primary material being carbonized in this experiment
- **Denim** — previously used successfully; shown as a reference sample
- **Wood** — another material Robert has graphitized; shown as a shiny, conductive example
- **Activated carbon** (fish pond filter grade) — used to bury the sample and exclude oxygen while absorbing off-gases
- **Stainless steel pot** — donates trace iron to the process, acting as a catalyst source
- **Stainless steel wire** — placed around the sample to provide additional iron catalyst contact
- **Iron (Fe)** — primary graphitization catalyst; migrates through the carbon precursor forming graphite/graphene
- **Nickel and Cobalt** — mentioned as alternative graphitization catalysts
- **Hydrochloric acid** — used post-process to wash out iron, yielding ferric chloride as evidence of iron migration
- **Ferric chloride** — byproduct detected after acid wash, confirming iron catalyst activity
- **Sulfur** — noted as a concern when carbonizing car tires

## Techniques and Methods
- **Catalytic graphitization** — burying a carbon precursor (cloth/wood) between layers of stainless steel wire in activated carbon, then heating in a kiln to 700–900°C for ~4 hours
- **Oxygen exclusion by burial** — packing the sample tightly in activated carbon inside a sealed stainless steel pot to prevent combustion
- **Temperature soak protocol** — ramping to target temperature, soaking for 4 hours, then cooling naturally (kiln off)
- **Multi-temperature sample testing** — running parallel experiments at 700°C, 800°C, and 900°C for unfamiliar materials to find the optimal graphitization window
- **Acid washing** — post-process HCl wash to remove residual iron catalyst and confirm graphitization via ferric chloride detection
