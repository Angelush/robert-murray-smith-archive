## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a working continuously variable transmission (CVT) built almost entirely from 3D-printed parts and rubber dog toys. Two solid rubber rings (toroids) sit in a printed cradle, and tilting a pair of balls between them changes the output speed ratio smoothly without changing input speed. He argues this mechanism has real-world utility — particularly for wind turbines — where maintaining a constant output speed regardless of variable input is essential.

## Key Topics
- Concept behind a ring-based continuously variable transmission (CVT)
- Using dog toys (solid rubber rings) as the traction rings of the CVT
- 3D-printed cradle, ball housings, and parallelogram steering linkage
- How tilting the balls changes the gear ratio continuously
- Practical application: keeping a motor/generator at constant speed (e.g. wind turbines)
- Files shared freely on Thingiverse and Tinkercad

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Solid rubber dog toys (rings) — 3¼-inch and 6-inch diameter, used as the traction toroids
- 3D-printed PLA parts (cradle, ball housings, parallelogram linkage, extension bars)
- 8 mm steel axle bar / 6 mm pin stock
- Skate bearings: 22 mm OD × 8 mm ID × 7 mm thick (×4, two per ring support)
- Bearings: 12 mm × 6 mm × 4 mm (×2, for the pillar/ball seat)
- Bearings: 12 mm × 8 mm × 4 mm (×1 per ball unit, inside the ball housing)
- Steel washers (to press on inner bearing races)
- Adhesive/glue (to fix plastic stop in place)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in Tinkercad followed by FDM 3D printing of all structural parts
- Press-fit bearing installation into printed housings
- Parallelogram linkage mechanism to tilt both balls simultaneously in the same direction
- Dual-ball arrangement to prevent cage splaying under load
- Ratio change by tilting ball axles relative to the ring plane (toroidal CVT principle)
- Files published to Thingiverse for open community use

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Ball rotation direction doesn't matter:** Robert clarified multiple times that he tried the ball orientation both ways and it made no difference to performance, so he chose whichever assembly was easiest. He notes the contact point between ball and ring may affect this, but in practice either works.
- **Governor / feedback mechanism:** Robert mentioned he was pondering using a centrifugal governor as an automatic feedback mechanism to maintain constant output speed.
- **Confirmed CVT classification:** He agreed with commenters that it is essentially a ratchet CVT / toroidal CVT — just a version buildable at home.
- **Files are free:** He confirmed he saves designs to Thingiverse and Tinkercad and prefers to give them away rather than sell them.
- **Parallelogram control rod geometry:** He confirmed the control rod does cross the centre of rotation, which is why the cage is offset — this ensures the ball tilt is symmetric.
