## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reviews the HALOT X1, a resin 3D printer from PioCreat (a company closely linked to Creality but focused on industrial and dental markets). He unboxes and tests the machine, highlighting its novel inverted-tank motion system and quick-release print bed, and prints transparent objects — including a Fresnel lantern glass and a Wollaston prism — with impressive clarity. His overall verdict is very positive, though he notes the quick-release bed mechanism didn't work as advertised out of the box.

## Key Topics
- PioCreat's background: industrial pellet printers and dental resin printers, now entering the domestic market
- The HALOT X1 Kickstarter campaign (launched April 1, 2025)
- Inverted print mechanism: the resin tank moves rather than the build plate
- Quick-release build plate design and its real-world performance
- Printing transparent/clear objects with resin vs. filament
- Hardware specs: 10.1" 16K mono screen, 4×9 µm pixel size, dual lead screws, diecast magnesium arm
- Automatic Feed Unit (AFU) with RFID resin recognition
- Slicer software walkthrough (proprietary + Chitubox Basic/Pro)
- Comparative philosophy: resin and filament printers as complementary tools

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water-washable clear resin (Nova 3D brand)** — used for test prints; chosen for low odour and ability to wash with water rather than IPA
- **IPA (isopropyl alcohol)** — mentioned as the standard washing agent for non-water-washable resins; noted as smelly
- **NАЦF film** — high-heat, high-resistance FEP-type film lining the resin tank
- **Non-yellowing clear resin** — mentioned in author comments as what he used; ~£40/litre

## Techniques and Methods
- **MSLA/resin 3D printing** — UV-masked layer-by-layer curing in a resin tank
- **Inverted tank motion** — resin tank moves up and down while build plate remains stationary (novel to this machine)
- **Quick-release build plate** — twist-handle mechanism intended to eject the cured print without scraping
- **Post-cure UV treatment** — 30-minute cure cycle in a wash-and-cure station (or window sunlight)
- **Water washing** — rinsing prints in water (enabled by water-washable resin) instead of IPA
- **RFID-based resin recognition** — automatic parameter setting when using branded resin bottles
- **Wi-Fi file transfer** — sending sliced files wirelessly to the printer
- **USB file transfer** — alternative to Wi-Fi using a USB drive

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Resin clarity and post-curing:** The transparency quality is partly due to the 16K screen improving layer density, but the resin itself matters a lot. He used a **non-yellowing clear resin at ~£40/litre** and confirmed post-curing is essential.
- **Print speed correction:** He clarifies the claimed speed is **170 mm per hour**, not per second (correcting a potential misunderstanding in the video).
- **Resin safety philosophy:** He expresses a pragmatic view — avoid drinking or bathing in resin, but beyond that, safety thresholds are personal. He acknowledges some people think he's too casual about it, and that's fine.
- **Future plans:** He intends to **experiment with multiple resins** to push clarity further, and teases a follow-up video exploring more possibilities with the printer.
- **FLG (graphene) dispersion note** (in a related comment thread): Proper dispersion of additives like FLG requires serious equipment (e.g., a K600 mixer, >£1,000) — simply stirring is insufficient.
