## Overview
Robert demonstrates the concept of serpentine coils — a winding geometry designed to eliminate the "dead" wire sections in conventional coils that contribute weight and resistance without generating electricity. He introduces a 3D-printed winding jig (designed by his friend Peter/Grendel) that makes winding serpentine coils straightforward, and walks through the geometry and measurements needed to size a coil for a specific stator.

## Key Topics
- Why conventional coils have inactive wire segments (end-turns that don't cut magnetic flux)
- How serpentine coils reduce dead copper and improve power-to-weight ratio
- The 90-degree rule for electromagnetic induction (wire, field, and motion must all be mutually perpendicular)
- The serpentine coil winding jig designed by Peter (Grendel) — available on Thingiverse/Tinkercad
- Step-by-step process: calculating wire length, setting jig width, winding, taping, and bending into the serpentine shape
- Fitting the finished coil onto a stator body (65 mm, 12-slot, 60 mm diameter stator)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper wire (magnet/coil wire)
- Neodymium magnet (used for demonstration of induction principles)
- M6 bolts (to lock jig positions)
- Adhesive tape (to secure wound coil before removal from jig)

## Techniques and Methods
- Electromagnetic induction demonstration using a single wire and permanent magnet
- Serpentine coil winding using a 3D-printed adjustable jig (semi-circular end pieces with sliding pegs)
- Jig sizing calculation: `(stator_length × number_of_slots) + (π × diameter × 2)`, plus 10% error margin
- Winding 200 turns onto the jig, taping at the correct pitch (6.5 cm intervals), then removing and bending into the serpentine/crown shape
- Fitting the coil between stator lug points
