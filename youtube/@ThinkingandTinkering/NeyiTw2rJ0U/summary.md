## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a surprisingly simple and cheap method for making DIY bone-conducting headphones using a small motor salvaged from a toy drone. The idea was originally sent to him by a viewer (mickykick11), who had discovered 45 years earlier that connecting a motor to one's teeth allowed hearing music through bone conduction. Robert shows that tucking the vibrating motor behind the ear produces clear audio via bone conduction, without any specialist parts.

## Key Topics
- Bone conduction audio principle — sound transmitted through bone rather than air
- Using a toy drone motor as a bone transducer
- Viewer-sourced idea (credit to mickykick11)
- Demonstration on glass vs. behind the ear
- Low cost and accessibility of the build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Toy/kids' drone motor (harvested from a cheap children's drone, ~£10 for four motors)
- Wires (stripped and attached to the motor)
- Glass (used as a surface demonstration of vibration)

## Techniques and Methods
- Stripping and rewiring a salvaged drone motor
- Connecting the motor to an audio source via input jack (same method as his "sound bug" video)
- Routing audio through an amplifier (noted as likely necessary due to motor size/power)
- Placing the motor against bone (behind the ear) for conduction
- Demonstrated vibration on a glass surface as an audible test

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief social responses, but a few contain useful technical clarifications:
- **Wiring:** Connected to an amplifier via the audio input jack — same method as his "Sound Bug" video ("just clipped the wires onto the jack pin").
- **Amplification:** He notes the motor likely needs to be run through an amp; the motor size affects output.
- **Motor shaft:** You can snip the shaft off the motor with a pair of pliers — it just vibrates, no spinning needed.
- **Enclosure idea:** He expressed enthusiasm for a viewer's suggestion to 3D-print a housing, noting it "would look so cool" and that commercial bone headphones are expensive.
- **Music choice:** Uses copyright-free music deliberately to avoid takedowns.
- **Tone:** Repeatedly emphasises this was a fun, accessible project rather than a serious commercial product comparison.
