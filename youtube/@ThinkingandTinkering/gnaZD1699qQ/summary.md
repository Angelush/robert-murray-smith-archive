## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a very low-friction magnetic bearing using simple, inexpensive materials. The bearing uses four repelling magnets to levitate a rotating axle assembly, with a ballpoint pen tip providing the single point of contact to constrain lateral movement. He concludes by noting the design is practical for sensitive motor research and similar low-friction applications.

## Key Topics
- Constructing a cradle/frame from uPVC foam board (Cintra board)
- Arranging permanent magnets in repulsion to achieve magnetic levitation
- Using a ballpoint pen ball as a low-friction point bearing
- Demonstrating spin duration with a small propeller on a cork float
- Potential applications in sensitive motor or generator research
- Copper rotating in a magnetic field as an incidental observation (eddy current / near-battery effect)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **uPVC-covered foam board** (sold as "Cintra board" in the US, available from builders' suppliers in the UK) — used for the cradle/frame
- **Permanent magnets** (12.5 cm center-to-center spacing) — four base magnets plus two rotor magnets, all oriented pole-same-direction for repulsion
- **Copper pipe fitting** — used as the rotor housing/axle (noted as slightly heavy; plastic suggested as a lighter alternative)
- **Ballpoint pen** — tip ball used as the low-friction point contact bearing
- **Adhesive tape** — used to size the pen barrel to fit the pipe
- **Glass or tile fragment** — placed under the ball tip to provide a hard-wearing contact surface
- **Cork** — used as a lightweight rotor body for the propeller demonstration
- **Small propeller** — attached to cork to demonstrate spin duration

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting and gluing foam board into a cradle frame
- Gluing magnets with consistent pole orientation (all norths pointing the same direction) to create a repulsion levitation field
- Inserting magnets into copper pipe fittings to form the floating rotor assembly
- Wrapping a ballpoint pen barrel with tape to achieve a press fit in the pipe
- Adding a hard surface (glass/tile) beneath the ball-tip contact point to reduce wear
- Blowing on a propeller to demonstrate near-frictionless sustained rotation

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Simpler version in mind:** Robert mentions he has an even easier version of this bearing concept planned, suggesting a follow-up project.
- **Earnshaw's theorem:** In response to a comment about full magnetic levitation, Robert correctly cites Earnshaw's theorem as the reason passive magnetic levitation in all axes is impossible, and suggests **bismuth** (a diamagnetic material) as a potential path to true levitation.
- **Eddy currents:** Agreeing with a commenter ("as Mike said"), Robert confirms that the copper rotor spinning in the magnetic field generates **eddy currents**, and notes it is "almost a battery" — an interesting electrical side-effect of the design.
- **Active control in applications:** Responds that in some real bearing applications (e.g., industrial), active control systems are used to stabilise magnetic bearings.
- **Material choice note:** Clarifies that he mentions the uPVC board specifically because viewers always ask what he's using; acknowledges plywood works equally well, but the foam board is faster and requires fewer tools.
- **Encouraging experimentation:** Expresses hope that the video inspires viewers to experiment themselves, though admits he might be "dreaming on that one."
