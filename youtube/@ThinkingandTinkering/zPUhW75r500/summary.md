## Overview
Part 8 of Robert's DIY gasification series, documenting the first live test of his home-built gasifier unit. He fires it up using charcoal, successfully produces combustible syngas, and demonstrates it burning at a striking distance from the output pipe. The main takeaway is that the unit works well — in fact, too well — producing more gas than needed, requiring future tuning to reduce airflow and output volume.

## Key Topics
- First live ignition test of the completed gasifier unit
- System layout: burner → cyclone filter → cooler → pump → output pipe
- Downdraft gasification principle (pump creates suction to pull combustion gases through)
- Charcoal as recommended first-burn fuel
- Historical context: gasifiers were widely used during WWII, initially fuelled by charcoal
- Attempting to run a generator from the syngas output
- Problem of excessive gas production / too-high vacuum
- Plans for future tuning: reducing draught, switching to wood pellets, possible gas collection tank

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Charcoal** — used as the first-burn fuel; historically preferred for early gasifier runs
- **Syngas (producer gas)** — the combustible output gas generated by the unit
- **Newspaper** — used to ignite the fuel load
- **Wood pellets** — mentioned as a planned future fuel alternative
- **Ethanol** — mentioned in comments as a possible conversion product from the syngas

## Techniques and Methods
- **Downdraft gasification** — fan/pump draws air downward through the fuel bed, converting biomass to syngas
- **Cyclone filtration** — used to remove particulates from the gas stream before it reaches the cooler and pump
- **Gas cooling** — a cooler stage in the system pipeline
- **Flare test** — lighting the output gas to verify combustibility and assess flow rate
- **Generator coupling (attempted)** — leaning the output pipe near the generator intake to test if it would run on syngas (unsuccessful due to excessive gas velocity)

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief acknowledgements, but several contain substantive follow-up points:

- **Pump necessity explained:** The pump is needed to initiate gas flow; once the engine is running it creates its own draw, but without the pump there is no flow to start production — a "chicken and egg" situation.
- **Too-fast flow prevents ignition:** Compared it to a propane torch running too fast — you must turn it down to get it lit, as excess velocity prevents proper air/fuel mixing.
- **Planned fixes:** Intends to reduce the draught (slower blower or a restrictor plate on the pipe) and switch to wood pellets as fuel.
- **Gas collection tank:** Mentioned as a possible solution to manage excess output.
- **Ethanol conversion:** Noted that the syngas can apparently be converted to ethanol — something he's considering exploring.
- **Cold conditions:** Mentioned filming was done in −3°C weather, which made the outdoor test physically difficult ("had to have a bath to unfreeze my joints").
