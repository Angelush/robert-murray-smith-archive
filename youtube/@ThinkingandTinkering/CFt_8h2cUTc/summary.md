## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to intercalate graphite using a sulfuric acid/nitric acid solution, then exfoliate it into expanded graphite nanoplatelets as a precursor to graphene. The process involves soaking large-flake graphite overnight in the acid mixture, then rapidly expanding it via direct heat (torch) or microwave. The result is a dramatic volumetric expansion that can then be sonicated or shear-mixed to produce graphene or graphene nanoplatelets.

## Key Topics
- What intercalation means in the context of graphite/graphene (forcing species between graphene layers)
- Preparation of an intercalation solution using sulfuric and nitric acids
- Importance of graphite flake size (50–150 µm optimal)
- Two exfoliation methods: direct flame (torch) vs. microwave
- Post-exfoliation processing (sonication or shear mixing) to produce graphene nanoplatelets
- Safety precautions for working with strong acids

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sulfuric acid (96%)** — primary intercalating species, 180 ml used in a 9:1 ratio
- **Nitric acid (69%)** — oxidizing agent to open graphite layers, 20 ml used
- **Large-flake natural graphite** — feedstock, ideally 100–150 µm particle size
- **Water (DI water)** — for washing intercalated graphite after acid treatment
- **Solvent (unspecified)** — mentioned for dispersing exfoliated material prior to sonication

## Techniques and Methods
- **Acid intercalation** — soaking graphite flakes in H₂SO₄/HNO₃ mixture overnight to force acid molecules between graphene layers
- **Washing** — rinsing intercalated graphite to remove surface acid while retaining intercalated species
- **Thermal exfoliation via torch** — applying direct flame to rapidly vaporise intercalant and expand layers
- **Microwave exfoliation** — placing intercalated graphite in a 700 W microwave on high power as a more effective alternative
- **Sonication / shear mixing** — post-exfoliation step to fully separate nanoplatelets into graphene

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Graphite particle size is critical:** 45 µm works well; below ~50 µm the expanding gases escape out the sides rather than forcing layers apart, giving poor results. He recommends ~50 µm minimum, with 100–150 µm being ideal.
- **Acid sourcing (UK/US):** Strong acids are available commercially — 96% H₂SO₄ as drain cleaner, 37% HCl as brick cleaner in DIY stores; concentrated HNO₃ can be mail-ordered. He buys his supplies from APC Pure and Mistral NI (both on eBay).
- **Graphite sourcing:** He buys large-flake graphite from Graphexel in 20 kg quantities; he does not sell it himself.
- **Paper credit:** The method is based on Mercado's paper (Tour group), which is available online.
- **Post-microwave processing:** After microwaving, wash in DI water, then sonicate or mechanically exfoliate.
- **Fumes warning:** The process generates a large amount of sulfuric acid fumes — a fume hood is essential, not optional.
- **Alternative intercalants:** Potassium intercalation was suggested by a commenter as potentially useful; Robert noted it could be worth exploring. He also mentioned he likes the formic acid intercalation route.
- **He is a registered lab**, which eases acid procurement; he acknowledges restrictions vary by country.
