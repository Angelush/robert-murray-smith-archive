## Overview
Robert builds a simple flux switching device from a salvaged microwave oven transformer and neodymium magnets to test whether permanent magnets can assist electromagnetic work. Using a homemade deviation meter, he finds the combined flux-switched configuration requires roughly one-quarter the power (~4.8W) of a bare electromagnet (~19.2W) to produce the same mechanical deflection. He presents this as a compelling, easily replicable experiment that hints at possible over-unity effects, while acknowledging the rough measurement margins.

## Key Topics
- Flux switching using E-I lamination cores from a microwave oven transformer (MOT)
- Magnetic flux containment within a closed iron circuit
- Comparison of electromagnet performance vs. electromagnet + permanent magnet configuration
- DIY force/deviation measurement using a mass-on-spring indicator
- Power efficiency: 4× improvement in work done per watt when flux switching is applied
- Implications for motors, generators, and possible over-unity claims
- Encouragement for independent replication

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Microwave oven transformer (MOT)** — donor component for both the E-I lamination core and copper primary winding wire
- **Neodymium magnets** — high-strength permanent magnets placed in the magnetic circuit
- **Copper wire (~50g of MOT primary)** — wound onto bobbins to form the electromagnet coils
- **Steel/iron E-I laminations** — form the flux-conducting core of the device
- **Acrylic** — used as structural base for the deviation meter
- **Hacksaw blade** — used as a spring element in the deviation meter

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and repurposing of a microwave oven transformer (sawing out the central tang to recover E and I laminations)
- Construction of a closed magnetic circuit (U + I core) without welding
- Winding electromagnet coils from recovered transformer wire onto bobbin cores
- Wiring two coils in parallel with matched polarity (both poles North)
- Homemade deviation meter: two I-laminations glued to a hacksaw-blade spring, mounted in acrylic, used as a proportional force indicator
- Comparative power measurement: volts × amps (watts) for equal mechanical deflection, bare electromagnet vs. flux-switched configuration
- DC bench supply powering the coils at ~6V
