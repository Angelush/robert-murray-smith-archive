## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits a secondhand bookshop and discovers a 1933 issue of *The Model Engineer* magazine containing an article about a liquid rectifier. He demonstrates how to build a working half-wave rectifier from kitchen ingredients — aluminium, lead, and a sodium bicarbonate solution — successfully running a DC motor from an AC supply. The broader message is a meditation on the internet as a tool: valuable for inspiration, but best paired with physical exploration and hands-on discovery.

## Key Topics
- The value of secondhand bookshops as curated, focused sources of knowledge vs. the overwhelming abundance of the internet
- Types of rectifiers: mechanical, fluid, and electrical
- History of early electrical rectifiers (mercury vapor, copper oxide, selenium oxide)
- Construction and demonstration of a liquid (electrolytic) rectifier using kitchen materials
- Half-wave rectification and its ability to drive a DC motor from AC power
- Philosophy of learning through doing and physical exploration

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda)** — used as a saturated electrolyte solution
- **Aluminium strips** — one electrode of the rectifier (positive); two strips would form an electrolytic capacitor
- **Lead strip** — the second electrode (negative), paired with aluminium to create asymmetric conduction
- **6V AC supply** — power source for the demonstration
- **DC brushed motor** — load used to demonstrate rectification

## Techniques and Methods
- **Liquid (electrolytic) rectification** — exploiting the asymmetric conductance between aluminium and lead electrodes in a sodium bicarbonate electrolyte to pass current preferentially in one direction
- **Half-wave rectification** — only one half of the AC cycle is passed, sufficient to spin a DC motor in a single direction
- **Electrolytic capacitor analogy** — two aluminium strips in the same solution would form a capacitor rather than a rectifier, illustrating the importance of the dissimilar metal pair
