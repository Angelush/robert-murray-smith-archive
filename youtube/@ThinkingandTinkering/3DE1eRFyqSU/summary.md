## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests whether the hemp-based bioplastic he developed in a previous video — made from hemp, casein, and tea — can be processed using injection moulding. Using a machine donated by Kings School Canterbury, he successfully injection moulds the material into a disc shape, marking a promising first result while acknowledging significant optimisation work remains.

## Key Topics
- Demonstration of injection moulding hemp/casein/tea bioplastic
- Temperature calibration for the injection moulding process
- Material properties post-moulding (rubbery vs. hard output)
- Mix ratio optimisation as a next step
- Fibre form factor (long bast fibre vs. chopped fibre)
- Biodegradability and commercial potential of the material

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Hemp bast fibre** — the structural reinforcement component; donated as long fibre, with chopped fibre (~0.5 cm) preferred for moulding
- **Casein** — protein-based binder component of the bioplastic
- **Tea** — third component in the hemp plastic formulation (role not elaborated in this video)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Injection moulding** — plastic pellets loaded into a hopper, plunger used to force molten material through a nozzle into a disc mould
- **Temperature sweep** — incrementally testing moulding temperatures (starting at 200°C, stepping down to 80–90°C in 10°C increments) to find the optimal processing point
- **Mix ratio investigation** — recognised as a necessary next step to achieve hard rather than rubbery output
