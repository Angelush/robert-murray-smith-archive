## Overview
This video is a guided walkthrough of the Queen's Mine graphite site, likely located in Sri Lanka, where Robert and associates hold mining rights. The mountain was originally excavated by the British but abandoned at the end of colonialism, leaving the deposits largely intact. The tour reveals an exceptionally rich graphite deposit, with veins running throughout the entire mountain and active extraction underway in two shafts.

## Key Topics
- History of the Queen's Mine — abandoned by the British post-colonialism, not due to exhaustion
- Overview of the site's vein structure across multiple old and active mine entrances
- Active drilling and tunneling operations following discovered veins
- Scale of graphite deposits — veins as wide as the shaft, pockets potentially holding ~20 tons
- Mine safety considerations when tunneling through rock
- CAD (core) drilling to probe vein extent through walls

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — the primary deposit; vein graphite in lump form, veins ranging from a few inches to ~1 meter thick; pockets estimated at up to 20 tons. Author comments clarify this is Sri Lankan vein graphite with lump sizes up to ~50 kg per lump, which he notes is of considerably higher quality than Turkish open-cast graphite (mostly powder) or kish graphite.

## Techniques and Methods
- **Tunneling/drifting** — horizontal tunnels driven through rock to intersect veins
- **Shaft sinking** — vertical shafts (~20–25 ft deep) following discovered veins downward
- **Drilling** — rotary drilling used to break rock and advance tunnels; old British drill holes still visible in rock faces
- **CAD (core) drilling** — used to probe through walls and confirm vein extent beyond the current working face
- **Visual vein tracing** — following vein strike direction (N/S/E/W) to plan extraction paths
- **Geological scanning** — university geology head surveyed the area and identified at least 8 additional surface veins

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On the mine's current status:** Robert confirmed in a later comment that the site is still there but has been experiencing operational difficulties, and that he visited the year prior to his reply.
- **On graphite quality and type:** He clarified this is Sri Lankan vein graphite, notable for large lump sizes (up to ~50 kg per lump, down to a few microns). He contrasted it favourably against Turkish open-cast graphite (lower quality, mostly powder) and kish graphite (cheap). He noted price varies widely depending on size, purity, and type.
- **On his personal interest:** He expressed keen ongoing interest in the project, implying relevance to his own graphite research work.
- **On his location at time of replies:** He was in British Columbia and about to travel to Nova Scotia, noting that would make him and another commenter "neighbours" in Canadian terms.
