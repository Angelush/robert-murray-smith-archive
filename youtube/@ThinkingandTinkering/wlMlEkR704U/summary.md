## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple, low-cost wind generator by salvaging a DC fan motor from a Nissan Micra (or any car) and extending its blades with sections cut from standard plastic drainage pipe. The build requires minimal tools and materials, and in testing the generator produced up to 20 volts. Robert considers it one of the cheapest and simplest functional wind generators possible to build in about half a day.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a scrapyard car radiator fan motor as a wind generator
- Fabricating extended turbine blades from drainage pipe
- Blade geometry: pitch, leading/trailing edges, and twist compensation
- Attachment of new blades to the existing motor hub
- Outdoor testing and voltage output
- Trade-offs between blade count, efficiency, and ease of construction
- Potential power output (~100 W) and practical mounting considerations

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Car radiator fan motor (DC)** — salvaged from a Nissan Micra or similar vehicle; acts as the generator
- **100 mm (4-inch) diameter plastic drainage pipe** — approximately 5 mm thick, used to fabricate extended turbine blades; a 60 cm section cut into quadrants yields four 50 cm blades
- **Bolts and nuts** — used to fix new blades onto the original hub's mounting tabs

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a 60 cm section of drainage pipe into four equal quadrants by marking and sawing along the pipe's axis
- Trimming the blade base to remove the excess and fit flush to the hub
- Drilling three mounting holes per blade, positioned slightly toward the centre (rather than the leading edge) to compensate for the existing twist in the original fan hub's connection points
- Bolting fabricated blades onto the original hub tabs
- Hand-held outdoor test to measure open-circuit voltage output

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Car radiator fans are weather-rated:** He confirmed the motor is a car radiator fan, noting it already endures more weather than typical outdoor conditions — dismissing durability concerns.
- **Power output ~100 W:** Robert repeatedly states the generator produces around 100 W, but clarifies this figure came from a viewer who built one themselves; he does not know the wind speed at which it was measured.
- **Blade count trade-off:** He acknowledges that an odd number of blades improves efficiency, but chose four for stability and ease of construction — explicitly accepting the efficiency penalty as a design trade-off.
- **Even blade numbers can stall:** He noted that even-numbered blade configurations can stall, which is a known downside.
- **PVC UV degradation is overstated:** He pushed back on concerns about UV degradation of PVC pipe, calling it "slight not significant" and attributing the exaggerated concern to window industry marketing.
- **Mounting and wiring solution:** Suggested mounting the generator on a swivel and routing the wires down the centre of the support pole.
- **Alternators as an alternative motor:** Agreed alternators are a good choice for this project, noting they already have built-in voltage regulation.
- **Using a jigsaw:** He chose a jigsaw for blade cutting because more people own one.
- **Follow-up video:** Directed several viewers to a related video at `https://youtu.be/8RFQG4dglSs` for further information.
