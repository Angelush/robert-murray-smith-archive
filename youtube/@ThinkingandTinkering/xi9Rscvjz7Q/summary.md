## Overview
This video, presented by Steve Mills (a collaborator of Robert Murray-Smith), demonstrates using conductive ink and a microwave to cut glass bottles into artistic shapes such as vases. The technique is shown on both a small bottle and a thick square coffee jar, with a water-filled plate used to absorb excess microwave energy. The main takeaway is that this ink-based cutting method handles difficult shapes (corners, thick glass) far more easily than traditional wire or glass cutters.

## Key Topics
- Using conductive ink to cut glass bottles into decorative shapes
- Managing microwave energy with a water-filled plate during the cutting process
- Cutting irregular and thick glass (square coffee jar with corners)
- Potential for upcycling wine bottles and glass jars into vases and art pieces

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Conductive ink — applied as a fine line around the bottle to direct the cut
- Acetone — used to clean the glass surface before applying ink
- Electrical tape — used as a guide to achieve a straight, even ink line
- Water — placed in a small plate inside the microwave to absorb excess microwave energy

## Techniques and Methods
- Cleaning glass with acetone prior to ink application
- Using electrical tape as a masking guide for a clean ink line
- Painting an ellipse or custom shape with conductive ink to produce non-straight cuts
- Microwave heating to propagate a crack along the ink line
- Removing the piece promptly when arcing/discharge begins to die down
- Wearing protective gloves when handling freshly cut glass

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are brief and informal, but a few useful points emerge:
- Encouraged viewers to try variations themselves and report back ("if it works you could let us all know")
- Confirmed they tested around half a dozen bottles and jars — two gave poor results, but the rest were "super cool," suggesting a reasonable success rate
- Mentioned he would test a test tube as a follow-up experiment
- Noted the technique is not well suited for cutting tiny holes
