## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a step-by-step guide to building two small supercapacitors from scratch — one using graphene oxide and one using activated carbon — demonstrating that high-performance energy storage devices can be assembled on a benchtop in minutes. He explains the six fundamental components of a supercapacitor, walks through construction and material choices, and tests both devices, achieving a remarkable ~4 farads in a single 1 cm² activated carbon cell. The video concludes with a strong encouragement to experiment, as many material combinations remain unexplored.

## Key Topics
- The six-component structure of a supercapacitor (two current collectors, two active material layers, one separator, one electrolyte)
- Survey of current collector options (aluminium, nickel, copper, graphoil, zinc, magnesium, steel, lead)
- Active carbon materials: activated carbon (fish tank grade) vs. graphene oxide
- Separator options for DIY use (kitchen towel, tissue paper, glass fibre)
- Electrolyte types: neutral (sodium sulfate), acidic (sulfuric/phosphoric acid), alkaline (KOH, NaOH, LiOH)
- Symmetric vs. asymmetric supercapacitor designs
- Electrode fabrication: dip coating, doctor blade, spin coating, and hot-plate drying
- Binder selection and ratios for active material inks
- Capacitance measurement using the Sencore LC1002 analyzer
- Scaling potential and specific capacitance discussion (~500–700 F/g estimated)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide** — homemade via the Hummers method; used as active electrode material dispersed in water
- **Activated carbon** — fish tank grade, blended to fine dust and dispersed in acetone with binder
- **Graphoil (graphite foil)** — primary current collector used in the build; ~50 µm thick, chemically resistant
- **Aluminium foil** — kitchen foil; suitable with neutral electrolytes like sodium sulfate
- **Nickel foil** — recommended for alkaline environments (e.g., KOH)
- **Copper foil/tape** — available from hardware stores; mentioned as an option
- **Lead foil** — hand-beaten; noted as stable in sulfuric/phosphoric acid environments
- **Zinc, magnesium (printer's plate), steel** — mentioned as additional current collector candidates
- **Acetone** — used as solvent/carrier for activated carbon ink
- **Cellulose acetate** — binder used in this specific build (dissolved in acetone)
- **PVA, PTFE, CMC (carboxymethyl cellulose / wallpaper paste), SBR** — alternative binders; 5–10% by weight recommended
- **Sodium sulfate** — neutral electrolyte used in the demo
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — alkaline electrolyte option; requires nickel or compatible collector
- **Lithium hydroxide, sodium hydroxide** — additional alkaline electrolyte options
- **Sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid** — acidic electrolyte options; corrosive, care required
- **Manganese dioxide, vanadium pentoxide** — transition metal oxides for asymmetric capacitor designs
- **Carbon felt** — hobby-shop sourced; mentioned as a usable active material
- **Celgard** — polypropylene separator from the commercial 500 F cell shown for reference
- **Bismuth** — additive mentioned (in comments) to suppress electrolysis at higher voltages
- **Kitchen towel / Japanese tissue paper / toilet roll / glass fibre** — DIY separator options

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dip coating** — electrodes dipped into active material slurry (carbon or graphene oxide) to deposit a film
- **Doctor blade coating** — mentioned as a more controlled alternative to dip coating
- **Spin coating / skip coating** — cited as further deposition options
- **Hot plate drying** — graphene oxide coated graphoil dried at ~50 °C
- **Symmetric cell assembly** — same active material on both electrodes; no initial polarity (gains polarity after first charge/discharge cycle)
- **Asymmetric cell assembly** — different active materials on each electrode (e.g., activated carbon vs. MnO₂); inherent polarity
- **Sandwich assembly** — electrodes layered with separator and wetted with electrolyte
- **Charge/discharge testing** — 1.2 V charge, discharged through a small motor; current draw monitored (~40–45 mA)
- **Capacitance measurement** — Sencore LC1002 capacitor/inductor analyzer used to measure farads
- **Compression** — mentioned as a further optimisation step to reduce ESR and improve collector contact

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was active in the comments and added several useful clarifications:

- **Acetone is a solvent, not a binder** — he explicitly corrected commenters who confused the two; the binder used in this video was **cellulose acetate**, dissolved in acetone. PVA in water is a straightforward alternative.
- **Binder options and ratios** — PVA, SBR, CMC (wallpaper paste), and PTFE all work; aim for **5–10% binder by weight** of active material.
- **Do not use acids with aluminium foil** — will corrode the collector; use sodium sulfate (start at 1 M) with aluminium instead.
- **Electrically conductive electrolytes will short the cell** — the electrolyte must be ionically conductive only.
- **Rapid voltage drop usually indicates a short** — caused by a torn separator, insufficient binder allowing active material migration, or contacts touching the electrolyte.
- **Supercapacitors vs. batteries on cost** — batteries ~$100–200/kWh; supercapacitors ~$100,000/kWh; power density is the supercap's advantage, not energy density.
- **Higher voltage is possible** with additives like **bismuth** to remediate electrolysis side-reactions.
- **Motor spin threshold** — the small motor used begins spinning at ~0.2 V.
- **Grafoil is simply graphite foil** — also sold under names "graphoil" and "flexible graphite"; easily found on eBay.
- **Record performance noted** — he later achieved **9 farads on 1 cm²** with three dip coats and compression of the graphene electrodes.
- **Symmetric vs. asymmetric explained in comments** — symmetric cells start with no fixed polarity and gain it after the first charge/discharge cycle; asymmetric cells have inherent polarity determined by the choice of materials.
- **Start small** — strongly recommended beginners build several tiny prototypes first, as "the devil is in the detail."
