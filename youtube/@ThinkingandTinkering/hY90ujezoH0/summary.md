## Overview
This video, presented by Luke (a collaborator on Robert's channel), demonstrates combining a whirlpool turbine with a TENG (Triboelectric Nanogenerator) rain/flow energy harvester to scavenge additional electrical energy from the same water flow. The experiment confirms that even after the turbine has extracted most of the mechanical energy, the TENG can still recover a useful residual signal — achieving around 185–190 microamps. The core takeaway is that energy scavenging layers can be stacked to improve overall yield from a single water source.

## Key Topics
- The distinction between potential and kinetic energy in water flows
- Water and wind as fluid energy sources, and why kinetic energy was chosen for this experiment
- A viewer-suggested hybrid concept: placing a TENG beneath a whirlpool turbine
- Energy scavenging from "spent" flow after turbine extraction
- Measured output of the combined system (~185–190 µA)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium (used as an electrode layer in the TENG setup)
- Conductive ink (referred to as "our ink", applied over the aluminium for the TENG)
- Builder's board (used as a mechanical support/holder for alignment)

## Techniques and Methods
- Whirlpool turbine energy generation from flowing water
- TENG (Triboelectric Nanogenerator) configured as a rain/flow energy harvester, placed beneath the turbine outlet
- Stacking/layering two independent energy harvesting mechanisms in series on a single water stream
- Electrical output measured in microamps using a PiP (portable instrument) setup
