## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a low-friction, magnetically assisted bearing using steel knitting needles and basic craft materials. The bearing works by using opposing magnets to support a needle point in a small indented cradle, dramatically reducing friction. He tests it with a paper daisy wheel that spins freely with just a breath of air.

## Key Topics
- Constructing a simple magnetic bearing cradle from foam board and aluminium strips
- Using opposing (attracting) magnets to levitate and support a rotating shaft
- Demonstrating the low-friction result with a spinning daisy wheel
- Suggestions for improving performance with finer needle points or lubrication

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Foam board (also called builders board, fascia/soffit board, or Cintra board in the US)
- Aluminium strips (used as cradle end-pieces)
- Neodymium/stack magnets (arranged in attracting north-south orientation)
- Steel knitting needle (must be steel to interact with magnets)
- Super glue (for assembling the cradle)
- Oil (mentioned as a potential improvement for the bearing point)
- Paper daisy wheel (used as a demonstration rotor)
- Darning needle (suggested as a better alternative due to finer point)
- Ballpoint pen tip (suggested as another fine-point alternative)

## Techniques and Methods
- Punching a small indentation into aluminium strips to create a pivot seat for the needle point
- Gluing aluminium strips and magnets to foam board cradle using super glue
- Stacking multiple magnets to increase magnetic support force
- Balancing magnet attraction to offset the weight of the rotating needle shaft
- Using a blown paper daisy wheel as a qualitative test of bearing friction
