## Overview
Robert tests whether the "forever wick" (also called the "magic wick," a carbon-based wick with a copper pipe surround) can serve as a direct drop-in replacement for a conventional wick in a garden heater. After initial failure and a 20-minute burn test using paraffin oil, he concludes it cannot be a straightforward replacement, but explains the underlying physics that make it workable under the right conditions.

## Key Topics
- Comparison of the forever/magic wick against standard wicking material
- Why the forever wick fails as a direct replacement
- The role of heat conduction in the wick's operation
- How heating the oil at the intake reduces viscosity to enable wicking
- The advantage of using heavier oils due to the preheating effect
- Tuning parameters: copper pipe length and wick length

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Carbon (core material of the forever/magic wick)
- Copper pipe (surrounds the carbon wick)
- Paraffin (fuel used in the garden heater test)
- Vegetable oil (mentioned as a usable alternative due to the preheating effect)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting and inserting the forever wick into a garden heater
- Priming/preparing the wick before attempting ignition
- Extended burn test (~20 minutes) to rule out residual fuel in the wick as a variable
- Adjusting copper pipe length and wick length to optimise performance
