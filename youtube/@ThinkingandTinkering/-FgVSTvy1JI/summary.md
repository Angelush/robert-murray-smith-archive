## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the surprisingly underappreciated history of the bicycle, arguing that it shaped not only modern industrial practices (assembly lines, advertising, lobbying) but also drove major social change — including the emancipation of working-class Europeans and women. He closes by celebrating the bicycle's ongoing relevance as both a global product and a rich source of parts for upcycling projects.

## Key Topics
- Albert August Pope (1878) and his pioneering use of mechanized production, advertising spend, and patent litigation — practices later adopted wholesale by Henry Ford
- The 1885 Rover safety bicycle and its role in the emancipation of the working class and women across Europe
- Evolution of bicycle designs: velocipede (1817), Dandy horse, boneshaker, penny-farthing, and the modern safety bicycle
- The diamond frame: adopted for cost and ease of manufacture, not mechanical necessity
- Military use of bicycles in World War II
- The first electric bicycle (1897)
- Scale of production (~100 million bicycles/year) and waste (~15 million discarded in the UK annually)
- Upcycling bicycle parts into generators, wind turbines, smoothie makers, and more

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wood — used in early cartwheel-style bicycle wheels (boneshaker)
- Cast iron — early bicycle frames
- Tubular steel (diamond frame) — replaced cast iron; cheaper and lighter to mass-produce
- Wire spokes — used on penny-farthing wheels
- Solid rubber — penny-farthing tyres
- Dunlop (pneumatic) tyres — improved comfort, not mechanical performance
- Aluminium — modern frame material
- Carbon fibre — modern frame material
- Bamboo — cited as a viable modern frame material
- Cardboard — mentioned as an example of unconventional frame material

## Techniques and Methods
- Assembly-line / mechanized production (introduced to bicycles by Pope, later adopted by Ford)
- Chain drive to rear wheel (key innovation of the safety bicycle)
- Diamond frame construction (structural geometry enabling cheap mass production)
- Repurposing/upcycling bicycle components into: wind turbines, generators, blenders/smoothie makers, freewheels, and saws
