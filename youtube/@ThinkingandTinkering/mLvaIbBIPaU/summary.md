## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates his third attempt at replicating the Flynn motor, focusing on whether the device can generate usable electricity. He rewound the coils with salvaged thin-gauge wire, drove the input coils with an oscillating current (~50 kHz, ~12 V), and measured approximately 50–100 millivolts out of the output coils — a modest but encouraging result that confirms the principle is working. The video concludes with plans to build a more controlled version with a known turn count to allow proper measurements.

## Key Topics
- Replication of the Flynn motor device
- Coil rewinding using salvaged wire from a fan motor
- Input/output coil wiring configurations (parallel input, series output)
- Use of an oscillating (tank) circuit as the driver
- Output rectification via a diode bridge and smoothing capacitor
- Reference to Patrick Kelly's free ebook of alternative energy circuits
- Plans for a more controlled, measurable follow-up build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Thin-gauge magnet wire (salvaged from a fan motor, gauge unknown)
- Two black coils (rewound)
- Pins rewound with the same wire
- Smoothing capacitor
- Diode bridge rectifier

## Techniques and Methods
- Coil rewinding (filling to capacity, unknown turn count)
- Parallel wiring of input coils, series wiring of output coils
- Oscillating/tank circuit drive at approximately 50 kHz
- Diode bridge rectification of output signal
- Capacitor smoothing of rectified output
- Ad-hoc prototyping to quickly validate concept before committing to a controlled build
