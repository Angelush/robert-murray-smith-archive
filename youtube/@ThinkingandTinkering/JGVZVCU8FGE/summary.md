## Overview
In this follow-up to video 1700, Robert Murray-Smith converts his simple tin-can methanol burner into a proper infrared space heater by adding two key components: a reflective aluminium backing and a stainless steel mesh mantle. The mantle glows red-hot and radiates significant infrared heat, which the reflector directs outward — producing a surprisingly powerful and long-lasting heater built almost entirely from recycled household items for around £2.55.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the methanol burner from video 1700 (pea and tomato tin construction)
- Role of carbon felt as a wick material
- Building an aluminium reflector from an old PC case to direct infrared output
- Using 20cm cake tins as the heater housing
- Fabricating a tubular stainless steel mesh mantle to glow red and emit infrared radiation
- Fuelling with methanol, burn times (~4–5 hours per fill)
- Simple multi-burner strategy as an alternative to a continuous fuel feed
- Safety considerations: CO monitoring, ventilation, safe extinguishing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — primary fuel; Robert sources it on Amazon at ~£1.55/litre
- **Carbon felt (100% pure carbon)** — used as the wick, 5mm thick, lining the inner can
- **Stainless steel mesh** — formed into the cylindrical mantle; sold for vermin-proofing soffits/veg gardens
- **Aluminium sheet** (from an old PC case) — bent into a semicircular reflector
- **Tin cans** (pea and tomato tins) — structural components of the burner and mantle cap
- **20cm cake tins** — used as top and bottom housing panels
- **Brass hardware** (handles, feet) — decorative and functional fittings

## Techniques and Methods
- Constructing a wick-based alcohol burner from nested tin cans lined with carbon felt
- Bending aluminium sheet freehand into a semicircular (approximating parabolic) reflector
- Forming stainless steel mesh into a tubular mantle by wrapping, folding, and pinching
- Assembling housing from cake tins bolted to the reflector
- Infrared heating via a glowing mesh mantle — flame heats the mesh, mesh radiates IR
- Fuel feeding through a small hole (1mm nail/push-pin) to prevent air traps
- Extinguishing safely: remove mantle with tongs, then cap the can with a tin lid

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Safety (repeated emphasis):** Always use a CO/CO2 monitor with any open-flame device — this applies equally to gas cookers, boilers, and wood burners. Ventilation is essential; a flue is simply a vent and cannot be sealed.
- **Extinguishing:** Lift the mantle off with tongs, then place a cap (e.g. bottom of a pea tin) over the flame to smother it.
- **Cost:** The heater cost him approximately £2.55 to build; brass fittings were optional aesthetics.
- **Reflector:** Just needs to be reflective and heat-resistant; he used two halves of an old aluminium PC case.
- **Fuel alternatives:** Paraffin works but is sooty in this configuration — best suited to lighter fuels (methanol, ethanol, IPA). Methanol from Amazon at ~£1.55/litre.
- **Yellow flame mix:** 40:40:20 methanol:ethanol:water produces a yellow flame.
- **On formaldehyde concerns:** Corrected the chemistry — partial oxidation to formaldehyde requires a mixed iron molybdate catalyst under specific conditions not present here; oxygen consumes formaldehyde above 450°C.
- **CO emissions:** Combustion in this setup is largely complete and CO output is very low, but a CO monitor is still recommended as standard practice.
- **Usage surface:** He places the heater on a 600mm × 600mm × 12mm ceramic tile.
- **Multi-feed:** Could be done with a pipe, flask, and valve, but adding two burners in rotation is simpler.
- **Wick materials:** Many materials work as wicks; carbon felt is his preference but not the only option.
