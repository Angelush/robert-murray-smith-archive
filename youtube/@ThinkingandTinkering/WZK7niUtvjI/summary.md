## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a simple homemade cuprous oxide solar cell using copper mesh rather than the traditional copper plate method. He argues that using fine woven copper mesh and oven-oxidizing it produces a more uniform, controlled cuprous oxide (Cu₂O) coating — including nanocrystal "hairs" at higher temperatures — resulting in a better-performing cell than the conventional hotplate approach. While the output remains modest (around 0.2V), he frames this as an introductory step toward developing genuinely useful homemade solar cells.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of the traditional copper plate/hotplate cuprous oxide solar cell method
- Why copper mesh is superior to copper plate as a substrate
- The two forms of copper oxide and their semiconductor properties
- Controlled oven oxidation as a better fabrication process
- Nanocrystal growth at higher temperatures and its benefits
- Assembly of the cell with electrolyte and current collector
- Future videos planned to improve on this design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper mesh** — fine woven mesh (used in battery electrodes); the primary substrate
- **Cuprous oxide (Cu₂O / Copper(I) oxide)** — red, photoactive, native p-type semiconductor; the desired product
- **Cupric oxide (CuO / Copper(II) oxide)** — black, non-photoactive; formed first during oxidation and removed
- **Grafoil (graphite foil)** — used as the current collector on the counter electrode side
- **Salt water (sodium chloride solution)** — traditional electrolyte for these cells
- **Separator** — placed between electrodes in the cell assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- **Oven oxidation** — heating copper mesh in air at 230°C for 30 minutes, or preferably 400°C for 10 minutes, to form Cu₂O
- **Nanocrystal growth** — at 400°C, Cu₂O forms as perpendicular nanocrystal "hairs" on the wire surface, increasing surface area
- **Cell assembly** — layering treated copper mesh / separator / salt water electrolyte / grafoil current collector between plastic sheets
- **Outdoor light testing** — placing the assembled cell in direct sunlight to measure voltage output
