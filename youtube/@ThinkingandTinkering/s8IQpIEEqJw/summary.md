## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the history of Tesla's mechanical oscillator — nicknamed the "earthquake machine" — and reveals that his ongoing air engine / linear alternator project is functionally identical to it. He walks through the construction of a new rotary valve chest for the device and demonstrates the reciprocating piston action, with plans to add magnets and a coil to generate electricity.

## Key Topics
- Tesla's original mechanical oscillator and how it accidentally shook a quarter of New York
- The conceptual link between a linear alternator and the Tesla earthquake machine
- Design and fabrication of a rotary valve chest to control piston direction
- How the valve is mechanically actuated by lugs on the piston rod
- Strategies for energy extraction: magnets on the piston or push rod moving through a coil
- The role of inertia (via added mass) in carrying the piston through top dead centre
- Plans to build a "water battery" air supply (inspired by "Prepper Tech EE") instead of bellows or a vacuum cleaner

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Acrylic block (salvaged from disassembled LCD screens) — used for all three valve chest plates
- 12 mm aluminium tubes — used as port entry pipes in the valve chest
- 12.5 mm polypropylene pipe — used to connect valve ports to the piston chamber
- Skate bearing (8 mm thick, pressed into a 22 mm hole) — central bearing for the rotating valve disc
- Brass rod/guide — used to prevent the actuating lugs from dropping and fouling the valve
- Magnets — planned addition to the piston or push rod to enable electricity generation

## Techniques and Methods
- Bolting three acrylic blocks together in registration before drilling, to ensure aligned holes across all plates
- Hole-saw cutting to produce the circular valve disc from acrylic
- Marking port positions at 45° intervals (90° separation) for inlet and exhaust
- Rotary valve actuation via piston-rod lugs striking a screw pin on the valve disc
- Manual blow-test to verify piston reciprocation before a powered air supply is available
- Planned spring addition to the piston to sustain oscillation

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Springs on the piston:** Robert confirms he is likely to add springs to the piston to sustain oscillation.
- **Free-piston direction:** He acknowledges the device is heading toward a free-piston design ("that is where we are heading").
- **Mass placement:** Agrees that adding a mass at the end of the piston rod would also work well — "it's horses for courses."
- **Scope of the project:** States he plans to do only a couple more things with this device before moving on, to keep the channel balanced.
- **Historical note:** Points out that independent examples of resonant mass damping existed in Japan since the 15th or 16th century, so the phenomenon was not unique to Tesla.
- **Liquefaction query:** Notes that liquefaction also requires the presence of water and other conditions, not just vibration alone.
- **Private videos:** Clarifies that any private videos are related to occasional consultancy work, not withheld public content.
