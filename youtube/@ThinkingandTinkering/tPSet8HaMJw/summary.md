## Overview
Robert tests an aluminium brazing rod (Durafix EasyWeld) while fabricating a rotor for a Tesla turbine wind generator. He demonstrates the technique on sheet aluminium components, shares practical tips on surface preparation and heat application, and concludes that the product is genuinely useful and easier than TIG welding for non-critical aluminium fabrication work.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to aluminium brazing rods (marketed as "welding" rods but actually brazing)
- Tesla turbine design — specifically the involute/snail-shell casing geometry for wind applications vs. the circular pump configuration
- Construction of a rotor from sheet aluminium roofing material
- Hands-on trial of Durafix EasyWeld brazing rods purchased from eBay
- Surface preparation methods (brush vs. sandpaper)
- Torch selection and heat management technique
- Plans for completing the Tesla wind generator (bearings, brackets, generator attachment)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sheet aluminium** (roofing/window surround gauge) — used to fabricate the rotor discs and involute casing
- **Durafix EasyWeld aluminium brazing rods** — flux-core low-temperature brazing rods; ~40 GBP for 30 rods; described as forming a part-mechanical bond
- **Flat steel bar** — bent into a circle to form the involute casing wall
- **60-grit sandpaper** — used for surface preparation instead of the supplied brush
- **Butane/propane mix torch fuel** — standard torch gas used for heating

## Techniques and Methods
- **Aluminium brazing** using low-temperature flux-core rods (not true fusion welding)
- **Surface preparation by abrasion** — 60-grit sandpaper found superior to the supplied stainless steel brush, especially on rods, tubes, and sharp corners
- **Heat-then-withdraw technique** — heating the base metal, removing the flame, then rubbing the rod onto the hot metal (analogous to soldering practice) for a better bond
- **Mechanical smearing** — working the rod across the joint rather than letting it flow freely like solder
- **Metal bending** — flat bar bent by hand/metal bender into a circular/involute profile for the turbine casing
- **Wood backing for clamping** — using wood instead of metal clamps to reduce heat sink effect and speed up heating time

## Robert's Own Replies
From his comment replies, Robert added several useful clarifications:

- **Bearings placement**: Bearings will be mounted on external bearing blocks rather than inside the casing, because the casing material is too thin and had to match the sidewall gauge.
- **Rod identification**: Confirmed the rod used was a Durafix product; speculated a commenter's similar rod was likely "a Durafix product 0.8mm by 60cm flux core."
- **Comparison to TIG welding**: Stated he finds aluminium brazing "a lot easier than TIG."
- **HHO torch**: Has tried a small HHO torch but found it not up to more than small jobs; would need to build a larger one.
- **Repair use case**: Enthusiastic about using it to repair accidentally scored or damaged pieces rather than scrapping them.
- **Oxide paper vs. brush**: Agrees oxide/sandpaper works well in awkward spots (rods, tubes, sharp corners), consistent with his on-camera tip.
- **Compared to other aluminium joining methods**: Described alternative methods as "a real mess" to get right, finding this noticeably easier — "but horses for courses."
- **General enthusiasm**: Plans to keep experimenting; "there is such a lot to like about it."
