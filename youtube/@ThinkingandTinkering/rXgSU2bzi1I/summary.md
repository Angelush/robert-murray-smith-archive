## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed prototype of the "Darwin wind turbine" — an omnidirectional wind capture structure featuring eight angled fins and a cone that funnels wind onto a small generator. Testing with a fan at 2.7 m/s shows the Darwin design produces a few milliwatts while a bare propeller produces nothing, validating the core concept. He concludes the design is promising for urban environments but acknowledges many parameters (baffle size, spacing, pipe dimensions) still need optimization.

## Key Topics
- Assembly of a 3D-printed Darwin wind turbine prototype using fins and a cone
- Explanation of the octagonal, omnidirectional design philosophy for urban wind capture
- Live testing at 2.7 m/s using a fan, comparing the Darwin structure vs. a bare PC-fan generator
- Venturi effect: the cone accelerates wind speed to overcome generator cogging torque
- Discussion of flat-panel vs. hyperbolic funnel designs and ease of fabrication
- Outstanding design unknowns: baffle size, fin spacing, pipe geometry, hexagon vs. octagon shape

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed PLA/filament parts (fins/vanes, cone, capture body)
- Crazy glue (cyanoacrylate) for assembly
- PC fan repurposed as a generator
- LED (used as a load indicator during testing)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of modular components (fins, cone, capture housing)
- Cyanoacrylate bonding of printed parts
- Wind speed measurement using an anemometer
- Comparative bench testing: Darwin structure vs. bare propeller generator
- Venturi-effect wind acceleration via tapered cone
- Theoretical power estimation from swept area and wind speed
