## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a quick and low-tech method for making functional gears without a laser cutter, 3D printer, or machine shop. By wrapping and gluing power transmission timing belt around an acrylic disc, a working gear can be produced in minutes. The technique is practical enough for use in actual machines and works across a range of gear sizes.

## Key Topics
- Making gears without specialist tools or workshop equipment
- Using power transmission (timing) belts as ready-made gear teeth
- Cutting acrylic discs as gear blanks using a hole saw
- Superglue bonding of belt to disc
- Gear ratio demonstration (small gear driving large gear and vice versa)
- Availability of timing belts via Amazon/eBay in multiple sizes and tooth profiles

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **10 mm acrylic sheet** — used as the gear blank, cut into discs with a hole saw
- **Power transmission / timing belt** — available in various sizes and tooth pitches; used as the gear teeth; noted to be resistant to chemicals, heat, and abrasion
- **Superglue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to bond the belt to the disc; described as formulated to adhere to belt material with bond strength equal to the material itself

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting circular gear blanks from acrylic sheet using a large hole saw
- Wrapping timing belt around the disc circumference and trimming to length
- Gluing the belt flush to the disc rim with superglue
- Sizing the disc diameter to match the belt: rough-cut the belt, then shave the disc down incrementally until the belt fits snugly

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Fitting the belt to the disc:** Start with the disc slightly oversized, cut the belt to approximate length, then carefully shave small amounts off the disc until the belt fits. Neatness at the join depends on workmanship, so take care.
- **Tolerances and failure rate:** Use a larger tooth pitch if unsure, and don't aim for perfection — he has made many of these with only one failure (caused by removing too much material from the disc). They will never be high-precision gears, but are accurate enough for most practical uses.
