## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith responds to local government bans on disposable barbecues by building a low-cost solar-powered barbecue using a solar thermal evacuated tube. The build uses 3D-printed parts, a mirror-effect spray paint reflector, and threaded rods, and successfully cooks sausages in about 20 minutes. He argues this DIY approach — costing around £10–15 — is a practical and affordable alternative to expensive commercial solar cookers.

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Solar thermal evacuated tube** — the primary heat-collecting component; 500mm long, 58mm outer diameter; available from ~£5–10 each
- **Rust-Oleum Mirror Effect spray paint** — used to create a reflective curved mirror surface inside the 3D-printed reflector housing
- **8mm threaded bar/rods** — used for the hinge mechanism and to hold the assembly together (43cm lengths)
- **Sheet stainless steel** — bent into a U-shape to form the food shelf/tray
- **PLA or similar filament** (implied) — used for the 3D-printed structural parts

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — four structural parts designed in Tinkercad (files linked in video description) including end caps, feet, hinge section, and reflector housing
- **Mirror spray painting** — Rust-Oleum Mirror Effect applied to the interior curved surface of the reflector to concentrate solar energy
- **Solar thermal focusing** — orienting the parabolic reflector to concentrate sunlight onto the evacuated tube (same principle as his earlier solar kettle build)
- **Metal bending** — stainless steel strip bent into a U-channel to create a food shelf with a plastic handle
- **Angle alignment** — reflector set at 45 degrees for optimal solar capture
