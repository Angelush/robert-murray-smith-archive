## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a touch-sensitive lamp entirely from upcycled materials — an old wine bottle and a wooden venetian blind — combined with a small commercial kit containing conductive ink, a touch switch, and copper tape. The finished lamp (nicknamed the "giraffe lamp") offers four touch-activated brightness levels and requires no specialist tools beyond basic woodworking equipment.

## Key Topics
- Distinction between "found objects" and upcycling (objects with a previous functional life)
- Building a lamp stand by cold laminating venetian blind slats over a curved former
- Cutting wooden rings to hold the wine bottle securely
- Assembling a lamp holder from a disassembled old lamp
- Wiring a touch switch module and connecting it via conductive ink
- Painting conductive ink onto the wooden stand to create a touch-sensitive surface

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Wine bottle** — cut in half; the top half becomes the lampshade, the body forms the lamp base
- **Wooden venetian blind slats** — cold laminated to form the curved stand arms
- **Cascamite (Casca-lite)** — powdered wood resin boat-building glue used for cold lamination; workable for ~1 hour after mixing
- **Conductive ink** — painted onto the wood to create the touch-sensitive surface
- **Copper tape / copper strip** — used to connect the touch wire to the conductive ink
- **LED tape** — placed around the rough cut edge of the bottle to cover it
- **Hot glue** — used to fix the bottle and lamp holder in place
- **Electrician's tape** — wrapped around the metal lamp pipe to create a tight fit
- **Dowels (6 mm)** — used to join the laminated arm sections into a 3D structure
- **Varnish** — applied as a finish to the woodwork
- **Crimps** — used for permanent electrical connections (preferred over twist caps in the UK)
- **Lead tape (from a stained glass supplier)** — clarified in comments as the metallic tape visible on the bottle edge

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cold laminating** — gluing thin wood slats together over a curved former and leaving them to set, producing curved structural pieces (analogous to making one's own plywood)
- **Hole cutting** — using hole-saw cutters to make two concentric rings from scrap timber to cradle the bottle
- **Forming without a jig** — using found objects as impromptu formers rather than a purpose-built forming jig
- **Lamp holder disassembly and re-use** — salvaging the fitting from an old lamp to slide into the bottle neck
- **Touch switch wiring** — connecting the module's four wires (neutral, lamp live, supply live, touch wire) using crimped connections; wiring varies by country
- **Conductive ink painting** — applied to any wooden surface that should respond to touch
