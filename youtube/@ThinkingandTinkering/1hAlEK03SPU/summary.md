## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a rebuilt analog brushless motor controller — originally designed in videos 1017 and 1019 — driving a prototype switched reluctance motor at 12 volts. The video was prompted by a viewer comment and shows how rotor geometry (specifically a "leading edge") determines the direction of rotation. The motor is explicitly a proof-of-concept prototype, held together with superglue, intended to validate design ideas before committing to a more robust build.

## Key Topics
- Rebuilding the analog brushless/switched reluctance motor controller from earlier videos (1017, 1019)
- Connecting the controller to a three-phase prototype reluctance motor running on 12V DC
- How a **leading edge** on the rotor geometry controls the direction of rotation
- The philosophy of cheap, fragile prototyping to test concepts before investing in a proper build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Superglue (used to hold the prototype motor together)

## Techniques and Methods
- Analog motor control (three-phase DC-fed controller with a rotary adjustment and switch)
- Rotor geometry shaping to enforce a defined rotational direction via a leading edge
- Low-cost prototype-first approach: build fragile and fast to verify concepts, then rebuild stronger if proven

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Prototype fragility:** Robert repeatedly emphasises the motor is "basically held together with superglue" and is only intended for concept-checking. Running it faster risks it flying apart. A stronger rebuild would be needed for higher-speed operation.
- **Cost-conscious prototyping:** He explains his rationale — rebuilding a stronger version first would be costly in time and money, and if the design were wrong it would be wasted effort.
