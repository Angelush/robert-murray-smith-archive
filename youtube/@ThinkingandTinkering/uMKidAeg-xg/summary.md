## Overview
Robert discusses MIT's announcement of the world's thinnest and lightest fuel cell, designed to power implantable medical devices. The device is 400 nanometers thick, uses glucose as fuel, and relies on cerium oxide (ceria) as its core proton-conducting material. Robert argues the real significance isn't the thinness itself, but that ceria conducts protons at body temperature — a potential breakthrough that could enable room-temperature solid oxide fuel cells at larger scales.

## Key Topics
- Fundamentals of fuel cells vs. batteries (anode, cathode, electrolyte; internal vs. external reactants)
- History of fuel cells: Sir William Grove (1838), Francis Thomas Bacon's alkaline fuel cell, NASA applications
- MIT's glucose-powered implantable fuel cell — specs and construction
- Cerium oxide (ceria) as a room-temperature proton conductor
- Applications in implantable medical devices (pacemakers, neural dust)
- Connection to Robert's own research into clay-based proton exchange membranes
- Potential to bring solid oxide fuel cell operating temperature down from 500–1000°C to ambient/body temperature

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Cerium oxide (ceria / jeweler's rouge)** — the core solid electrolyte/proton conductor in the fuel cell; biocompatible, sterilizable up to 600°C
- **Glucose** — the fuel source; reacted at the anode to release protons and electrons
- **Zirconium** — mentioned as a biocompatibility reference (used in tooth implants)
- **Clay** — referenced in Robert's own work on proton exchange membranes; proposed to mix with ceria for room-temperature ceramic fuel cells

## Techniques and Methods
- Solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) construction at nanoscale (~400 nm thick film)
- Glucose oxidation at anode to split into protons and electrons
- Proton conduction across a ceramic membrane at body temperature
- Sterilization via high-temperature tolerance of ceria (up to 600°C)
- Proposed: mixing ceria with clay to fabricate ceramic plates operable at room temperature
