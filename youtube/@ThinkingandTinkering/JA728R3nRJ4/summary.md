## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith buys two cheap children's bikes from a local store with the intention of welding them together to create a rideable quad bike. The video covers the disassembly of the frames, the welding process to join them, fabricating a simple push-rod steering system, and mounting a seat. The build is incomplete by the end — the rear drive and motor still need fitting — with a follow-up video promised.

## Key Topics
- Concept: combining two kids' bikes into a quad bike for an adult rider
- Frame stripping and disassembly
- Welding the two frames together with cross-bracing
- Fabricating a push-rod steering linkage for the front wheels
- Repurposing scavenged parts (dining chair, treadmill bar, old handlebars)
- Plans for a rear drive axle and electric/motorised propulsion

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel tube/pipe (used as cross-brace, sourced to fit original axle holes)
- Steel angle iron (welded to front forks for steering mounts)
- Steel bar from a treadmill (repurposed as a front bumper/light mount)
- Steel-legged dining chair (repurposed as the seat)
- Handlebars from an old bike

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of boxed bikes (removing chain, drivetrain, wheels, cables)
- Use of a crankshaft puller tool to remove bottom bracket/cranks
- MIG/arc welding frames together at multiple points ~60 cm apart
- Fabricating a push-rod steering system using angle iron brackets on the front forks
- Improvised fabrication from salvaged/scavenged parts
