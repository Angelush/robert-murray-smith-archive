## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces compliant mechanisms — flexible mechanical structures that transmit force through elastic deformation rather than rigid parts — using a 3D-printed ratchet demo he found on Thingiverse. He explores their nature-inspired design aesthetic, drawing parallels to tree branches, and argues that biomimicry combined with 3D printing opens up exciting possibilities for low-cost, efficient mechanisms. His main takeaway is that compliant mechanisms represent a shift away from the brutality of Industrial Revolution-era engineering toward more elegant, naturally-inspired design.

## Key Topics
- Definition and history of compliant mechanisms (flexible mechanisms achieving motion through elastic deformation)
- The bow and arrow as one of humanity's oldest compliant mechanisms (~72,000 years old, Blombos/Sibudu Cave)
- The philosophical arc from Enlightenment "clockwork universe" thinking through the Industrial Revolution to nature-inspired design
- A 3D-printed ratchet/spring mechanism sourced from Thingiverse and modelled in Tinkercad
- Advantages of compliant mechanisms: low part count, reduced cost, low friction, energy storage
- Disadvantages: limited rotation range, stress concentration at flex points, reliance on 3D printing or injection moulding
- Biomimicry as a design philosophy — drawing on natural structures like tree branches
- Art Nouveau aesthetic parallels with compliant mechanism geometry
