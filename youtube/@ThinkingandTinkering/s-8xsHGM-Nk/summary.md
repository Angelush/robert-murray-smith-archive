## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a brief history of Nikola Tesla and the Tesla coil before introducing the Slayer Exciter — a simpler, hobbyist-friendly circuit that achieves similar high-voltage, high-frequency output. The video walks through building a Slayer Exciter from scratch, demonstrating wireless power transfer by lighting fluorescent and CFL bulbs without direct electrical connection.

## Key Topics
- Nikola Tesla's life, inventions, and legacy (induction motor, polyphase AC, wireless power)
- The Wardenclyffe Tower project and its goal of wireless power distribution
- History and operating principles of the Tesla coil (electromagnetic induction and resonance)
- Introduction to the Slayer Exciter: its inventor, design philosophy, and components
- Step-by-step construction of a Slayer Exciter, including coil winding and circuit assembly
- Live demonstration of wireless power transfer using the completed circuit

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVC pipe** (~1.5 inch diameter) — used as the coil former for the secondary winding
- **22 gauge (6 mil) enamelled wire** — used for the secondary coil (~300–400 turns wound)
- **12 gauge wire** — used for the primary coil (~5 turns)
- **2N2222A NPN transistor** — acts as the switching element in the circuit
- **LED** — used as a diode for feedback and as a circuit status indicator
- **10K resistor** — biases the transistor base
- **9V battery** — power source used in the demonstration
- **Perfboard** — used to mount and organise the circuit components
- **Duct tape** — used to secure wire ends during coil winding
- **CFL (compact fluorescent lamp)** — lit wirelessly in the demonstration

## Techniques and Methods
- **Coil winding using a lathe** — secondary coil wound with controlled tension to achieve tight, gap-free turns; ends secured with tape
- **Manual coil winding** — alternative for those without a lathe
- **Enamel stripping** — scraping enamelled wire ends with a razor blade to make electrical contact
- **Perfboard circuit assembly** — components mounted on perfboard with a transistor socket to allow easy replacement
- **Polarity checking** — swapping primary coil wire connections until the LED lights to confirm correct winding orientation
- **Wireless power demonstration** — lighting fluorescent and CFL bulbs held near the energised coil
