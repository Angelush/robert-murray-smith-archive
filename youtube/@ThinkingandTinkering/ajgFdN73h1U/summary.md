## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith assembles and field-tests a 3D-printed foldable HAWT (horizontal-axis wind turbine) with collapsible blades and a planetary gearbox driving a serpentine-coil generator. The turbine performed impressively — producing roughly 9–10 watts at 4.5 m/s wind speed for under $40 in materials — but the top planet carrier sheared due to low infill and unfavourable print grain direction, prompting a planned redesign using stainless steel pins and bearings.

## Key Topics
- Final assembly of the collapsible wind turbine, including fitting the planetary gearbox into the generator housing
- Press-fit nose cap and blade folding/retention mechanism using clips
- Field test results: wind speed threshold, voltage, current, and power output
- Gear failure analysis: planet carrier shear due to 20% infill and print grain orientation
- Proposed improvements: stainless steel shelf pins to replace plastic pins, added bearings
- Cost comparison: DIY build (~$40) vs. commercial equivalent (~$400)
- Discussion of applying the same generator/gearbox architecture to a VAWT arrangement
- TinkerCAD files offered freely via the video description

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **3D-printed PLA** — used for gearbox housing, planet carriers, nose cap, and blade hub; printed at 20% infill (identified as insufficient for the top gear stage)
- **Stainless steel shelf pins** (25 mm × 6 mm) — proposed replacement for plastic planet-carrier pins; ~£5 per dozen
- **Soil pipe** — used for turbine blades (cheap but aerodynamically compromised)
- **Cyanoacrylate (super glue)** — mentioned in comments as the adhesive used during assembly
- **Carbon fibre filament** — noted in comments as an alternative to standard PLA for higher-strength prints
- **Silicone oil** — mentioned in comments as a preferred lubricant for the gears

## Techniques and Methods
- **Planetary gear assembly** — stacking sun gear, planet gears, and ring carrier into a three-stage gearbox
- **Press-fit construction** — cap and gear stack inserted into the gearbox housing without fasteners
- **Clip retention** — temporary spring clips used to hold folded blades in the deployed position
- **3D printing via TinkerCAD** — all custom parts designed and made available as free STL files
- **Wind turbine field testing** — measuring cut-in wind speed, output voltage (~100 V), and current (~90–100 mA) with a handheld anemometer

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Adhesive used:** confirmed cyanoacrylate glue was used during assembly.
- **Blade aerodynamics:** clarified that the soil-pipe blade works in both directions — it can act as either a drag device or an airfoil due to its scoop profile; soil pipe is aerodynamically suboptimal but hard to beat on price.
- **Material upgrade:** confirmed carbon fibre filament exists as a stronger alternative; PVC foam board was also linked as a blade material option.
- **Lubrication:** suggested silicone oil as his preference for the gears.
- **Broader project context:** noted that this turbine is part of working out the gearing system for "the Darwin" — a larger related project — so the video serves multiple design objectives beyond a standalone wind turbine.
- **Design intent:** wanted a snap-fit, portable design accessible to anyone ("even someone with thumbs for fingers").
- **Magnetic gearboxes:** acknowledged the idea is in his mind and he has done magnetic gearboxes before.
- **Transparency about failure:** deliberately showed the gear failure because he wanted to demonstrate that things don't always work first time.
- **Next steps:** confirmed he planned to have the update ready within a day; next iteration likely in a VAWT arrangement.
- **Self-description:** "I am a teacher at heart."
