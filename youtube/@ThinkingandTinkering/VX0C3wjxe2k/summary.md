## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains his end-to-end workflow for making 3D printing projects, covering how he gets ideas, which CAD tools he uses and why, his printer and filament choices, and his broader philosophy toward making. The central takeaway is that there is no single "best" tool or process — what matters is developing an approach that suits your own workflow, budget, and goals, and not being afraid to fail and iterate.

## Key Topics
- Sources of project ideas (viewer questions, museum visits, desire to learn a new skill)
- CAD software selection: Tinkercad, FreeCAD, and Fusion 360 used interchangeably based on task
- Why 80–90% of models are simple primitives, making Tinkercad sufficient most of the time
- Using FreeCAD for helical screws and gears; Fusion 360 for team/sharing contexts
- Workflow of combining parts from multiple CAD programs into one final model
- 3D printers he uses (received for review): Anycubic Kobra S1, Elegoo Centauri Carbon, Flashforge AD5X
- Printer selection criteria: reliability, cost, ability to leave unattended
- Filament usage: predominantly PLA for prototyping, ~4–5 rolls per week
- Iterative prototyping philosophy: print, find mistakes, correct, reprint
- Anti-procrastination philosophy: jump in, accept failure as part of learning, stay job-focused

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA** — primary filament used for all prototyping due to low cost and ease of printing
- **PETG** — used occasionally for more demanding prints
- **Carbon fiber-filled filament** — used for final versions of proven designs (~£30–50/roll)
- **Wood-filled and metal-filled filament** — mentioned as exotic options tried
- **Transparent (clear) filament** — mentioned among materials used
- **TPU** — flexible filament, mentioned as part of his wider experience
- **ABS** — mentioned but rarely used

## Techniques and Methods
- **Iterative CAD-to-print prototyping** — draw, print in PLA, identify errors, correct, reprint until fit is confirmed before using exotic materials
- **Multi-program CAD workflow** — complex shapes (e.g. helical spirals) modelled in FreeCAD or Fusion 360, exported as STL, then imported as primitives into Tinkercad for final assembly
- **Unattended printing** — printers selected for AI spaghetti-failure detection and camera monitoring so prints can run unsupervised
- **Job-focused skill acquisition** — learning only the specific CAD/tool skills needed for the current project rather than comprehensive study
