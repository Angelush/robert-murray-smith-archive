## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces gravity batteries — historical battery designs from the late 1800s/early 1900s that use gravity to keep reactive layers separated rather than mechanical separators. He demonstrates two types: a zinc-copper primary cell and a zinc-bromide rechargeable cell, arguing these old designs deserve revival and improvement with modern materials for off-grid applications.

## Key Topics
- What gravity batteries actually are (stratified liquid electrolyte cells, not perpetual motion devices)
- Historical context: late 19th-century technology from *The Model Engineer and Amateur Electrician* (c. 1898–1901)
- The philosophy of updating historical designs with modern materials
- Construction and operation of a zinc-copper sulfate primary gravity cell
- Maintenance of the zinc-copper cell (siphoning zinc sulfate, replenishing copper sulfate)
- Construction and operation of a zinc-bromide rechargeable gravity cell
- Charging characteristics of bromine-based batteries (current density per electrode surface area)
- Safety features of the gravity design (bromine sinks to bottom, oil layer seals the top)
- Using sodium bromide as an assistant salt to maintain ionic conductivity

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper foil** — negative electrode substrate in the zinc-copper cell; grows thicker as copper deposits onto it
- **Zinc sheet/plate** — anode in both cells; folded into a block and positioned near the top of the solution
- **Copper sulfate (CuSO₄)** — cathodic electrolyte in the primary cell; placed at the bottom
- **Zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄)** — anodic electrolyte in the primary cell; lighter, sits on top; also byproduct of cell discharge
- **Zinc bromide (ZnBr₂)** — electrolyte for the rechargeable cell; noted for extremely high solubility (up to 5–7 mol)
- **Sodium bromide (NaBr)** — assistant salt recommended at 1 mol alongside 5 mol ZnBr₂ to maintain conductivity
- **Bromine (Br₂)** — forms at the carbon electrode during charging; denser than ZnBr₂ solution, sinks to bottom
- **Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)** — mentioned as an alternative electrolyte additive (not used here due to safety)
- **Machine/motor oil** — poured on top as a seal to prevent evaporation, salt creep, and bromine off-gassing
- **Butyl rubber** — used as an adhesive to mount the zinc plate to the jar wall
- **Carbon electrode (HDPE-backed, carbon-painted)** — positive electrode in the zinc-bromide cell

## Techniques and Methods
- **Gravity stratification** — exploiting density differences between copper sulfate and zinc sulfate solutions (or bromine and zinc bromide) to keep cell layers separated without a physical membrane
- **L/S-shaped copper foil construction** — shaping the copper electrode to fit the jar bottom and allow easy access for maintenance
- **Mechanical recharging** — periodically siphoning off spent zinc sulfate solution and replenishing with fresh water and copper sulfate crystals
- **Oil sealing** — floating a layer of machine oil on top of the electrolyte to prevent evaporation, salt creep, and gas escape
- **Electrochemical charging of the ZnBr₂ cell** — applying ~7 V (demonstration voltage) at 20–50 mA/cm² based on carbon electrode surface area
- **Making zinc bromide from precursors** — referenced as a separate video: reacting sodium bromide with zinc sulfate
