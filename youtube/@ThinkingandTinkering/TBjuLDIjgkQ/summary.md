## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a low-cost flywheel using a salvaged bicycle wheel filled with post-mix concrete. The video walks through the full process from preparing the wheel as a mold to curing, balancing, and sealing the finished flywheel. The key takeaway is that a functional, heavy flywheel (~25 kg) can be made cheaply and easily from widely available materials, with the bicycle wheel's spokes acting as built-in reinforcement.

## Key Topics
- Motivation for building a DIY flywheel when a commercial one is unavailable
- Using a bicycle wheel as a concrete mold
- Properties of post-mix/post-crete concrete and curing times
- The difference between hardened and fully cured concrete
- Balancing a flywheel by removing or adding mass
- Sealing concrete to prevent dusting
- The role of spoke reinforcement in concrete flywheel strength

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bicycle wheel** — stripped of tyre, gear cluster, and axle; used as the mold/frame
- **Post-mix / Post-crete** — fast-setting concrete mix; sets in ~10–15 minutes, cost cited as ~£4.50/bag
- **Gaffer tape** — used to seal the plastic cover over the dished side of the wheel
- **Plastic sheet / cardboard** — used as a base to cap one side of the wheel mold
- **PVA glue (50% PVA / 50% water)** — suggested as a concrete sealant
- **Concrete sealant (commercial)** — alternative sealing option from DIY stores
- **SBR (Silicone Bonded Rubber / Styrene Butadiene Rubber)** — mentioned as another concrete sealant option
- **Car body filler** — suggested as a finish coat for a smooth, paintable surface

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mold preparation** — capping the dished side of the bicycle wheel with plastic sheet and sealing with gaffer tape to contain the concrete
- **Casting** — dry-pouring post-crete into the wheel and sprinkling water on top to set it in place
- **Curing** — leaving the cast overnight for handling strength; notes that keeping concrete damp for up to 14 days maximises crystal growth and final strength
- **Balancing** — mounting the wheel on its axle, identifying the heavy low point, then removing concrete with a wire brush or hammer and chisel until the wheel holds any position freely
- **Sealing** — painting the cured surface with PVA solution, SBR, commercial concrete sealant, or body filler to prevent dust and surface erosion

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Concrete flywheels are a serious technology.** Robert pushed back on sceptics, noting that concrete flywheels are actively being researched for green energy storage at thousands of RPM. He suggested viewers search "concrete flywheel" to find high-tech examples.
- **The bicycle wheel provides inherent containment.** He highlighted that the steel rim acts as a band around the concrete, which meaningfully raises the safe RPM compared to bare concrete.
- **Mass is the point.** To a commenter questioning why concrete was used, he clarified: "a flywheel needs mass — all you are doing with the concrete is adding mass — a lot of it."
- **Accessibility over perfection.** He acknowledged that cast iron wheels or other alternatives might perform better, but his design philosophy is to offer solutions others can replicate easily and cheaply — an imperfect flywheel that can actually be built beats a perfect one that can't.
- **Post-crete cost.** He noted he paid £4.50 a bag, framing it as significantly cheaper than alternatives like resin casting.
- **Centering advantage of the bike wheel.** When someone suggested using circular concrete forms, he noted that the bicycle wheel has a ready-made, accurate centre — a practical advantage over improvised molds.
