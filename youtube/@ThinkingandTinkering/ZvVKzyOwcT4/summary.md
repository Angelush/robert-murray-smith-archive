## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith shares a quick update recommending a commercially available uPVC cleaner (Cesto Type 10) as an effective solvent for liquid phase exfoliation of graphene. While acetone-water mixtures are safer for home use, Type 10 — predominantly ethyl acetate with co-solvents — produces stable graphene colloidal dispersions after extended low-power sonication. The video is a practical tip for DIY graphene makers, with a note on volatility hazards.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of acetone-water as a graphene exfoliation solvent
- uPVC cleaner (Cesto Type 10) as a superior, accessible alternative solvent
- Co-solvent systems and Hansen solubility parameters (referenced briefly)
- Safety considerations for volatile solvents in home/garage settings
- Availability and legal status of ethyl acetate-based solvents in some countries

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acetone-water mixture** — previous solvent used for safety reasons in home settings
- **Cesto Type 10 uPVC cleaner** — commercially available co-solvent mixture, predominantly ethyl acetate; recommended for liquid phase exfoliation
- **Ethyl acetate** — primary component of Type 10; noted for its pear-like smell and high volatility
- **Graphene** — the target material being produced via liquid phase exfoliation; forms a colloidal dispersion (not a true solution)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Liquid phase exfoliation (LPE)** — dispersing graphene in a solvent using mechanical energy
- **Low-power sonication bath** — primary method used; requires extended sonication (~8 hours) to produce a stable dispersion
- **Homogenizer** — mentioned as an alternative previously used with acetone-water
- **Co-solvent formulation** — using a blend of solvents to match Hansen solubility parameters of graphene

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Purchasing**: He buys Type 10 from a local DIY store — no specialist supplier needed.
- **Single-layer graphene**: Confirmed it is achievable; points to research from Trinity College Dublin as the basis.
- **Co-solvent science**: Acknowledges there is solid research behind co-solvency for graphene; recommends Google Scholar for deeper reading.
- **Colloidal dispersion vs. dissolving**: Corrects a commenter — graphene doesn't dissolve; it forms a colloidal dispersion.
- **Why Type 10 over pure reagents**: Pure acetone is actually poor for graphene dispersions and only works as part of a co-solvent system. Clinical/lab-grade solvents often require a license; Type 10 does not. The key science behind solvent selection is **Hansen solubility parameters** — he directs viewers to his other videos for more detail.
- **No written recipe**: He declines to write out a detailed recipe due to the time involved, directing people to rewatch the video instead.
