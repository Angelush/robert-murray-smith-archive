## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses a professional BK Precision 8500 programmable DC load — equipment from his battery research work — to take proper measurements of his "Darwin" wind turbine design. The turbine produces approximately 11 milliwatts under the test conditions, roughly twice the ~4 milliwatt output expected for that wind speed and turbine size, which Robert attributes to the Darwin design collecting twice as much air as a conventional turbine.

## Key Topics
- Introduction of the BK Precision 8500 programmable DC load and its use in battery/solar testing
- Adapting lab-grade measurement equipment for wind turbine characterisation
- Live measurement of the Darwin turbine prototype (a PC-fan-based test model)
- Comparison of measured output (~11 mW) versus expected output (~4 mW) for equivalent wind speed and rotor size
- The Darwin turbine's efficiency advantage attributed to its doubled air-capture geometry

- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Fixed-resistance DC load testing using a BK Precision 8500 programmable DC load
- Taking peak/top-figure readings from a fluctuating wind-turbine output as a best-case estimate
- Data logging capability of the BK 8500 noted (power-curve analysis over time) — mentioned in comments rather than the main video
- Using an LED with voltage readings as a simpler alternative for most YouTube demonstrations

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Data logging clarification:** Robert notes the BK Precision is also a data logger — the proper workflow is to log data over time and analyse the full power curve after the experiment, not just read instantaneous values.
- **Test model context:** He confirms this is just a prototype built from a PC fan, and says it performed well for what it is.
- **Design defence — sealed sides:** In response to suggestions about blocking the non-facing side, Robert explains the device is constructed from wedges so the side not facing the wind is already effectively behind a wall.
- **Bottom cone:** He acknowledges that some suggested design ideas (relating to airflow redirection) are already incorporated via the bottom cone of the Darwin design.
- **Standardised testing:** He finds the idea of a standardised Darwin-turbine test rig interesting but notes the practical hurdles — it would need to be a permanent rig, and getting agreement on a standard would be difficult.
- **Name change:** He mentions he changed the turbine's name (possibly to "Everyman's Aeromine") because the previous titles felt dull.
- **General stance on improvements:** Consistently encourages commenters to actually build their suggested improvements and share real-world results rather than just theorising.
