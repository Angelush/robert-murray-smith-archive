## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build low-cost, functional machine-head tuning pegs for a homemade banjo ukulele using salvaged and inexpensive hardware. The design is inspired by headless guitar tuning systems and uses wing nuts sliding in aluminium heat-sink channels to create a screw-tensioned string holder. The finished set of four tuners costs around £5 and took about two hours to make including filming.

## Key Topics
- The problem with common DIY tuning peg solutions (wooden pegs, bolt/nut pegs) not holding tune
- Inspiration from headless guitar tuning head designs
- Step-by-step fabrication of a sliding wing-nut tuning mechanism
- Sizing and fitting four tuners into a single aluminium channel assembly
- Cost breakdown and sourcing of parts

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **4mm wing nuts** — modified to act as the string-catching tuning element
- **M4 × 70mm pan head bolts** — the threaded rod that drives the wing nut along the channel
- **Aluminium heat sink / cooling fin** (salvaged from an old television) — cut into guide channels and a back plate
- **Super glue** — used to fix the back plate to the channel assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- Filing the wing nut flanges flat with a flat file (in a vice) to fit the channel slot
- Using a needle file to round the string-contact notch and prevent string cutting
- Sawing the heat sink to length, retaining small "wings" for mounting holes
- Using a drill as a lathe to reduce the bolt head diameter with a file (removed ~30 thousandths of an inch)
- Drilling 4mm holes in the back plate to align with the channel centres
- Assembly with super glue for the back plate; string tension provides the primary mechanical retention

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed the aluminium component is a **heat sink** (clarifying a viewer question).
- Noted that if he were to **revise the design he would now use lock nuts** for better tuning stability.
- Reported that after several days of use on his ukulele **the head keeps good tune**, requiring only a screwdriver and a tuning app to adjust.
- Expressed interest in seeing other builders' tensioner designs and ukulele builds, inviting people to share their own videos.
- Light-heartedly noted to one commenter that his interests extend beyond graphene.
