## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests a range of alternative fuels in the DIY alcohol can heater he built in video 1702, comparing ethanol, isopropanol (IPA), a water-diluted ethanol/methanol blend ("JZ mix"), and a vegetable glycerin/methanol mixture. He demonstrates each fuel's burn characteristics and concludes that the heater works on a surprisingly wide range of alcohol-based fuels. He also addresses safety concerns raised in comments, emphasising that the heater should be treated like any other open-flame device.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the original methanol can heater from video 1702
- Testing ethanol as a fuel — hotter flame but more expensive than methanol
- Testing isopropanol (IPA / rubbing alcohol) — more aggressive, yellow flame, useful if already on hand from resin 3D printing
- Testing the "JZ mix" (40% ethanol, 40% methanol, 20% water) — yellow flame, good cost/heat balance
- Testing a vegetable glycerin and methanol mixture — relevant for biodiesel makers with glycerin waste
- How to extinguish the burner (snuffing like a candle)
- Safety guidance: ventilation, fire safety, CO/CO₂ monitors, treating it like any open-flame heater
- Addressing comments about formaldehyde emissions from methanol combustion

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — original fuel from video 1702; burns with a blue flame; absorbs atmospheric moisture
- **Ethanol / bioethanol** — hotter than methanol, more expensive; burns with a blue flame
- **Isopropanol (IPA / rubbing alcohol)** — more aggressive, yellow-tinged flame; common byproduct of resin 3D printing
- **"JZ mix"** — 40% ethanol, 40% methanol, 20% water; burns with a yellow flame
- **Vegetable glycerin** — byproduct of biodiesel production; mixed 50/50 with methanol for testing
- **Carbon felt** — 100% carbon, 5 mm thick, used as the wick/wicking material inside the burner
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** and **carbon monoxide (CO)** — primary combustion emissions discussed
- **Formaldehyde** — mentioned as a theoretical concern from methanol combustion; addressed and dismissed under open-flame conditions

## Techniques and Methods
- Two-can burner assembly: one can with lid removed, one with four holes punched in the base and a small nail hole at the top
- Lining the inner can with carbon felt (or alternative wicking materials: denim, cotton, bandages, Kevlar fire-torch wick)
- Priming a heavier or water-containing fuel by squeezing a small amount of ethanol into the lip of the can before igniting
- Extinguishing the burner by removing the top mantle and snuffing with a cover (fire blanket or a fitted tin lid) — same method as snuffing a large candle
- Visual burn assessment conducted in a darkened room to observe flame colour and behaviour
