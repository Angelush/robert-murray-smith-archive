## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith documents his decision to move his workshop from a rented studio unit into his home garage, prompted by realising that 3D printers take up far less space than he assumed. Using the move as a practical opportunity, he walks through how to set up an effective tinkerer's workspace on a budget, arguing that the essential toolkit is simpler and cheaper than most people expect.

## Key Topics
- Motivation for downsizing from a rented unit to a home garage
- How to clear out and organise an existing garage or shed
- The importance of dedicated, committed workspace for creative making
- Essential bench tools for a starter workshop
- 3D printers as a genuinely affordable and space-efficient tool
- A multimedia making philosophy: combining plastic, traditional materials, and hand tools

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D printer filament (~£15 per roll, compared favourably to the cost of timber)

## Techniques and Methods
- Decluttering and space reclamation: ruthlessly removing items unused for 3+ years
- Workshop layout design within a standard UK small car garage (4 m × 7 m)
- Repurposing second-hand kitchen cupboards to build a presentation/filming area
- Fixing tools (pillar drill, bench vise) directly to the workbench for stability

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief, informal exchanges ("lol", "cheers mate") rather than substantive clarifications, but a few recurring themes emerge:

- **On 3D printer "slow speed" criticism:** He argues it is only a problem if you stand and watch — the machine runs unattended while you do other things, which hand tools cannot match without "magic elves."
- **On hoarding junk "just in case":** He acknowledges the temptation but says it is no reason to drown in things you never throw away for that one hypothetical use a decade away.
- **On why he keeps a minimal, findable inventory:** Because his videos must be replicable by viewers, he deliberately avoids holding onto obscure items that others cannot source — a box of junk is less useful to him than to a private maker.
- **On why 3D printers are worth it:** He has addressed this at length in other videos; the short answer is they are useful across a wide range of skill levels, budgets, and time constraints.
- **On a good bench vise:** "A good vice is worth its own weight in gold."
