## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith addresses a frequently asked question about the efficiency of his Tesla turbine experiments, arguing that "efficiency" is meaningless without context. He explains that efficiency must always be considered relative to a frame of reference — whether energy conversion, cost per kilowatt, or human effort — and concludes that the Tesla turbine's real potential lies not in raw thermodynamic efficiency but in its dramatically lower manufacturing cost.

## Key Topics
- Why "efficiency" is a contextual, not absolute, metric
- The Betz limit and its implications for wind turbine design
- Energy efficiency vs. cost efficiency (cost per kilowatt delivered)
- Solar cell efficiency under ideal vs. real-world conditions
- The Tesla turbine: low cost of construction as its primary advantage
- "Free energy" reframed as renewable energy requiring no ongoing human input
- Airflow requirements for the Tesla turbine demonstration and scaling challenges

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Betz limit clarification:** Robert provides a detailed explanation of why Betz's Law applies to all Newtonian fluids — if all kinetic energy were extracted, airflow would stop, blocking fresh air from entering. Geometry constrains maximum efficiency to 59.3%. He pushes back on a commenter who implied MIT students had a better grasp, noting most people asking about efficiency "don't have a clear idea what they are asking about."
- **Prototype plans:** He mentions having a neighbour with a garage who has offered to lend a car for testing a Tesla turbine prototype.
- **Practical mindset:** Repeatedly encourages commenters to "try it and see" — experimentation over speculation.
- **Cascaded turbines:** On the idea of stacking turbines on the same airstream, he notes energy loss is likely but compares it to a triple-expansion engine — worth investigating.
- **Conductive plastics:** An aside to commenter Dan Murphy — Robert mentions needing a water-impermeable plastic with resistivity around 30–40 ohms per square, suggesting ongoing materials research.
- **Renewables framing:** Agrees with a commenter that "renewables are really a strategy," hinting at a broader systems-level view of energy policy.
