## Overview
Robert demonstrates iterative improvements to a simple DIY hovercraft, starting from an open plenum design (an upturned bowl with a hoover motor) and progressively increasing efficiency by adding concentric bowls that create Coanda nozzles. The experiment is based on Edward Kelleher's US Air Force-sponsored thesis on skirtless hovercraft design, combining traditional hovercraft lift with the Coanda effect for significantly better performance.

## Key Topics
- Open vs. closed plenum hovercraft designs and their relative efficiency
- The Coanda effect and its application to hovercraft lift
- Skirtless hovercraft design principles from Edward Kelleher's thesis
- Iterative DIY prototyping using household materials
- Air curtain formation and directional airflow management

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Hoover motor (vacuum cleaner motor) — used as the fan/lift source
- 110mm pipe — used to raise and reposition the motor
- Plastic bowls (three identical, stacked concentrically) — form the plenum chambers and Coanda nozzles
- Plastic ring/disc — deflects airflow inward at ~45 degrees
- Door/window weather seal (foam strip) — used as a gasket/spacer between components

## Techniques and Methods
- Open plenum configuration: single inverted bowl with motor, least efficient baseline
- Closed plenum conversion: inner bowl inserted to direct airflow outward rather than straight down
- 45-degree airflow deflection: adding a plastic ring/disc to angle the exiting air inward
- Triple-bowl Coanda nozzle stack: three concentric bowls forming layered Coanda nozzles that force air jets inward, creating a high-pressure lift region
