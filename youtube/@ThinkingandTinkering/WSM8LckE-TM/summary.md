## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make urea-formaldehyde resin — a hard white plastic used in kitchen countertops, cupboards, and MDF — from just three readily available ingredients. The video responds to criticism of a previous video by showing a simpler, reproducible process. He concludes by hinting at future experiments mixing the resulting plastic with graphene to test strength improvements.

## Key Topics
- Making urea-formaldehyde resin plastic at home/in the lab
- Properties of the resulting plastic (hardness, brittleness, common industrial applications)
- Safety precautions when handling formaldehyde and concentrated sulfuric acid
- Condensation polymerisation as the underlying chemistry
- Planned future experiments with graphene reinforcement

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Urea** — sold as fertilizer (~£3–4 for 5 kg); primary constituent of urine; 10 g used
- **Formaldehyde (formalin)** — has serious health/safety reputation; 20 ml used; noted to work as the monomer
- **Sulfuric acid (96% concentrated)** — used as a catalyst (1 ml, added drop by drop); yellow gloves required
- **Sodium carbonate** — recommended nearby as a spill neutraliser
- **Graphene** — mentioned as a future additive to test strength improvement

## Techniques and Methods
- Dissolving urea in formaldehyde solution with a stirring rod until fully dissolved
- Casting into an aluminium foil mould
- Drop-by-drop addition of concentrated sulfuric acid while stirring to initiate the exothermic condensation reaction
- Demoulding while still soft; allowing to air-dry to final hard state

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Alternative catalysts:** Sulfuric acid is catalytic (not consumed); ammonium chloride can be substituted but requires leaving the mixture overnight. Metal salts like aluminium sulphate are also standard industrial catalysts; heating for four hours is needed in that process, making the acid route better for quick demos.
- **Industrial context:** Urea-formaldehyde resin is identified as one of the most-produced plastics in the world. The commercial equivalent is cascamite glue (water-soluble).
- **Formaldehyde-free / food-safe version:** Possible by washing the product; free formaldehyde content must reach ~0.09 ppm, which would need laboratory testing to verify.
- **Nitrogen-doped carbon foam:** Robert speculates the urea-formaldehyde resin would yield a nitrogen-doped carbon foam if pyrolysed, making it potentially interesting for further materials work.
- **Urea sourcing:** If urea fertiliser is unavailable, he bluntly suggests: "pee in a bucket and wait for it to dry."
- **Reaction speed trade-off:** The acid-catalysed route is too fast for practical moulding applications; using a base slows it down. He encourages experimentation with alternatives like benzaldehyde.
- **The steam is just water** — clarification offered to commenters concerned about the vapour produced during the reaction.
- **Renewables and biodegradables:** Robert notes his personal interest leans toward renewable and biodegradable materials, though he acknowledges synthetic plastics chemistry as a "huge and interesting area."
