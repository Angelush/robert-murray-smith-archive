## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits Crabble Corn Mill in Dover, a working watermill built in 1812, to explore the history and mechanics of gears. Using the mill as a hands-on demonstration, he explains how gears solve the fundamental engineering problem of capturing, transmitting, and transforming power from a slow, heavy water source into the high-speed rotation needed to grind flour. He draws a direct line from medieval milling technology to modern power generation, arguing the same principles apply today.

## Key Topics
- The history of hand-grinding grain and the origin of the phrase "the daily grind"
- Overview of Crabble Corn Mill (Dover): built 1812, run by volunteers, 10 millstones in 5 pairs, capacity of 40 tons of grain per month
- History of gears: records dating to 4th century BC China and the Antikythera mechanism (~100–150 BC)
- The five functions a gear system must perform: capture power, move it, change its direction, apply it, and change its speed
- The three mechanical functions of gears: **transmission** (speed change), **force** (torque multiplication), and **rotational direction change**
- Types of gears mentioned: spur gears, worm gears, bevel gears
- Historical applications of watermills beyond flour: trip hammers, forges, bellows, paper making, lumber sawing
- Parallels between medieval water mill gearing and modern power generation (wind, wave, water, etc.)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Millstones (stone pairs, each weighing approximately 1 ton)
- Grain (wheat/corn — 60 kg shown in the hopper)

## Techniques and Methods
- Corn grinding using paired millstones driven by a waterwheel
- Gear-based speed increase: large gear meshed with small gear to step up rotational speed
- Torque/speed trade-off via gearing: small fast gear drives large slow gear with greater force
- Direction change via spur gears (reversal), worm gears (90° rotation), and bevel gears (cornering)
- Power transmission from primary source (water) through a gear train to an output application
