## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith walks through the contents of his graphene supercapacitor kit (available via IndieGoGo campaign) and demonstrates how to build a simple symmetric graphene supercapacitor from scratch. He emphasises that this is a beginner's experimenter kit—success comes from iterative small-scale practice rather than expecting professional results immediately, and he demonstrates the finished device powering a small helicopter motor for 25 seconds.

## Key Topics
- Contents and purpose of the graphene supercapacitor kit
- Role and selection of carbon materials (graphene, Ketjenblack, activated carbon, graphite)
- Role and selection of binders and their correct proportions
- Electrolyte selection and molarity calculation for zinc sulfate and sodium sulfate
- The Celgard polypropylene separator and its suitability for aqueous/zinc-ion systems
- Graphfoil as a corrosion-resistant current collector
- Step-by-step fabrication of a graphene supercapacitor electrode and cell
- Charging, discharging, and basic performance testing
- The importance of experimentation, small-scale practice, and patience for beginners

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — primary active carbon material for the supercapacitor electrode (15 ml supplied)
- **Ketjenblack 600 (Ketjenblack EC-600)** — hollow spherical carbon nanoparticles (~5–9 nm), used as a high-surface-area carbon or conductive additive
- **Activated carbon (coconut fibre, steam-activated)** — conventional supercapacitor carbon; included for comparison experimentation
- **Graphite (5 µm)** — used as a conductive additive (~10 wt%) to improve electrode conductivity
- **Sodium silicate** — alkaline binder for water-based electrode systems
- **Carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) / Styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR)** — 70/30 blend binder, alternative to sodium silicate
- **Zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄)** — neutral salt electrolyte; enables higher voltage (~1.8 V) due to +2 charge carrier; MW ~287 g/mol
- **Sodium sulfate (Na₂SO₄)** — neutral salt electrolyte, standard reference; enables ~1.6 V
- **Distilled / deionised water** — solvent for electrode paste and electrolytes
- **Celgard 7350 polypropylene separator** — specifically designed for aqueous/zinc-ion transport systems
- **Graphfoil (graphite foil, 100×100 mm)** — corrosion-resistant current collector

## Techniques and Methods
- Weighing electrode components to milligram precision using a jeweller's micro-scale
- Grinding/mixing carbon and binder with a pestle and mortar to form a uniform black paste
- Painting electrode paste onto graphfoil using a fine brush
- Drying the electrode with a hair dryer
- Calendaring: lightly pressing the coated electrode in a vice wrapped in plastic to improve contact and film density
- Cutting the coated graphfoil in half to produce two symmetric electrodes
- Assembling the cell: electrode / Celgard separator / electrode sandwich
- Preparing a 1 M zinc sulfate electrolyte (5.74 g ZnSO₄ in 20 ml deionised water)
- Allowing electrolyte to soak into electrode to displace trapped gas before testing
- Charging from a DC power supply and discharging through a resistive/motor load
- Qualitative performance testing: timing motor discharge (helicopter motor, 25 seconds for a 1.5 cm × 1.5 cm cell)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Supercapacitor charging from batteries:** For the small cells made with the kit, connecting directly to a battery is fine (initial short-circuit effect lasts only a second or two). Larger devices would need a proper power supply since supercapacitors can act as a dead short initially.
- **Kit availability:** Confirmed the kit is listed on IndieGoGo as a general "supercapacitor kit," not specifically labelled a graphene kit.
- **Electrolyte concentration:** Endorsed a commenter's point that 1 M is a guideline only — experimenting both above and below (even down to 0.001 M) can yield surprising results; more is not always better.
- **Mixing slurries:** Noted he normally uses an MTI vacuum mixer rather than a magnetic stirrer for electrode slurries.
- **Doping and pseudocapacitance:** Hinted that with some of the doping work he has been doing, interesting results may be achievable (responding to a suggestion about pseudocapacitive behaviour).
- **Video pace:** Acknowledged the video is dense and fast-paced at 23 minutes; apologised for the speed.
