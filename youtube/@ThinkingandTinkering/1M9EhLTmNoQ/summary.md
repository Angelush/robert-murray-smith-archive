## Overview
Robert provides an update on his rubberised conductive graphene ink, which he first developed in a previous video as a casein-and-sulfur-based rubber replacement. By adding 5% graphene and processing through a three-roll mill, he reduced the ink's resistance from ~2 kilohms to 55 ohms (normalised to 6.6 ohms/mil/square), bringing it in line with — or better than — commercially available conductive paints. The ink is flexible, waterproof, sticks strongly to plastic, and is made entirely from water, milk, carbon, and sulfur with no volatile organic compounds.

## Key Topics
- Follow-up to previous video on casein/sulfur rubber replacement ink
- Problem with original formulation: high resistance (~2 kΩ) making it impractical as a conductive ink
- Addition of homemade graphene (~5% by weight) to reduce resistance
- Processing through a three-roll mill and vacuum degassing
- Square resistance measurement method and film thickness normalisation
- Comparison to commercially available conductive paints (6.6 Ω/mil/sq outperforms them)
- Potential applications: heated clothing, flexible EMI shielding, conductive paint for plastics
- Environmental and safety credentials of the ink's composition
- Waterproofness of the cured film

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Casein** — protein from milk, forms the rubber/biopolymer base
- **Sulfur** — used to vulcanise/crosslink the casein to create rubber-like properties
- **Graphite** — used in the original formulation for conductivity
- **Graphene** — homemade, ~5 g/litre concentration; added at ~5% to dramatically reduce resistance
- **Water** — solvent/carrier; ink described as water-soluble
- **Milk** — source of casein biopolymer
- (No petroleum solvents or volatile organic compounds)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Three-roll mill processing** — used to disperse graphene uniformly into the ink matrix
- **Vacuum degassing** — removes air bubbles from the ink after milling
- **Square conductance measurement** — probes placed at equal spacing to estimate sheet resistance
- **Paint film thickness measurement** — using a paint film thickness meter (designed for automotive use, minimum resolution ~1 micron) to measure ink layer on plastic
- **Resistance normalisation** — converting measured resistance to ohms per mil per square for comparison with commercial products (divide by 25, multiply by measured thickness in microns)
- **Brush application** onto plastic substrate
