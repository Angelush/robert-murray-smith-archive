## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates an improved method for constructing an Archimedes screw using recycled CDs as the helical vanes. The key discovery is that enlarging the central hole of a CD from 15mm to 48mm dramatically increases the disc's flexibility, allowing it to conform snugly to ABS pipe and be heat-welded into a functional helix. He tests the resulting screw as a potential hydro-generator, spinning it with a blower to validate the blade pitch.

## Key Topics
- Modifying CDs by enlarging the central hole to increase flexibility
- Heat-welding CD discs together to form a helical screw shape
- Stretching the assembled helix onto ABS pipe
- Testing the screw as a wind/fluid-driven rotor
- Discussion of using the screw as a hydro-generator (water flowing down it to generate power)
- Community suggestions for improvements (double-lapping joints, alternative adhesives)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **CDs (compact discs)** — used as the raw material for the screw vanes; DVDs noted as harder to work with
- **ABS pipe (40mm diameter)** — the central shaft/pipe the helix is stretched onto
- **Sellotape** — used to hold discs flat during heat welding
- **Polycarbonate sheet** — mentioned by commenters as an alternative; Robert notes it can shrink/warp under excess heat
- **HDPE sheet** — referenced by Robert in comments as a material he uses
- **Methylene chloride / methylene dichloride** — recommended by Robert in comments as a suitable adhesive for polycarbonate (sold as Tensol 12)
- **Sacrificial CD material** — used as filler/solder material during the heat-welding process

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hole enlargement** — drilling out the CD centre from 15mm to 48mm to increase disc flexibility
- **Surface preparation** — scratching off the coating on CDs with a trimming knife
- **Heat welding / plastic soldering** — using a soldering iron with sacrificial plastic material to butt-join CD discs at their edges
- **Helix assembly** — stacking and welding multiple discs, then stretching the resulting spring-like stack along a pipe
- **Adhesive bonding** — gluing the helix in place on the pipe after stretching
- **Blower/wind testing** — using an upward-blowing fan to test rotor pitch and spin behaviour

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Generator intent**: Robert clarifies he plans to pour water down the screw to generate electricity, not pump water upward — using it as a hydro-generator in a pipe.
- **Joint quality is the critical problem**: He identifies the disc-to-disc joint as the weak point, noting it must withstand the weight of water; the pipe-to-CD seal is less critical.
- **Polycarbonate warning**: He cautions that polycarbonate tends to shrink and warp under too much heat, making it tricky for this application.
- **Recommended adhesive**: He points to **methylene chloride / methylene dichloride** (Tensol 12) as a good adhesive for polycarbonate.
- **HDPE material**: Confirms he uses HDPE sheet.
- **Double-helix suggestion**: He welcomes the community suggestion to double-lap the joints and finds the double-helix concept interesting.
- **Passive generation idea**: He considers using a roofline turbine vent in a forced draught configuration as a passive generator application.
- **Reversibility limitation**: Notes that a screw optimised for pumping cannot simply be reversed to act as a generator.
- **Microwave forming**: Mentions the possibility of microwaving the discs as a forming technique, finding it clever.
- **Encouraging tone**: Repeatedly emphasises this is outside his core expertise but he is happy to share the iterative process openly.
