## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith upcycles a 1970s bedside table bought for £5 from a charity shop into a functional Dutch stove — a traditional foot/body warmer. He converts the furniture into an insulated wooden box with air holes, then creates a resistive heating element by painting conductive ink onto a kiln tile, powering it with a 24V DC supply to produce a safe, ~60W foot warmer.

## Key Topics
- The philosophy of "heating the person, not the place" for energy efficiency
- The history and concept of the Dutch stove as a foot/body warmer
- Upcycling charity shop furniture into functional devices
- Making a stone/tile resistive heating element using conductive ink
- Using thermal mass to store and radiate heat
- Voltage-based temperature control (no active control circuit needed at this scale)
- Power consumption and resistance calculations (10 ohms, 24V, ~2.5A, ~60W)
- Alternative heat sources for the same form factor (carbon fiber, burning embers, heat batteries)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **1970s bedside table** — solid wood/fiber, the base structure of the build
- **Kiln tile** — used as both thermal mass and heating element substrate; rated to 1600°C (overkill, but on hand)
- **Conductive/graphene ink** — Robert's own product, painted in three layers onto the tile to create the heating element
- **Copper tape ("slug tape")** — used as electrodes/bus bars on the tile; Robert notes it's called slug tape because slugs won't cross it (used around plant pots)
- **Masking tape** — used to define the painted area
- **Strap hinges** — added to a drawer to create the stove door
- **Concrete, paving slabs, slate, stone** — mentioned as freely available alternatives to the kiln tile
- **Carbon fiber / carbon felt** — mentioned as alternative heating element materials
- **Sand and carbon (in pecans)** — referenced as a "heat battery" approach used by a collaborator (Kerry X2)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of furniture (removing legs, drawers) and reshaping with a table saw
- Drilling air holes in the wooden box for convection
- Adding a hinge to a drawer to create an opening door
- Painting conductive ink in multiple layers onto a ceramic/stone substrate to achieve target resistance (~10 ohms)
- Mounting the heating element on drawer runners for easy insertion/removal
- Voltage-based passive temperature control — balancing energy input against heat dissipation rate

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Video 1761** is the follow-up covering heat control circuits, referenced multiple times in response to viewer questions about temperature regulation.
- **"Slug tape" explanation:** Copper tape is called slug tape in the UK because slugs won't cross it — gardeners put it around plant pots. It is not a specialist electronics product; it's widely available.
- **Kotatsu connection:** Robert confirms that placing a larger version of this under a coffee table and draping a blanket over it is essentially a Japanese kotatsu. He seems genuinely pleased by the connection.
- **Any heat source works:** He repeatedly emphasises that the internal heat source is flexible — a ceramic heater, a light bulb, almost anything will do.
- **Stone orientation:** On whether to insulate the bottom of the stone, he says the stone captures downward heat that would otherwise be lost, but adds honestly: "I strongly suspect it barely matters either way."
- **Carbon monoxide concerns:** Dismisses worries with humour, noting these devices have been used for centuries "with not one death from carbon monoxide poisoning being reported — ever!"
- **Cost/value:** Unbothered by any suggestion the furniture was worth more — "something is actually worth what someone will pay."
- **Conductive ink chair seat reference:** Points viewers to an earlier chair seat cover video for a similar application of the same ink technique.
