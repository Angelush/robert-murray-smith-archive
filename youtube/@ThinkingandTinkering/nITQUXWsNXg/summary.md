## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to convert a cheap pillar drill (drill press) into a slot cutter by fitting a router bit and bolting an angle iron guide to the table. He then uses the slotted timber to build a linear peg gear mechanism — a rack-and-pinion style device drawn directly from Pollum's Mechanical Alphabet — illustrating how basic tools can be adapted creatively when a full machine shop is out of reach.

## Key Topics
- Adapting a budget drill press for slot cutting using a router bit
- Safety considerations when using a router bit in a drill press
- Pollum's Mechanical Alphabet (a collection of mechanical models by a Swedish inventor, held in Stockholm museums)
- Building a linear peg gear (rack-and-pinion analogue) from MDF and dowel
- The difference between reciprocation in a straight line vs. an arc
- Plans for a future worm gear / Archimedes screw hydrogeneration project
- Potential application of the linear peg gear to a Bennett's Doubler electrostatic generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- MDF (medium-density fibreboard) — used to build the linear peg gear frame
- Dowel — used for pegs in both the rack and the pinion disc
- Angle iron — bolted to the drill press table as a cutting guide
- Pine / soft timber — mentioned as the primary material for slot cutting with this method
- Plywood — mentioned in comments as tending to chip when cut across the grain

## Techniques and Methods
- Slot cutting on a drill press: fitting a router bit in the chuck, setting the press to its highest speed, and feeding timber against a fixed guide
- Making incremental plunge cuts rather than full-depth passes to avoid vibration
- Drilling a row of holes along the edge of a timber strip and pressing in dowel pegs to form a linear gear rack
- Constructing a peg-studded disc to act as the rotary driver (pinion) for the rack

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Safety context:** Robert acknowledges the technique is unconventional but notes he has been doing it on the same old drill for about 8 years in soft materials (pine, plastics) without issue — he views risk as highly dependent on the operator and frequency of use.
- **Material behaviour:** He explains that MDF cuts woolly (fibres curl and fluff), while plywood chips when cut against the grain but cuts smoothly with it.
- **Tool philosophy:** He reiterates that no tool is inherently safe — safety comes from the person's skill and handling, not the tool itself (specifically addressing comparisons between routers and drill presses).
- **Aluminium:** He notes he has heard of people successfully using this slot-cutting method on aluminium.
- **Milling bits:** For harder materials he would prefer a milling bit over a router bit.
- **Community:** He expresses appreciation for comments that add constructive safety tips rather than simply saying "don't do it."
