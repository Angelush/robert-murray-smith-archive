## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates and compares three methods for producing high-surface-area carbon "snakes," ranging from the classic safe sugar-and-acid reaction to a dramatic, explosive reaction using a toxic precursor chemical. The video is framed around practical interest in making amorphous carbon for potential use in batteries or supercapacitors, though Robert is candid that the experiments are also simply fun to watch.

## Key Topics
- Three distinct methods for producing carbon snakes, compared by safety and spectacle
- The chemistry of acid dehydration of sucrose (carbon-hydrogen-oxygen separation)
- Combustion-driven carbon production using baking soda as a CO₂ source
- The use of nitroaniline as a high-drama carbon precursor
- Practical considerations for using the resulting carbons (contamination, further processing)
- UK regulatory context for purchasing concentrated sulfuric acid

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Table sugar (sucrose)** — ~50g; primary carbon source in methods 1 and 2
- **Sulfuric acid (96%)** — 30mL for sugar method; 2mL for nitroaniline method; acts as dehydrating agent
- **Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)** — mixed 4:1 with sugar; thermally decomposes to release CO₂ during combustion
- **Isopropyl alcohol** — used as fuel to ignite the sugar/baking soda mixture
- **Nitroaniline (4-nitroaniline)** — ~4g; yellow powder; toxic; a precursor to dyes; produces the most explosive/dramatic reaction when combined with sulfuric acid and heat

## Techniques and Methods
- **Acid dehydration (Method 1):** Concentrated sulfuric acid poured into table sugar strips out hydrogen and oxygen as water, leaving a porous carbon "snake" via steam generation through the molten mass
- **Combustion with bicarbonate (Method 2):** Sugar and baking soda (4:1 ratio) burned using isopropyl alcohol as fuel; baking soda decomposes on heating, releasing CO₂ that inflates the carbon structure; produces very light, clean carbon
- **Nitroaniline + sulfuric acid (Method 3):** Nitroaniline heated on a Bunsen burner/tripod then reacted with a small amount of sulfuric acid; reaction is slow to initiate then becomes sudden, explosive, and dramatic; requires stepping away from the setup

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Amorphous carbon:** Clarified that all three methods produce amorphous carbon, not graphitic carbon — important for anyone hoping to use the material directly in supercapacitors or batteries
- **Further processing needed:** Suggested catalytic graphitization using an iron salt as a route to improve the carbon quality; offered to make a follow-up video on this for members
- **Cleaning sulfuric acid-contaminated carbon:** Washing is the main option; heating would work but generates dangerous acid fumes
- **Baking soda method safety:** Described it as "nice and safe — a good one for the kids" and encouraged viewers to try it themselves as it is "cheap, easy and safe"
- **Most dramatic method:** Confirmed the nitroaniline reaction is his personal favourite and the most dramatic: *"I love that last one mate — it just SHOOTS out"*
- **UK licensing for sulfuric acid:** Noted that concentrated sulfuric acid (above 15%) requires an EPP (Explosives Precursors and Poisons) licence in the UK; costs ~£40; requires proof of identity, proof of address, a self-certification that you are not depressed/suicidal/a maniac, and a countersignature. Provided the official government link for obtaining the licence and described the legislation as "poorly thought out."
- **Fire alarm:** Acknowledged with humor that the fire alarm going off during filming was unplanned: *"you couldn't plan stuff like that mate"*
