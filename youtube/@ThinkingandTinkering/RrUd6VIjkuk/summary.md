## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a novel method for cleanly cutting glass bottles using a conductive "ink" (a susceptor material) and a domestic microwave oven. After scoring the bottle with a standard bottle cutter, the ink is painted along the score line; microwaving on the defrost setting heats the ink, which propagates a clean fracture around the glass. The technique is safer and more reliable than traditional methods like the burning-string trick, and works even on thick-walled bottles.

## Key Topics
- Bottle cutting as a craft/upcycling technique (making glasses, lamps, etc.)
- Limitations of conventional bottle-separation methods (burning string, tapping)
- Using a conductive susceptor ink as a targeted heat source along a score line
- Microwave settings and timing for different glass thicknesses
- Listening for the audible cracking/popping sound that signals successful fracture propagation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Susceptor ink** — the central material; applied along the score line to absorb microwave energy and generate localised heat
- **Glass bottles** — two examples shown: a thin-walled bottle (~10 seconds) and a thicker bottle (~20–30 seconds)
- **Fuel-soaked string** — mentioned only as the traditional alternative technique being replaced

## Techniques and Methods
- **Bottle scoring** — using an eBay bottle cutter to create a light, even score line around the circumference; heavy pressure is not required
- **Ink application** — painting the susceptor ink directly over the score line and allowing it to dry before microwaving
- **Microwave fracturing** — placing the scored, ink-painted bottle in a domestic microwave on the **defrost (low power)** setting for 10–30 seconds, depending on glass thickness
- **Auditory monitoring** — listening for the cracking/popping noise as confirmation that the stress fracture is propagating cleanly
