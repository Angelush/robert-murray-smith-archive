## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores thermochemical energy storage — materials that release heat when water is added and recharge by drying — through a series of live demonstrations using zeolite, sodium hydroxide, quicklime, and urea. He then introduces the Eindhoven Heat Battery, developed by Eindhoven Technical University, which uses potassium carbonate to store surplus industrial waste heat and deliver it to homes. The key takeaway is that this "salt in a tin" concept has a long history but is now backed by serious engineering and European funding, making it a credible near-term game changer for domestic heating in the Netherlands.

## Key Topics
- Historical context: the 1956 World Symposium on Practical Use of Solar Energy (Phoenix, Arizona) and the *Popular Mechanics* report
- Maria Telkes and her pioneering solar thermal house using Glauber's salt as a phase-change material
- The principle of thermochemical heat storage: water absorption releases energy; drying restores it
- Live demonstrations of exothermic reactions (zeolite, sodium hydroxide, quicklime) driving a Stirling engine and a Peltier device
- Endothermic reactions (urea) used to create a cold side for Peltier electricity generation
- The Eindhoven Technical University heat battery ("GINGER") using potassium carbonate
- Modular cell design, 70 kWh units, and a 200 kWh stack capacity
- Capturing industrial waste heat below 150 °C (otherwise discarded) to charge the battery
- Real-world trials: four homes (two in Netherlands, one in Poland, one in France)
- European development funding and the goal of transitioning 3.5 million homes off gas

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glauber's salt (sodium sulfate / Na₂SO₄)** — historical phase-change material used by Maria Telkes; absorbs water and releases heat
- **Zeolite X13** — aluminosilicate clay with galleries that absorb water violently, generating near-boiling temperatures; normally used for ammonia removal in fish ponds
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — highly exothermic when dissolved in water; used to drive a Stirling engine (gloves required)
- **Quicklime (calcium oxide / CaO)** — commodity chemical; reacts with water to reach ~300 °C; can char wood surrounds
- **Urea** — principal component of urine; endothermic dissolution in water, used to create the cold side of a Peltier device
- **Potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃)** — selected by the Eindhoven team after testing hundreds of materials; withstands repeated wet/dry cycling
- **Graphite / carbon** — mixed with potassium carbonate in the Eindhoven battery to improve heat transfer
- **Graphite foil** — used by Robert as a thermal contact pad between components in his bench demonstrations

## Techniques and Methods
- **Thermochemical hydration/dehydration cycling** — wet the material to release heat; dry it (e.g., in sunlight) to recharge
- **Peltier (thermoelectric) generation** — exploiting the temperature differential between a hot reacting material and an ambient cold surface to generate electricity directly
- **Stirling engine as a heat-difference demonstrator** — low-temperature Stirling run from chemical heat (zeolite, NaOH, quicklime) or cold (urea)
- **Graphite foil thermal bonding** — improving thermal contact between an irregular tin can and a Peltier device
- **Modular cell stacking** — Eindhoven approach: dividing the potassium carbonate bed into discrete cells and discharging them in stages to improve efficiency
- **Industrial waste-heat capture** — transporting dry salt modules to industrial sites where sub-150 °C waste heat is available to recharge them, then returning them to households
