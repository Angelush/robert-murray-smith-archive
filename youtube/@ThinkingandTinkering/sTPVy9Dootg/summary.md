## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a belt drive system for his paper bicycle project. Rather than using a conventional chain and pedals, he constructs a large rear drive cog by repurposing a bicycle wheel rim and a timing belt, mimicking the drivetrain design of an e-scooter. The result is a low-cost, fabricated belt-drive sprocket bolted directly to the rear wheel.

## Key Topics
- Belt drive systems vs. chain drive for bicycles
- Gear ratio considerations (6:1 to 8:1 target range)
- Reusing e-scooter drivetrain components on a custom bike
- Constructing a large drive cog from salvaged bicycle parts and a timing belt
- Alignment and fastening techniques for joining two rims

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Timing belt** — used inside-out to form the teeth of the large rear drive cog
- **Steel-rimmed kid's cycle wheel** — stripped of spokes; the steel rim accepts welding and bolting
- **Resin** — poured into the rim assembly to create a flat, flush surface for the belt
- **Cyanoacrylate (super glue / "crazy glue")** — used to bond the timing belt to the rim
- **Bolts and nuts** — six bolts used to join the two rims; nuts used as spacers
- **Rubber cement / acrylic glue** — mentioned as acceptable alternatives to cyanoacrylate

## Techniques and Methods
- **Inside-out timing belt cog** — timing belt flipped inside-out and fitted around a bicycle rim to create a toothed sprocket (referencing video 1084)
- **Super glue alignment trick** — two rims temporarily bonded with super glue, drilled through together for perfect bolt hole alignment, then separated and re-bolted
- **Resin filling** — epoxy/resin poured into the cog rim assembly to level the surface before gluing the belt
- **Six-bolt rim joining** — rims fastened together with bolts; bolt heads on the tire side are rounded so the tire can seat over them

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Seatpost length:** The frame/post is intentionally left overlong so he can find the right fit once wheels are on — he'll saw a bit off after sitting on it. "Better too long than too short."
- **Spacers:** Confirmed he is using nuts as spacers in the rim bolt assembly.
- **Rear stays:** Confirmed the rear stays are parallel by design.
- **Pedal option:** He deliberately left the rear sprocket intact on the bike wheel so that, if he ever takes it on the road, he could add a pedal-and-chain system alongside the motor drive.
- **Silicon tyre fill:** Skeptical it would work — "most silicons are air cure and in a tyre filled with silicon there is no air — so no cure" — but expressed curiosity to hear if anyone tries it.
- **Belt maintenance:** Says he won't bother with any belt treatment given the low speeds expected, but suggests checking Suzuki's recommendations for their belt-driven superbike if needed.
- **Degradation:** Acknowledged everything degrades over time — "not even the hills live forever."
