## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the theory behind sheet resistance and demonstrates how to build a simple square probe (bus bar method) for measuring it. He walks through the physics of resistivity and sheet resistance, constructs the probe from scrap copper and builder's board, and uses it to measure the sheet resistance of a piece of graphite foil, obtaining a reading of approximately 1–1.6 ohms/square.

## Key Topics
- What sheet resistance is and how it differs from bulk resistance
- The formula: R = ρ × (L/W×T), and how it simplifies to Rs = ρ/T for a square geometry
- Why the physical size of the square doesn't matter, only that length equals width
- The bus bar method as a practical, low-cost alternative to the van der Pauw (4-point probe) method
- Importance of ohmic contact (no PN junctions, no Schottky barriers, no oxides)
- Construction of a square probe from common/scrap materials
- Taking a sheet resistance measurement of graphite foil

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper** — used for the bus bars; sourced from scrap lightning conductor rod with plastic casing
- **Builder's board** (polyurethane foam with PVC covering) — used as the insulating spacer to hold bus bars at correct separation
- **Brass terminal block / brass fittings** — soldered on as meter contact points
- **Superglue** — used to bond the assembly; noted to cure very strong on builder's board
- **Graphite foil** (~100 µm thick) — the test sample measured with the finished probe
- **Solder** — used to attach brass contacts to the copper bus bars

## Techniques and Methods
- Bus bar method for sheet resistance measurement (two parallel conducting bars separated by a distance equal to their length)
- Filing and cleaning metal surfaces for good ohmic contact
- Soldering brass contact fittings onto copper bus bars
- Clamping the probe to improve contact quality and measurement stability
- Using a multimeter set to resistance (ohms) to read sheet resistance directly in Ω/square

## Robert's Own Replies
One substantive reply stands out among the otherwise brief acknowledgements:

- Robert acknowledges there are known design debates around the bus bar probe — for example, some argue the probe should use **concentric circles** to avoid current flow disparity at the ends of the bars. He concedes this is a valid criticism but defends the rectangular bus bar design as **entirely fit for purpose for the home researcher/hobbyist**, where simplicity and low cost matter more than theoretical perfection.
