## Overview
This follow-up teardown video walks through the internal components of a homemade magnetic hot plate stirrer built using conductive ink, copper tape, and basic materials. The presenter disassembles the device to explain how each of the four main components works, highlighting that the resistive heater achieves approximately 99% efficiency converting electricity to heat. The build demonstrates that functional lab equipment can be constructed primarily from acrylic, aluminium sheet, epoxy resin, and conductive ink.

## Key Topics
- Teardown and component walkthrough of a DIY magnetic hot plate stirrer
- Resistive heating using conductive ink on an aluminium plate
- Motor assembly with an epoxy resin disc and repurposed hard drive magnets
- Temperature regulation via thermocouple and microcomputer heat controller
- PWM/RPM motor speed control
- Construction choices: acrylic enclosure, aluminium sheet, heat-resistant adhesive
- Preview of an upcoming full build guide series on homemade lab equipment

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — painted onto aluminium sheet to form the resistive heating element
- **Copper tape** — two strips used as electrical contacts on the heat plate
- **Aluminium sheet** — substrate for the heat plate and motor base
- **Epoxy resin** — cast into a disc form for the motor rotor
- **Acrylic** — used for the transparent enclosure box
- **Hard drive magnets** — two magnets repurposed for the motor disc
- **Additional strong magnets** — two more magnets of opposite polarity added to the disc for balance and spin weight
- **Heat-resistant glue** — rated to 130°C, used to bond acrylic components

## Techniques and Methods
- Resistive heating: conductive ink deposited between copper tape contacts on aluminium
- Thermocouple temperature sensing: sensor sits beneath the heat plate and feeds back to the temperature controller
- Epoxy resin casting: poured into a cardboard circle mould with a rubber seal, with the motor held in place during curing
- Rotary truing: cured epoxy disc spun at 14 V while sandpaper is applied to achieve a smooth, round profile
- PWM speed control: motor speed regulated via a PWM/RPM controller module
- Wiring organisation: all negatives grouped on one side, positives routed through a rear hole for future mains connection
