## Overview
This video explores the theoretical foundations of electrochemical cells (batteries, supercapacitors, and fuel cells) through the lens of thermodynamics. Robert explains why electrochemical cells can exceed the efficiency limit of heat engines (Carnot efficiency) by introducing Gibbs free energy and the Nernst efficiency. Along the way, he offers an accessible and opinionated reinterpretation of entropy as the energy required to maintain a system's structural arrangement, rather than the conventional "tendency toward disorder."

## Key Topics
- Carnot efficiency and the thermodynamic limits of heat engines
- How the Nernst efficiency of electrochemical cells can exceed the Carnot efficiency
- Gibbs free energy (ΔG = ΔH − TΔS) and its three states (positive, zero, negative)
- Enthalpy and how it is measured (bomb calorimeter)
- Entropy: critique of the "disorder" interpretation; Robert's preferred framing as energy required to maintain structural arrangement
- The second law of thermodynamics — its scope, limitations, and dependence on entropy
- The distinction between laws as *descriptions* of systems versus laws as *constraints* on systems
- The applicability of thermodynamic laws only to closed systems, and uncertainty about whether the universe is closed

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steam (transfer fluid of the industrial revolution)
- Petrol/gasoline (transfer fluid of internal combustion engines)

## Techniques and Methods
- Bomb calorimetry (mentioned as the method for measuring enthalpy)
- Carnot efficiency calculation: η = 1 − T_cold / T_hot
- Gibbs free energy equation: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
