## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores friction drives as a potential alternative to gears for a 2-axis positioning device he is building. He explains the fundamental physics of friction drives, walks through their pros and cons relative to gears, and then builds and demonstrates a simple 3D-printed prototype to validate the core principles. His central takeaway is that the spring providing the normal force is the most critical and commonly overlooked component of any friction drive.

## Key Topics
- Robert's amateur philosophy: doing engineering for the love of it, not for pay
- His in-progress 2-axis planar positioning system (currently using gears)
- Why gears have drawbacks (backlash, expense) that motivate exploring alternatives
- What a friction drive is: torque transfer via static friction between surfaces with a normal force applied
- Real-world examples of friction drives (bicycles, buses, cars, trucks, locomotives, wheelchairs)
- Pros and cons of friction drives: simple, quiet, adjustable, cost-effective vs. more wear and limited torque
- The potential of friction drives for nanometer-scale accuracy in applications like 3D printing
- Design and assembly of a 3D-printed test friction drive with a conical-rim wheel and bobbin
- The critical role of the spring in providing normal force and limiting torque

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA** — used to 3D print the test friction drive prototype; noted as low friction coefficient but acceptable for prototyping
- **Rubber** — cited in the bicycle tire analogy as a high-friction-coefficient material
- **Springs** — used to apply the normal force onto the drive surfaces and take up wear

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — all prototype parts fabricated in PLA
- **Conical rim design** — wheel rim has a conical cross-section that self-centers when pressed into the bobbin, maximising contact area
- **Spring-loaded normal force** — springs press the drive wheel into the bobbin to maintain friction contact and compensate for wear
- **Step-up / step-down drive configuration** — the design can operate as either depending on which element is driven
- **Prototype-first experimentation** — building a simplified test rig to validate principles before integrating into the target machine
