## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds two wooden caterpillar toys as Christmas presents for his grandchildren — an automaton version for his 3-year-old grandson (featuring a cam-driven crawling mechanism with a spinning butterfly overhead) and a simpler pull-along version for his 1-year-old granddaughter. The video walks through the full build using inexpensive wooden breadboards, explaining cam mechanics along the way, and makes a case for handmade gifts over mass-produced plastic toys.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: potential toy shortages at Christmas 2021 and the value of handmade gifts
- Introduction to cam mechanics — eccentric drilling, throw calculation, and offset phase angles
- Building a camshaft and cradle from wooden discs to animate a caterpillar body
- Friction gear mechanism to drive a spinning butterfly above the automaton
- Using wooden breadboards as a cheap source of quality hardwood
- Dowel joinery as a screw-free construction method for children's toys

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wooden breadboards (light and dark hardwood, e.g. beech) — primary structural material, ~£8 for four
- Wooden ball — used as the caterpillar's head
- Plywood discs — cut and shaped into butterfly wings
- Dowel rod (9 mm "lolli sticks") — used for axles and joints
- EPDM rubber / inner tube / elastic bands — wrapped around friction gear wheel for grip

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting discs from breadboards with a hole saw
- Sanding discs flat
- Eccentric hole drilling to create offset cams (calculating throw as the difference between two radii)
- Phasing/offsetting cams on a shared axle to produce a travelling wave motion
- Dowel joinery: splitting the dowel end with a wood sliver to lock it in place (no screws)
- Friction gear transmission from camshaft to butterfly armature
- Pre-drilling a block before shaping it (for the butterfly body)
- Nibbling plywood discs to form wing shapes
