## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith converts a discarded plywood cable drum into a vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) using a squirrel-cage rotor design, mounted on a repurposed exercise bike frame. He is refreshingly candid that he does not know if it will work — the point is simply to try. The turbine successfully spins in winds of just 8–10 mph, validating the concept and setting up the next phase of attaching it to a generator.

## Key Topics
- Converting a found cable drum into a vertical axis wind turbine rotor
- Squirrel-cage blade design cut from the drum's own plywood core
- Testing rotational inertia and behaviour in low wind speeds
- Integration plan: mounting onto an exercise bike flywheel with magnets for generation
- Robert's philosophy of experimentation over expertise

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Plywood cable drum (salvaged)
- Steel rods (part of the cable drum structure)
- Plywood strips (5 cm wide, cut from the drum's inner core, used as blades)
- Broom handle (used in the test cradle)
- Bearings (fitted to the test cradle uprights)
- VDU (monitor) stand (repurposed as a support)
- Exercise bike frame/flywheel (intended mounting platform)
- Magnets (planned for gluing to the flywheel for generation)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a cable drum and re-purposing its core plywood as rotor blades
- Cutting plywood into 5 cm strips and gluing/clamping them at an angle around the drum frame (squirrel-cage arrangement)
- Fabricating a rough test cradle with bearings on broom-handle uprights
- Outdoor wind testing at approximately 8–10 mph wind speed
- Car-top elevation testing to get the rotor into a clean airstream

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief encouragements, but several notable points emerge:

- **Water wheel adaptation**: He acknowledges the drum would make a good water wheel but notes the practical barrier — most people don't have access to flowing water, and there are legal restrictions on placing structures in rivers.
- **Magnetic bearings**: He mentions that thrust bearings would work well as an alternative to magnetic bearings for this build.
- **Spinning behaviour as a signal**: He offers an interesting observation that the spinning motion itself may be revealing an asymmetry in the design — possibly related to the Magnus effect (rolling cylinders creating lift and pressure differentials).
- **Philosophy of experimentation**: He elaborates on his "jack of all trades" mindset, quoting the full version of the proverb — *"jack of all trades and master of none, but so often better than master of one"* — and stresses that the key is to bring all your skills to bear, try things, and not get paralysed searching for the "best" solution.
- **Failures off-camera**: He openly admits he does not film failures, of which there are many, and expresses a strong distaste for people who claim to know everything, calling it "the certainty of the ignorant."
- **Vertical vs horizontal axis**: He says he basically couldn't decide between the two — either is good.
