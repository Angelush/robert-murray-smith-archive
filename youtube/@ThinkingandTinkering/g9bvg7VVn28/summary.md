## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a DIY generator using a rotor previously constructed from an old speaker (detailed in video 1030/103) and salvaged coils from shaded-pole induction motors found in microwave oven fans. By connecting the coils in series with correct polarity, he demonstrates that the generator can produce measurable AC voltage, achieving around 30 volts when spun with a drill — a promising result for a device built entirely from recycled parts.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a speaker magnet assembly as a generator rotor
- Extracting shaded-pole motor coils from microwave oven fans
- Building a housing from cintaboard (builders board)
- Wiring coils in series and correcting polarity for additive voltage
- Potential applications: wind turbine, water/paddle-wheel generator
- Suggested improvements: stronger magnets, reducing air gap between magnets and coil cores

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Speaker magnet** — repurposed as the rotor's permanent magnet
- **Cintaboard / builders board** — used to fabricate the rotor housing drum
- **Shaded-pole motor coils (with iron cores)** — salvaged from microwave oven cooling fans; used as the stator coils
- **Skater (skateboard) bearings** — used in the rotor assembly (noted in author replies)

## Techniques and Methods
- Extracting shaded-pole coil assemblies from fan motors by unscrewing/drilling out fasteners, removing the rotor drum, then squeezing the frame with pliers/vice to pop the coil and iron core out as one piece
- Cutting squares in the plastic housing to push iron-core blocks closer to the rotor magnets, reducing the air gap to improve flux coupling
- Testing individual coils for correct polarity by spinning the rotor and measuring voltage with a multimeter, then reversing connections on coils that oppose each other before wiring in series
- Connecting multiple coils in series to sum their output voltages
- Driving the rotor with a hand drill to test generation at speed

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirms he used **skater (skateboard) bearings** in the build
- Acknowledges the current output is likely only **milliamps**, not high current
- Notes he should build a **coil-winding machine** to speed up future builds
- Mentions he has built prototypes **up to 1 metre diameter**, suggesting the design scales well
- Highlights an interesting possibility: placing **both motor coils and generator coils on the same rotor pole pieces** to create a **rotary converter**
- Generally pleased with the result for something "lashed together" from recycled parts
