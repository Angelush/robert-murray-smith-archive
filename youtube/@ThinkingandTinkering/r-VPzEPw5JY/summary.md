## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests a homemade Archimedes screw generator built from recycled CDs and expanding foam, demonstrating it in a car park using buckets of water. The device is designed as a low-power energy scavenger intended for shallow, slow-moving water such as drainage ditches or roof downspouts, inspired by a Japanese engineer's similar concept. The test is a success, generating voltage and charging capacitors, proving the concept works even with a mismatched motor.

## Key Topics
- Energy scavenging from shallow, slow-moving water
- Archimedes screw as a micro-hydroelectric generator
- DIY construction from recycled materials (CDs and expanding foam)
- Inspiration from Japanese drainage-ditch energy scavenging systems
- Potential applications: drainage pipes, roof downspouts, small streams
- Suggested improvements: double helix design, matched generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- CDs (used as screw blades)
- Expanding foam (used as screw body/former)
- NEMA stepper motor (repurposed as a generator)
- Rectification circuit
- Capacitors

## Techniques and Methods
- Repurposing a NEMA stepper motor as an electrical generator
- Rectifying AC output from the motor for DC capacitor charging
- Bucket-pour water flow test as a proof-of-concept method
- Screw mounted at an incline (~15 cm water depth) to create flow-driven rotation

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Output figures:** Confirmed approximately **2.5 V and 100–120 mA** from the device during testing.
- **Mass production:** Notes the design would be "super simple to mass produce and therefore could be very cheap indeed."
- **Generator type:** Speculates the ideal matched generator would be a **BLDC motor**, like those used in cheap wind turbines.
- **Kaplan turbine comparison:** Believes a Kaplan turbine requires high flow rate, whereas this design suits high-mass but low-flow-rate streams.
- **UK stream access:** Acknowledges England does have streams but notes people "aren't really supposed to block them up."
- **Tolerances and sealing:** Agrees tight tolerances would improve efficiency but would increase cost; considers this an acceptable trade-off for a scavenger device.
- **Philosophy on lash-up testing:** Emphasises that a simple prototype test only needs to confirm two things — (1) it works to some degree, and (2) you now have a minimum baseline — pushing back against criticism that it "isn't generating much."
