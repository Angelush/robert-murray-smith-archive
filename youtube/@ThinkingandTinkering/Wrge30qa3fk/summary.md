## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith investigates whether the electrical output of his SWAG (a carbon-ink-coated electrochemical device) is influenced by the ionic charge of dissolved salts rather than atomic size. Testing sodium chloride (+1), magnesium chloride (+2), and aluminium chloride (+3) at equal molarity confirms his hypothesis: higher cation charge produces higher voltage output. He then discovers that aluminium chloride alone enables the device to act as a thermal battery, with output rising significantly as temperature and fluid flow increase.

## Key Topics
- Effect of cation charge (+1, +2, +3) on SWAG output voltage
- Comparison of sodium chloride, magnesium chloride, and aluminium chloride solutions
- Spontaneous voltage generation in aluminium chloride without mechanical dipping
- Thermal battery behaviour: voltage increase with temperature
- Effect of fluid flow rate across the SWAG plate on output voltage

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium chloride (NaCl)** — 0.5 M solution, cation charge +1, ~3 mV output
- **Magnesium chloride (MgCl₂)** — 0.5 M solution, cation charge +2, ~4 mV output
- **Aluminium chloride (AlCl₃)** — 0.5 M solution, cation charge +3, ~6 mV output; also generates ~1.5 V spontaneously on immersion
- **SWAG ink** — conductive carbon-based ink painted onto a plastic substrate
- **Copper strips** — used as current collectors on the SWAG plate
- **Plastic substrate** — base material for the SWAG plate

## Techniques and Methods
- Dip-testing: immersing the SWAG plate into each salt solution sequentially, with a water rinse between tests
- Voltage measurement across a 10-ohm resistor using a millivolt meter
- Heating with a hot plate to test thermal response (room temp 23 °C → 33–36 °C)
- Fluid flow agitation (pump/stirrer) to increase fluid velocity across the plate surface

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Cation charge is the key variable:** Confirms KCl would behave like NaCl because it also has a +1 cation — it is the charge on the cation, not the element, that governs output.
- **HCl as an accessible alternative:** Suggests hydrochloric acid (spirit of salt / muriatic acid) as a readily available high-charge option, purchasable at builders' yards; combined with kitchen foil it would be sufficient to replicate the experiment.
- **Current is AC, not DC:** Explains that current flow depends on the direction of water flow across the surface — dipping in produces one polarity, pulling out reverses it.
- **Mechanism clarification:** Corrects a commenter's description, noting the process is adsorption/desorption and electric double-layer formation, not simple up-and-down motion — this is important because it governs the relationship between charge and the surface.
- **Thermal battery confirmation:** Agrees with a commenter's suggestion that the double-layer mechanism is "probably right, which is why it operates as a temperature battery."
- **UV test:** Reports having tried UV illumination with no result.
