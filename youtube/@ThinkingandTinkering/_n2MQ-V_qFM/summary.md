## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a scrap Nissan AC blower motor — previously featured in another video — by fabricating a larger custom rotor from an acrylic disk and slats cut from plastic venetian blinds. The assembled rotor is spun in the wind to demonstrate the motor functioning as a simple generator. The project proves that a working wind generator can be built from salvaged and low-cost materials without specialist tools.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a car AC blower motor as a wind generator
- Building a custom rotor from recycled/scrap materials
- The importance of rotor diameter relative to motor weight
- Demonstrating proof-of-concept wind generation
- Accessibility of the build (no 3D printing required)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Acrylic sheet (cut into a large circle as the rotor base)
- Plastic venetian blind slats (cut into ~6 cm strips for rotor blades)
- Super glue (cyanoacrylate adhesive, used to attach blades)
- Clear plastic sheet (formed into a nose cone)
- Solo/Scotch tape (to temporarily fix the cone for removability)
- Nissan AC blower motor (pancake-style permanent magnet DC motor salvaged from a scrap car)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting venetian blind slats to uniform 6 cm lengths for rotor blades
- Super-gluing blades flush around the edge of an acrylic disk, cutting them square so they rest in place while adhesive cures
- Rolling and taping clear plastic sheet into a nose cone
- Bolting the motor directly to the back of the rotor assembly
- Freehand spin-up test to confirm rotor turns freely and generates output

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Work in progress / output not yet measured:** Wind conditions were poor at time of filming so he didn't measure power output; plans to test properly when wind picks up.
- **Design limitations:** The current design won't scale beyond a few inches in height; scaling up is acknowledged as the key challenge.
- **Motor choice:** Confirms the blower motor is a permanent magnet DC motor, which he notes is inherently well-suited to generator use.
- **Vertical operation:** The rotor does spin in a vertical orientation, though not as efficiently as horizontal.
- **Construction tip:** Cutting blades square lets them rest naturally against the disk edge while the glue sets, making assembly easier than it looks.
- **Future directions:** Considered a wind-scoop design; endorses 3D printing as the better route for refined versions, but deliberately used accessible methods so more people could replicate it.
- **Encouragement:** Repeatedly encourages commenters to just try it themselves ("go for it mate").
