## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the phenomenon of creating a fire vortex using angled blades or slots around a small flame, investigating the critical geometry needed to sustain it. He demonstrates that a specific number of blades at a specific angle is required, and successfully creates a powerful vortex using pieces cut from a fire extinguisher cylinder. The video serves as a proof-of-concept investigation ahead of building a full vortex stove.

## Key Topics
- The vortex flame effect and what causes it
- Critical blade count and angle required to generate a stable vortex
- Using recycled materials (fire extinguisher casing) to prototype the vortex geometry
- Adding a chimney to the vortex setup and observing the draw effect
- Plans to order steel and build a proper vortex stove

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Paraffin — used as the initial fuel source for the demonstration flame
- Metal foil container — used to hold the paraffin
- Fire extinguisher casing (110 mm diameter steel tube) — cut and bent to form the vortex blade geometry
- Steel — ordered for the subsequent full stove build

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting and bending a fire extinguisher tube into curved blade segments (half-centimetre bends inward and outward) to induce a vortex
- Iterative geometry testing — trialling different blade counts and angles to find the critical configuration
- Adding a chimney section above the vortex to observe and enhance the updraught effect
- Comparative demonstration with and without the vortex blades to show the effect clearly
