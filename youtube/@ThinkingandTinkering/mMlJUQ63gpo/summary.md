## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith continues his plastic trike build series by adding steering and wheels, then mid-project decides to convert the trike into a quad by duplicating the rear wheel assembly for the front. The vehicle is tested by being pushed around a car park, and Robert confirms it handles well and plans to motorise it in a follow-up video.

## Key Topics
- Adding push-rod steering to the plastic trike chassis
- Fabricating wheel assemblies using threaded bar, extension nuts, and metal sleeves
- Decision to convert the trike into a quad for better motorisation suitability
- Iterative, prototype-as-you-go design philosophy
- Human-powered load test (pushed by Luke) to verify structural integrity before adding a motor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 12 mm diameter steel pipe (used for push rods and steering components)
- Threaded bar (used as axles for wheel sets)
- Metal sleeves (fitted over threaded bar for wheel support)
- Extension nuts (used to connect wheels to the threaded bar axles)
- L-brackets and flat steel brackets (for attaching wheel sets to the plastic frame)
- Recycled HDPE/plastic sheet (the laminated plastic used for the chassis and steering block)
- Leftover wheels from a previous project (Luke's)

## Techniques and Methods
- Flattening and bending steel pipe by hand/knee to form push rods
- Drilling holes in pipe and plastic to create steering linkages
- Laminating circles of plastic sheet to form a steering pivot block
- Using a steel pipe sleeve inside the pivot block to stabilise the steering bolt
- Assembling a dual-wheel rear (and then front) axle from threaded bar, sleeves, and extension nuts
- Gluing plastic pads onto the chassis as mounting points, then bolting brackets through them
- Iterative prototyping — changing from trike to quad mid-build
