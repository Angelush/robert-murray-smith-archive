## Overview
Robert demonstrates how he is redesigning the original Darwin wind turbine — previously built from wood with flat vents that rattled loudly — into a quieter, flapless 3D-printed plastic version. The updated design was inspired by a paper model created by his collaborator Max Kennedy, which showed that a chimney-pot-style enclosure can direct airflow upward through a central vent without disturbing the rear airflow. Robert walks through the entire design process in Tinkercad and prints test blades to verify the concept works.

## Key Topics
- Background on the original Darwin wind turbine and its noise problems
- Max Kennedy's contribution: a flapless paper model based on chimney-pot design principles
- Designing the blade shape in Tinkercad using primitive shapes
- 3D printing individual test blades (5 cm × 2 cm) and checking octagon assembly
- Verifying that stacked blade rings slot together correctly
- Demonstrating that blowing air through the prototype directs airflow upward as intended
- Advocacy for Tinkercad as an accessible entry point for 3D design and making

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar filament (implied by 3D printing context, not explicitly named)
- Paper (used by Max Kennedy for the prototype models)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD modelling in Tinkercad using primitive shapes (tube, box/block)
- Using Tinkercad's ruler, midpoint snapping, and array/replicate tools
- Mirroring and rotating components to achieve octagonal geometry (135° internal angle, 22.5° blade rotation increments)
- Exporting STL files from Tinkercad, slicing in Cura, and FDM 3D printing
- Stacking printed rings to verify mechanical fit before committing to a full assembly
- Manual airflow test (blowing through prototype) to confirm directional airflow behaviour

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Credit to Max Kennedy**: Robert repeatedly emphasises that the clever insight was Max's — applying the chimney-pot design concept to wind turbines is genuinely original thinking, and Robert wants viewers to watch and like Max's video.
- **Printer stack height**: He confirms he stacked four blade rings in his final model because four is the maximum his printer can accommodate in one print; a scaled-up version is planned if testing succeeds.
- **Why Tinkercad over Fusion 360**: His reason is simply that Tinkercad is easy and accessible, especially for absolute beginners who need to learn to think in 3D — not everything needs the most powerful tool.
- **Testing philosophy**: He encourages commenters who have design ideas to model and test them rather than theorise ("make a model and test it mate"), and notes FEMM modelling was suggested for one design variant.
- **Future plans**: He mentions considering a filament extruder project at some point, and is aware of Lightforge as a company doing related work.
- **Embedded jokes**: He reveals he deliberately hides jokes in his videos to see who is paying attention; only four commenters caught the joke in this video, which he finds delightful.
