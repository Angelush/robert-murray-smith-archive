## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a mechanical oscillator he built using a 3D-printed frame, a rubber-band-powered escapement, neodymium magnets as weights, and rubber bands as springs. He contextualises the build within the history of timekeeping — from the verge foliot (13th century) through the pendulum and balance wheel — explaining that all these devices share the same underlying physics as electronic oscillators. The takeaway is that harmonic mechanical oscillators are a foundational technology with applications ranging from clocks to earthquake machines.

## Key Topics
- The escapement mechanism as the "heart" of a clock
- Periodicity of a pendulum and how length determines swing period
- History of mechanical oscillators: verge foliot (1237), pendulum (credited to Huygens, 16th century), balance wheel (Robert Hooke, 1658; improved by Huygens, 1674)
- Balance wheel accuracy improvements through higher beat rates (up to 40 Hz)
- Building a custom mechanical oscillator using a rubber-band motor
- Tuning the oscillator by adjusting weight and spring tension
- Connection between mechanical harmonic oscillators and electronic oscillators
- Potential application as a Tesla-style "earthquake machine"

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Rubber bands** — used as springs and as the power source (rubber band motor)
- **Neodymium magnets** — used as adjustable weights on the oscillator arm
- **M12 nut** — used as a weight inside the pendulum holder
- **3D-printed parts (PLA or similar)** — light blue stand with slide and cream-coloured pendulum holder adapter, designed in Tinkercad

## Techniques and Methods
- Designing parts in Tinkercad and 3D printing them
- Inserting a bearing into the pendulum holder centre
- Attaching the oscillator to an escapement mechanism powered by a rubber band motor
- Tuning oscillation frequency by sliding the stand to vary rubber band tension
- Tuning oscillation frequency by adding or removing neodymium magnet weights
- Winding the rubber band motor to power the escapement

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed the oscillator is imperfect ("it jumps — it's by no means perfect — it's just a demo of the idea").
- Noted that **ruby** was historically used as a material in watch mechanisms (in addition to other jewels implied).
- Mentioned that a **Victorian penny placed on a pendulum can alter timekeeping by 2/5ths of a second per day** — a neat historical fact about fine-tuning clock rates.
- Confirmed harmonic oscillators are used in **thousands of applications**: car suspension, anti-earthquake systems, and many others.
- Pointed viewers to previous videos on the Tesla earthquake machine (linked one: `https://youtu.be/PROayNOP1q4`).
- Expressed his philosophy: he investigates and builds things as a way of understanding ideas himself, and far more projects don't make the final cut than do.
- Dismissed any "free energy" claims related to similar-looking devices as fake.
