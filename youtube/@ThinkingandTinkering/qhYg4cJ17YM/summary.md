## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the Wind Catcher system developed by a Norwegian company, drawing a direct analogy to the RAID (Redundant Array of Inexpensive Disks) concept from computing. He argues that arrays of smaller turbines can outperform single large turbines in energy output, cost, and longevity, suggesting this cross-industry thinking represents a smarter path forward for wind energy.

## Key Topics
- The historical analogy between oversized 1980s hard drives (prone to catastrophic failure) and increasingly large modern wind turbines
- The physics problem with giant turbines: energy scales with the square of blade radius, but weight and cost scale with the cube
- Tip speed limits: at 100-metre blades, the tip approaches supersonic speed, limiting further scaling
- The Wind Catcher system: a grid array of many smaller turbines covering the same swept area as a large turbine
- Efficiency gains from using the full square area (including corners) vs. a circular swept area
- Redundancy benefits: losing one small turbine in an array is trivial vs. losing an entire large turbine
- Comparison of design lifetimes: 20–25 years (large turbines, blades replaced ~every 10 years) vs. 50 years (Wind Catcher)
- Robert's broader philosophy: cross-pollinating ideas between unrelated fields enriches problem-solving
- Brief mention of his own "wind wall" projects as conceptually aligned with Wind Catcher

## Techniques and Methods
- **RAID-inspired redundancy applied to wind energy**: distributing generation across many small turbines rather than concentrating it in one large unit
- **Array geometry optimisation**: using a square grid layout to capture corner areas and effectively double the usable swept area compared to a circular rotor
- **Smaller turbine arrays**: allowing higher rotational tip speeds without hitting material or supersonic limits, enabling greater energy capture per unit area
