## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a follow-up to a previous video demonstrating the reduction of graphene oxide to graphene using hydrochloric acid and aluminum foil. In this update, he scales up to a more concentrated version (10 mg/mL in 35% HCl) to produce higher-concentration graphene ink. He primarily uses the video to warn viewers about the vigorous, exothermic nature of the reaction that he neglected to mention previously.

## Key Topics
- Scaling up graphene oxide reduction for graphene ink applications
- Safety warnings for working with concentrated (35%) hydrochloric acid
- Reaction behavior: exothermic onset, frothing, and heat generation
- Visual confirmation of successful reduction (deep black coloration)
- Practical lab technique for managing a vigorous reaction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide** — starting material being reduced
- **35% hydrochloric acid (HCl)** — reducing agent; used at 10 mg/mL concentration
- **Aluminum foil** — reacts with HCl to drive the reduction; covered in aluminum oxide which must be etched through first before the main reaction begins
- **Graphene** — the black product of the reduction, intended for use in conductive inks

## Techniques and Methods
- Reduction of graphene oxide to graphene via HCl and aluminum foil
- Adding aluminum in small amounts to control the reaction rate
- Swirling/stirring the flask rather than aggressive mixing
- Performing the reaction in a conical flask or beaker with ample headspace for frothing
- Conducting the reaction outdoors as a safety precaution
- Wearing gloves; having water supply nearby for immediate rinsing
