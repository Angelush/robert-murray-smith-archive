## Overview
Robert waterproofs the internal electronics of a PC fan (previously repurposed as a wind turbine generator) using Plasti-Dip rubber coating, then tests it under a running tap to see if it can function as a water turbine. The experiment succeeds, generating approximately 1.7 volts and lighting an LED, demonstrating that a simple dip-coat waterproofing treatment is sufficient to convert a PC fan into a working water turbine without affecting the mechanical tolerances.

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dip-coating** — submerging the fan's internal electronics in Plasti-Dip to apply a conformal rubber layer
- **Air drying** — allowing the Plasti-Dip coating to fully cure before reassembly
- **Tap water flow testing** — holding the reassembled fan under a running tap to spin the blades and measure electrical output
- **Voltage measurement** — using a multimeter to verify generator output

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments add several useful clarifications and follow-up ideas:

- **Scope of the experiment:** He explicitly notes the video was about demonstrating that waterproofing doesn't interfere with the fan's operation — electrical output optimisation is a separate matter he didn't pursue.
- **Corrosion vs. insulation:** He clarifies the Plasti-Dip treatment is primarily about **corrosion prevention** (steel core, solder joints, bearing seals), not electrical insulation.
- **Pelton wheel comparison:** He acknowledges the fan blade geometry resembles a Pelton wheel, noting he was "struck by how much the fan blades looked like a water turbine."
- **Large fan blades:** He reasons that larger blades would increase torque requirements and power draw, potentially exceeding what the small fan's wiring can handle — but says it's easy enough to experiment with.
- **Rainwater harvesting application:** He recommends trying this in rainwater gutters or drainpipes, and specifically mentions **rainwater power scavenging videos** and the use of a **bell siphon** as a promising combination.
- **Dual energy source idea:** In response to combining wind and water, he notes: *"wind is pressure and water is gravity — so we would be using two sources."*
- **Long-term durability:** He flags the bearing seals as his main concern for sustained submersion, alongside gradual corrosion of the steel core and solder joints.
