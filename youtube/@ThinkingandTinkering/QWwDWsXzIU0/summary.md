## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a passive solar evaporative cooling effect using oasis foam and carbon felt — the same materials used in a previous water purification experiment. He observes a striking 17°C temperature drop (from 32°C ambient down to 15°C) at the wet foam surface and proposes this as the basis for a low-energy, solar-powered air conditioning system using a split-system design with circulating fluid pipes.

## Key Topics
- Evaporative cooling as a side discovery from a solar water purification experiment
- Observed temperature differential of ~17°C below ambient using wet oasis foam and carbon felt in direct sunlight
- Comparison of commercial air conditioning approaches (phase-change refrigerant, split systems, mobile units)
- DIY ice/water evaporative cooler designs and their humidity drawbacks
- Proposed design for a solar-assisted split-system air conditioner using oasis foam, carbon felt, and a small pump

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oasis foam** — floral/craft foam used for its exceptional water-wicking properties; forms the evaporative cooling medium
- **Carbon felt** — layered over the oasis foam; also demonstrated strong wicking ability; absorbs solar energy to drive evaporation

## Techniques and Methods
- **Evaporative cooling** — wetting oasis foam and carbon felt, exposing to sunlight and ambient air to achieve sub-ambient temperatures
- **Temperature measurement** — inserting a thermometer probe into the wet foam to read surface temperature vs. ambient
- **Split-system heat exchanger concept** — proposed routing fluid pipes through the cooled foam externally, then circulating cooled fluid indoors to a heat exchanger, separating the cold and hot sides
- **Small pump circulation** — proposed as the only active energy input, keeping overall power consumption minimal
