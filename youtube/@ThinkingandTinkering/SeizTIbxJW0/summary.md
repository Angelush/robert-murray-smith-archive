## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a pedal-powered wooden generator frame entirely from MDF and salvaged materials, following up on a previous wooden gears video. The build centres on a large 72-tooth hand-cut cog meshing with a 24-tooth cog to achieve a 3:1 mechanical advantage, driving a pulley that can be connected to any motor or generator. The project demonstrates that a functional human-powered mechanical transmission can be made with basic woodworking tools and scrap materials, though the electrical output ultimately depends on whatever generator is attached.

## Key Topics
- Designing and cutting large wooden cogs (gears) from MDF
- 3:1 gear ratio using a 72-tooth and a 24-tooth cog
- Drawing large circles using a simple improvised trammel compass
- Building pedal cranks and swivelling pedal platforms from timber
- Making a wooden pulley with a V-groove to connect to a motor/generator
- Using salvaged bearings and broom handle as axles
- Repurposing a dining chair as the rider's seat frame
- Discussion of generator selection and realistic power output expectations

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **MDF (medium-density fibreboard)** — primary structural material for cogs and frame
- **Plywood** — noted as a preferable alternative to MDF for durability
- **Broom handle (25 mm diameter)** — used as axles, matching the salvaged bearing bore
- **Bearings from an old motor (25 mm)** — used to mount the large cog on the frame
- **8 mm skater bearings** — used in the pedal platform to allow swivel
- **8 mm steel rod** — pedal platform pivot
- **Gorilla Glue** — adhesive for gear assembly joints
- **Screws and angle screws** — structural fasteners, angle screws acting as cotter pins
- **Blank timber** — pedal cranks, pedal blocks, pulley blanks
- **Dining chair (repurposed)** — seat and support frame
- **Threaded bar** — mentioned in comments as an alternative axle material Robert acknowledged he wished he had used

## Techniques and Methods
- **Improvised trammel compass** — a strip of plastic with two holes drilled at the target radius, screwed at the centre, used to draw large circles
- **Uniform tooth spacing** — 72 teeth on the large cog requires one tooth every 5 degrees; marked and drilled systematically with a 16 mm bit
- **Broom-handle axle assembly** — broom handle pressed through gear centres and secured with Gorilla Glue plus angle screws acting as cotter pins
- **Hole-saw pulley cutting** — pulley discs cut from blank timber with a hole saw, then paired and glued/screwed, with a V-groove cut into the rim
- **Pedal crank construction** — timber crank arm with a blocking piece, glued and screwed; 8 mm rod inserted for the pedal bearing seat
- **Skater-bearing pedal pivot** — 8 mm skater bearings pressed onto the rod to allow the foot platform to swivel freely

## Robert's Own Replies
The comment replies are mostly brief and conversational, but several contain useful technical follow-up:

- **MDF vs. plywood and peg gears:** Robert clarified that MDF will not last long because the gear teeth will wear. He recommended using plywood for a rebuild, or ideally making **peg gears** (where individual pegs form the teeth) because worn pegs are easy to replace individually — a traditional technique for wooden machinery.
- **Flywheel use of the large cog:** He noted he considers the large cog better used as a **combined flywheel**, smoothing out pedal strokes, rather than drilling decorative holes in it (as some commenters suggested).
- **Generator preference:** Robert explicitly stated he is **a fan of axial flux generators** and was on the lookout for a car alternator to attach to the machine. He also hinted that a previous alternator project had been "ruined" and he needed to source another.
- **Battery work:** In response to a comment assuming he had given up on batteries, he pushed back firmly, suggesting the more likely explanation is that he is involved in something he cannot yet discuss publicly.
- **Belt vs. gears:** He agreed a belt drive would have been more practical, but noted it would have meant skipping the large wooden cogs — which he clearly found worthwhile for their own sake.
- **Moisture and gluing:** He acknowledged a commenter's tip about moisture improving Gorilla Glue bonds, noting that living in England means plenty of ambient moisture anyway.
- **Scalability:** He emphasised that the pulley output is generic — "basically you could bolt anything you want onto that."
