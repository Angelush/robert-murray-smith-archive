## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple home pyrolysis setup using a paint can, copper pipe, and a primus stove to produce syngas from wood. By heating organic material (wood or plastic) in a sealed, oxygen-free environment to 300–500°C, combustible syngas is generated and ignited at the pipe outlet. The video is part of a series on gasification, showing pyrolysis as a key intermediate step toward cleaner combustion of heavier fuels.

## Key Topics
- Pyrolysis as a component of the gasification process
- Building a simple paint-can pyrolysis reactor from common parts
- Generating and igniting syngas from wood
- Application to plastic feedstocks (especially HDPE)
- Connection to earlier videos on heavy fuel oils and future work on methanol distillation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Wood / wood chips** — primary feedstock for pyrolysis demonstration
- **Charcoal briquettes** — the solid residue left after wood pyrolysis
- **HDPE plastic** — noted as leaving no solid residue after pyrolysis
- **Vegetable oil / paraffin / diesel** — referenced from video 1705 as producing smoky flames without pyrolysis
- **Syngas (synthesis gas)** — the combustible gas product driven off during pyrolysis
- **Methanol** — mentioned as distillable from wood via a similar setup, covered in a future video
- **Copper pipe (15 mm)** — used as the gas outlet tube with compression fittings

## Techniques and Methods
- **Paint-can pyrolysis reactor** — sealing wood inside a lidded paint can to exclude oxygen
- **Compression fittings** — used for reusable, solder-free gas-tight connections
- **Heating on a primus/primer stove** — heat source to drive pyrolysis at 300–500°C
- **Syngas ignition** — lighting the gas at the copper pipe outlet to demonstrate combustibility
- **Plastic pyrolysis** — same setup applied to HDPE to produce fuel gas with no solid residue

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Energy balance clarification:** In this demo more energy is put in than retrieved (just driving off water and gas from a few twigs), but in real industrial use the heat from combustion is recycled to drive the pyrolysis and gasification stages — yielding net fire, heat, and extra gas.
- **This is a principle demonstration:** He repeatedly emphasises this is a demo of a standard industrial process; efficiency in real-life applications is well-recorded and good.
- **Gasification context:** Pyrolysis is already part of a broader gasification loop, where something is already being burned to supply heat.
- **On-demand system idea:** He floated the idea of making the setup on-demand rather than continuous, thinking it might be more practical.
- **Plastic feedstocks:** Confirmed that carpet (depending on composition) and other plastics could be processed the same way.
- **Future relevance:** Stated the technique "will be needed later" in the series, signalling it feeds into upcoming gasification and fuel-production projects.
