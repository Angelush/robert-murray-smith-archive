## Overview
Robert converts a pressure washer into a sandblaster using a cheap £20 kit from Amazon, using it to strip a front steering subframe down to bare metal. The video demonstrates that a pressure washer can substitute for an expensive air compressor setup, with practical tips on sand type, bucket shape, and safety.

## Key Topics
- Why home air compressors are inadequate for sandblasting
- Using a pressure washer as the pressure medium instead of compressed air
- The £20 sandblaster kit (venturi-style attachment) and its components
- Importance of ceramic nozzles for longevity and replaceability
- Correct orientation of the sand intake to prevent clogging
- Choice of abrasive media (glass frit vs. builder's sand vs. Karcher-branded product)
- Bucket shape optimisation for consistent sand flow
- Keeping sand dry to prevent clogs
- Sand recovery, drying, and sifting for reuse
- PPE recommendations

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glass frit** — used as the abrasive medium; effective but expensive
- **Kiln-dried builder's sand** — suggested as a cheaper alternative to test
- **Karcher-branded blasting media** — commercial product, ~£33 for a 25 kg sack; planned for comparison
- **WD-40 / light oil coating** — mentioned in comments as a post-blast rust prevention method

## Techniques and Methods
- **Wet sandblasting** — attaching a venturi-style kit to a pressure washer (Kärcher K4) to propel abrasive media with water pressure
- **Correct nozzle orientation** — keeping the sand intake tube pointing upward to prevent water from wetting and clogging the sand
- **Tall, narrow bucket setup** — using a tall thin bucket with a drilled lid to maintain a sand column around the intake spike for uninterrupted flow
- **Sand drying and sifting** — spreading used sand to dry and sifting out debris before reuse
- **PPE use** — face shield recommended to protect against backfiring wet sand and grit
