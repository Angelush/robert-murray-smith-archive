## Overview
Robert builds a low-cost DC generator by combining the gear train from a salvaged exercise bike with a printer motor, achieving approximately 12V at 2–3 amps output. The core insight is that universal motors (such as those from vacuum cleaners) require high rotational speed to generate useful power, so the multi-stage belt-and-pulley system from the exercise bike provides the necessary gear ratio. The finished unit can be converted into a wind turbine by attaching blades to the input shaft.

## Key Topics
- Why universal motors from vacuum cleaners make viable DC generators
- The electromagnetic force equation (E = BLv sinθ) and how velocity dominates output
- How series-wound motor field current and armature current interact to affect generation
- The importance of high gear ratios to make slow human (or wind) input produce useful electrical output
- Using an exercise bike's multi-stage belt-and-pulley gear train as a ready-made speed multiplier
- Choosing a printer motor over a vacuum cleaner motor for its cylindrical drum shape (easy cog integration)
- Potential conversion into a wind turbine

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Universal motor** (salvaged from discarded vacuum cleaners) — originally tested as the generator
- **Printer motor** (large cylindrical drum type) — ultimately used as the generator/cog
- **Exercise bike gear train** — provides the multi-stage speed multiplication (large pulley → small pulley → flywheel)
- **Toothed belt** (sourced from Canterbury Auto) — used to couple the flywheel to the motor
- **12V LED light strip** — used to demonstrate the generator output

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling salvaged vacuum cleaners and exercise bikes to extract usable components
- Wrapping and gluing a toothed belt around the exercise bike flywheel to create a large cog
- Using the printer motor's cylindrical drum directly as the small cog (eliminating the need to fabricate one)
- Bolting the gear train and motor together into a compact assembly with a hand-crank input
- Open-circuit voltage and ammeter testing to verify output
