## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a laser-flashed graphene supercapacitor using a cheap desktop laser and borax-treated paper. The video follows up on a previous episode (1932) about flash graphene, this time completing the supercapacitor fabrication and measurement. The key takeaway is that this process is highly accessible — almost any affordable laser will work, and the results are impressive even at small scale.

## Key Topics
- Flash graphene production using a laser (405–450 nm wavelength)
- Comparison of electrical vs. laser flashing methods
- Using borax-treated paper as the substrate for laser-induced graphene
- Interdigitated electrode patterns for supercapacitors
- Capacitance measurements with and without electrolyte
- Accessibility of the technique for home experimenters

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Kapton tape** — original substrate used for laser-induced graphene
- **Paper** — alternative substrate, but requires fire retardant treatment before lasing
- **Borax (sodium tetraborate)** — used as a fire retardant soak for paper to prevent burning during lasing
- **Aluminium strips** — used as contact probes for capacitance measurement
- **Electrolyte** (unspecified) — added via tissue paper to dramatically increase capacitance
- **Tissue paper** — used as a separator/carrier for the electrolyte

## Techniques and Methods
- **Laser-induced graphene (LIG)** — scanning a laser over a carbon-containing substrate to produce graphene in situ
- **Interdigitated electrode design** — drawing interlocking finger patterns in laser software to create electrode geometry
- **Borax soaking** — soaking paper in sodium tetraborate solution and drying overnight to make it laser-safe
- **Capacitance measurement** — using a Rigol DM3058 capacitance meter with aluminium strip contacts, tested dry and with electrolyte
