## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith and his collaborator Luke each transform donated car seats (courtesy of Aiden from Canterbury Automotive) into two distinct motorized vehicles — Robert modifies an existing tricycle by mounting the car seat onto it, while Luke builds a quad entirely from scratch. The video culminates in a race across a car park, and closes with a reflection on two valid maker approaches: modifying an existing build versus building from the ground up.

## Key Topics
- Using donated car seats as the basis for motorized vehicles
- Modification of an existing tricycle vs. scratch-building a quad
- Reuse of components from previous builds (video 1205 and video 1175)
- Ackermann steering implementation on the tricycle
- Lead-acid battery power for the drivetrain
- Bolt-on mounting for reversibility (trike can be restored)
- A head-to-head race between the two finished vehicles

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Front car seats (donated from Canterbury Automotive)
- Shopping trolley frame (used as the base chassis)
- Back axle (repurposed from a dual-differential build, video 1175)
- Tricycle front end (repurposed from the video 1205 mobility scooter)
- 8 mm steel bar (used through the swing arm)
- Lead-acid battery (mounted in a carrier under the seat)
- Steel brackets and pipe clamps (for frame attachment)

## Techniques and Methods
- Welding: cross brace, subframe, uprights, and battery carrier bracket
- Drilling holes in uprights to accept the 8 mm swing-arm bar
- Bolt-on/removable mounting of the car seat to the trike frame (preserving the original tricycle)
- Ackermann steering geometry applied to the front foot-steering system
- Component reuse and upcycling across multiple project episodes
- Scratch-build fabrication (Luke's quad, built from the ground up)
