## Overview
Robert demonstrates a DIY solar cell made by drawing lines of HB and 6B pencil on paper, which functions as a thermocouple — generating voltage from heat differences rather than light. Inspired by viewers James Ross and Alex Sania who suggested using the device from his previous video (1720) as a solar cell, he tests it outdoors using a Fresnel lens to concentrate the sun's heat, achieving measurable millivolt output. The key takeaway is that while a single junction produces little energy, hundreds could be easily drawn or printed to yield significant power.

## Key Topics
- Thermocouple principle applied to solar energy harvesting (heat, not light)
- Pencil-drawn junctions as a low-cost DIY alternative to conventional solar cells
- Comparison with copper oxide home solar cells (noted as inferior)
- Scalability: printing or drawing arrays of hundreds of junctions
- Fresnel lenses and micro lens arrays for concentrating infrared radiation
- Ongoing research into infrared/heat-based solar harvesting

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **HB pencil** — one electrode/trace of the thermocouple junction
- **6B pencil** — second electrode/trace of the thermocouple junction
- **Paper** — substrate for the drawn cell
- **Wood** — used as a cold backing surface to create a thermal differential

## Techniques and Methods
- Drawing pencil lines (HB + 6B) on paper to form a thermocouple junction
- Using a **Fresnel lens** to concentrate solar heat onto the junction
- Measuring output with a **multimeter** (voltage in millivolts)
- Proposed: **printing** multiple junctions at scale for greater output
- Proposed: **3D-printed micro lens arrays** as an alternative to Fresnel lenses
