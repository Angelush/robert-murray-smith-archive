## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates an interesting property of his teaspoon-based wind turbine: it continues to generate power across a wide range of wind angles, not just when the wind hits it head-on. Testing both outdoors and with a lab fan, he shows the turbine maintains consistent output (~10 volts) when rotated up to 60 degrees off-axis in either direction — a 120-degree effective sweep — making it well-suited for urban environments where wind direction is variable or gusty.

## Key Topics
- Overview of the spoon-disc wind turbine design and its low-cost, low-skill construction
- Outdoor testing of the turbine (vs. previous lab fan testing)
- Discovery and demonstration of the turbine's wide angular operating range
- Explanation of the 120-degree swept area (±60 degrees from axis)
- Behaviour at 180 degrees — the one orientation where the turbine stops
- Suitability for fixed installation on buildings in "confused wind" urban environments

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Plastic teaspoons (glued to a disc to form the rotor)
- A disc (rotor base)
- Glue (adhesive for teaspoon assembly)
- A motor/generator (coils salvaged from a microwave, per author comments)
- Permanent magnets (mounted on the rotor support; author notes placing them further out is advantageous)
- A cone (referenced in comments as part of the assembly)
- A resistor (used as a simple electronic load during measurement)

## Techniques and Methods
- Gluing plastic teaspoons to a disc to form turbine blades
- Testing with a controlled lab fan for repeatable measurements
- Outdoor wind testing for real-world validation
- Voltage measurement with a lab-quality rigid meter across varying wind angles
- Connecting the turbine to a resistive load to verify electrical output

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Energy physics clarification:** Gives a precise figure — at 1.2 m/s wind speed with a 16.5 cm radius, there is ~91 mW of available energy; applying the Betz limit, maximum extractable power is ~50 mW. The motor only affects conversion efficiency, not this ceiling.
- **Motor source:** The generator coils were salvaged from a microwave; he acknowledges they are not optimally specified but worked fine for the demonstration.
- **Against cowl/duct designs:** Explicitly states he dislikes the cowl approach — it adds cost and complexity, and the gain is less than simply running two turbines in series ("in a row").
- **Scaling strategy:** His preferred scale-up method is a "wind wall" — many turbines of this size arrayed together rather than building one large unit.
- **Angular range confirmation:** Directly states "well 60 degrees either way to the wind" in reply to a commenter.
- **Swivel solution:** Suggests mounting on a swivel so it never faces directly away from the wind.
- **Turgo turbine analogy:** Notes the design behaves similarly to a Turgo turbine, with air as the working fluid.
- **Magnets further out:** Agrees with a commenter that placing magnets further from the centre (now possible with the large cone) is a better configuration.
- **Open-source philosophy:** "You know me mate — I won't patent — I think ideas belong to everyone."
- **Community development:** Acknowledges he is one person and explicitly hopes others will pick up the design and develop it further.
