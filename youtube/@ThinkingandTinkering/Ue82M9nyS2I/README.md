## Overview

Robert Murray-Smith argues that science functions as a belief system — a religion that refuses to acknowledge itself as one — built on philosophical foundations that cannot be proven, primarily the assumption that matter exists and that human senses accurately perceive reality. He draws on Berkeley, Bertrand Russell, and Descartes to show that science's empirical bedrock is shakier than its practitioners admit, and further critiques the social and institutional machinery of academic science, which resists paradigm shifts and punishes unorthodox thinkers. His conclusion is that the spirit of inquiry matters more than the institutional trappings of "Science," and that anyone genuinely curious about the world is an "investigator" in the truest sense.
