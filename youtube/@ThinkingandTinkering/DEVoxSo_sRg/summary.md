## Overview
Robert converts a broken vacuum pump's induction motor into a Frenette heater — a device that uses the shearing force of oil between rotating surfaces to generate heat. He disassembles the pump, modifies the motor stator with oil circulation channels, and demonstrates that the improvised heater raises oil temperature from ~8°C to ~40°C in roughly five minutes. The main conclusion is that a salvaged motor can be adapted into a working Frenette heater with minimal effort, validating the concept before further refinement.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Frenette heater concept and how it works (shear force on oil)
- Why Frenette heaters are normally difficult to build (require precision-machined interlocking rings)
- Disassembly of a broken vacuum pump and extraction of its induction motor
- Modification of the motor stator to serve as a shear heater: adding an aluminium end plate and cutting oil circulation channels
- Bench test using a hand drill to spin the rotor in oil, with temperature monitoring
- Discussion of observed oil leaks and planned improvements (better seals, dedicated drive motor with pulley)
- Confirmation that the concept works and intention to quantify efficiency in a follow-up video

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oil** — used as the working fluid in the shear/Frenette heater; subjected to viscous shearing by the spinning rotor
- **Aluminium** — used to fabricate a cover plate to seal the open face of the stator
- **Steel laminations** — the stator core of the induction motor (salvaged component)
- **Copper windings** — part of the induction motor stator (noted during disassembly)
- **Cast iron** — material of the vacuum pump body (noted but not used in this build)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Disassembly by drifting** — knocking the motor stator out of its aluminium housing using a hammer and drift
- **Sawing and extraction** — cutting through the stator laminations to free them, then pulling from the other side
- **Oil channel cutting** — machining grooves into the stator sides to allow oil circulation around the rotor gap
- **Improvised shear mixing / Frenette heating** — spinning the rotor inside the oil-filled stator cavity to generate heat via viscous shear
- **Temperature monitoring** — using a thermocouple/temperature probe to measure oil temperature rise during the test
- **Hand drill as drive source** — coupling a cordless drill to the rotor shaft as a temporary power input
