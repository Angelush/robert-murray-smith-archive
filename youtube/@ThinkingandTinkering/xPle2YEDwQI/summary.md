## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simplified method for synthesising nickel oxyhydroxide (NiOOH), a key active material for nickel-iron battery plates. The approach improves on his earlier nickel-oxide-based method by using more accessible household chemicals. The sodium hypochlorite (bleach) acts as an oxidising agent to push the reaction beyond simple nickel hydroxide and yield the desired oxyhydroxide precipitate.

## Key Topics
- Purpose of nickel oxyhydroxide in nickel-iron (Edison) batteries
- Why this method is simpler than a previous nickel-oxide-based route
- Role of sodium hypochlorite as an oxidising agent
- Double displacement reaction chemistry
- Preparation and use of the precipitate as battery plate material

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — caustic soda / drain cleaner; 16 g dissolved in 200 ml water
- **Sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl)** — thin household bleach (≈5% solution); 150 ml added; acts as oxidising agent
- **Nickel chloride (NiCl₂ · 6H₂O)** — green nickel salt used for electroplating; 26 g in 100 ml water; the nickel source
- **Water** — solvent throughout

## Techniques and Methods
- Dissolving sodium hydroxide in water to form an alkaline base solution
- Combining NaOH solution with bleach to create an oxidising alkaline medium
- Controlled addition (with stirring) of nickel chloride solution to precipitate nickel oxyhydroxide
- Visual monitoring of precipitation (blackish-brown jelly)
- Settling, filtering, and washing the precipitate for use as battery plate material

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirms the chemicals are readily available from DIY stores or eBay.
- Clarifies that nickel chloride hexahydrate contains 6 molecules of water per molecule of NiCl₂; weighing a mole of the hexahydrate still gives one mole of nickel chloride.
- Recommends Leonard Janus's advice on "turquoise battery" information as "first class."
- Mentions he has a KOH gel electrolyte demo planned for the near future.
- Confirms quantities can be scaled by adjusting for molarity.
