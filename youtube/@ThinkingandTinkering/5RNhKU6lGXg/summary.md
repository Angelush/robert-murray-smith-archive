## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a working solenoid made from a strip of paper painted with conductive ink, wrapped around a nail. He shows it successfully magnetises the nail when powered, allowing it to pick up an unmagnetised needle. The video teases a broader goal of eventually fabricating a painted electric motor using the same conductive ink.

## Key Topics
- Constructing a solenoid from painted conductive ink on paper
- Demonstrating electromagnetic behaviour (magnetising a nail to pick up a needle)
- Troubleshooting a flawed initial demonstration (compass already magnetised)
- The potential path from a painted solenoid toward a painted motor
- Brief mention of audio equipment upgrade (Zoom H2N microphone)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Conductive ink (referred to as "our ink" / "ring" ink — likely a carbon or silver-based conductive paint sold via a linked product)
- Paper strip (substrate for the painted coil)
- Iron nail (solenoid core)
- Masking tape (used to electrically isolate the nail from the coil)
- Sewing needle (unmagnetised ferromagnetic test object)
- Compass needle (used in — and discarded from — the first demonstration attempt)

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting a conductive ink trace onto a paper strip to form a coil winding
- Wrapping the painted paper strip around a nail to create a solenoid geometry
- Using masking tape as an insulating layer between core and winding
- Demonstrating magnetisation by proximity pick-up of an unmagnetised needle rather than compass deflection
