## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines Toyota's 2023 announcement of an ammonia-powered internal combustion engine car, exploring whether it could realistically challenge electric vehicles. He focuses on a new University of Illinois Chicago paper describing a lithium-battery-based electrochemical process for producing ammonia that is green and roughly six times cheaper than competing green ammonia methods, concluding that while challenges remain, this advances both the ammonia car concept and the broader hydrogen economy.

## Key Topics
- Toyota's ammonia car announcement and the CEO's claim it would "kill" electric vehicles
- Existing uses of ammonia engines in shipping and farming
- The three core problems of ammonia as a vehicle fuel: production, safe storage/handling, and harmful exhaust emissions
- The Haber-Bosch process and its energy intensity as the dominant ammonia production method
- A new electrochemical ammonia synthesis method using a half lithium battery
- Ammonia as a hydrogen carrier/storage medium for the hydrogen economy

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ammonia (NH₃)** — the primary fuel/chemical under discussion; also proposed as a hydrogen carrier
- **Hydrogen** — feedstock for ammonia synthesis; also end-use fuel in the hydrogen economy
- **Nitrogen** — second feedstock for ammonia; obtained by liquefaction and distillation of air
- **Hydrogen sulfide** — byproduct contaminant in conventional hydrocarbon-cracking hydrogen production
- **Nitrogen dioxide (NO₂)** and **nitrous oxide (N₂O)** — harmful exhaust emissions from burning ammonia
- **Iron catalyst** — used in the traditional Haber-Bosch process
- **Cobalt oxide** — used in earlier related University of Illinois work on ammonia synthesis from wastewater nitrates, with sunlight
- **Lithium** — key component in the new electrochemical synthesis cell (half lithium battery)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Haber-Bosch process** — high temperature, high pressure synthesis of ammonia from hydrogen and nitrogen over an iron catalyst
- **Hydrocarbon cracking** — conventional commercial route for hydrogen production (noted as polluting)
- **Liquefaction and distillation of air** — industrial method for obtaining nitrogen
- **Electrochemical ammonia synthesis via lithiation** — new method using half a lithium battery cell, charged at 6 V / 100 mA per cm², in the presence of nitrogen and hydrogen to directly produce ammonia
- **Photocatalytic ammonia synthesis** — earlier University of Illinois work using cobalt oxide and sunlight to convert nitrates in wastewater to ammonia (20% yield)
- **Electrolysis of ammonia** — mentioned as a route to release hydrogen from ammonia for use as a hydrogen fuel
