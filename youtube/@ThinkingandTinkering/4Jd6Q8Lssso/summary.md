## Overview
This short follow-up video revisits Robert's homemade supercapacitor experiment, presenting energy density calculations and a direct performance comparison against a commercial supercapacitor. Despite using only a single electrode layer versus 12 in the commercial unit, his device stores more energy (2 J vs 1.82 J), and he demonstrates that the active material achieves up to 9 F/g in a compressed version. The main takeaway is that his liquid/printable graphene-based approach is competitive with commercial technology and could scale to a ream-of-paper-sized 2,000 Ah battery.

## Key Topics
- Performance comparison between homemade and commercial supercapacitors (2.7 V / 0.5 F commercial vs. 1 V / 4 F homemade)
- Energy calculations: coulombs, joules, and the formula E = ½CV²
- Gravimetric capacitance: measuring active material weight to derive farads-per-gram
- Scalability projection: ream-of-paper form factor yielding ~2,000 Ah
- Cycle life: device retains 97.4% capacity after 10,000 charge/discharge cycles
- Printable ink pathway — dip-coat method as a route to printed supercapacitors

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite/graphene active material** — used as electrode material, wiped from current collectors for weight measurement
- **Graphoil current collector leads** — noted as making no measurable contribution to capacitance
- **Electrolyte** — water-based; Robert notes in comments that water dissociates at 1.2 V, setting the effective voltage ceiling, and that the electrolyte can be modified to raise operating voltage

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of commercial supercapacitor for internal inspection
- Gravimetric analysis: wiping active material from current collectors and weighing on a microscale to calculate farads-per-gram
- Energy density calculation using E = ½CV²
- Dip-coat deposition of active material, with drying to achieve printable ink consistency
- Long-duration cycle testing (thousands of cycles over months)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Electrolyte voltage tuning:** Water dissociates at 1.2 V; adjusting the electrolyte (testing for outgassing and drying) is the way to push operating voltage higher.
- **Actual operating voltage:** Some of his devices run at 2.15 V, others at 1.2 V; the demo used 1 V for conservative calculation.
- **Scalability maths (detailed):** At 9 F/cm², on an A4 sheet with 500 double-sided cells per ream, that equals ~5.6 MF per ream → ~1,867 Ah (not 2,000 — he was rounding).
- **Version 2 in development:** Hints at a next-generation device with **4 Ah/g** energy density, weighing ~1 kg including case — which he says could give a robot like ASIMO 24,000 A at 2.5 V.
- **Solid electrolytes:** Mentions NASICON and LISICON as good options but notes they are expensive.
- **Company context:** All development is for his company Edison; not taking on outside work; beta testing underway at 3 sites.
- **Experimentation ethos:** Encourages viewers not to expect immediate results — he has run hundreds of thousands of experiments himself; sharing failed results is just as valuable.
