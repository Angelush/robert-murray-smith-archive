## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the concept of replacing mechanical springs in a Da Vinci-inspired catapult with magnets acting as a contactless spring. He demonstrates a 3D-printed "magnetic battery" — a holder that keeps two like-pole magnets in registration so their repulsive force stores energy, which is then released to fire the catapult. The core insight is that magnet repulsion behaves identically to a spring but without the fatigue and failure associated with physical contact.

## Key Topics
- History of spring-based energy storage in catapults (Da Vinci design, Roman artillery using twisted rope/sinew)
- Magnets as contactless springs — both attractive and repulsive modes
- Design and 3D printing of a magnetic energy storage device ("magnetic battery" / "magnetic spring")
- Integration of the magnetic spring into a Da Vinci catapult as a replacement for PLA leaf springs
- Ratchet-and-pawl mechanism for storing and releasing energy

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA (polylactic acid)** — used for 3D printing the catapult and magnetic spring housing
- **Neodymium/permanent magnets** — two magnets with like poles facing each other to provide repulsive spring force
- **Rope and sinew (historical reference)** — twisted materials used in Roman torsion artillery

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D modelling in Tinkercad** — designing the magnetic spring housing and catapult components
- **FDM 3D printing** — fabricating the housing, gears, and catapult parts in PLA
- **Magnet pole alignment** — orienting like poles to face each other to achieve repulsive spring behaviour
- **Gear and rack mechanism** — using a rack-and-pinion to cock the catapult arm
- **Ratchet and pawl** — holding stored energy until release
- **Gluing components** — securing the gear to the drum using adhesive through alignment holes
