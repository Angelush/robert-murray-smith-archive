## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history and chemistry of Sorel cements — a family of non-Portland cements invented roughly 150 years ago — demonstrating several practical recipes using magnesium oxide and zinc oxide as alternatives to conventional cement. He explains why these materials are gaining renewed interest due to the significant CO₂ emissions of Portland cement production, and walks through their properties, limitations (mainly water sensitivity), and newer water-resistant variants. The main takeaway is that workable, low-carbon cements can be made at home from cheap, widely available materials.

## Key Topics
- Fundamental difference between cements (crystal growth and interlocking) and geopolymers (polymerization reaction)
- CO₂ problem with Portland cement manufacturing and motivation for alternatives
- History of Sorel cements (~150 years old, attributed to chemist Sorel)
- Zinc oxide + zinc chloride as the original Sorel cement
- Magnesium oxide + magnesium chloride cement (historical use: billiard balls)
- Magnesium oxide + magnesium sulfate (Epsom salts) cement
- Water sensitivity as the shared weakness of chloride- and sulfate-based Sorel cements
- Phosphate-based MgO cements as a water-resistant alternative, and the phosphate scarcity concern
- Boric acid as a retarder for phosphate cements
- Newer MgO cement variants: MgO + magnesium carbonate, activated MgO (surface-hydroxylated), and MgO + fumed silica
- Setting time control through water content

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zinc oxide (ZnO)** — powder; original Sorel cement base; also used in suntan cream and paints; cheap and widely available
- **Zinc chloride (ZnCl₂)** — used as saturated solution to activate zinc oxide cement; causes very fast set at high concentrations
- **Magnesium oxide (MgO)** — primary cement base for most recipes discussed; white powder
- **Magnesium chloride (MgCl₂)** — activator for MgO cement; same leaching problem as zinc chloride version
- **Magnesium sulfate (Epsom salts, MgSO₄)** — used at 40% by weight with 60% MgO; water-activated dry blend
- **Ammonium phosphate** — insoluble phosphate hardener for water-resistant MgO cement
- **Potassium phosphate** — alternative insoluble phosphate hardener; the widely known internet recipe
- **Boric acid** — added to phosphate/MgO mixes as a set retarder to improve workability
- **Magnesium carbonate** — used in a newer MgO cement formulation with water
- **Fumed silica (SiO₂)** — silicon dioxide used at 10–40% with MgO; large surface area improves bonding; mixed with water into a gel before combining with MgO
- **Calcium oxide (CaO)** — basis of Portland cement; mentioned for contrast, not used in demonstrations
- **Water** — activator and set-time controller in most recipes

## Techniques and Methods
- **Saturated solution preparation** — dissolving zinc chloride in water until no more dissolves, to create the activating liquid
- **Pre-wetting the oxide powder** — dampening ZnO or MgO to a cream before adding the chloride solution, to extend working time from seconds to ~1–2 hours
- **Ratio control for set time** — using 5–10% chloride solution for slow set; up to 40% gives near-instant set
- **Dry blending** — mixing MgO and Epsom salts powders (60:40 by weight) in a blender to produce a fine dry mix, then activating with water
- **Fumed silica gel preparation** — pre-mixing fumed silica with water into a thick gel to make it handleable before combining with MgO (10–40% SiO₂ to 60–90% MgO)
- **Casting and curing** — pouring paste into moulds; initial set 1–4 hours depending on recipe; full strength over 7 days
- **Shrinkage measurement (from comments)** — casting a bar, measuring wet and dry dimensions to quantify shrinkage

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Epoxy coating** reduces leaching from water-sensitive Sorel cements to near zero, making them more practical in damp environments.
- **MgO bonds extraordinarily well to hemp**, suggesting composite applications.
- **Sorel cements bond very well to organics and carbon structures** broadly; he encourages experimentation with composite additions (e.g. MCF fibres), noting the combinations are vast and shrinkage should be individually measured (wet vs. dry bar test).
- **Geopolymers vs. cements distinction**: silicate-based work is more accurately geopolymer (chemical reaction) rather than cement (physical crystal interaction) — an important clarification for anyone exploring silicate routes.
- **Seawater use** is problematic even for phosphate versions, as chloride ion concentration means diffusion and leaching would still likely occur.
- **Talc** (magnesium silicate) is worth trying as a silica source.
- **China is the world's greatest producer of magnesium oxide**, relevant to sourcing and supply chain.
- **Portland cement itself** takes ~2 weeks of kept-damp curing to properly achieve full set — noting the comparison with Sorel cement curing times is fairer than it first appears.
- **Casting and detail holding**: MgO cements will cast and hold fine detail, which he found interesting for moulding applications.
- He acknowledged not knowing the zinc oxide formula was still in active use and found that interesting when a commenter pointed it out.
