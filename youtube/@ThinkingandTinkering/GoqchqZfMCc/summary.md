## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a graphite-based conductive ink he formulated specifically for electroplating organic materials such as leaves, flowers, wood, bone, and paper. The ink is designed to adhere to irregular organic surfaces, remain conductive under electroplating bath conditions, and preserve fine surface detail like leaf veins. He demonstrates the full process — from painting the ink onto a rhododendron leaf to copper-plating it in a simple copper sulfate bath.

## Key Topics
- The challenge of formulating an ink suitable for electroplating organic substrates
- Ink composition and its required properties (adhesion, conductivity, bath resistance, surface conformity)
- Application technique: multiple thin coats at 90° angles
- Achieving target resistance (~150–200 ohms) after three coats
- Burnishing for improved conductivity and metallic sheen
- Setting up a basic copper electroplating bath at home
- Demonstration on a rhododendron leaf and a bird skull
- Sale of the ink (now discontinued; DIY formulation videos made instead)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — primary conductive filler in the ink
- **Triglycerides / alkyds** — binder components in the ink formulation
- **Polyvinyl acetate (PVA)** — binder component
- **Water** — solvent/carrier
- **Sodium chloride (NaCl)** — additive in the ink
- **Polyethylene glycol (PEG)** — additive in the ink
- **Copper sulfate** — electroplating bath (80 g per litre of water)
- **Copper wire / copper pipe** — used as the sacrificial anode
- **Neodymium (neo) magnets** — used as a neat trick to connect D-cell batteries in series; noted to be nickel-coated

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting conductive ink in multiple thin coats, each at 90° to the previous, to build up uniform conductivity
- Resistance testing with a multimeter to confirm adequate conductivity (~150–200 ohms target)
- Burnishing the dried ink layer to achieve a metallic sheen and lower resistance
- Basic copper electroplating: copper sulfate bath, sacrificial copper anode (positive), coated object as cathode (negative)
- Using a 12V/500mA wall adapter or two D-cells in series (connected via neodymium magnets) as the power supply
- Swirling the object in the bath to promote even deposition

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Coat count:** Suggests around 12 coats for best results ("slow and steady will get a good result").
- **Conductive fillers:** Confirms graphite, zinc flake, copper nanoparticles, and graphene can all be used; graphite is recommended as the easiest starting point.
- **Waterproofing:** For outdoor/wet use, a waterproof binder (e.g. polyurethane) is needed rather than the standard formulation.
- **Commercial ink discontinued:** He no longer sells the ink and directs people to his DIY formulation videos instead.
- **Preparing objects:** To coat something (e.g. fabric), simply soak it in the conductive ink, dry it, and electroplate.
- **Durability:** The ink is in a polymer matrix so it doesn't shed or flake under normal decorative use, but would fail under sustained mechanical stress (e.g. continuously flexing rubber bushings).
- **Graphene/graphite additives:** Additives affect conductivity significantly and help prevent graphene restacking, but must be balanced carefully.
- **Paper electronics:** Suitable for paper-based electronics, but not recommended for supercapacitors due to insufficient surface area.
- **Power capacity:** Notes a 2 kW wall heater was made using the ink, demonstrating significant current-carrying capability.
- **Guarding the full formulation:** Admits he kept certain details of the ink private to prevent commercial exploitation, calling it a thin line between helping and being foolish.
