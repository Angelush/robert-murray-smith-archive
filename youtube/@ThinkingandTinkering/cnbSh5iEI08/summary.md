## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple wood tongue drum in approximately two hours using recycled pine bed slats as keys and a repurposed speaker box as the resonator body. The video emphasizes accessibility and low cost, showing that a functional musical instrument can be made from salvaged materials with basic tools. The key design insight is using individually bolt-on keys, which simplifies both construction and tuning.

## Key Topics
- Sourcing free materials from a discarded wooden bed
- Repurposing a speaker cabinet as the drum body
- Designing and cutting eight keys (one octave) from pine bed slats
- Creating bearing blocks with embedded nuts for bolt-on key attachment
- Cutting a rebate channel in keys to improve resonance in cheaper timber
- Tuning approach: removing/filing material from individual keys
- Trade-offs between rebate cuts and slot-based sliding tuning systems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Pine bed slats — primary material for tone keys, salvaged from a discarded double bed
- Speaker cabinet/box — repurposed as the resonator body
- 8 mm bolts (decorative) with repair washers — used to mount keys to bearing blocks
- 8 mm drill bit — for through-holes in keys and bearing blocks
- 13 mm drill bit — for counterbore to recess nuts below the surface
- Wood glue — for fixing bearing blocks inside the box
- Screws — added to reinforce the older/rickety speaker cabinet

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting around knots in pine to obtain clear, structurally sound timber
- Drilling a two-stage counterbore (8 mm through + 13 mm shallow) to embed nuts flush
- Hammering nuts into counterbores to create reusable threaded fastening points
- Cutting a rebate channel along the underside of each key to improve vibration in low-quality timber
- Sizing keys by marking the midpoint, then incrementing each key by 1 cm to achieve different pitches across an octave
- Tuning by sanding, filing, or cutting material from individual keys to raise pitch
- Painting the interior black to conceal blemishes (suggested, not demonstrated)
