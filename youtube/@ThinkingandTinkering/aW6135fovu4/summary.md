## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the O-Wind omnidirectional wind turbine, applying a four-point framework for evaluating new wind turbine claims. Using criteria borrowed from renewable energy advocate Paul Gipe (windworks.org), he finds the O-Wind lacking in research backing, relevant team experience, independent validation, and real performance data, and concludes that prospective investors should wait for verifiable results before committing to the company's upcoming crowdfunding campaign.

## Key Topics
- Robert's personal enthusiasm for DIY wind generation
- The "wild west" problem of unverified wind turbine claims in media
- Paul Gipe's four-point framework for assessing wind turbine credibility
- Application of the framework to the O-Wind turbine specifically
- The importance of distinguishing genuine innovation from hype or carnival-style promotion
- The distinction between a turbine spinning in wind vs. actually generating electrical energy efficiently

## Techniques and Methods
- Google Scholar searches to check for peer-reviewed backing behind press claims
- LinkedIn profile review to assess inventor/founder experience
- Press release analysis to identify hype vs. substance
- Wind tunnel testing (noted as something O-Wind has done, but without output data)
- Four-point credibility framework applied as a structured assessment tool:
  1. Hype check (press coverage vs. research papers)
  2. Team experience check
  3. Third-party validation check
  4. Presence of real performance data vs. glossy marketing
