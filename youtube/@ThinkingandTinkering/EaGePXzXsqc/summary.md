## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple method for reclaiming metallic silver from old photographic film negatives using a concentrated sodium hydroxide solution. The process dissolves the gelatin and dye layers coating the film, releasing the silver as a metallic precipitate that can be collected by filtration. While the yield per film is small, it adds up if you have large quantities of old negatives.

## Key Topics
- Silver recovery from photographic film negatives
- Chemistry of photographic film layer structure
- Preparation and use of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution
- Filtering and collecting the resulting silver precipitate
- Reusability of the sodium hydroxide bath

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — oven/drain cleaner; used as the stripping solution at ~1–1.5 mol concentration (8–12 g per ~200 mL water)
- **Photographic film negatives** — source material; composed of an acetate or plastic base, a gelatin layer containing silver salts, and dye layers also containing silver salts
- **Silver / silver salts** — the target product, precipitated as a metallic silver compound
- **Gelatin** — coating on the film that dissolves in the NaOH solution
- **Filter paper** — used to collect the silver precipitate

## Techniques and Methods
- Dissolving photographic film in a concentrated NaOH solution (exothermic reaction — caution required when adding water to NaOH)
- Stirring the film strips in the solution until the coating clears from the plastic base
- Visual inspection to confirm complete stripping (film turns clear, solution goes cloudy)
- Gravity filtration through filter paper to isolate the metallic silver precipitate
- Reusing the NaOH bath repeatedly until it is saturated
