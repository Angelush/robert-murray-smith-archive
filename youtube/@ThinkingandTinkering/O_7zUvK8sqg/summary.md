## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a microwave turntable synchronous motor to examine its unconventional coil-around-magnet arrangement, then scales up the concept using a hand-wound 500-foot coil mounted in a repurposed exercise bike frame. He demonstrates that the single-coil design does generate measurable voltage (up to ~1.3V) and current (~1.5mA), concluding that while output is modest, the simplified single-coil construction has real potential as a more economical alternative to multi-coil generator designs.

## Key Topics
- Internal construction of a microwave turntable synchronous motor
- Unconventional coil geometry: coil looped *around* the magnet rather than facing it
- Scaling the motor's coil arrangement into a larger generator
- "Sufficiency vs. efficiency" philosophy for DIY energy projects
- Single-coil generator output testing and limitations
- Design improvements needed (lower resistance, tighter coil-to-magnet gap)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Microwave turntable synchronous motor (synchronous motor, ~4–5 RPM)
- 500 feet of 38 SWG (Standard Wire Gauge) magnet wire
- Blue and red plastic bobbin (hand-fabricated)
- Permanent magnets (all north-facing orientation)
- Repurposed exercise bike frame

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of a synchronous motor (removing tabs, cover, gears, and base plate)
- Hand-winding a single-coil bobbin (500 ft of 38 SWG wire)
- Mounting a rotor magnet assembly into a modified exercise bike as a test rig
- Measuring open-circuit voltage and current output with a multimeter while hand-spinning the rotor
