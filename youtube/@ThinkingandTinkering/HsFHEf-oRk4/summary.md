## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces harmonic drives (strain wave drives), explaining their history, operating principles, and applications, before showcasing his own 3D-printed split ring epicyclic gear — a variation that overcomes some of the traditional harmonic drive's limitations. The key takeaway is that by using two ring gears with a small tooth count difference and slightly different moduli, a massive gear reduction can be achieved in a compact, printable design.

## Key Topics
- History of harmonic/strain wave drives (developed by C.W. Musser in the 1950s)
- Operating principles of harmonic drives: wave generator, flex spline, and circular spline
- Advantages of harmonic drives: high torque density, zero backlash, compactness, precision
- Disadvantages: manufacturing complexity, limited angular range, lower efficiency, flex spline fatigue/cracking
- The split ring epicyclic gear as a solution to those disadvantages
- Gear tooth count design: 18-tooth sun, 21-tooth planets, 57-tooth and 60-tooth ring gears (3-tooth difference)
- Use of slightly different gear moduli (2.0 vs 1.98) to allow the two ring gears to mesh with the same planets
- Massive gear reduction achievable with this design
- 3D printability and practical demonstration
- Use of animation in the video (Robert soliciting viewer feedback on it)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Skateboard bearings (22 mm × 8 mm × 7 mm thick) used for the central drive shaft

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM) of the gear assembly, designed in Tinkercad
- Files shared on Thingiverse for community use
- Planetary gear arrangement with three planets at 120° spacing
- Exploiting modulus mismatch (mod 2.0 vs mod 1.98) to force two ring gears of different tooth counts to share the same planet gears
- Holding one ring gear as the fixed base plate while driving the sun gear to produce high-ratio output from the second ring gear
