## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history and practical relevance of water motors — hydraulic engines powered by mains water pressure rather than flow — drawing from model engineering books published in the late 1800s to early 1900s. He builds a replica based on the Whitney Motor Company design (c.1900–1910) as a demonstration of turbine-based water engine construction. His main takeaway is that these forgotten technologies offer surprisingly relevant solutions for modern concerns like off-grid power generation and emergency battery charging.

## Key Topics
- History of water motors, dating back to the 1600s, with peak popularity in the late 19th and early 20th centuries
- Comparison between Victorian-era energy concerns and modern ones (power supply, battery charging)
- How water motors work: using pressure head rather than flow (unlike water wheels)
- UK mains water pressure then vs. now (~30–40 PSI then, ~15–30 PSI now)
- The Whitney Motor Company and its turbine-based water engines (~quarter horsepower)
- Alternative water turbine designs: Pelton wheels and Tesla turbines
- Practical applications: historical use in railways, factories, Miele's first washing machine, electricity generation
- Potential modern application: attaching a water motor to a NEMA stepper motor as an emergency battery charger
- The value of revisiting historical engineering solutions alongside modern developments like TENG generators

## Techniques and Methods
- Building a turbine-based water motor modelled on the Whitney Motor Company design
- 3D printing Pelton wheel buckets (with note that printed versions should be used as molds for metal casting due to pressure requirements)
- Coupling a water motor to a NEMA stepper motor for electricity generation
- Using an expansion tank filled with air to handle water hammer (back pressure from sudden valve closure)
- Pumped/elevated water storage as a head-of-pressure energy reservoir
