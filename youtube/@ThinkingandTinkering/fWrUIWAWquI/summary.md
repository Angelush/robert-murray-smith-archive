## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith covers the promise of deep geothermal energy and the startup Quaise Energy, which is using gyrotron technology originally developed for nuclear fusion at MIT to drill 20 km into the Earth's crust. The video explains the four types of geothermal energy, the historical challenges of deep drilling, and why Quaise's approach — melting rock rather than mechanically cutting through it — could be a breakthrough. The key takeaway is that Quaise's $21 million funding round, backed by Mitsubishi, marks a significant step toward making deep geothermal a viable part of the 21st-century energy mix.

## Key Topics
- The enormous theoretical energy potential of geothermal heat (0.1% of Earth's heat = 20 million years of human energy needs)
- The four types of geothermal energy: natural hot springs, enhanced geothermal, advanced (closed-loop) geothermal, and super hot rock geothermal
- Historical deep-drilling failures: the Kola Borehole (Russia, 1970s) and German ocean-floor attempt (1980s)
- The gyrotron: a millimetre-wave energy device from nuclear fusion research repurposed for rock drilling
- Quaise Energy (spun out of MIT's Plasma Research/Fusion group in 2018) and its goal to drill 20 km in ~100 days
- Supercritical water as a high-energy-density working fluid
- Quaise's $21 million funding round and Mitsubishi backing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Supercritical water** — the intended working fluid at 20 km depth (~500°C, very high pressure), already used industrially in coal plants; offers a quantum leap in energy density over normal steam
- **Cement / vitreous lining** — used to case conventional boreholes against collapsing porous rock; a major cost driver at depth
- **Binary fluid / single fluid** — used in closed-loop advanced geothermal heat-exchange systems

## Techniques and Methods
- **Gyrotron millimetre-wave drilling** — fires megawatt-power beams to melt/vaporise rock, ~100× faster than mechanical drilling; forms a self-sealing glassy tube as it advances
- **Enhanced geothermal** — drill into porous rock, inject water, extract steam via a second borehole
- **Advanced (closed-loop) geothermal** — circulate a working fluid through a sealed pipe loop sunk into hot rock; no water injection required
- **Natural hydrothermal extraction** — exploit existing near-surface hot springs and steam vents (e.g., Iceland, California, Yellowstone)
- **Supercritical water cycle** — extract heat at supercritical conditions for higher thermodynamic efficiency
