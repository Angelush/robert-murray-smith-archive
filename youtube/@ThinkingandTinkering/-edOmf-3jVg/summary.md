## Overview
This video explores the historical use of animals in warfare, from ancient times through the mid-20th century. Robert surveys a range of real military programs that attempted to weaponise animals as delivery systems for explosives or as guided weapons, most of which ended in failure. The central takeaway is that while necessity drove many creative and desperate ideas during wartime, animals proved too unpredictable and unreliable as military assets, and technology ultimately offered a more practical alternative.

## Key Topics
- General history of animals in warfare (horses, dogs, pigeons, elephants, dolphins, sea lions)
- Soviet anti-tank dogs (1930s–1943): training, deployment, and catastrophic failure
- Japanese "demolition wolves": similar concept targeting bunkers, terminated December 1943
- American bat bombs (Project X-Ray, 1943–1944): incendiary bats targeting Japanese cities
- Historical animal bomb carriers (oxen, donkeys, mules, pigs, monkeys — including pitch-and-fire tactics)
- Project Pigeon / Project Orcon: B.F. Skinner's pigeon-guided bombs
- Revival of pigeon use by the US Coast Guard (1979) for sea search-and-rescue
- Ethical debates and practical limitations of using animals in warfare

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Explosives (strapped to dogs and other animals)
- Incendiary devices (miniature waistcoat-mounted timers worn by bats)
- Pitch (used to coat animals set alight as incendiary weapons)

## Techniques and Methods
- Operant conditioning (B.F. Skinner's reward-based training for pigeon-guided bombs)
- Hunger-based conditioning (Soviet anti-tank dogs trained with food placed under tanks)
- Impact detonation (replacing the original drop-and-retreat mechanism for tank dogs)
- Timed incendiary release (bat bombs dropped at dawn to exploit roosting behaviour)
- Visual target tracking by pigeons (pecking at windows in a bomb nose cone to steer it toward ships)
- Pigeon-assisted aerial search (US Coast Guard, 1979, spotting orange debris at sea)
