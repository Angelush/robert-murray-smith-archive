## Overview
Robert salvages two discarded vacuum cleaners found on the roadside and extracts their series-wound universal motors. He demonstrates that these motors function on DC power, then builds a simple proof-of-concept hovercraft by mounting one motor in a large mixing bowl to act as a plenum chamber. The experiment succeeds, showing that a vacuum cleaner motor alone is sufficient to lift and float the platform.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging and disassembling discarded vacuum cleaners
- Identifying and testing series-wound universal motors
- Principle of plenum chambers in hovercraft design
- Proof-of-concept construction of a hoverboard/hovercraft from scavenged parts
- Potential to scale up to a rideable hoverboard

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Two vacuum cleaners (one Vax, one Hoover brand) — salvaged from the roadside
- Series-wound universal motors (one per vacuum cleaner; the Vax had a separate DC brush motor plus a vacuum motor)
- Large plastic mixing bowl — used as the plenum chamber
- Storm sealant — used to seal the motor mount and wire feed-through
- DC power supply (tested at approximately 30 V)
- Solder and wires

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of vacuum cleaners (locating and removing screws, gentle persuasion with a hammer)
- Motor continuity/function testing using a bench DC power supply
- Reversing motor direction by swapping DC polarity
- Cutting/drilling a hole in the mixing bowl for motor fitment
- Sealing wire feed-throughs with storm sealant
- Hovercraft plenum chamber construction and hover test
