## Overview
This is the second video in Robert Murray-Smith's kiln-free pottery series, focused entirely on the specific recipe and method for making a geopolymer modelling material from cat litter, sodium silicate, and magnesium oxide. He demonstrates mixing and sculpting a small face mask, then curing it in a household oven at 180°C, with the finished piece coming out rock hard. He also shares an insight that this material may be useful to traditional potters as a pre-drying stabiliser, potentially eliminating pot explosions during kiln firing.

## Key Topics
- How to identify the correct type of cat litter to use (clumping/clay-based only)
- The full recipe with proportions for each ingredient
- Mixing and kneading technique to produce a workable dough-like material
- Sculpting a face mask as a demonstration piece
- Oven curing vs kiln firing
- The material's nature as a geopolymer rather than vitrified clay
- Potential use of the recipe as a clay pre-drying/stabilising step for traditional kiln pottery

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Cat litter (clumping, clay-based)** — 600 g; used as the clay source; must be clumping type (not chalk/quartz or wood-based varieties)
- **Bentonite** — mentioned as one type of clay found in some cat litters; produces a lighter grey colour
- **Sodium silicate / sodium metasilicate** — 120 g powder, made up to a 40% by-weight solution in 500 mL water; acts as the geopolymer binder; polymerises to silica on curing
- **Magnesium oxide** — stated as 500 g during mixing (03:41) and restated as 600 g in the closing recap (07:40); used as a filler/thickener, analogous to flour in bread dough
- **Chalk and quartz sand** — found in non-clumping lightweight cat litters; explicitly to be avoided
- **MDF board** — used as a slightly absorbent, non-stick working surface
- **Water** — 500 mL, used to make up the sodium silicate solution

## Techniques and Methods
- Selecting the correct cat litter by reading the label for "clumping" or "clay"
- Pouring the sodium silicate solution over the dry cat litter granules and allowing full absorption
- Hand-picking stones from cheaper cat litter grades (or wet-sieving as an alternative)
- Kneading magnesium oxide powder into the wet clay until evenly distributed
- Storing unused material in a sealed plastic bag to prevent air drying
- Sculpting by hand using a short pipe section as a former/mould
- Oven curing at 180°C for 1 hour (he extended from a planned 30 minutes)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Chemistry**: The sodium silicate polymerises to silica during curing — this is the hardening mechanism.
- **Temperature tolerance**: He estimates the material may withstand up to ~600°C, but advises checking Google Scholar to confirm before relying on it.
- **Sodium silicate ratio**: The 40% solution is fairly standard; the critical factor is the ratio of silicate to clay, not the volume of water. The workable range is roughly 10–40%, so precision is not critical.
- **Used vs unused cat litter**: Probably makes no practical difference, though people may be squeamish about using used litter.
- **Magnesium silicate as a substitute**: Would be very difficult to use — it is essentially talcum powder and insoluble.
- **Making sodium silicate from scratch**: Possible by mixing with sodium hydroxide and heating; he acknowledges there are YouTube videos on this.
- **Finishing**: Recommends varnishing the cured piece.
- **Sand casting**: Notes that sodium silicate is already used for sand casting metals.
- **Hydrocal**: Describes it as roughly 95% plaster of Paris with a small amount of OPC (ordinary Portland cement).
- **Used cat litter and batteries**: Responded to a joking comment by noting it actually might work as a battery component — a speculative but genuine aside.
