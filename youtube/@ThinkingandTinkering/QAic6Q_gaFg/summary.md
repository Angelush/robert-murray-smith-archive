## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits Deal Castle (built 1539, Kent Coast) to argue that engineering is far more than the textbook definition — it is the application of science, mathematics, logic, politics, economics, and psychology to solve constrained real-world problems. Using Henry VIII's fortress as a case study, he demonstrates how cost, speed, available materials, and social context drove every design decision. He concludes that medieval castles are not only historical artefacts but a source of practical ideas directly applicable to modern engineering challenges.

## Key Topics
- Defining engineering: broadening the standard definition to include history, social context, and human behaviour
- Deal Castle as a canonical engineering case study
- Henry VIII's strategic and financial constraints driving design choices
- The trade-offs of iron versus gunmetal cannons
- Repurposing local and stolen materials to minimise cost
- Medieval technologies (treadle crane, inglenook fireplaces, turnspits, worm gears, pumps) as sources of modern innovation
- Castles as "a window on the past and a doorway to the future"

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Iron** — used for Henry VIII's cannons instead of gunmetal; cheaper and faster to produce but less durable
- **Gunmetal** — the conventional cannon material of the period, more expensive
- **Beach pebbles** — used in castle walls and floors as a low-cost local aggregate
- **Beach sand** — mixed with lime to form mortar
- **Lime** — locally acquired, used as the binding agent in mortar
- **Dressed stone (robbed masonry)** — sourced from dissolved monasteries for the castle's facing
- **Copper** — added to silver coins by Henry VIII to debase the currency and raise funds
- **Silver** — debased by Henry VIII as a financing strategy

## Techniques and Methods
- **Low-profile castle design** — sunken layout to reduce vulnerability to cannon fire
- **Circular tower arrangement** — six towers in concentric circles for full 360° defensive coverage
- **Sloped cannon floors** — angled flooring so recoil rolled guns back into firing position automatically, reducing reload time to ~4 minutes with 3–5 crew
- **Naval guns on wheeled trucks** — adapted for land fortification reuse
- **Treadle (hamster-wheel) crane** — medieval construction lifting technology
- **Low-ceiling room construction** — passive heat retention strategy
- **Inglenook fireplaces** — efficient heat distribution
- **Turnspits** — mechanical roasting mechanisms
- **Drawbridge cantilever and worm gear** — mechanical advantage for raising/lowering the gate
- **Onsite water pumps** — self-supply infrastructure within the castle
