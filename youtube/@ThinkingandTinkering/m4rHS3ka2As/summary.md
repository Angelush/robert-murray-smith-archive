## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a DIY marine alcohol stove from cheap cake tins and kitchen items purchased for around £8 total — a fraction of the $300+ price of commercial marine stoves. He walks through the full fabrication process using basic metalworking tools, producing a functional stove that burns methylated spirits or ethanol. The video also introduces a follow-up challenge: adapting the same stove design to run on waste cooking oil for use in a tent.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: commercial marine stoves cost $300+ but are mechanically simple
- Sourcing cheap materials from a local store (total ~£8)
- Step-by-step construction of the stove body and burner
- Role of mineral wool as a fuel-absorbing medium (prevents fuel sloshing at sea)
- Fuel options for alcohol stoves
- Extinguishing the flame with a simple metal flap
- Preview of a follow-up project: a multi-purpose stove for tent use running on waste cooking oil

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **20cm cake tin (250g, £2.50)** — forms the main stove top/shroud
- **20cm sponge/sandwich tins (£1 each, ×3)** — used for upper and lower stove sections
- **Metal trivet (£1.50)** — repurposed as the cooking grid/shroud frame; stainless steel
- **Milk pan (£3.50)** — used as the lower base/fuel section; has stove paint finish (not Teflon)
- **Expanded metal lath (stainless steel)** — cut to a circle and placed on top of mineral wool inside the burner
- **Mineral wool (Minera / kiln wool)** — packed into the burner to absorb and hold fuel, preventing slosh; Robert notes glass fibre, rock wool, or cotton can substitute
- **Methylated spirits / ethanol / denatured alcohol** — primary fuels for the stove
- **Waste cooking oil** — mentioned as target fuel for the follow-up tent stove variant
- **Pop rivets** — for fastening the expanded metal and sections together
- **M4 nuts and bolts** — used to fix and align the top section to the base

## Techniques and Methods
- **Drilling** — pilot holes followed by ~40mm holes in cake tins for airflow and burner openings
- **Metal bending / forming** — bending the trivet grid at 90° for a tight fit; folding over cut tin edges using hammer and former
- **Edge rounding/rolling** — tapping sharp cut edges over using a hammer and lump of metal to prevent injury and stiffen the plate
- **Angle grinding** — cutting the stainless steel expanded metal lath to a circle
- **Pop riveting** — securing the mesh cap and internal components
- **Bolting** — four M4 nuts and bolts used as alignment pegs between stove sections
- **Force fitting** — slightly undersized tin hammered in with a wood block to create a tight spring-fit joint
- **Handle removal** — cutting the milk pan handle and flattening the remaining aluminium tabs

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On gimbals:** Most commercial marine stoves don't include gimbals — this is deliberate so manufacturers can also sell them to campers. The gimbal is usually part of the cabinetwork the stove fits into, essentially just a metal-lined swinging tray. He says he has never seen a double gimbal arrangement.
- **On the Origo stove:** Confirms the design is "basically a copy of the Origo — a very nice stove."
- **On mineral wool safety:** Deflects to the boating industry as authority — it is standard marine use material.
- **On pan coatings:** The tins have stove paint, not Teflon. Plain steel pans with no coating can be sourced if preferred. The trivet is stainless steel.
- **On a thermocouple:** Agreed one could be added to the design.
- **On tent stove follow-up:** Specifies it would need to be "a tent stove vented to the outside."
- **On fuel choice:** Simply advises "burn clean fuel."
- **On a Dutch stove variant:** Points viewers to his earlier Dutch stove video as a related design that could be adapted for similar purposes.
- **On rolling/folding edges:** Notes that rolling edges also stiffens the sheet metal, which he considers a secondary benefit.
- **On arguments about fuel/design choices:** Humorously declines to enter debates, noting both camps make valid points and it ultimately comes down to personal preference.
