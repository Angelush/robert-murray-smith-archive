## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a "green" (non-toxic) method for reducing graphene oxide back to graphene using vitamin C (ascorbic acid) instead of the conventional but hazardous hydrazine. The process was inspired by his earlier work reducing copper oxide to copper metal with ascorbic acid. The result is a stable black colloidal dispersion of graphene in water suitable for further experiments.

## Key Topics
- Why graphene oxide must be reduced to graphene for practical use
- Hazards of the standard hydrazine reduction method and the motivation for a safer alternative
- Ascorbic acid as a green reducing agent for graphene oxide
- Role of ammonia in preventing agglomeration and maintaining colloidal stability
- Two heating methods: ultrasonic bath vs. hot water bath

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide solution** — starting material (~200 mL), golden-brown in colour, produced in a previous experiment
- **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)** — reducing agent, added at approximately one teaspoon per 200 mL
- **Ammonia (household, 5–10%)** — added at 2 mL per 200 mL (100:1 ratio) to prevent clumping and aid the reaction
- **Hydrazine** — the conventional reducing agent; mentioned only as a dangerous, restricted alternative to avoid
- **Polyethylene glycol (1%)** — mentioned in comments as a dispersant to prevent graphene restacking
- **Isopropanol (1%)** — mentioned in comments alongside PEG as a dispersant

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ultrasonic bath sonication** — 50 W sonic bath, run for ~1 hour to drive the reduction reaction with heat
- **Hot water bath** — alternative to sonication; water heated to 80 °C, sample held for ~1 hour
- **Colloidal dispersion** — graphene is produced as a colloidal dispersion (not a true solution); Tyndall test noted in comments as a way to confirm this
- **Vacuum filtration + heat treatment** — mentioned in comments (200 °C, 1 hour, flow-direction filtering) as a post-processing route to thin or thick films

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Colloidal dispersion, not a solution:** Robert clarifies this is a colloidal dispersion (verifiable by Tyndall test), not a true solution. If reduced at too high a concentration, the graphene restacks to graphite almost immediately.
- **Dispersants for stability:** To prevent restacking, he sometimes adds 1% polyethylene glycol and 1% isopropanol, combined with dilution and pH balance to maintain the colloidal nature via surface charges on the graphene sheets.
- **Concentration matters:** If the concentration is too high, agglomeration is visibly observable.
- **Post-processing into films/sponges:** Vacuum filtration of a well-dispersed solution followed by heating to 200 °C for 1 hour (flow-direction filtering preferred) can produce thin films, thick films, or sponges with interesting properties.
