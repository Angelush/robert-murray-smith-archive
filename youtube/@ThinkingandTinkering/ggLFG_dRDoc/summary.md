## Overview
Robert provides a progress update on his structured carbon battery project, focusing on a key improvement: switching from polyvinyl acetate binder to a 70:30 carboxymethyl cellulose/styrene-butadiene rubber (CMC/SBR) blend — the same binder system used in commercial lithium iron batteries. This change produces flexible, tough, highly conductive electrode plates that are self-supporting, eliminating the need for metal current collectors and reducing battery weight, size, and cost by at least a third. The update also outlines the next milestone: scaling up to Group 27 battery format, with a goal of ten such units being sufficient to power a car.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of the previous polyvinyl acetate binder and reasons for switching
- CMC/SBR binder system as a DIY-accessible alternative to commercial battery binders
- Self-supporting, flexible, freestanding battery electrode plates
- Elimination of current collectors from the battery stack
- Transition from button cell testing to Group 27 format
- Plans for roll-to-roll industrial printing of electrode plates
- Target specifications: ~5 kWh at 12 V, ~10 kg per Group 27 unit
- Independent third-party verification (Warwick University and ORNL mentioned in comments)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) / polyvinyl acetate** — previous binder; easily available as white wood glue but not optimal
- **Carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC)** — new binder component; available as "Solvite" concrete additive from DIY stores; confirmed in comments as the same product
- **Styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR)** — new binder component; sold as a concrete flexibiliser at DIY stores
- **Active material** — carbon-based electrode material (hydrothermal carbonisation product; see separate video series)
- **Electrolyte** — mentioned as part of final assembly; composition not specified in this video
- **Separator** — one of the three layers in the simplified cell stack

## Techniques and Methods
- **Binder reformulation** — switching from PVA/PVAc to 70:30 CMC:SBR blend at 5–10% binder-to-solids ratio by weight
- **Electrode plate fabrication** — casting/printing active material ink with new binder to produce flexible, freestanding plates
- **Roll-to-roll printing** — plates to be industrially printed at 1–30 m lengths at 1.6 m/min
- **Three-layer cell assembly** — active material | separator/electrolyte | active material (no metal current collectors)
- **Button cell assembly** — small-scale cells punched from plates for verification testing
- **Group 27 cell assembly** — plates printed to size and slid into a lead-acid battery housing, then filled with electrolyte
- **Hydrothermal carbonisation** — active material synthesis method (48 hours at ~160 °C), referenced in comments and covered in dedicated prior videos

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Binder ratio:** 70 g CMC to 30 g SBR; binder-to-solids ratio in the ink is typically 5–10% by weight.
- **Solvite = CMC:** Confirmed that the concrete additive "Solvite" is carboxymethyl cellulose.
- **Active material process:** Hydrothermal carbonisation — approximately 48 hours at 160 °C. Laser processing is being considered but not yet implemented.
- **Resistivity:** Bulk resistivity of the material is approximately 7 × 10⁻⁷ ohm·meters; internal resistance of a cell measured at 0.2 ohms.
- **Battery specifications:** 40 Ah at 12 V nominal; approximately 14 V when fully charged.
- **Electrode composition:** Entirely ink and binder — no metal substrate; self-supporting confirmed.
- **Discharge behaviour:** Cells discharge all the way down to zero volts.
- **Heat generation:** Very little heat produced during operation.
- **No patents planned:** Most of the information is already published on the channel; Robert does not intend to patent.
- **Active material is open:** Robert intentionally refers to it generically as "active material" to encourage experimentation; viewers are encouraged to try his materials or develop their own.
- **Independent verification:** Warwick University and Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) are lined up for independent testing.
- **Focus going forward:** Concentrating on a 12 V lead-acid battery replacement using the Group 27 form factor.
