## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses the Algo Laser Alpha Mark 2 diode laser cutter to design and build a fully functional wooden orrery (a mechanical model of the solar system) from basswood. He walks through the design process in TinkerCAD, the laser cutting, and the step-by-step assembly of gears, planet carriers, and the base structure. The key takeaway is that the laser cutter dramatically accelerated fabrication — roughly an hour of cutting versus a day of design — and the lantern/cage gear system used is a reusable building block for other machines.

## Key Topics
- Introduction and review of the Algo Laser Alpha Mark 2 (20W diode laser)
- Philosophy of laser cutting as a speed-optimised equivalent of hand tool work
- Design of an orrery in TinkerCAD — Sun, Earth, Moon frameworks and all mechanical parts
- Laser cutting basswood parts with minimal charring
- Assembly of planet carriers, lantern/cage gears, driven gear, base, legs, and handle
- Gear train simplification during the build (removing one intermediate gear)
- Sharing files on Thingiverse (STL and SVG) for community reuse and remixing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Basswood (3 mm sheet)** — primary material for all laser-cut parts; chosen for its pleasant burning smell and availability
- **6 mm × 30 mm wooden dowels** — used extensively as axles, gear pins, and structural supports
- **Wood glue** — used throughout assembly to bond layers and fix components
- **Black acrylic (8 mm)** — mentioned as cuttable in one pass at maximum power (not used in this build)
- **Clear acrylic** — noted as *not* cuttable by blue diode lasers (mentioned as a limitation)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Laser cutting** with the Algo Laser Alpha Mark 2 (20W blue diode) at pre-tested basswood settings
- **Stacking and laminating** 3 mm basswood layers to achieve 6 mm and 9 mm effective thickness for gears and structural parts
- **Lantern/cage gear construction** — assembling two discs with dowel pins to create a lantern gear (also functions as a cycloid/hypocycloid gear equivalent)
- **Spacer-based alignment** — using cut spacers to control axial positioning and ensure parts spin freely
- **TinkerCAD 3D modelling** followed by SVG export for laser cutting (or STL export for 3D printing)
- **Framing button** on the laser for workpiece placement; smart camera accessory mentioned for improved placement accuracy
- **Multi-pass cutting** noted as available for thicker stock

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirms he built an orrery years ago but has never done a video on it, calling it a "good idea" — suggesting this video was partly inspired by community requests.
- Enthusiastically endorses someone's idea to make the sphere components into **Christmas decorations** ("I love the Christmas idea mate").
- Notes he spent too much time studying the **Antikythera mechanism**, humorously implying it influenced his design thinking.
- Clarifies a gear design point: **backlash is necessary** — without built-in backlash a gear simply wouldn't move.
- Acknowledges the orrery is **not to correct astronomical dimensions/ratios**, just a mechanical model.
- Suggests that a CNC router with a pin bit could perform similar work, moving in X, Y, and Z.
- Philosophically reflects: *"I am a passing phase and I have passed — but then all things pass — not even the hills live forever"* — a characteristically poetic aside about legacy and impermanence.
