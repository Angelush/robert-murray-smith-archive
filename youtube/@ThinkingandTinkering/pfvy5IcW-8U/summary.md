## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed model of the Archimedes Drive — a friction-based drive system developed by IM Systems — explaining how it works as a toothless planetary gear set using compressible hollow rollers. He builds the model using TPU-printed rollers (mimicking the hollow, springy properties of ping pong balls from his earlier NF friction drive work) and concludes that while plastic home-printed versions suffer from tolerance issues and wear, the concept offers no-backlash torque reduction/increase with great commercial potential in metal production versions.

## Key Topics
- Why hollow, springy components (like ping pong balls) improve friction drive performance by reducing fatigue and improving torque transmission
- The Archimedes Drive by IM Systems: a friction drive using compressible hollow cylinders
- How the Archimedes Drive functions as a planetary gear system without teeth
- 3D-printed model assembly walkthrough using TPU rollers
- Limitations of home 3D printing for friction drives (tolerance, wear)
- The no-backlash property and its reversibility (useful for e.g. wind turbines)
- Mention of NuVinci's CVT bicycle drive and friction-assist liquids
- Commercial potential of metal production versions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ping pong balls** — used in prior NF friction drive; cited for accuracy, low cost, and springiness
- **TPU (Thermoplastic Polyurethane)** — flexible/squishy filament used to 3D print the compressible rollers
- **Friction-assist liquid** — used by NuVinci to increase surface grip in their bolt-based CVT drive (exact composition unspecified; Robert speculates it may be a sticky liquid; mentions honey as a humorous example)
- **Shear thickening gels** — mentioned by Robert in comments as a relevant material for improving friction drive grip

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing with TPU filament for compliant/squishy mechanical components
- Assembly of a planetary gear-style friction drive (carrier, rollers, clips, sun, drum/ring body)
- Gluing axle components into printed parts
- Engaging gears before dropping sub-assemblies in to reduce binding during assembly
- Testing torque transmission and speed reduction by hand
