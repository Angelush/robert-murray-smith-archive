## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to adapt an outdoor rocket stove for indoor use by constructing a steel box enclosure that acts as a baffle chamber, forcing exhaust gases to recirculate before venting up a house chimney. The modification is presented as simple and inexpensive, using basic sheet steel and welding. The key takeaway is that any rocket stove can be converted for indoor use as long as exhaust gases are baffled before reaching the flue.

## Key Topics
- Why a bare rocket stove cannot be used directly in a house chimney (all heat escapes straight up)
- The baffle box principle: enclosing the stove chimney forces gases to recirculate and surrender heat before exhausting
- Cutting down the stove chimney height from 800mm to 500mm to fit the intended application
- Fabricating a steel box enclosure from sheet steel (200×500mm plates)
- Tack welding vs full welding as a strategy for alignment and correction
- Attaching a flue pipe outlet to connect to a house chimney
- Optional insulation inside the box (Robert's view: minimal benefit for a room heater)
- Optional heat exchanger/manifold arrangements for extracting more heat from exhaust gases
- Testing draw direction with the box fitted

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Sheet steel plate — 200×500mm sections used to fabricate the baffle box
- Steel pipe — welded on as the flue outlet connector
- Insulation — mentioned as an optional addition inside the box (Robert is skeptical of significant benefit)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting the rocket stove chimney to a shorter height (800mm → 500mm)
- Tack welding panels into a box before full welding, to allow adjustment
- Drilling a hole in the box panel and welding on a pipe section for the flue outlet
- Baffle-based heat retention: gas forced to travel around one or more baffles before exhausting
- Visual draw-testing by observing flame direction to confirm the box hasn't choked airflow

- **On complexity:** Repeatedly emphasized keeping things simple for beginners; he deliberately avoided over-engineering this build.

- **On steel thickness:** Confirmed he used 3mm steel; noted 12mm seems too heavy.
