## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a low-cost method for making homemade copper conductive paint using copper sulfate and ascorbic acid (vitamin C). The ascorbic acid reduces the copper sulfate to nanoscale copper particles, which are then mixed with gum arabic as a binder and burnished to produce a highly conductive paint. The resulting ink achieves approximately 0.33 ohms/cm resistance — dramatically better than commercial alternatives like Bare Conductive's carbon-based ink at ~430 ohms/cm.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: high cost of silver-based conductive inks and limitations of commercial alternatives
- Chemistry: reduction of copper sulfate by ascorbic acid to produce copper nanoparticles
- Formulation: suspending copper nanoparticles in a gum arabic binder to create paintable ink
- Application: painting conductive traces on paper and lighting an LED
- Oxidation problem: copper nanoparticles oxidise over time, degrading conductivity
- Mechanical annealing (burnishing) as an accessible alternative to thermal sintering
- Comparison with Bare Conductive ink on conductivity

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper sulfate** — main copper precursor; available as root killer or pond algaecide, ~£3/kg from chemical suppliers
- **Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C)** — reducing agent; ~£5/kg from chemical suppliers
- **Hot water (~70°C)** — solvent for both solutions
- **Gum arabic (Winsor & Newton)** — binder to make the powder paintable; water-soluble; ~£3/bottle from art stores
- **Silver nitrate** — mentioned as the expensive standard alternative (~£25/small bottle), rejected on cost grounds
- **Carbon / graphite** — mentioned as an alternative low-cost conductive ink base
- **Acrylic medium** — noted as an alternative binder (water-resistant once dry)
- **Gallium-indium alloy** — mentioned in comments (75.5 parts Ga : 24.5 parts In) as another conductive ink option
- **Polyurethane varnish / clear lacquer** — suggested in comments as protective coating to prevent oxidation
- **Xenon flash** — mentioned in comments as capable of photonically reducing copper oxide back to copper

## Techniques and Methods
- **Chemical reduction / wet synthesis** — dissolving copper sulfate and ascorbic acid in separate hot-water solutions, then combining to precipitate copper nanoparticles
- **Sedimentation and decanting** — allowing nanoparticles to settle, pouring off the supernatant liquid
- **Formulation as paint** — mixing dried/damp copper powder with a few drops of gum arabic binder
- **Mechanical annealing (burnishing)** — rubbing the dried paint film hard with a ball-end spatula to compact particles and achieve a shiny, low-resistance copper trace
- **Thermal annealing** — mentioned but impractical on paper: heating to ~250°C in an oven to burn off volatile compounds and sinter particles
- **Electroplating bath** — noted in comments as an alternative use for the copper sulfate/ascorbic acid solution
- **Surface protection** — over-painting with clear lacquer/varnish to prevent oxidation of finished traces

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was highly active in the comments over several years. Key clarifications and follow-up insights include:

- **Oxidation timeline and storage**: nanoparticles oxidise quickly due to their high surface-area-to-volume ratio; keep them in solution until ready to use; expect 3–6 months before traces lose conductivity unless protected. Red (copper oxide) particles can be recovered with a xenon camera flash; black particles are ruined.
- **Protecting finished traces**: recommends over-painting with clear lacquer, polyurethane varnish, or acrylic medium to prevent oxidation.
- **Binder alternatives**: gum arabic, acrylic medium, polyvinyl acetate, polyurethane, carboxymethyl cellulose, egg yolk — experimentation encouraged; artists' colour recipe lists are a useful reference.
- **Waste disposal**: the supernatant should not be poured down the sink carelessly — unreacted copper salts are harmful to aquatic life.
- **Recipe confirmation**: 5 g ascorbic acid, 1 g copper sulfate, ~200 mL water at 70°C, plus a few drops of gum arabic.
- **Inkjet / ballpoint pen use**: particle size must be controlled to below 0.45 µm for inkjet; would also need curing. He was aiming to load the ink into a ballpoint pen.
- **Graphite as cheap alternative**: recommends graphite mixed with a binder as an easy and cheap conductive ink if copper is too much trouble.
- **Mechanochemical synthesis**: flagged as an interesting direction for producing nanoparticles; papers available on Google Scholar.
- **Gallinstan/gallium-indium**: provided a recipe (75.5 Ga : 24.5 In in deionised water at 50°C) as a liquid-metal ink alternative.
- **Thermal annealing caveat**: requires an inert atmosphere to avoid oxidation; a DVD laser lacks sufficient power.
- **Application to non-conductive surfaces for electroplating**: suggests spraying the object with surface glue, dusting with the copper powder, and burnishing — should plate up well without needing a binder.
- **Particle size note**: approximately 4 nm; this is why the powder appears salmon-pink rather than the reddish-orange of bulk copper.
