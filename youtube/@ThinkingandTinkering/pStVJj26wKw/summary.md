## Overview
Robert demonstrates photonic (flash) sintering of two types of copper-based inks — copper nanoparticles and an organometallic copper ink — applied as dried spots on paper. He shows that brief exposure to a high-intensity flash unit dramatically improves the conductivity of the copper nanoparticle sample (from non-conductive to ~184 kΩ), while the organometallic ink requires significantly more flashing to achieve the same effect.

## Key Topics
- Photonic/flash sintering as a method for converting copper-based inks into conductive traces
- Comparison of copper nanoparticles vs. organometallic copper ink response to sintering
- Real-time conductivity measurement before and after flash exposure
- Safety note on flash unit intensity

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper nanoparticles** — homemade, described as blackish/deep golden in solution, greenish when dried; highly responsive to photonic sintering
- **Organometallic (metal-organic) copper ink** — homemade; less responsive to sintering in this test, requiring longer exposure
- **Paper** — used as the substrate for the ink samples

## Techniques and Methods
- Photonic sintering (flash sintering) using a high-intensity flash unit
- Painting/spotting ink samples onto paper and allowing to dry before testing
- Four-point-style conductivity probing (probes held ~1 cm apart) to measure resistance before and after sintering
