## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith finds an abandoned tumble dryer on his commute and disassembles it to salvage the large steel drum, which he converts into a wind turbine rotor by cutting and bending blade shapes into it. The project demonstrates a low-cost, scrap-sourced approach to building a functional rotor, mounted on skateboard bearings. The finished rotor spins freely and responsively, with Robert noting the next step would be adding magnets and coils to generate electricity.

## Key Topics
- Disassembly and parts salvage from a discarded tumble dryer
- Internal mechanics of a tumble dryer (shaded pole motor, impeller, heating element)
- Converting a steel drum into wind turbine rotor blades by cutting and bending
- Mounting the rotor using skateboard bearings on a central pole
- Low-cost, upcycled wind turbine construction philosophy

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel drum** (from tumble dryer) — main rotor body, either stainless or zinc-coated steel depending on the dryer model
- **ABS plastic** — tumble dryer bottom chassis
- **Mica** — insulating material on the heating element
- **Heating wire** — resistive wire rolled into a coil for the heating element
- **Aluminium** — rotor bars in the shaded pole motor's induction rotor
- **Skateboard bearings** (standard 22mm inner ring) — used as the rotor pivot bearing

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of consumer appliance using screwdrivers, drill, and hammer
- Marking blade cut lines on the drum with a Sharpie and angle guide
- Cutting longitudinal blade slots with an angle grinder
- Making T-cuts at one-third intervals top and bottom to free blade tabs
- Bending blade tabs outward by hand, pliers/grips, and hammer to approximately 36° to the tangent
- Filing sharp cut edges smooth
- Bearing mount: cone-tipped steel pole (turned down from 12mm to 8mm) rests on inner bearing ring; rotor plate rests on outer ring; weight carried by ball bearings

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Rust/weatherproofing:** The drum steel is either stainless or zinc-coated (designed to endure years of wet/dry cycles in service), so corrosion should not be a concern — though he'd paint it just to make it look nice.
- **Balancing:** He intentionally left the rotor unbalanced at this stage; he plans to balance it once the magnets are fixed on.
- **Aerodynamic improvement:** A commenter suggested adding a cone to the inside of the drum hub; Robert thought this was a good idea worth trying.
- **Safety / handling sheet metal:** Robert is unapologetic about not wearing gloves, comparing it to cutting vegetables — safe handling technique is the key, not PPE.
- **Next steps:** He teases an update video ("wait until you see the update lol"), implying magnets and coils will follow.
- **Ease of spinning:** He was genuinely surprised by how little force was needed to get the rotor turning.
- **Motor/coils material:** He wouldn't use copper for the generator coils primarily due to cost.
