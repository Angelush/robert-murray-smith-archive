## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith compares a standard production pedestal fan blade against a custom 3D-printed fan blade inspired by Samara seed geometry and featuring tubercles (bumpy leading edges). Using a hand drill and anemometer as a simple test rig, he finds the tubercle blade dramatically outperforms the standard blade — moving significantly more air while also being noticeably quieter — concluding the design is well worth pursuing for applications such as PC fans.

## Key Topics
- Tubercle (bumpy leading edge) blade design inspired by humpback whale fins and Samara seeds
- Comparison of a standard pedestal fan blade versus a 12-blade tubercle fan assembly
- Correct blade orientation and twist direction relative to rotation direction
- Simple at-home performance testing using a hand drill and anemometer
- Potential applications: PC fans, VAWTs (Vertical Axis Wind Turbines), and general fan use
- Files available on Thingiverse for community download and experimentation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA filament (implied material for 3D-printed blades; confirmed 30 cm diameter in comments)
- Steel rod (used to mount the blade in the hand drill test rig)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM) — printing 12 tubercle blades and assembling them on a fan hub
- Hand drill as a rotation driver for informal bench testing
- Anemometer measurement to compare airflow output between the two blade designs
- Qualitative acoustic comparison (listening for noise difference between designs)
