## Overview
Robert demonstrates a simple, accessible method for reducing graphene oxide (GO) back to graphene using nascent hydrogen — a "green" approach requiring only aluminium kitchen foil and dilute hydrochloric acid. The reaction takes about 30–60 minutes at mild heat (~35–40°C) and produces a black graphene suspension that can be cleaned up with water washes. He briefly showcases a downstream application: formulating the resulting graphene into a conductive ink currently achieving ~50 ohms/cm.

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide (GO)** — starting material, golden-brown solution at 1 mg/mL
- **Aluminium kitchen foil** — source of aluminium that reacts with HCl to generate nascent hydrogen
- **Hydrochloric acid (HCl), 10% solution** — prepared from 35% HCl diluted with deionised water; drives the nascent hydrogen reaction
- **Aluminium chloride** — green-coloured byproduct formed during reaction, removed by excess HCl and water washing
- **Hydrazine** — mentioned as a highly effective but dangerous/restricted reducing agent (not used)
- **Sodium borohydride** — mentioned as another effective but hazardous reducing agent (not used)
- **Ascorbic acid** — mentioned as a prior green reduction method used by Robert (not used in this video)
- **Deionised water** — used for diluting HCl and for washing the final product

## Techniques and Methods
- **Nascent hydrogen reduction** — aluminium foil reacts with HCl in the GO suspension, generating highly reactive atomic hydrogen at the moment of formation, which reduces GO to graphene
- **Mild heating** — reaction carried out on a radiator surface at ~35–40°C
- **Mechanical stirring** — periodic stirring with a glass rod to keep GO submerged as hydrogen froth lifts it
- **Sedimentation washing** — adding large volumes of water and decanting after settling, repeated until neutral pH
- **Conductive ink formulation** — mixing reduced graphene with binders and additives to produce a printable conductive ink
