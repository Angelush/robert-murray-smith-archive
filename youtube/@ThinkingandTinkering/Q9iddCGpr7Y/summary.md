## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a concise taxonomy of Stirling engine types, explaining the core thermodynamic principle — air expanding when heated and contracting when cooled — and how inventors have implemented this in a wide variety of mechanical configurations. He highlights the work of New Zealander Ted Warbrook, who sought to eliminate moving pistons entirely, approaching thermo-acoustic engine territory. The video sets context for an upcoming build of a Warbrook engine replication to eventually pair with a rocket stove.

## Key Topics
- The fundamental operating principle of Stirling engines (heat → expansion → contraction → work)
- The three classic Stirling configurations: Alpha, Beta, and Gamma (defined by piston arrangement)
- Displacer engines and how a displacer (e.g. polystyrene) moves air without forcing it
- Fluid piston engines (water as the piston)
- Laminar flow and thermo-acoustic engines (air moves itself due to thermal properties)
- Thermal lag and how the time difference between heating and cooling can be exploited
- Free piston engines (NASA's diaphragm-based approach)
- Ted Warbrook's engine concept — eliminating all moving pistons
- Planned replication of the Warbrook engine for a rocket stove project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Air** — the working fluid in all Stirling engine types
- **Polystyrene** — used as a lightweight displacer in low thermal difference engines
- **Water** — used as a fluid piston in fluidized/laminar flow engine variants

## Techniques and Methods
- **Displacement** — using a solid object (displacer) to push air between hot and cold zones without forcing it mechanically
- **Fluid piston technique** — replacing rigid pistons with water, which conforms to any shape
- **Laminar flow / thermo-acoustic operation** — allowing air to move naturally from hot to cold zones without a physical displacer
- **Thermal lag exploitation** — using the time delay in heating/cooling cycles to extract usable work
- **Free piston / diaphragm design** — removing crank mechanisms (NASA approach)
