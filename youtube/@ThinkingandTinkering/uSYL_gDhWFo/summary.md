## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith critically examines electric composters (such as the Lomi, Vitamix, and Pela), arguing that they are fundamentally food dehydrators rather than true composters. He concludes that they are expensive, not genuinely eco-friendly across their lifecycle, and solve a problem that is better addressed simply by buying and wasting less food.

## Key Topics
- What electric composters actually are (re-badged bread makers / food dehydrators)
- Why dehydration is not composting — microbes required for composting are killed above ~65°C, while these machines operate at ~120°C
- The misleading "grinding" claim — the slow-turning bar inside does not grind, only agitates
- Energy use (~1 kWh per cycle) and the broader environmental cost of manufacturing, shipping, and disposing of these devices (mostly ABS plastic)
- The salt problem — processed food scraps contain added salt, which can damage garden soil if the dried output is applied directly
- The only real solution to food waste: buy less and eat what you buy
- Global food waste statistics (1.3 billion tonnes/year, ~$1 trillion USD; UK ~6.7 million tonnes/year, ~£400/household)
- Limited legitimate use cases: volume reduction for flat-dwellers with no composting access, or cold-climate storage until spring composting can begin
- Comparison to hoverboards as a short-lived consumer craze destined for landfill

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **ABS plastic** — primary material in the device housings; noted as a disposal/environmental concern
- **Salt** — present in processed food scraps; risk of soil salinisation if dried output is applied to gardens
- **Water** — the only thing actually removed by the machines during their "composting" cycle

## Techniques and Methods
- **Food dehydration** — what these machines actually perform; driving off water via heat (~120°C) with a slow-turning stir bar
- **Composting** — the biological process (microbial breakdown, ~65°C range) that these machines cannot perform
- **Mulching / dig-and-wait** — valid alternative: burying dried scraps and waiting ~2 months for actual composting to occur
- **Cold storage** — storing food scraps in a bin over winter as a no-energy alternative to dehydrating them

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments add several useful clarifications and extensions:

- **On Thunderf00t's review**: Robert acknowledges Thunderf00t was "basically right" but finds his tone too harsh and condescending — "he says some sensible stuff he just says it badly at times."
- **On the bread-maker comparison**: Repeatedly emphasises that a standard bread maker (~$60) is mechanically equivalent to these $500–$700 devices and would do the same job.
- **On composting timescales**: Notes genuine confusion in the literature — guidelines range from an hour to two years depending on temperature — suggesting the composting process is less well understood by the public than assumed.
- **On dehydration energy cost**: Acknowledges that even for the flat-dweller use case, better storage until municipal collection is probably cheaper overall, since dehydration consumes meaningful energy.
- **On adding water back**: Points out the absurdity that to actually use the dried output as compost you would need to re-hydrate it — "removing the water to add it back in — just crazy really."
- **On a follow-up video**: Mentions he was actively building his own compost heap at the time and hints at a follow-up video on composting due "in a few days or so."
- **On drone umbrellas**: Confirms with amusement that a Japanese company is actually manufacturing them.
- **Personal philosophy**: Deflects praise about his intelligence — "I just ask questions and have a sense when something doesn't seem right to me."
