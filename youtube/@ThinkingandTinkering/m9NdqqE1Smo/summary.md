## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history of the pantograph and its refined successor, the eidograph — a precision drawing instrument invented by mathematician William Wallace in the 1800s. He designs and 3D prints a fully functional eidograph from scratch using only primitive shapes, demonstrating how it can enlarge, reduce, or copy drawings by adjusting pivot positions along its arms.

## Key Topics
- History of the pantograph as a scaling/copying tool and its origins as a guarded guild secret
- The story of Christoph Schiner reverse-engineering the pantograph mechanism
- William Wallace's mathematical refinement of the pantograph into the eidograph
- Design and 3D printing of a working eidograph from primitive shapes (cubes, cylinders, cones)
- The mechanical principle: parallelogram linkage with a chain connecting two rotating arms
- How adjusting the pivot position along the arms controls enlargement, reduction, or 1:1 copying
- Practical demonstration copying/enlarging a square
- Suggestions for modifications and improvements (longer arms, rollers for stability)
- Files shared on Thingiverse for community use

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar 3D printing filament (implied throughout)
- Ball bearings (used as weights to keep pencil in contact with paper)
- Glue (to fix one rectangular slot component in place)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D modelling from primitives (cubes, cylinders, cones) — no complex CAD required
- ISO thread generation via search bar in modelling software
- Designing a 3D-printable chain link (58 links printed and snapped together to replace original wire)
- Clamp design using a circular stress-relief cut to act as a leaf spring for holding pencil/pointer
- Assembly: shaved/trimmed screws to fit slots, clamp plates bolted over pivot joints
- Parallelogram linkage: chain ties the two arm gears so they rotate by equal degrees
- Pivot adjustment along arm slots to set scale ratio (enlarging vs. reducing vs. copying)
- Adding ball bearing weights to maintain pencil-paper contact during tracing
