## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a functional rocket stove using only large-diameter plumbing fittings — no welding, no specialist tools, and no prior building experience required. The stove is assembled by hand-tightening standard pipe components and sealing the joints with high-temperature silicate putty, making it accessible to virtually anyone. The result is a working, aesthetically pleasing rocket stove suitable for outdoor cooking and heating.

## Key Topics
- The challenge of building a rocket stove for someone with no tools, space, or experience
- Using oversized plumbing fittings (4-inch / 100mm) as the primary structural components
- Assembly process: hand-tightening parts, applying high-temperature sealant, and waiting for cure
- The rocket stove draw ratio (1A:2A:3A:4A) and Robert's skepticism about its strictness
- Adapting the design for indoor use (referenced as a separate video)
- Aesthetic choices — brass handle, steampunk styling
- Cost breakdown and where to source parts in the UK

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Large-diameter plumbing fittings (4-inch / 100mm black iron / malleable iron)** — T-junction, 90-degree elbow, cap, socket/barrel fittings (called "scoreboard fittings"), plain nipples, and a half-threaded nipple; sourced from BES (online pipe fitting supplier)
- **High-temperature silicate putty** — rated to 1200°C, used to seal all joints; available from B&Q and Lowe's (US); Robert also links a Rutland brand product on Amazon in the comments
- **Steel pipe (100mm / 4-inch, 500mm length)** — the chimney/riser section; sourced from Metals4U, cut to size, cost ~£11
- **Brass handle** — decorative lid-lift knob (not functional/insulating — Robert admits it requires a glove)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hand assembly only** — all fittings are threaded and assembled hand-tight; no welding or power tools required
- **High-temperature sealant application** — silicate putty is packed into all knuckle joints and around the feet to seal smoke gaps and bond components once cured
- **Pipe-within-fitting insertion** — the 100mm steel pipe is inserted into the 110mm T-fitting until it seats against the internal shoulder, then sealed in place
- **Cure/dry time** — assembled stove left overnight before first firing
- **First firing** — initial burn to dry out remaining moisture from the sealant

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Durability concerns**: Robert pushes back on commenters questioning the stove's durability, pointing out that most stoves are made from cast iron and that the fittings here are malleable/cast iron — he asks skeptics what heat they think the stove reaches and why they assume it won't hold up.
- **Zinc galvanised fittings warning**: He clarifies that you *can* use galvanised fittings but you must do several burn-in runs outdoors to burn off the zinc first, and warns not to stand over it — he used black iron fittings specifically to avoid this and notes they are also cheaper.
- **Cost and sourcing**: Repeatedly states the build costs around **£80–90 in the UK** (more than welding, but no skill needed). He recommends **pipeworksuppliers.co.uk** and **metals4u** for cut-to-size pipe. Minimum parts are just the T-piece and elbow; the rest can be cut pipe with sealant.
- **Indoor use**: Directs many commenters to a separate video he made on converting this outdoor cooking stove to an indoor heater.
- **Cooking use**: Confirms the stove can cook on it — just needs a trivet.
- **Damper**: Agrees a damper on the front air intake is a good idea and says he plans to add one.
- **Sealant product link**: Shares an Amazon link to Rutland Stove Gasket Cement as a suitable product.
- **Y-fitting**: Notes he used a T-fitting because he didn't see a Y-fitting at the time, but says he would have used a Y if he had spotted one.
- **Ash/cleanup**: Reassures commenters that rocket stoves produce very little ash — a full day's burn leaves only a small amount to clean out.
- **Update planned**: Mentions repeatedly that he was planning a follow-up update video with a full parts list and links.
