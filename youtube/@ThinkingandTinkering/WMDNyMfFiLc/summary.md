## Overview
Robert explores a 150-year-old mechanical concept — the expanding pulley — and traces its appearance across three very different inventions: the short-lived Yankee Bicycle Company's CVT drivetrain (1991–1992), the Fletcher Capstan Table (a luxury expanding dining table worth up to $770,000), and his own 3D-printed variable coil winder. The central takeaway is that this elegant, simple mechanism from Victorian-era mechanical reference books keeps resurfacing as the heart of clever modern designs.

## Key Topics
- The Yankee Bicycle Company (1991–1992), founded by Royce Husted, and its expanding-pulley-based CVT
- Continuously variable transmissions (CVTs) in small machines (scooters, lawnmowers, bicycles)
- The Fletcher Capstan Table by David Berman Fletcher (work begun 1997), updating an 1835 design by Robert Jupe
- The expanding pulley as a canonical mechanism: Hiscox's Mechanical Movements #1322 and *507 Mechanical Movements* #224
- Robert's 3D-printed variable coil winder using a cam-follower expanding pulley design
- Potential future development: having the mechanism 3D printed in metal as a prototype bicycle CVT drive

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed plastic parts (designed in TinkerCAD) — used for the coil winder prototype
- Metal — mentioned as a future material for a more durable bicycle drive prototype
- Drive belt — discussed as a viable option for transmitting power (noted as popular on Dutch bikes)

## Techniques and Methods
- Expanding pulley mechanism: variable-diameter pulley achieved by radially sliding hyperbolic pegs via a cam follower system
- Cam follower / guide lane design: rotating a central cog moves pegs in and out to change circumference
- Locking mechanism: a protrusion on the adjustment ring drops into an indentation to lock the selected diameter
- 3D design in TinkerCAD and fabrication via desktop 3D printing
- Turn-counting integration: a full cog gear engages a separate counting mechanism to track coil revolutions
- Commercial 3D printing services (mentioned as route to metal prototyping)
