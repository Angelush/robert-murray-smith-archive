## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith refurbishes a commercial deep fat fryer acquired cheaply by his neighbours who are setting up an industrial kitchen. He replaces the original worn-out mechanical temperature controls with a modern PID controller, K-type thermocouple, and solid-state relay, while also adding improved safety features including a thermal cutout switch and a relay latching circuit for the start/stop buttons.

## Key Topics
- Disassembly and cleaning of a commercial deep fat fryer
- Replacing old mechanical temperature controls with a PID controller system
- Selecting matched components (PID range, thermocouple type) for accurate low-range temperature control
- Adding safety features: thermal cutout switch and emergency stop
- Implementing a relay latching circuit for momentary start/stop buttons
- Wiring the PID controller, solid-state relay, and thermocouple

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Stainless steel (fryer body construction)
- Steel wool / wire wool — used for cleaning
- Red seed oil — the cooking oil the end-users intended to use (smoke point ~230°C)
- Chrome/silver spray paint — for cosmetic repainting of panels
- Heat, grease, and oil resistant wire — used for internal wiring
- Heat shrink sleeving — for colour-coding and insulating wire ends

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and deep cleaning of commercial kitchen equipment
- Component matching for PID controller accuracy (matching thermocouple range to expected operating temperature)
- Installation of a bi-metallic strip thermal cutout switch (rated ~220–240°C, sourced from a microwave oven) against the oil bowl
- Wiring a PID controller to a solid-state relay (SSR) following the manufacturer's diagram
- Relay latching circuit using a double-pole double-throw (DPDT) relay with momentary normally-open (green) and normally-closed (red) push buttons
- Installation of an IP-rated enclosure for the front-panel switches
- Use of a timed relay (30-minute limit) as an additional safety cutoff
