## Overview
In this video, Robert Murray-Smith builds a permanent vortex rocket stove from pre-cut steel components, following up on his earlier investigation (video 1730) into vortex geometry. He fabricates and welds together a vortex chimney (using three segmented fins at 120° intervals) and a rocket stove body, then tests the completed unit on wood fuel. The result works well — roaring with visible vortex airflow — though Robert notes the chimney needs to be longer for better draft, and considers an oil burner adaptation as a future improvement.

## Key Topics
- Design and fabrication of a vortex chimney using three circular arc segments welded at 120°
- Construction of a rocket stove body with a 45-degree angled fuel feed and hinged fuel door
- Joining a square stove body to a round vortex chimney using sliced ("granulation") tabs
- Live test burn on wood to verify vortex airflow and heat output
- Observations on chimney draft length and fuel feed efficiency
- Discussion of future adaptations (oil burner, longer chimney, hopper feed)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel pipe, 4-inch diameter, 200 mm long** — main vortex chimney section
- **Steel pipe, 4-inch diameter, 50 mm long** — shorter chimney segment
- **Steel pipe, 5-inch diameter, 200 mm long** — outer vortex housing, cut into thirds for fins
- **Steel tube, 4-inch, 400 mm long with 45° angle cut** — rocket stove body/burn chamber
- **Door hinge (steel plate)** — used to create the hinged fuel feed flap
- **Concrete mold feet (scrap steel sections)** — used as transitional adapter feet between stove body and chimney
- **6×4-inch steel section with 100 mm hole** — second adapter foot
- **Wood** — fuel used during initial test burn
- **Used motor oil / vegetable oil / paraffin** — mentioned as preferred fuel alternatives for a future oil burner conversion

## Techniques and Methods
- Marking and cutting a steel ring into equal thirds (120° segments) for vortex fins
- MIG/arc welding fins onto the chimney ring at 120° spacing with ~1 cm gap
- Cutting a hole in the stove body tube and welding the angled fuel feed section
- Welding a door hinge to a steel plate to create a hinged fuel feed flap
- Slicing ("granulating") the top edge of the stove body tube to create bendable tabs for joining to the round chimney
- Bending tabs inward with a hammer and welding to secure the joint
- Heavy use of an angle grinder to clean up welds and finished surfaces
- Test firing with wood to verify vortex airflow visually (looking down chimney) and audibly

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Vortex burns hotter and faster**: Robert repeatedly confirms the vortex design burns noticeably hotter but also consumes fuel faster than a standard rocket stove.
- **Oil burner already done**: He notes an oil burner version exists on his rocket stove playlist, and paraffin is a suitable fuel.
- **Chimney length**: He acknowledges the short chimney is a limitation and intends to address draft improvement.
- **Fuel feed / hopper**: He is working toward an automatic fuel feed system and considers a hopper as a likely solution, but is stuck finding a suitable valve.
- **Ash output**: The stove produces very little ash.
- **Pellets as fuel**: He confirms wood pellets would work fine.
- **Scale**: He sees this primarily as a space heater and confirms it produces substantial heat from a compact size.
- **Metric/imperial**: He uses both measurement systems fluently, explaining he grew up during England's transition period and thinks naturally in both.
- **Community engagement**: He actively encourages viewers to try adaptations (glass viewing port, oil conversion, mass heater integration) and to share results.
