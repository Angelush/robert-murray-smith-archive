## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to convert an automotive glow plug into a cheap, simple pyrography (wood-burning) tool. The build requires minimal parts and basic tools, and the finished tool can be powered by a small battery or low-voltage power supply. He concludes that for just a couple of pounds, anyone can make a functional pyrography tool to burn patterns, names, or artwork onto wood.

## Key Topics
- What glow plugs are and why they make good heating elements
- Re-threading the glow plug body to accept standard M10 nuts
- Attaching positive and negative wires using terminal block brass inserts and washers
- Handle options for the tool
- Voltage control as a means of regulating temperature
- Practical pyrography demonstration on wood

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glow plug** — automotive diesel glow plug (tip often platinum or iridium), ~£3 each; used as the heating element
- **M10 × 1.5 nut and washer** — standard metric fasteners used to clamp the negative wire
- **Brass terminal block insert** — 5 A brass insert sliced from a standard terminal block; fits the positive terminal
- **Fire brick** — drilled to act as an insulating handle
- **High-temperature sealant** — rated to 1200 °C; alternative handle/grip material
- **Kapton tape** — rated to 400 °C; used to secure the fire brick handle
- **Gum Gum** (mentioned as alternative grip/sealant)
- **Ceramic blanket** — mentioned as another possible insulating handle material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Re-threading** the glow plug body with an M10 × 1.5 tap to accept standard nuts
- **Mechanical wire clamping** using stacked M10 washers and nuts for the negative terminal
- **Terminal block disassembly** to extract a brass insert as a positive terminal connector
- **Drilling fire brick** to form a heat-resistant handle
- **Voltage control** (2–6 V from a bench supply or battery) to regulate tip temperature
- **Freehand pyrography** — writing and drawing directly on wood with the heated tip
