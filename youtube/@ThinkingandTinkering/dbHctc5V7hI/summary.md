## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith replicates a simplified version of the Karpen cell — a historical electrochemical cell originally patented in 1924 — using conductive graphene ink painted on PET film and aluminum roofing foil in a sodium carbonate solution. The experiment produces a measurable voltage (~656–680 mV open circuit) and current (~21.4 mA) from just 5 cm² of electrode area, demonstrating that a rough home replication of Karpen's third cell type is achievable with accessible materials.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Karpen cell and its historical background (1924 French patent)
- Three versions of the Karpen cell, focusing on the most accessible third type (two dissimilar conductors)
- Home replication using conductive ink and aluminum foil in sodium carbonate electrolyte
- Open-circuit voltage and loaded voltage/current measurements
- Discussion of the cell's longevity claims (~73 years of runtime)
- Comparison with air batteries and solar cells raised in comments
- Practical notes on electrolyte choices (washing soda vs. bicarbonate vs. borax)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene/conductive ink** — painted onto PET film as one electrode
- **PET (polyethylene terephthalate) film** — substrate for the ink electrode
- **Aluminium roofing foil** (~5 cm²) — second electrode
- **Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃, washing soda)** — electrolyte dissolved in water
- **Sodium bicarbonate** — noted as a working but inferior alternative electrolyte
- **Borax (sodium tetraborate, Na₂B₄O₇)** — clarified in comments as chemically distinct from washing soda
- **Gold and silver / sulfuric acid** — materials used in the original high-purity Karpen cell (not replicated here)
- **Graphene oxide** — mentioned in comments; noted to be an insulator and therefore unsuitable

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting conductive ink onto PET substrate to form an electrode
- Constructing a two-electrode galvanic cell in a beaker
- Measuring loaded voltage across a 10-ohm resistor
- Measuring open-circuit voltage
- Calculating current from voltage/resistance readings

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Electrolyte choices:** Confirms washing soda (Na₂CO₃) works best; bicarbonate works but less well; clarifies borax is a chemically different compound (Na₂B₄O₇).
- **Two-carbon variant:** Confirms he also successfully tried the two-different-carbons version of the cell.
- **Air battery parallel:** Agrees there is an air battery element to this incarnation of the cell; finds the commenter's suggestion of a parallel with solar cells "very intriguing."
- **Moisture/phase dependency:** Speculates the cell likely requires a moist/wet state to function — if dry, it probably won't work — but acknowledges uncertainty.
- **Graphene oxide:** Points out it is an insulator and therefore not a suitable electrode material.
- **Aluminium trade-offs:** Acknowledges aluminium is consistent with Karpen's patent but notes its environmental cost in reclamation/recycling.
- **Philosophy on experimentation:** Reiterates his preference for testing ideas directly in experiment rather than debating theoretical possibilities at length.
- **73-year claim:** Humourously notes "we will know in 73 years" whether the longevity claim holds up.
