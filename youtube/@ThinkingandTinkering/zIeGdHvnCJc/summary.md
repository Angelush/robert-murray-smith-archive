## Overview
Robert converts a wood-burning rocket stove (built in video 1736) into a liquid fuel vortex heater by cutting away the fuel-feed section and replacing it with an ethanol burner. He demonstrates how adding a door to the chamber creates a powerful vortex effect that dramatically improves combustion efficiency. The prototype shows promise as a stable, controlled, and spillage-resistant heater design that he intends to develop further.

## Key Topics
- Converting a rocket stove into a liquid fuel vortex burner
- The role of the vortex chamber door in controlling airflow and combustion
- Using carbon felt wicks for liquid fuel burning
- Adding a steel mantle to capture and radiate heat
- Plans for an external fuel reservoir and pre-warming system for heavier oils

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ethanol** — used as the liquid fuel in the bean can burner
- **Carbon felt** — used as a wick around the edge of the burner can
- **Steel plate / mantle (150 mm)** — placed above the chamber to capture radiated heat
- **Bean/pecan can** — repurposed as the burner head

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a rocket stove body to isolate the combustion chamber
- Using a wick-based liquid fuel burner inside a vortex chamber
- Fitting a door ("dorometer") to initiate and control the vortex airflow
- Placing a steel mantle above the flame to maximise heat capture and radiation
- Concept of routing a fuel tube down the centre of the pipe to pre-warm heavier oils before combustion
