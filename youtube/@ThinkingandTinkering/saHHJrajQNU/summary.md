## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith shares a recipe for homemade air-drying modelling clay developed by his friend Graham Healy, a local polymath and sculptor. The clay produces a dough-like material that dries to an almost porcelain finish, is non-toxic, and can be sanded smooth. Robert demonstrates the recipe by sculpting a Venus de Milo-style figurine built over a paper-and-masking-tape armature on a copper pipe stand.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to Graham Healy and the origin of the clay recipe
- Full recipe and ingredient ratios for the air-drying clay
- Armature construction using copper pipe, breadboard, tissue paper, and masking tape
- Application technique for covering an armature with the clay
- Finishing properties: smoothing with water, sanding after drying, porcelain-like surface

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Toilet paper** (1–1¼ cups, blitzed to loose fibres) — the fibrous base of the clay
- **Tile adhesive** (1 cup, white waterproof non-slip type) — primary binder
- **PVA glue** (¾ cup) — adhesive and plasticiser
- **Mineral oil** (2 teaspoons) — adds workability
- **Vinegar** (1 teaspoon) — likely acts as a preservative/modifier
- **Flour** (~1 kg) — bulk filler, kneaded in to achieve dough texture
- **Water** — used during sculpting to smooth the surface
- **Copper pipe** — armature spine
- **Masking tape** — armature skin/form-building
- **Tissue/newspaper paper** — armature bulk fill

## Techniques and Methods
- Blitzing toilet paper to break it into loose fibres
- Mixing PVA, mineral oil, and vinegar separately before combining with fibres
- Adding tile adhesive to the wet fibre mix
- Kneading in flour until a dough-like consistency is achieved (adjusting wetness with flour or PVA)
- Building a lightweight armature from copper pipe, scrap paper, and masking tape before applying clay
- Smoothing the clay surface with a wet hand or finger during sculpting
- Sanding the dried piece for a refined porcelain-like finish

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Not waterproof:** Robert explicitly confirms the clay is not waterproof; long-term water/moisture exposure would soften it due to the high flour content.
- **Mould separator:** For making moulds, he suggests leaving out the flour to create a thinner, separator-like mix.
- **Chess set suggestion:** He enthusiastically endorsed a commenter's idea of using the clay to make a chess set.
- **PVA safety:** He reassured someone not to worry about PVA being in the recipe.
- **Shrinkage/change:** He estimates only "a few percent" dimensional change (2–3% at most), presumably on drying.
- **General enthusiasm:** Describes it as "an awesome craft material" and acknowledged he filmed a finished result rather than the full mixing process, joking that nobody wants to watch him play with flour and toilet paper for an hour.
- **Cost:** Agrees with a commenter that commercial alternatives are "far too expensive."
