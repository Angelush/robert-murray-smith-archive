## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith designs and 3D-prints a spur gear differential (also called a "spur differential"), explaining how it differs from the more common bevel differential found in most cars. He models the entire assembly in Tinkercad using only basic primitives, then physically assembles the printed parts, demonstrating how the differential splits or combines rotational power between two output shafts while allowing them to turn at different speeds.

## Key Topics
- History and variants of differentials (hypocycloid, freewheel-based, planetary/CVT, bevel, and spur gear types)
- The spur gear differential as used historically by Rolls-Royce, Toyota, and Lancia
- Designing the differential entirely in Tinkercad using primitive shapes
- Component breakdown: main carriage, two sun gears, six planet gears, bevel drive gear, flanges, spacers, and clips
- Step-by-step physical assembly of the 3D-printed parts
- Demonstration of differential action — one output shaft continuing to spin when the other is braked
- Files published to Thingiverse for free reuse and modification

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar FDM filament (implied by 3D printing context)
- Glue (used to fix the bevel gear, clips, and handle onto axles)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD modelling in Tinkercad using only built-in primitive shapes
- FDM 3D printing of all mechanical components
- Manual assembly: press-fitting, clip fastening, and gluing sub-assemblies
- Functional testing (hand-spin check) before final frame installation

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirms the design is "pretty straight forward" and encourages others to explore and share their own differential builds.
- Clarifies a terminology point raised in comments: the correct name is **Torsen** (not "torsion") differential, and notes it works on a different principle — the inability to back-drive a worm gear — making it distinct from the spur differential despite visual similarities.
- Expresses enthusiasm for the broader potential of differential mechanisms, noting that differentials are interesting partly because of their ability to act as power-combining/splitting devices — hinting at robotics applications ("a mechanoid — that would be awesome").
- Encourages a commenter who built a limited-slip differential from gears alone to share their work, affirming it is "more than possible."
- Notes he produced both a CAD walkthrough and a physical assembly video intentionally, because different viewers learn better from different formats.
