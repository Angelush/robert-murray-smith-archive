## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses the Aufero Laser 2 diode laser cutter/engraver to design and cut parts for a functional air engine from black and yellow acrylic, then assembles them. The video doubles as a real-world capability test of the machine, pushing it beyond its rated specifications — cutting 5mm acrylic and yellow acrylic, neither of which it was officially rated for. The finished engine runs successfully off a vacuum cleaner.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to air engines and why Robert prefers build projects over decorative engraving
- Setting up the Aufero Laser 2 and using LightBurn software to frame and run cuts
- Cutting 5mm black acrylic (beyond the machine's rated thickness) in two passes
- Testing the laser on yellow acrylic (also not in the official spec)
- Gluing cut parts together into a working air engine
- Brief product disclosure (machine sent by Sino Small on behalf of Otter)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Black acrylic (5mm)** — primary structural material for the engine parts; cut in two passes at 150 mm/min, full power
- **Yellow acrylic (5mm)** — secondary material for additional parts; tested as an off-spec challenge
- Small plastic pieces — used as spacers/risers to lift the acrylic off the bench so cut pieces can drop through

## Techniques and Methods
- **Laser cutting** with the Aufero Laser 2 diode laser at 150 mm/min, 100% power
- **Two-pass cutting** to get through 5mm acrylic (vs. the single-pass rated thickness)
- **Focus setting** using the physical spacer/banger method to set head height from the material surface
- **Framing** in LightBurn to preview and align the cut boundary over the workpiece before cutting
- **Gluing** cut acrylic parts together to assemble the final engine
- **Ventilation** — noted as essential when cutting acrylic due to fumes

## Robert's Own Replies
- On comparing the Aufero diode laser to a CO2 laser for making air engines: Robert clarifies they are fundamentally different machines using different techniques on different materials — both *could* make an air engine, but the results would be very different. He uses the analogy of a piano vs. a trumpet playing the same song.
- On cutting certain unspecified materials (likely thick or transparent stock): he doubts the laser could cut them, estimating it "wouldn't cut them before Christmas 2075" — i.e., it would engrave but not cut through.
- Several brief friendly acknowledgements ("cheers mate") to commenters, with no additional technical content.
