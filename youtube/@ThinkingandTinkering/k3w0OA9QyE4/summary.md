## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores Edison's 1878 "Phonomotor" (vocal engine) — a device that converts sound into mechanical motion — and builds a modern version using a 3D-printed horn, a cellophane membrane, a coil, and neodymium magnets to scavenge electrical energy from sound. The experiment successfully generates enough voltage to light an LED by yelling into the horn, demonstrating sound-to-electricity conversion as a proof of concept for acoustic energy harvesting.

## Key Topics
- Edison's 1878 Phonomotor patent and its original mechanical design
- The principle of acoustic energy scavenging
- Modern reinterpretation using electromagnetic induction instead of a pawl-and-ratchet mechanism
- How speakers work in reverse (as microphones/generators)
- Sound collection horns vs. open speakers for efficiency
- Practical limits of sound energy (a football crowd of 10,000 cannot boil an egg)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Cellophane (Saran/cling film)** — used as the vibrating membrane; sourced from electrician's tape backing
- **Neodymium magnets (50 mm × 5 mm)** — two used, attached to the membrane assembly
- **Coil (from a turntable motor)** — glued to the faceplate, forms the generator element
- **Double-sided tape** — used to mount the membrane and magnets
- **3D-printed horn and faceplate** — designed in Tinkercad, published on Thingiverse

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (Tinkercad/Thingiverse) to fabricate the horn and faceplate
- Heat-shrinking cellophane with a hair dryer to tension the membrane drum skin
- Attaching a coil-and-magnet arrangement to the membrane to generate voltage via vibration
- Voltage measurement with a multimeter while vocalising into the horn
- LED illumination as a qualitative proof-of-concept test

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Rubber as membrane**: Robert cautions that rubber would act as a damper, absorbing energy rather than transmitting it — making it a poor membrane choice.
- **Magnet-on-film vs. coil-on-film trade-off**: Placing the magnet on the membrane (heavier, acts as a spring → longer oscillation) vs. the coil on the membrane (lighter → easier to move, but shorter oscillation). Efficiency ultimately depends on the distance between coil and magnet, not simply which component is on the membrane.
- **Windmill variant**: A lighter membrane with a magnet would move in a lighter breeze but would not necessarily be more efficient overall.
