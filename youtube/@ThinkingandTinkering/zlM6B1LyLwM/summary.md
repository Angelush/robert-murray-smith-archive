## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple mathematical formula for calculating the correct tongue lengths needed to produce specific musical notes on a tongue drum (or any percussion instrument). Using salvaged wooden flooring as his starting material, he derives a material-specific constant `k` from a single measurement, then applies it to compute tongue lengths for a full octave. The key insight is that by keeping material thickness and width constant, frequency depends only on length.

## Key Topics
- Finding salvaged wooden flooring as a free material source
- The relationship between tongue/bar length and resonant frequency
- Deriving the material constant `k` from a single sample measurement
- Using the formula `L² = k / f` to calculate lengths for any desired note
- Building musical instruments: tongue drums, xylophones, glockenspiels, wind chimes, tubular bells
- Looking up note frequencies from a reference table (e.g. Wikipedia)
- Cutting pieces slightly long and filing down to fine-tune pitch

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Salvaged wooden flooring planks (unspecified hardwood)
- 6 mm aluminium sheet (cut into bars to demonstrate the technique)

## Techniques and Methods
- Using free frequency-analysis software (phone or computer app) to measure the resonant frequency of a sample piece
- Deriving the material constant `k = L² × f` from one known-length sample
- Applying `L² = k / f` to calculate required lengths for each target note
- Cutting material slightly oversized, then filing/trimming down to achieve exact pitch
- Verifying tuning with a frequency recorder
