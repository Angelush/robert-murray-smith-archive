## Overview
A friend named Ben Berger shared a video showing a mysterious improvement to wind turbine performance — one that was later taken down. Robert investigates by replicating the concept: stacking two sets of three 3D-printed blades at a 60-degree offset on a single turbine hub. A side-by-side fan test demonstrates a clear and striking increase in rotational speed, suggesting significantly better energy capture.

## Key Topics
- A disappeared YouTube video showing an unexplained wind turbine improvement
- Replication of the "mystery" dual-layer blade arrangement
- 3D-printed blade design (previously featured in video 2012)
- Strengthening 3D-printed blades with super glue
- Why stacking two sets of blades at 60° outperforms simply adding more blades
- Betz limit analogy: too many blades block airflow like a solid disc
- Potential application in a Darwin wind turbine configuration
- Patent status of the discovered technique (argued to be public domain once published)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to coat 3D-printed blades to prevent brittle fracture along layer grain lines (brand mentioned: Volden)
- **3D-printed PLA or similar filament** — used for the blades and base hub

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** of turbine blades (printed vertically so layers run along the blade length, then coated with super glue for strength)
- **Super glue reinforcement** of FDM-printed parts to overcome inter-layer brittleness
- **Dual-stage blade stacking** — two sets of three blades offset by 60°, functioning as sequential turbine stages
- **Side-by-side fan comparison** — controlled indoor test using a fan at ~0.8 m/s to compare single vs. dual-stage rotor performance
- **Gear-based hub design** — allows attachment of one or two generators
