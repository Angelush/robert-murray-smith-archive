## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the INN Engine by eRex Motors, a novel internal combustion engine design from Granada, Spain, which uses opposing pistons connected to rollers that drive a hyperbolic paraboloid cam rather than a traditional crankshaft. He builds a 3D-printed model of the mechanism in Tinkercad to demonstrate how it works, and contextualises it within the broader debate about the future of petrol engines and the practicality of full electrification.

## Key Topics
- Scepticism about the UK's 2030 petrol car ban and resource constraints on battery supply
- The "self-charging hybrid" as a political workaround keeping petrol engines alive
- The INN Engine design: opposing pistons driving a hyperbolic paraboloid cam plate
- 3D modelling and printing of the engine mechanism in Tinkercad
- The engine's role as a range extender for EVs rather than a standalone petrol engine
- Missing components needed for a functional engine: flywheel, driving force, and timing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Lead acid batteries (discussed in context of proposed European ban)
- Petrol (as the intended fuel for the INN Engine)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D CAD modelling in Tinkercad to replicate the hyperbolic paraboloid cam and engine assembly
- 3D printing of engine components (cam, carrier, roller holders, top plate, housing)
- Use of thrust bearing (41×11) and centre race/scatter bearing in the cam assembly
- Integration of a chain drive mechanism (referenced from a previous 2015 video)
- Manual demonstration of cam rotation to verify the mechanism's operation
