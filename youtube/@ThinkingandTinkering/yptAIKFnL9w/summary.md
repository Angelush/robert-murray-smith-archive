## Overview
Robert salvages a DC motor from a broken Black & Decker detail sander and repurposes it as a hand-spun generator. By calculating the motor constant (RPM per volt), he demonstrates that spinning it by hand can produce around 5–6V, enough to light an LED strip. The video is a quick, playful proof-of-concept for turning small appliance motors into simple generators.

## Key Topics
- Extracting a DC motor from a faulty detail sander
- Understanding the motor constant (RPM/volt) and reversing it for generation
- Using terminal blocks as improvised shaft couplers
- Building a hand-spun "swinging" generator
- Alternative motors (hand blender) that would work equally well

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Black & Decker Mouse detail sander (donor appliance)
- Small DC motor (from the sander, ~4mm shaft)
- Terminal blocks (3A/2mm, 5A/3mm, 15A/4mm, 30A/6mm) — used as shaft couplers
- Bent metal bar with threaded end — arm/coupling component
- Plumb bob weight — attached to arm to create swing momentum
- 12V LED lighting strip — used as the load/output indicator
- Hand blender DC motor (mentioned as alternative, ~8,000–10,000 RPM, 240V)

## Techniques and Methods
- Calculating motor constant: dividing rated RPM by rated voltage (13,000 RPM ÷ 240V ≈ 54 RPM/V)
- Reverse-engineering a motor as a generator by manually driving the shaft
- Using a terminal block screw fitting to couple a fabricated arm to the motor shaft, locating a flat spot on the shaft for alignment
- Hand-spinning the arm to generate voltage under load
