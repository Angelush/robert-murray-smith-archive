## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith begins a multi-part series aiming to build a vertical-axis wind turbine capable of generating at least 1kW for under £100, using primarily salvaged materials. Part 1 focuses entirely on constructing the rotor drum: cutting blades from uPVC rainwater gutter, building the drum frame from salvaged office chair bases and aluminium honeycomb panels, and achieving a well-balanced spinning assembly with only half a millimetre of runout.

## Key Topics
- Project goal: 1kW+ output for under £100 using salvaged/scrap materials
- Choice of uPVC rainwater gutter (112mm) as blade material in preference to standard PVC pipe
- Use of a simple cutting jig for consistent blade lengths
- Salvaged office chair base assemblies as the drum's structural support and bearing mounts
- Aluminium honeycomb panels (salvaged from smart boards) for the large rotor end discs
- Salvaged printer bearings (47mm OD / 25mm ID) as the rotor bearings
- Blade mounting strategy: uPVC pipe peg sections glued with superglue and filled with resin
- Blade attachment angle: 36 degrees off the tangent
- Balancing technique using a pen as a reference gauge

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **uPVC rainwater gutter (112mm)** — primary blade material, cut into slats
- **Aluminium honeycomb panels** — salvaged from smart boards; used for the two large rotor end discs (~80cm diameter)
- **Office chair base leg assemblies** — salvaged; used as structural spokes and bearing seats
- **Ball bearings (47mm OD / 25mm ID)** — salvaged from a printer; used as rotor bearings
- **50mm steel pipe** — used as central axle reinforcement inside the chair leg assembly
- **uPVC pipe sections** — cut into short pegs for mounting blades to the disc rim
- **Superglue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to bond uPVC blade pegs and blades; noted as standard UK building-industry adhesive for uPVC with 25+ year durability
- **Resin** — planned to be poured into the peg pipe sections to fill the honeycomb cells and create a permanent fixing
- **Electrical tape** — used to bind blade slats together for simultaneous edge-trimming

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cutting jig** — a block of wood bolted to the bench to ensure all blade sections are cut to identical 60cm lengths
- **Stack-and-trim** — binding all cut blade slats together and running them through the chop saw simultaneously to equalise edge alignment
- **Cabinet-scraper technique** — using a trimming knife blade held at an angle to shave and finish cut edges of the uPVC
- **String division for blade spacing** — wrapping string around the drum rim, dividing into thirds, and transferring marks to position three blades evenly
- **Pen-on-bench trueness check** — holding a pen stationary against the bench while spinning the drum to detect and quantify radial runout
- **Pipe-peg blade mounting** — gluing short pipe sections around the disc rim, then sliding blades in and bonding with superglue; planning to inject resin to lock the assembly permanently

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Blade angle**: He confirmed 36 degrees off the tangent came from reading a paper and he simply copied it — openly admitting he doesn't know for certain if it's optimal.
- **Superglue on uPVC**: Clarified that superglue is standard practice in the UK building industry for uPVC roofline and window frames, giving 25+ year bonds stronger than the base material; noted that epoxy resin does *not* bond well to this plastic.
- **Bearings**: Acknowledged the salvaged chair bearings lack thrust capability and he plans to add thrust washers as a workaround, accepting the risk that they may need replacing later.
- **Magnets**: Advised viewers to use ceramic (ferrite) magnets rather than neodymium — cheaper, and his earlier experiments showed neodymium is unnecessary; also noted neodymium magnets rust. Named **first4magnets** as his UK supplier.
- **Series length**: Expects the full build to run to 3–4 parts.
- **Installation plan**: Intends to mount the finished turbine on the flat-roof section of his house for testing.
- **Listing approach**: Explained he prefers to describe what *job* a component must do rather than specifying exact parts, so builders can adapt whatever scrap they find.
