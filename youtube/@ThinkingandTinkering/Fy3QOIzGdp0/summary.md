## Overview
This is part 2 of a tutorial on building a simple wooden automata — a rocking ship sculpture made from driftwood. Robert demonstrates how to design and fit a crank mechanism that converts rotary handle motion into a realistic rocking-at-sea motion for the ship, completing the build started in part 1.

## Key Topics
- Calculating crank throw based on desired range of motion
- Bending mild steel bar into a crank shape
- Drilling and aligning the crank hole through the ship and back block
- Using guide pins to control the rocking motion
- Finishing touches: handle decoration with beads, face plate reassembly

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Driftwood / timber (ship body and back block)
- 2 mm mild steel bar (used as axle/crank material)
- Coat hanger wire (suggested alternative to mild steel bar, ~3–4 mm)
- Glue (for fixing crank ends and face plate)
- Paint (minor shaping and painting of the ship noted in passing)
- Beads (decorative handle option)

## Techniques and Methods
- Calculating crank throw: half the total desired vertical travel distance (e.g., 1.6 cm travel → 0.8 cm throw)
- Bending wire/bar into a U-shape, feeding through a narrow timber slot, then closing and squaring the bends to 90°
- Drilling aligned holes through the ship body and into the back block for the crank axle
- Using existing standoff pins as guide pins to constrain the rocking motion (piston-style)
- Fitting additional guide pins if the ship rocks too loosely; leaving slight clearance to avoid locking
