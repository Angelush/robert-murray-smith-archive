## Overview
Robert explores the use of spiral geometry — particularly the Fibonacci spiral — in wind turbine design, tracing the concept from academic research papers through to the commercially available Liam F1 turbine. He reviews two key studies showing significant efficiency gains from spiral-blade configurations and demonstrates his own 3D-printed replication of the conical spiral turbine design.

## Key Topics
- The four main types of spirals found in nature (Fibonacci, logarithmic, golden ratio, Archimedes) and their practical equivalences from an engineering perspective
- Savonius wind turbines as the simplest baseline turbine design
- A research paper applying Fibonacci spiral geometry to Savonius-type blades, achieving a 14.1% improvement in power coefficient
- A 2018 paper constructing a conical spiral wind turbine from Fibonacci spiral blades, achieving 73.1% power extraction efficiency (with a theoretical ceiling of ~80%)
- The Liam F1 as a rare example of a spiral turbine making it from lab research into a commercial product
- Robert's own 3D-printed replication of the conical spiral turbine, including outdoor wind testing
- Availability of open-access papers on spiral wind turbines via Google Scholar

## Techniques and Methods
- Constructing conical spiral turbine blades by drawing a Fibonacci spiral on a flat sheet, cutting it out, and rolling it into a cone — repeated three times at 120° intervals
- 3D printing as an alternative fabrication method for the spiral blades
- Outdoor wind speed testing of the turbine (observed starting rotation at 0.9 m/s, measured at 1.6 m/s)
- Laboratory generation testing (referenced in linked video 1998)
