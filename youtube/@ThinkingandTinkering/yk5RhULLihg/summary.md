## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the history of epicyclic (planetary) gear systems from the ancient Antikythera mechanism through to the modern Wolfrom Drive, a compounded planetary gear invented by Urs Wolfram in 1912. The video explains how the Wolfrom Drive achieves extremely high gear reductions in a compact space by exploiting a lever effect created by stacking two slightly different-sized planet gears on a shared axle. Robert demonstrates a 3D-printed model and discusses the two practical challenges — gear indexing and tooth-size limits — that have kept it from widespread adoption.

## Key Topics
- The Antikythera mechanism and the distinction between heliocentric (orrery) and geocentric (antithera) solar-system models
- History of the sun-and-planet gear: from ancient Greece (~80 BC), through Agostino Ramelli's Book Wheel (1588), to James Watt's sun-and-planet gear
- How standard epicyclic/planetary gear sets work and their typical gear ratios (4:1–10:1)
- Gear compounding: stacking planetary stages in series vs. the Wolfrom approach
- The Wolfrom Drive: stacked dual-size planets on a shared axle creating a lever effect for very high ratios
- Calculating the Wolfrom gear ratio (planet ratio × lever effect)
- Why the Wolfrom Drive is not widely used: gear indexing requirements and tooth-size limitations
- Robert's 3D-printed Wolfrom Drive model, files shared on Thingiverse

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of gear components for demonstration
- Gear ratio calculation using tooth count or diameter ratios (ring ÷ sun = planet ratio)
- Compounding planetary stages in series (stacking)
- Wolfrom compounding: rigidly coupling two different-sized planet gears on a single axle to exploit a lever effect
- Gear indexing: aligning top and bottom planets so teeth mesh without jamming
