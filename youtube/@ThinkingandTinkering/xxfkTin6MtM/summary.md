## Overview
This is a competition entry video in which the creator attempts to build a homemade supercapacitor using cheap, household materials. Three cells are assembled from carbon-coated felt paper, aluminum foil current collectors, and a vinegar-salt electrolyte, then connected in series. After several charge/discharge cycles at 6V, the device successfully lights an LED, though the creator notes the aluminum corroded and acknowledges limited understanding of the underlying science.

## Key Topics
- DIY supercapacitor construction from recycled and household materials
- Using carbon-felt paper (from water filter cartridges) as electrode material
- Assembling layered electrochemical cells and connecting them in series
- Cycling/conditioning the supercapacitor to build charge capacity
- Demonstrating stored energy by lighting an LED

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon felt paper** — sourced from used household water filter cartridges; serves as the electrode material
- **Heavy-duty aluminum foil** — used as current collectors; corroded during use, requiring replacement
- **Paper towels** — used as separator between electrodes
- **Distilled vinegar** — component of the electrolyte solution
- **Table salt** — mixed with vinegar to form the electrolyte
- **Expired alkaline batteries (circa 2005)** — cells salvaged for voltage; noted as unsuitable for carbon extraction due to alkaline chemistry
- **LED** — used as the discharge load to demonstrate stored energy

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting carbon felt into squares and soaking in the vinegar-salt electrolyte
- Layered stacking of current collector → felt electrode → paper towel separator → felt electrode → current collector
- Sealing the assembled cell in a plastic bag to contain moisture
- Applying mechanical pressure with boards placed on top of the cells
- Connecting three cells in series to increase voltage
- Conditioning by repeated charge/discharge cycles using a standard 6V battery charger setting
- Replacing a corroded negative-end aluminum current collector mid-process
