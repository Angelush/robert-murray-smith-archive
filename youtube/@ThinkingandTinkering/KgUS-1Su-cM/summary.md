## Overview
Robert demonstrates a DIY method for measuring the torque output of a 3D-printed wind turbine rotor using a worm gear mechanism. Rather than measuring volts or amps (which reflect the generator, not the rotor), he shows how torque — the effectiveness of a force in causing rotation — can be directly measured by hanging calibrated weights from a pulley driven through the worm gear. The key takeaway is that this low-tech approach gives a true measure of how well a rotor converts wind force into useful rotation.

## Key Topics
- What torque is and why it matters for wind turbine evaluation
- The formula for torque: τ = rF sin θ
- How a worm gear works and its properties (gear ratio, mechanical advantage, non-backdrivability)
- Building a practical torque-measuring device from 3D-printed parts
- Why volts/amps alone don't capture rotor efficiency
- Live demonstration using a hair dryer as a wind source

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed worm gear (worm wheel + worm, 20-tooth worm wheel giving 20:1 ratio)
- 8 mm bar (axle through the worm wheel)
- Bearings
- String
- 1 kg calibrated weight
- Hair dryer (used as a wind source for indoor testing)
- Wind turbine rotor (3D-printed)

## Techniques and Methods
- Torque measurement via worm gear and hanging weight: rotor drives the worm, which turns the worm wheel and a pulley, lifting a known mass; torque is calculated as mass × g × radius, then divided by the gear ratio (20:1)
- 3D printing parts designed in Tinkercad (files shared free in description)
- Using mechanical advantage (20:1 worm gear) to amplify small rotor torques into measurable weight-lifting force
- Substituting a hair dryer for wind during inclement weather

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On measurement accuracy:** Robert confirms the measurement error is "insignificant" and about the same as any other method — no need to worry about precision loss.
- **On the method's logic:** He clarifies that to test a rotor you simply hang weights and see if it can lift a calculated target weight for a given wind speed — or conversely, observe what it *does* lift and compare to theoretical capacity.
- **On dual generators:** He notes that running two generators off one rotor would require twice the input power, or each generator would yield half the energy for a given input.
- **On wind turbine efficiency:** He pushes back on scepticism, stating that wind turbines "are stunningly efficient if built well."
