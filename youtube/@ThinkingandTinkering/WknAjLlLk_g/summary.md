## Overview
A Penn State study demonstrated that placing a sheet of low-iron glass (Pilkington Optiwhite) over an existing solar panel acts as a passive radiant cooling layer, reflecting infrared radiation back into space. While the glass reduces direct solar cell output to 91% of baseline, the 24/7 cooling effect more than compensates, yielding a net increase in overall energy output. Robert highlights this as an exceptionally accessible DIY experiment anyone can replicate with off-the-shelf materials.

## Key Topics
- Penn State research on combining passive radiant cooling with solar panels
- How passive radiant cooling works (infrared reflection via the atmospheric window)
- Pilkington Optiwhite low-iron glass as a commodity cooling layer
- Trade-off between slight light absorption loss (9%) vs. heat reduction gains
- Combined solar + cooling system reducing installation footprint and cost
- Comparison to the Dutch vertical solar panel anomaly (5% efficiency gain from cooling alone)
- Suggestion to run a long-term (1-year) experiment to fully quantify the effect

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Pilkington Optiwhite glass** — low-iron commercial glass (~£3/sq ft) used as the infrared-reflective cooling layer placed over solar cells
- **Calcium carbonate** — used by NightHawkInLight as a pigment in a passive radiant cooling paint
- **Barium sulfate** — used by Tech Ingredients as a pigment in a passive radiant cooling paint

## Techniques and Methods
- **Passive radiant cooling** — reflecting infrared radiation through the atmospheric transparency window back into space to cool surfaces below
- **Glass overlay on solar panels** — placing a sheet of low-iron glass directly on top of a solar cell to combine IR reflection with electricity generation
- **Comparative efficiency measurement** — measuring solar cell output with and without the glass layer to quantify the absorption loss and net gain
- **Vertical solar panel mounting** — referenced Dutch technique of hanging panels vertically to passively cool them and achieve anomalous efficiency gains
