## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds and demonstrates a "floating magnet generator" — a wind-powered generator that uses magnetic bearings to suspend and spin a rotor with almost no friction. He walks through the construction in detail, showing that the key innovation is the low-friction magnetic bearing system rather than the generation method itself, which is a standard magnet-in-coil dynamo. The device generates millivolts just from a gentle breath of air.

## Key Topics
- Magnetic bearing design that levitates and stabilises the rotor against a glass surface
- Repurposing microwave oven parts (coil and fan) as generator components
- Building with "builder's board" (PVC-foam composite fascia/soffit material)
- How ring magnets create an outward radial force that eliminates rotor friction
- Demonstration of voltage generation by blowing on the device

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Builder's board** — PVC wrap with foam core, sold as roofline fascia/soffit material; used as the structural frame
- **Glass** — small piece used as a low-friction bearing surface
- **Ballpoint pen** — the ball tip rides against the glass to minimise contact friction
- **Neodymium/permanent magnets** — ring magnets and a central magnet (polarised radially, north-south across its face); create the magnetic bearing
- **Microwave oven coil** — salvaged and used as the generator stator
- **Microwave oven fan** — salvaged and used as the rotor body
- **Steel nut** — suggested as an alternative rotor core if a suitable magnet is unavailable
- **Superglue / crazy glue** — used to bond builder's board; noted to form joints stronger than the base material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Magnetic bearing construction** — ring magnets arranged so their repulsive force pushes the rotor radially outward against a glass surface, nearly eliminating axial and radial friction
- **Salvaging microwave oven components** — coil as stator winding, fan as rotor platform
- **Low-tech fabrication of builder's board** — cutting with hacksaw, carpet knife, or kitchen knife; bonding with superglue
- **Alternative rotor core winding** — threading magnets onto a nut as a substitute for a purpose-made radially-polarised magnet
- **Wind-driven operation** — rotor spun by blowing air across the fan blades

## Robert's Own Replies
- **The output is millivolts (mV)**, not volts — confirmed twice in replies.
- **The magnetic bearing is the real point of interest**: *"the only real thing here are the magnetic bearings mate — the generator bit is just a standard generator stuck in some magnetic bearings"* and *"the interesting thing is the bearing not the generation — the generation method is just standard."*
- **Wind-driven in this version**: explicitly confirms it is driven by wind/breath in this demo build.
- **Could be self-turning**: hints that with the right configuration it could spin itself.
- **Wobbling explained**: the rotor was built in four parts that weren't perfectly identical, and the field magnets had slight vertical misalignment — acknowledged as a known imperfection.
- **Pen ink left in intentionally**: the ballpoint pen was not cleaned out on purpose.
- **Scratch-built from scrap is encouraged**: *"you can also scratch build it from scrap laying around — that's even cheaper and you learn more."*
- **On critics**: takes a relaxed attitude — notes the interesting aspect is the bearing principle and defends it as a legitimate dynamo concept.
- **Tin sourcing tip**: modern pewter is 85–99% tin and could be a source; also notes 100 g tin ingots available on eBay for around £10 (in response to an off-topic materials question).
- **Production note**: the 8-minute video took a full day to make, offered in context of declining requests for additional PDFs or write-ups.
