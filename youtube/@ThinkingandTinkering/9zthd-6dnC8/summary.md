## Overview
Robert demonstrates a simple, low-cost jig for making straight cuts on metal cylinders, built from four casters and a block of wood with an angle grinder mounted on a hinged block. The video covers sourcing scrap metal from a local scrapyard, building the jig, and using it to cleanly cut steel fire extinguisher bodies, gas cylinders, and plastic soil pipe.

## Key Topics
- Sourcing scrap metal by visiting scrapyards and simply asking permission to look around
- Building a cylinder-cutting jig from four bench-mounted casters and a block of wood
- Mounting an angle grinder using a hose clip and a hinged wooden block
- Cutting fire extinguisher bodies into usable steel pipe sections
- Cutting the top off a small gas cylinder for use in a stove build
- Cutting large-diameter plastic soil pipe squarely

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel fire extinguisher bodies** — scrap sourced from Reate scrapyard in Kent; used for their tubular steel
- **Gas cylinder (small)** — cut to make a stove component (references video 1407 for safe emptying)
- **Plastic soil pipe** — large-diameter PVC/plastic drainage pipe, demonstrated as a secondary cutting use case

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cylinder-rolling jig** — four casters screwed to a bench (or plank) at the correct spacing so a cylinder rests in them and rotates in a single plane
- **Angle grinder mounting** — grinder fixed to a block of wood using a hose clip, with a hinge at the base allowing it to pivot down onto the workpiece
- **Bench-mounted cutting** — the jig holds round stock steady while it is rotated, producing a consistently square cut
- **Grinding/finishing** — grinder disc attachment used to clean and smooth cut edges after parting
