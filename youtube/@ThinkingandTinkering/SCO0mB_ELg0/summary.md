## Overview
Robert explains the three types of heat transfer (convection, conduction, radiation) and argues that convection — the dominant method used in UK homes — is the least efficient. He builds on his earlier carbon-felt wick oil burner (from video 1680) by replacing the ceramic plant pot with a stainless steel saucepan, demonstrating it reaches 53°C in 5 minutes and 77–78°C in 10 minutes, making it a more effective radiant heater.

## Key Topics
- The three modes of heat transfer: convection, conduction, and radiation — and why radiation is the most direct and efficient for personal warming
- Why convection heating is wasteful (warm air rises to the ceiling, heating unused space)
- How underfloor heating is more efficient due to heating at occupant level
- The ongoing debate about plant pot heaters: whether they work as radiant heaters or are useless for room heating
- Carbon felt as a long-lasting, non-burning wick material (burns only above 400°C)
- Comparison of wick materials: cotton, Kevlar, fiberglass, carbon fiber, and carbon felt
- The improvement: using a stainless steel saucepan (instead of a ceramic pot) as a radiant heat emitter over an oil flame
- Carbon monoxide safety warning
- Vegetable oil and waste oil as cheap, accessible fuel sources

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon felt** — 100% pure carbon, no plastics; used as the wick; durable up to 400°C+
- **Cotton wick** — standard lamp/heater wick; burns, limiting its lifespan
- **Kevlar wick** — non-burning, closely woven; functional but relatively expensive
- **Fiberglass** — mentioned as an alternative wick material
- **Carbon fiber** — mentioned as another potential wick material
- **Vegetable oil** — primary fuel used in the oil burner
- **Lighter oils** — noted to wick particularly well through carbon felt
- **Alcohol** — used as an ignition aid to pre-heat the wick
- **West oil** — mentioned as a cheap fuel option (~£5–10/litre)
- **Waste oil** — discussed as a low-cost fuel source
- **Stainless steel** — material of the saucepan used as the radiant emitter
- **Copper fitting** — used in earlier lamp experiments; conducts heat to the oil to aid wicking
- **Carbon monoxide** — byproduct of combustion; safety hazard flagged explicitly

## Techniques and Methods
- Rolling carbon felt and stuffing it into a petri dish with vegetable oil to create an oil wick burner
- Igniting the wick using either a blowtorch/windproof lighter, or by applying alcohol to the top and burning it off first
- Elevating a stainless steel saucepan on two bricks over the oil flame to create a radiant heat surface
- Temperature monitoring of the saucepan surface (implied use of an infrared or contact thermometer)
- Using a carbon monoxide alarm or adequate ventilation when operating the burner indoors
