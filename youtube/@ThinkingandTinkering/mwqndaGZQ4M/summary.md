## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces and demonstrates a mechanical curiosity: a gear system using two identically-sized gears that nonetheless produces a 2:1 reduction ratio — something long considered impossible. He explains gear ratio fundamentals before revealing and assembling a 3D-printed mechanism that defies the conventional assumption that same-size gears must yield a 1:1 output. As a bonus, the mechanism simultaneously splits power across two axles.

## Key Topics
- Fundamentals of gear ratios: circumference, pitch circle, and tooth count
- The conventional assumption that equal-size gears always give a 1:1 ratio
- A novel mechanism (epicyclic/spur differential in nature) that achieves 2:1 reduction with same-size gears
- Assembly of a 3D-printed demonstration model
- Power splitting across two output axles as an emergent property of the mechanism
- Availability of print files on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed gears, pegs, clips, and stand (printed from a Tinkercad design)
- Glue (used to fix one gear so it cannot rotate relative to its mount)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in Tinkercad
- 3D printing of mechanical components
- Manual assembly: press-fitting pegs, gluing fixed gear, clipping swing arms, attaching handle
- Physical demonstration using a registration dot to track output rotation

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Differential analogy:** Robert clarifies the mechanism is best understood as a type of differential — specifically a **spur differential** — rather than a simple gear pair. He notes that the dot moving during the demo already shows the ratio is not 1:1, implying viewers who are puzzled should think in those terms.
- **Epicyclic confirmation:** In response to a commenter, Robert confirms the mechanism is **epicyclic** in nature, which is an important technical classification.
