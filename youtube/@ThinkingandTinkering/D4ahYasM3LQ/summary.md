## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to design and 3D print spur gears in Tinkercad, then assembles them into a step-up gear train to drive a small serpentine-coil generator. The video combines CAD tutorial with a hands-on build, showing that even a small amount of copper wire and modest magnets can produce a surprising voltage when the gear train greatly multiplies rotational speed.

## Key Topics
- Designing metric spur gears in Tinkercad (module, tooth count, hole drilling)
- Creating a gear train with a step-up ratio (small-to-large-to-small alternating gears)
- Exporting STL files for 3D printing and slicing in Cura
- Building a hand-cranked generator using a serpentine (daisy) coil and neodymium magnets
- Practical reflections on design improvements (longer handle, wider base, bearings)
- Potential applications: handheld torch, wind-up radio

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA/3D-printed plastic** — used for all gear and frame components
- **N35 neodymium magnets** (1 cm × 2 mm) — embedded in the rotor disc, arranged N-S-N-S
- **0.2 mm copper wire** — wound as a serpentine/daisy coil, 50 turns per coil

## Techniques and Methods
- **Tinkercad CAD modelling** — placing ruler, searching the shape library for metric gear, setting module (tooth size) to 2, adjusting tooth count (20 and 40 teeth for 2:1 ratio), drilling an 8 mm axle hole, using Align and Group tools
- **STL export** — exporting from Tinkercad for slicing in Cura (or import into Autodesk Fusion 360)
- **FDM 3D printing** — printing multiple identical gears and frame parts
- **Serpentine/daisy coil winding** — winding wire around a printed former with gaps, then transferring onto the daisy disc
- **Magnet embedding** — pressing N35 magnets into rear indentations of the rotor, alternating polarity
- **Gear train assembly** — alternating small and large gears (small→large→small→large) to achieve high step-up ratio
