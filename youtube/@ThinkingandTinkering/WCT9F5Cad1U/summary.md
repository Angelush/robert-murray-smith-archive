## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the two fundamental categories of water turbines — reaction type and impulse type — and contrasts the Pelton wheel with the Turgo turbine. He covers how each design exploits different properties of water (mass vs. pressure) and which works best under different head and flow conditions. The video concludes by setting up a future project to build a Turgo turbine from spoons, noting its advantage over the Pelton wheel for DIY construction.

## Key Topics
- The two families of water turbine: reaction type (mass-driven) and impulse type (pressure-driven)
- How water head (high, medium, low) and flow rate determine which turbine type is appropriate
- History and design of the Pelton wheel (invented ~1876 by Pelton)
- History and design of the Turgo turbine (developed ~1919 by Turgo)
- Why the Pelton bucket shape is critical — splitting and reversing the jet for maximum efficiency
- Why spoons can simulate a Turgo turbine but not a true Pelton wheel
- Brief mention of the Francis turbine as another turbine type
- Upcoming DIY Turgo turbine build project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Spoons — proposed as DIY bucket elements for building a Turgo turbine

## Techniques and Methods
- Jet impingement on turbine buckets (impulse principle)
- Overshot water wheel configuration (water dropped onto the top of the wheel)
- Undershot water wheel configuration (flow past paddles at the bottom)
- Nozzle focusing to convert head pressure into a high-velocity water jet
- Pelton bucket geometry: jet hits centre ridge, splits into two streams, reverses direction
- Turgo geometry: jet enters at 20–30 degrees to the plane of the wheel, exits on the opposite side
- DIY Turgo construction by overlapping spoons and directing a jet onto them

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed that **Turgo is the surname of the inventor**, not a descriptive term.
- Noted that **Pelton wheels are sometimes built with two to four jets** to increase power output.
- Clarified that **air turbines are similar in principle but air is a compressible fluid**, unlike water, so the analogy only goes so far.
- Regarding Tesla turbine efficiency claims: Robert said he has seen figures ranging from **20% to 95%, with ~60% for gas turbines most commonly cited**, and flagged that there is significant misinformation circulating on that topic.
- Mentioned he personally **got less wet** using the Turgo design (a lighthearted practical observation from the build).
- Suggested viewers search **Google Scholar** for studies on related turbine topics.
- Expressed broader frustration that renewable/low-tech energy solutions exist but "we seem to lack the will" to implement them.
