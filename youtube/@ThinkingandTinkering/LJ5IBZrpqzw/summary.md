## Overview
In Part 6 of his DIY gasification series, Robert Murray-Smith completes the particle filter stage using a repurposed stainless steel vegetable steamer filled with sawdust, then plans out the air cooling stage that will follow. The video emphasises his philosophy of using improvised, recycled materials and designing the gasifier as a modular, transportable system. The episode ends with the air cooler components laid out and ready for fabrication in the next video.

## Key Topics
- Gasifier design philosophy: freedom, invention, and problem-solving with available materials
- Recap of the vortex/cyclone filter stage already built
- Construction and installation of the particle filter using a supermarket vegetable steamer
- Modular, breakdown-able design for portability
- Use of repurposed materials throughout the build (trampoline legs, table legs, display-stand steel stock)
- Planning and layout of the air-cooling radiator stage

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel vegetable steamer** — repurposed as the particle filter housing
- **Sawdust / wood chips** — used as the filter medium inside the steamer
- **Ordinary silicone sealant** — used to seal the steamer stacks (suitable at this stage because gas temperature stays below ~80–90 °C)
- **Silica sealant** — mentioned as the high-temperature alternative used earlier in the build
- **Trampoline legs (steel tube)** — donated by "Luke"; used as all gas pipework throughout the unit
- **Square steel stock, 25 × 50 mm** — sourced from repurposed display stands; used to fabricate the air cooler manifold/headers
- **Table legs (square steel tube)** — used as the cooling tubes in the air cooler

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cyclone / vortex filtration** — gas enters at an angle, outlet pipe below inlet; any vessel (fire extinguisher, cone, drum, propane tank) can serve as the body
- **Particle filtration** — gas forced through a bed of sawdust/wood chips; here achieved by filling the stacked vegetable steamer and sealing with silicone
- **Welding** — cross-section plate welded onto steamer base; holes drilled and table-leg tubes welded into header plates for the air cooler
- **Bolted modular assembly** — sections connect via bolts and slip-fit trampoline-leg pipes for easy breakdown and transport
- **Air cooling (planned)** — gas will pass through table-leg tubes in a radiator-style arrangement, cooled by ambient air contact

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Motivation for the project:** Robert states his primary reason for undertaking the gasifier build was to learn to weld — he considers it well worth the effort.
- **How the gas moves:** The system is *pulled* (drawn) through by the engine at the end, not pressurised — there is not much pressure in the lines.
- **Fuel options:** He does not intend to limit the gasifier to wood; he plans to experiment with various feedstocks to see what works.
- **Sawdust filter maintenance:** He is unsure how often the sawdust will need replacing, but notes that silicone can be used to make reusable gaskets — he may do a dedicated video on that technique.
- **Drain idea:** A commenter suggested adding a drain; Robert found this a useful idea he had not considered himself.
- **Gasket/seal tip:** Acknowledged a commenter's suggestion about gasket materials as a good tip.
