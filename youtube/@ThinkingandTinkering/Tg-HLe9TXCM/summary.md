## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a bio-photovoltaic (biophotovoltaic) solar cell using moss and simple conductive materials. He explains that it is essentially a microbial fuel cell driven by photosynthesis — the microorganisms at plant roots produce electrons — and that the system is carbon-net-neutral because plants consume the CO₂ produced. The build is intentionally simple, aimed at science fair projects and home experimentation, with output best captured via a supercapacitor.

## Key Topics
- What biophotovoltaics are and how they work (photosynthesis + root microorganisms = electricity)
- Reference to prior video (#1991) visiting Kew Gardens on the same subject
- Real-world examples: Plenty (Dutch company using grass on rooftops), Moss FM (experimental station generating ~4.5V), Green Fab Lab Barcelona (moss wall panels, phone charging)
- Why moss is the ideal plant: robust, ubiquitous, moisture-loving
- Step-by-step construction of a biophotovoltaic cell
- Using a supercapacitor to store and discharge the low output
- Practical demonstration: charging a supercapacitor over two days and spinning a motor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Moss** — primary biological material; collected from his roof
- **Graphite foil** — used as the bottom electrode (plant-friendly and conductive)
- **Carbon felt** — upper electrode layer; sourced from odor eaters / cooker hoods
- **Stainless steel mesh** — cited as an alternative electrode material
- **Zeolite clay** — used as separator between electrodes (alternatives: cotton wool, wood shavings)
- **Supercapacitor (500F, 2.7V, from Greencap)** — energy storage device (confirmed in comments)
- **Soil** — mentioned as necessary when using seed-based plants rather than moss

## Techniques and Methods
- Layering electrodes: graphite foil (bottom) → separator (zeolite clay) → carbon felt → moss (top)
- Connecting leads to the two electrode layers
- Slow charging into a supercapacitor over multiple days before drawing load
- Watering the moss regularly to maintain and improve output (moisture is critical)
- Using the stored supercapacitor energy to drive a small DC motor as a demonstration
