## Overview
Robert demonstrates how a bipolar stepper motor — the kind salvaged from a 3D printer — can function as a surprisingly effective high-voltage generator. By spinning the motor by hand and rectifying its two-phase AC output using a pair of full bridge rectifiers wired in parallel, he achieves nearly 30–34 volts DC at very low speed, enough to light an LED and charge a capacitor.

## Key Topics
- Bipolar stepper motors as improvised generators
- High-voltage, low-speed generation characteristics of stepper motors
- Two-phase AC output and full bridge rectification
- Parallel output configuration for combining rectifier outputs
- Practical demonstration: lighting an LED by hand-spinning the motor
- Oscilloscope capture of output pulse voltage

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Bipolar stepper motor (4-wire, 2-coil, 3D printer type)
- Full bridge rectifiers (×2)
- Capacitor (used as output storage)
- LED (used as load indicator)
- Cheap USB oscilloscope

## Techniques and Methods
- Identifying coil pairs by matching wires to the same pole
- Connecting each coil pair to its own full bridge rectifier
- Wiring rectifier outputs in parallel to combine phases into pulsed DC
- Adding output capacitance to smooth/store energy for LED driving
- Using a USB scope to capture and measure open-circuit output voltage
