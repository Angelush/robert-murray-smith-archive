## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a kinetic water turbine experiment using pressurized water fed through a 1.5mm nozzle onto a PC fan impeller blade. Building on his previous video (1487) which tested volume-based flow, this experiment achieves approximately 400 milliwatts (~half a watt) from just one liter of water every two minutes — his best result to date, outperforming Pelton and Turgo turbine designs. He concludes by weighing the trade-offs between pressure-based (kinetic) and gravity-fed (potential energy) approaches.

## Key Topics
- Distinction between potential energy and kinetic energy in water systems
- How increasing head pressure and restricting flow diameter converts potential to kinetic energy
- Continuation of turbine testing series (follows video 1487)
- PC fan blade as a low-cost turbine impeller
- Comparison of kinetic pressure approach vs. Pelton and Turgo turbine designs
- Trade-offs: pressure systems use less water and generate more power, but require existing pressure infrastructure

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PC fan blade** — repurposed as the turbine impeller
- **Water supply at 15–30 PSI** — mains pressure used as the energy source
- **1.5mm diameter nozzle/exit hole** — used to constrict flow and convert pressure to kinetic energy

## Techniques and Methods
- Restricting water flow through a 1.5mm nozzle to increase velocity and kinetic energy
- Measuring electrical output (volts and amps) with a resistive load attached to the turbine
- Comparing results across turbine blade types (PC fan vs. Pelton vs. Turgo)
- Benchmarking water consumption vs. power output (efficiency measurement)
