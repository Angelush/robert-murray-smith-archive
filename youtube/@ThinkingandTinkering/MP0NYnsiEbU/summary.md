## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores whether the blade design from NASA's Ingenuity Mars helicopter — engineered to fly in an atmosphere just 1% the density of Earth's — could be repurposed as an efficient wind turbine blade. He downloads NASA's freely available 3D model, extracts and smooths the blade geometry, 3D prints a set, and conducts a quick outdoor comparison test against a standard wind turbine blade design. The initial test is inconclusive due to low wind speeds but demonstrates the blade does spin, validating the principle that a propeller blade and a wind turbine blade are aerodynamically interchangeable.

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Aerodynamics equivalence (posted twice):** Robert shares a technical explanation confirming that the fundamental aerodynamics of a horizontal-axis wind turbine rotor and a propeller are identical — if the generator were run as a motor, the turbine would produce thrust efficiently. He notes the thrust coefficient at optimal conditions approaches the Betz limit of 8/9. He clarifies that differences in blade shape between propellers and wind turbines arise from scale and speed differences: wind turbine blades must be thicker because rotational speed is low (reducing stress-stiffening benefits) and incoming wind is more turbulent, favouring a thick profile with a smooth stall transition.
- **Blade chirality:** He notes the printed blades are already a mirror image and cautions against flipping them further. He adds that a left-hand vs. right-hand mirror simply reverses the spin direction, and that there is no functionally "wrong" configuration as long as all blades match.
- **Double rotor:** He agrees with a commenter's suggestion and expresses interest in a double-rotor set design.
- **Print time:** He mentions it takes approximately 14 hours to print a copy of the blade assembly.
