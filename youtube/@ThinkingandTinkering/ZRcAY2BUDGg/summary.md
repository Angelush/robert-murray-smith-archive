## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the Hoberman Sphere (expanding ball toy), explaining the deceptively simple "Hoberman mechanism" — a pair of bent rods with a central pivot — that underpins it. He then walks through designing and 3D printing the interlocking linkage parts in Tinkercad, from raw bar primitives to snap-fit connectors. His central thesis is that true genius lies in the elegant application of simplicity to produce unexpected complexity.

## Key Topics
- History of the Hoberman Sphere: invented in 1990 by Chuck Hoberman
- The Hoberman mechanism: a scissors-like scissor linkage (two bent rods, one central pivot)
- Step-by-step CAD design of the linkage bar in Tinkercad
- Creating semicircular bar ends using the semicylinder primitive
- Using work planes for precise alignment
- Adding pivot-point reference holes
- Using Tinkercad's built-in snap-and-socket primitives as pivot connectors
- 3D printing 30 identical pieces and assembling them into a sphere
- Philosophy: simplicity compounded produces emergent complexity; genius is the elegant use of simplicity

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD modelling in Tinkercad: building bar linkages from cube and semicylinder primitives
- Work-plane trick for aligning objects to a shared plane (holding the mouse key while moving the work plane, then pressing D)
- Mirror and copy operations in Tinkercad for symmetric parts
- Snap-and-socket fitting (built-in Tinkercad primitive) used as pivot joints at bar ends and centre
- Snap-hole-free-spinning variant for the receiving socket
- 3D printing the designed parts
- Physical assembly: interlocking snap fittings to form scissors, then arranging six scissor units into a sphere
