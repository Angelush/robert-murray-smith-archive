## Overview
A fan named Carl shares his entry for Robert Murray-Smith's "Made From Paper" competition, in which he attempted to build a robotic arm constructed almost entirely from paper and cardboard. The stepper motors lacked sufficient torque to drive the arm due to a pulley size mismatch, but the compliant paper flex-joints he designed worked remarkably well, offering a wide, low-friction range of motion.

## Key Topics
- Competition entry: building a robotic arm from paper with at least one moving part
- Stepper motor selection and torque limitations
- Pulley size mismatch causing mechanical disadvantage
- Compliant/flexible joints made from paper flexures
- Lessons learned for future iterations

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Paper (rolled and flat, used for pulleys, arms, and flexures)
- Cardboard (structural components)
- Glue (used throughout assembly)

## Techniques and Methods
- Rolling paper into cylindrical pulleys
- Constructing compliant flex-joints by wrapping paper flexures across joints to achieve wide range of motion
- Belt-and-pulley drive system for motor power transmission
- Glue assembly of paper and cardboard structural components
