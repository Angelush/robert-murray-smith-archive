## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to design and 3D print a functional steampunk-style pencil sharpener inspired by the Victorian-era "Gem" pencil sharpener patent. The video covers how to model spur gears and bevel gears in TinkerCad, then walks through assembling the printed parts with bearings, sandpaper, and glue into a fully working sharpener. The result is a deliberately over-engineered, durable mechanical device that Robert presents as both a practical tool and a gift-worthy object.

## Key Topics
- History and principles of gears (spur gears and bevel gears)
- Using TinkerCad to design metric gears and cone metric gears
- Gear parameters: module, number of teeth, pitch angle
- The Victorian "Gem" pencil sharpener as design inspiration
- Step-by-step assembly of the 3D-printed pencil sharpener
- Practical notes on 3D printing tolerances and gear meshing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar 3D-printing plastic (implied throughout)
- 6× ball bearings (20 mm × 15 mm × 4 mm)
- Sandpaper — Lived Green, 120 grit (used as the sharpening abrasive disc)
- Glue (used to fix handle pin, cap ring, and pillar assemblies)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD modelling in TinkerCad (metric gear and cone metric gear shapes)
- Adjusting gear module and tooth count to control size and tooth profile
- Boolean subtraction in TinkerCad to add a centre shaft hole to a gear
- Setting bevel gear cone angle (65°) to produce a 130° included angle between mating gears
- Press-fitting bearings into printed pillar housings
- Cutting sandpaper into a circular disc with a centre hole for the sharpening plate
- Positional fitting of the third pillar assembly using a sharpened pencil as a reference before gluing
