## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple, low-cost home method for producing graphene by blending graphite powder with water, soap (surfactant), and isopropanol in a standard kitchen blender. The method is based on a 2014 Nature Materials paper from Trinity College Dublin on shear exfoliation of graphite in liquid. The key takeaway is that this "green and safe" approach can yield usable few-layer graphene suitable for inks, conductive plastics, and transparent films.

## Key Topics
- Liquid-phase shear exfoliation as a graphene production method
- Scaling up graphene production cheaply and safely at home
- Role of surfactants in preventing graphene flakes from restacking
- Downstream applications: conductive inks, reinforced plastics, transparent conductive films
- Settling vs. centrifugation for separating graphene flakes by size
- Recycling the sediment to improve yield across multiple runs

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Micronized graphite powder** (5 microns or less; up to 350 micron also usable) — feedstock for exfoliation
- **Deionized water** — preferred solvent (tap water acceptable for low-grade results)
- **Eco foam soap ("echo")** — Robert's own soap product, used as surfactant; Fairy Liquid also tested successfully
- **Sodium cholate / sodium colate** — bile salt surfactant used in the original academic paper; also available as ox bile tablets
- **Isopropanol / isopropyl alcohol** — added at ~1:4 ratio to defoam the blend
- **NMP, DMF** — mentioned as alternative solvents for redispersing the final graphene
- **Nickel or iron salts** — mentioned (in comments) as catalysts needed to graphitize other carbons

## Techniques and Methods
- **Shear exfoliation in liquid** — blending graphite with surfactant solution at high speed for 20–75 minutes
- **Defoaming** — adding isopropanol (250 ml per liter) to suppress foam during blending
- **Gravity settling** — leaving the blended suspension for several days to separate flake sizes in lieu of a centrifuge
- **Decanting and reprocessing** — pouring off the supernatant and reusing the sediment repeatedly to improve yield
- **Vacuum filtration** — mentioned from the source paper as a method to form transparent conductive films
- **Centrifugation** — referenced as the lab method from the paper for size separation
- **Annealing** — noted in comments as necessary to remove residual soap after deposition on a surface

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Surfactant function**: Confirms the soap acts as a surfactant to prevent graphene flakes from restacking via van der Waals forces.
- **Soap type**: States that ordinary dish soap (essentially sodium lauryl sulphate) works fine; recommends consulting the Trinity College Dublin paper for specifics.
- **Water quality**: Tap water is acceptable for casual experimentation, but deionized water is necessary for a usable, higher-quality product.
- **Drying warning**: Strongly advises against drying the graphene — it will restack into graphite. Keep it in suspension until needed.
- **Resistance values**: Expects ~10–100 ohms per square for graphene films made this way; outside that range suggests a process or materials problem.
- **Separation**: Centrifuge or filtration both work; filtration can produce graphene paper useful for supercapacitors.
- **Long blending runs**: For 75-minute runs, warns that temperature approaches boiling — active cooling needed.
- **Annealing**: If depositing on a surface, the soap must be removed by annealing afterward.
- **Scale-up**: Notes he would use a high-shear homogeniser for larger production volumes.
- **Academic reference**: Cites the full paper — Paton, Varrla et al., *Nature Materials* 13, 624–630 (2014), doi:10.1038/nmat3944 — in response to a sceptic, defending the method's validity.
- **Graphitizing other carbons**: Requires nickel or iron salt catalysts under hydrothermal conditions — not achievable with this blender method.
- **Overall assessment**: Reports good yield and fine conductivity from this method, though still an order of magnitude below his other graphene production techniques.
