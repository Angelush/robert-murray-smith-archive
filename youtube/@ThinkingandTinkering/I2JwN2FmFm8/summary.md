## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith describes his proprietary solution-based method for producing graphene from graphite and proposes that this same method could be integrated into commercial graphite mining operations to improve both yield and product purity. He walks through conventional graphite mining and processing techniques before explaining how his cheap, safe, and reusable solvent system could serve as a post-mining refining step or even produce graphene as a value-added product at the mine head. He closes by actively seeking investors or commercial partners for this application.

## Key Topics
- Graphene production for energy storage devices (batteries and supercapacitors)
- Robert's undisclosed proprietary solution-based graphene exfoliation method
- Types of crystalline carbon and graphite (amorphous, large flake, vein)
- Conventional graphite mining: crushing to liberation size, flotation, air classification
- Graphite purity testing via loss on ignition
- Chemical leaching for purity beyond 94%
- Proposed integration of his solvent method into mining workflows
- Graphite end markets (crucibles/furnaces, batteries, gaskets, lubricants)
- IP licensing and investment opportunity

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — the raw material; occurs as amorphous, large flake, and vein forms
- **Graphene** — the target product, produced by exfoliating graphite in solution
- **Kerosene** — used as a flotation aid in conventional graphite processing
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — added during flotation to adjust pH and stabilize foam
- **Robert's undisclosed proprietary solvent** — described as extremely safe (drinkable), cheap, and reusable; dissolves/peels graphite flakes while non-graphite material sinks

## Techniques and Methods
- **Solution-based graphene exfoliation** — graphite is added to a proprietary solvent, left for 1–2 days, sediment discarded or reprocessed, and graphene filtered out
- **Crushing to liberation size** — rock crushed to the point where graphite flakes are freed from the host rock without over-crushing (which smears and degrades flakes)
- **Froth flotation** — crushed ore mixed with water, kerosene, and NaOH; hydrophobic graphite floats and is skimmed off in two passes (tailings → middlings → concentrate up to ~94% carbon)
- **Air classification** — zigzag air-blown separation using a cyclone, used as a water-free alternative to the first flotation stage
- **Loss on ignition (LOI)** — simple purity test; graphite burns off between 400–600 °C in an oxidising atmosphere; residue mass gives carbon content percentage
- **Pre-heating for amorphous graphite** — volatiles driven off before the main ignition step
- **Chemical leaching** — used to push purity beyond 94% by dissolving remaining mineral impurities
- **High-temperature recrystallisation** — graphite melts at ~3,550 °C in non-oxidising conditions; used historically to purify/recrystallise graphite
