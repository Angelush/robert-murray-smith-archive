## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores research from Australia showing that biochar made from spent coffee grounds can be added to concrete to make it up to 30% stronger. He explains the science behind pozzolanic materials dating back to Roman concrete, then demonstrates his own pyrolysis process for converting coffee grounds into biochar. The video highlights both the strength benefits and the broader environmental advantages of this technique.

## Key Topics
- The global scale of coffee ground waste (~10 billion kg/year, mostly landfilled)
- Roman concrete and the role of pozzolanic (volcanic) ash in its durability
- Why raw coffee grounds cannot be added directly to concrete (organic chemicals weaken it)
- Pyrolysis as the processing step: converting coffee grounds into biochar
- Substituting 15% of sand by volume with coffee biochar to achieve 30% stronger concrete
- The environmental problem of spherical sand scarcity and how biochar enables use of desert/jagged sand
- Carbon sequestration as an added benefit

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Coffee grounds** — the waste feedstock converted into biochar
- **Biochar** — porous, carbon-rich charcoal produced by pyrolyzing coffee grounds; added to concrete at 15% sand replacement by volume
- **Pozzolana / pozzolanic ash** — volcanic alumino-silicate ash used by Romans; reacts with lime and water to form binding compounds
- **Calcium hydroxide (lime)** — reacts with pozzolana in Roman-style concrete
- **Calcium silicate hydrate** — insoluble binding compound formed from the pozzolanic reaction
- **Calcium aluminate hydrate** — second binding compound from the pozzolanic reaction
- **Activated carbon** — used in Robert's kiln setup to absorb noxious off-gassing during pyrolysis
- **Cement** — binder in modern concrete
- **Sand** — aggregate in concrete; normally must be spherical (river/seabed); biochar allows use of angular/desert sand

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pyrolysis** — heating coffee grounds to ~350°C (660°F) without oxygen for ~4 hours to produce biochar; Robert demonstrates this using a sealed pot inside a kiln, surrounded by activated carbon to exclude oxygen and absorb off-gases
- **Biochar incorporation** — replacing 15% by volume of sand in a concrete mix with the resulting coffee-ground biochar
- **Sieving and washing** — post-pyrolysis processing of the biochar before use
- **Kiln construction** — referenced from a prior video series; Robert uses a large sealed pot as an improvised retort
