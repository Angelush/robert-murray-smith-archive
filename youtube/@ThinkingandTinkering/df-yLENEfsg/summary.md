## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a 3D-printed differential inspired by Leonardo da Vinci's 15th-century design, which da Vinci used in his spring-driven cart. The video explores the history of the differential — from ancient China and the Antikythera mechanism through to da Vinci — and demonstrates how lantern-and-pinion gears (the pre-bevel-gear solution for transmitting power at 90°) can be assembled into a functional differential. Robert's takeaway is that starting with a crude working model is often more productive than striving for complexity from the outset.

## Key Topics
- History of the differential (3rd century AD China, Antikythera mechanism ~87 BC, Leonardo da Vinci ~1400s)
- Da Vinci's spring-driven cart and his use of a differential for cornering
- How differentials work: combining/splitting power across shafts to allow wheels to turn at different speeds
- Lantern and pinion gears as the pre-1820s method for transmitting power at 90°
- The 1820s development of the bevel gear and its impact
- Designing and 3D printing the Da Vinci-inspired differential in Tinkercad
- Step-by-step assembly of the printed parts
- Demonstration of differential action (both outputs turning; one held, other still spinning)
- Files shared on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar 3D-print filament (implied by 3D printing; not explicitly named)
- Glue (used throughout assembly to fix spacers and axles)

## Techniques and Methods
- Tinkercad CAD design of lantern-and-pinion differential components
- FDM 3D printing of all parts (axles, lantern gears, spacers, base plate, turn handle)
- Manual assembly: threading axles, pressing lanterns onto joining pieces, gluing spacers selectively to allow free rotation
- Functional demonstration: hand-turning input handle, restricting one output to verify differential behaviour
