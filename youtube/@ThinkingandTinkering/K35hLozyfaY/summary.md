## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds on his previous video (1007) by experimenting with an improved McKibben pneumatic artificial muscle design. By repositioning a smaller balloon at the top of the braided/mesh sleeve — mimicking how a bicep muscle bunches up with tendons below — he increases contraction from 20% to 30%. His key insight is that concentrating expansion at the top of the muscle, rather than across its full length, is the path to improving McKibben muscle performance.

## Key Topics
- Review of the previous McKibben muscle design using a punch balloon and satsuma bag
- Comparison of standard braided-sleeve McKibben muscles vs. the balloon-based approach
- Biological analogy: bicep muscle vs. tendon structure
- Effect of balloon position and size on contraction distance
- Proposed design improvement: concentrating expansion at the top of the actuator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Punch balloon (large, thick — used in video 1007)
- Standard party balloon (smaller, used in this video)
- Satsuma/citrus mesh bag (acts as the braided sleeve)
- Braided sleeve (commercial McKibben muscle component)
- Silicone tube (used in traditional McKibben muscles)
- Zip ties and tape (for assembly/sealing)
- Air/pneumatic pressure (inflation medium)

## Techniques and Methods
- Constructing a McKibben-style pneumatic artificial muscle from a balloon inserted into a mesh bag
- Attaching balloon to a connector and securing with zip ties
- Comparing contraction length between two balloon configurations in a consistent experimental apparatus
- Observational comparison of contraction percentages (measuring displacement)
