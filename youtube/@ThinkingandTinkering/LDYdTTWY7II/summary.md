## Overview
Robert explores a salvaged BMW brushless DC fan motor obtained from Canterbury Automotive, testing its potential as a three-phase generator. He demonstrates that while it produces very little output at low speed (micro-amps by hand), it generates a useful 3–4 volts at ~0.6A when spun at speed using a bicycle wheel as a gear mechanism, concluding that the key to making it viable is a high gear-ratio drive system.

## Key Topics
- Identifying a BMW brushless DC motor vs. typical brushed 12V fan motors
- Disassembly: removing silicone sealant, Torx screws, and dead electronics board
- Coil and magnet arrangement of the motor (three-phase windings, ceramic magnets, thick copper)
- Using a rectifier bridge to convert three-phase AC output to DC
- Gear ratio concept: using a bicycle wheel against the small motor hub to achieve high RPM
- Measured electrical output at various speeds

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Silicone sealant (used to seal the motor casing; removed with a knife)
- Ceramic magnets (internal permanent magnets)
- Thick copper windings (three-phase coil set)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly using a knife (to dig out silicone) and Torx screwdriver
- Three-phase rectifier bridge to convert AC generator output to DC
- Improvised gear-up mechanism: motor shaft pressed against an inverted bicycle wheel to increase rotational speed
