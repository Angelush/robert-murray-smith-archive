## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a proof-of-concept axial flux generator built from salvaged parts of a Reebok exercise bike. He attaches 10 permanent magnets from the bike's resistance mechanism to the outside of the flywheel, positions a coil salvaged from a synchronous microwave motor beside them, and pedals to generate electricity. With just one coil, the setup produces up to 26V and ~2W, with Robert calculating that a full ring of 30 coils could theoretically yield around 60W.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a Reebok exercise bike's magnetic resistance assembly as a generator
- Axial flux generator design concept
- Using salvaged/found parts to keep costs near zero
- Voltage and current measurements from the prototype
- Scaling the design (more magnets and coils) for higher power output
- Potential applications including wind turbines and roof ventilators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Permanent magnets** — 10 chunky magnets salvaged from the bike's magnetic resistance shoe, attached to the flywheel rim
- **Super glue** — used to bond magnets to the steel flywheel
- **Steel flywheel** — acts as a backing surface; magnets grip it magnetically before gluing
- **Synchronous motor coil** (from a microwave) — used as the pickup coil in the generator
- **Wire** (flexible/inflexible) — used to measure and mark equal spacing around the flywheel
- **Nylon strapping** — mentioned as a recommended (but not yet fitted) band to retain magnets at high speed
- **Diode** — mentioned for rectification to charge a battery
- **Steel washers** — mentioned as an option for rebalancing the flywheel if needed

## Techniques and Methods
- **Axial flux generator construction** — magnets mounted on the face/rim of a spinning flywheel passing a stationary coil
- **Flywheel circumference marking** — wrapping wire around the rim, measuring total length, dividing by number of magnets to find equal spacing
- **Magnet bonding** — super glue plus magnetic attraction to steel as retention; nylon band suggested as additional safety measure
- **Full-wave rectification** — proposed to convert the alternating output (north-south magnet pairs produce AC) to DC for battery charging
- **Power calculation** — multiplying measured voltage (~26V) × current (~80–97 mA) to derive wattage (~2W per coil), then extrapolating to a full 30-coil ring (~60W)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Wind turbine application**: Robert sees the axial flux design as directly applicable to a wind turbine — it just needs more coils and magnets.
- **Roof ventilator**: He explicitly describes this prototype as "a trial run for the roof ventilator," suggesting a planned follow-up project.
- **Scaling**: He confirms ~30 magnets/coils in total would be needed for a complete ring.
- **Next steps**: He intends to buy copper wire to wind more coils and plans to test further configurations.
- **Powering everyday devices**: He mentions a computer as one of the things such a generator could realistically power.
- **Community support**: He genuinely hopes followers will donate spare magnets and coils so he can build the full version.
- **Detailed explanation pending**: He notes the full technical explanation is too long for a comment and promises a dedicated video.
