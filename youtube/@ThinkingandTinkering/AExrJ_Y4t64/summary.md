## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to significantly increase the strength of FDM 3D printed parts using two key strategies: orienting the print grain in the direction of applied force, and infiltrating PLA prints with cyanoacrylate (super glue) and an activator to create a composite material. The video concludes that even cheap PLA prints can be made surprisingly difficult to break with these simple techniques.

## Key Topics
- The inherent anisotropy of FDM 3D prints and how grain direction affects strength
- Comparing tensile strength of bones (test pieces) printed with different grain orientations
- Creating a PLA/super glue composite by infiltrating the print's layer lines with cyanoacrylate
- Using a cyanoacrylate activator (accelerator) to cure the glue quickly without finger-sticking
- Why oven-heating to fuse layers is a bad idea (warping and distortion)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA (Polylactic Acid)** — primary filament used; noted as cheap and freely available to Robert
- **Cyanoacrylate (super glue / crazy glue)** — used to infiltrate layer lines and form a composite with PLA
- **Cyanoacrylate activator (accelerator spray)** — used to cure the super glue rapidly in situ
- **Nylon** — mentioned in passing as the material used for gears in desktop printers

## Techniques and Methods
- **Grain orientation printing** — rotating the part in the slicer so layer lines run parallel to the primary stress direction, dramatically increasing tensile strength
- **Standard tensile test piece (dogbone/bone specimen)** — used to demonstrate and compare strength in different print orientations
- **Cyanoacrylate infiltration** — coating the printed part in super glue, allowing it to wick into layer gaps, then spraying with activator to cure and form a PLA–glue composite
- **Print settings noted for reference** — 0.2 mm nozzle, 0.2 mm layer height, 20% infill
