## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith shares a simple tip for improving 3D print adhesion: applying a glue stick to the print bed. He explains that as prints grew larger and approached the edges of his heated bed, uneven heat distribution caused lifting, and a glue stick solved the problem entirely. He also breaks down the chemistry of common glue sticks to demystify what they actually contain.

## Key Topics
- Print bed adhesion problems caused by uneven heat distribution on larger prints
- Using glue sticks (specifically Elmer's/Elu and Pritt brands) as a bed adhesion solution
- Chemical composition of modern glue sticks
- Comparison between branded "3D printer bed glue" products and standard glue sticks
- Application and removal technique for glue on a print bed

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVP (polyvinylpyrrolidone) / PVA (polyvinyl alcohol)** — traditional active adhesive ingredients in glue sticks
- **Potato starch and sugar** — 40% of Pritt's reformulated glue stick formula
- **Water** — 40% of the Pritt formula
- **Soap** — 10% of the Pritt formula; also makes it easy to wash off
- **Polyethylene glycol** — ~2%, used as an antifreeze/plasticiser to keep the stick from drying out
- **Isopropanol (isopropyl alcohol)** — used to release prints that have stuck too firmly to the bed

## Techniques and Methods
- Applying glue stick to a heated print bed (waiting for bed to reach temperature first, then wiping back and forth for an even coat)
- Raising bed temperature by 5°C as an alternative workaround for edge cooling (noted as imperfect)
- Using isopropanol spray to release over-adhered prints without damage
