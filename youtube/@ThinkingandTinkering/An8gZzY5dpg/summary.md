## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the limitations of electric vehicles and presents an alternative hydrogen generation system using aluminium and water (or potassium hydroxide). He demonstrates the reaction chemistry live, then traces the history of a promising 1987 patent by François Cornish — validated by BMW — that produced hydrogen on demand from aluminium and water, and asks why this elegant system never went anywhere.

## Key Topics
- Shortcomings of electric cars (cost, insurance, charging infrastructure, value retention)
- Japan's focus on hydrogen as an alternative
- The Yeno method: producing hydrogen from aluminium and potassium hydroxide
- Electrochemical energy and heat as co-products of the aluminium/KOH reaction
- The François Cornish patent (US 4702894, 1987) and its adaptation for vehicles
- BMW's proof-of-concept testing of the Cornish system
- Ingenuity (Israeli company, 2005–2007) and their expanded patent covering aluminium and magnesium
- Hydrogen-on-demand as a concept for vehicle propulsion

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — 5 mol concentrated solution; reacts with aluminium to produce hydrogen
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — mentioned as an alternative to KOH in the Yeno method
- **Aluminium (foil / wire / sheet)** — primary fuel/reactant consumed in the reaction
- **Potassium aluminate** — solid byproduct that sinks to the bottom of the reaction vessel
- **Water** — used in the Cornish/BMW version of the system instead of KOH solution
- **Aluminium oxide** — byproduct in the water-based version; noted as recyclable back to metal
- **Magnesium** — included in Ingenuity's expanded patent alongside aluminium
- **Hydrogen (H₂)** — the fuel gas produced, drawn off for combustion or fuel cell use

## Techniques and Methods
- Live bench demonstration of aluminium reacting in concentrated KOH solution (Yeno method)
- Electrochemical cell setup using a stainless steel pot as one electrode and aluminium as the other to extract electrical current simultaneously with hydrogen generation
- Cornish spark-erosion method: passing high-voltage current through an aluminium wire submerged in water, using a spark and rotating drum to ablate the oxide layer and expose fresh aluminium
- Hydrogen collection by drawing off gas produced at the reaction site
- Aluminium oxide allowed to sediment to the bottom of the tank for later removal/recycling
