## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to reduce graphene oxide back to graphene using a DIY pulse-light (photonic curing) unit he built. He applies a dilute graphene oxide solution to paper and plastic substrates, then flashes them with the pulse light to restore electrical conductivity. The video shows this approach is faster and more flexible than the previously popular LightScribe DVD drive method.

## Key Topics
- Photonic reduction of graphene oxide to graphene
- Comparison with the LightScribe DVD drive reduction method
- Drop-casting graphene oxide onto paper and plastic substrates
- Measuring electrical conductivity/resistance of reduced films
- Potential applications: transparent conductive films

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide** — in aqueous solution at ~0.5 g/L (described as a very weak solution), used as the starting material
- **Acetone** — used to clean the plastic substrate before drop-casting
- **Paper** — used as a substrate for drop-casting
- **Plastic (PET)** — used as a second substrate, acetone-washed prior to coating

## Techniques and Methods
- **Photonic curing / pulse-light reduction** — xenon flash lamp unit used to reduce graphene oxide to graphene in ~10–30 flashes (~30 seconds)
- **Drop-casting** — graphene oxide solution applied to substrates, spread around, and left overnight to dry
- **Sheet resistance measurement** — resistance tested with a multimeter at the 200 MΩ range before and after photonic treatment
- **Inkjet printing** — mentioned as a viable deposition route for graphene oxide on paper prior to flash reduction

## Robert's Own Replies
- The flash lamp uses a **xenon bulb** (confirmed in reply: "yep - it's xenon").
- He prefers the term **"photonic reduction"** over alternatives to avoid double entendre.
- He acknowledges the graphene oxide solution is **thin enough to go into an inkjet cartridge**, and considers that the likely route forward; also notes a commenter's resist-patterning idea is "very cool."
- He clarifies he is **measuring square (sheet) resistance**, not bulk resistance, and points viewers to his earlier video *"Conductive Ink Technical Info And Test Procedures"* at timestamp 6:25 for more detail.
- He shared a link to a related 2014 RSC *Nanoscale* paper as further reading.
- He notes that plasma treatment, while interesting, is **"a bit beyond the kitchen experimenter"** and fits his ethos of keeping experiments reproducible by anyone.
- He indicates he hasn't explored certain advanced routes because source materials are **not easy for the average person to obtain**.
