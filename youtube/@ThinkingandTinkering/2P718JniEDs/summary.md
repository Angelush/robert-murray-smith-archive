## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to construct a simple zinc-iodine gravity battery using minimal materials: a zinc foil anode, a copper mesh cathode wrapped in conductive HDPE plastic with an activated carbon cloth layer, and a zinc iodide electrolyte. The battery stores energy in the volume of electrolyte rather than the electrodes, charges at 1.2V (or higher), and is shown powering a small motor. The main takeaway is that this chemistry is easy to scale and build but best suited to continuous-load applications due to self-discharge from ionic diffusion.

## Key Topics
- Construction of a zinc-iodine gravity battery from scratch
- Role of the electrolyte as the primary energy store (volume = capacity)
- Electrode function: surface area determines current output, not energy capacity
- Charging mechanism as a plating battery (zinc deposits on negative, iodine on positive)
- Zinc dendrite formation during high-voltage charging and why it doesn't cause short circuits in this cell geometry
- Self-discharge caused by diffusion of iodine through the electrolyte
- Optimal use case: continuous/full-load operation rather than intermittent use
- Lithium-iodide chemistry as a related technology (used in pacemakers)
- Planned future work: gelling the electrolyte to reduce self-discharge

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zinc foil** — used as the anode; heavy, so it sits at the top of the gravity cell
- **Zinc iodide (ZnI₂) electrolyte** — made from zinc and iodine; excess iodine gives a yellow tint
- **Iodine** — active halogen species; excess visible as yellow coloration in electrolyte
- **Copper mesh** — core of the cathode
- **Carbon black-filled high-density polyethylene (HDPE)** — conductive and chemically resistant wrapping for the cathode; protects copper from iodine attack
- **Activated carbon cloth** — from an air filter; heat-sealed onto the cathode face to trap iodine in pores and improve performance
- **Carbon (general)** — noted for its high affinity for iodine

## Techniques and Methods
- **Gravity cell construction** — dense electrolyte sinks, zinc anode floats on top, cathode sits at the bottom
- **Heat sealing** — household iron used to seal carbon black HDPE around the copper mesh cathode and bond activated carbon cloth to it
- **Plating-mode charging** — zinc electrodeposited onto the negative electrode, iodine collected on the positive; current-driven rather than voltage-limited
- **Overvoltage charging** — demonstrated at 5.6V / 1A to speed up deposition, with acknowledged energy losses from uneven plating and dendrite shedding
- **Visual monitoring of iodine** — dark purple cloud observed forming at the cathode during charging, confirming iodine deposition
