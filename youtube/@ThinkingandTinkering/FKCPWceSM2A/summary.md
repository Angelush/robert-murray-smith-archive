## Overview
Robert installs a 500W electric motor, speed controller, and battery pack into the quad bike he built from two children's bikes in earlier videos. After wiring everything up with a polarity-reversing switch for forward/reverse, he takes it for a first test ride in a car park and declares it a success — fun to ride, a "real head turner," but identifies it clearly as a prototype needing better batteries and some ergonomic improvements.

## Key Topics
- Installing the motor, throttle, and motor controller into the completed quad frame
- Wiring the 24V battery pack from two 12V packs built in a previous video
- How the polarity-reversing switch provides forward, reverse, and neutral
- First test ride and performance impressions (~10–15 mph)
- Critique of the current design and planned improvements (battery capacity, crossbar removal, recessed battery well)
- Possibility of a bamboo version and a "girls' bike" step-through frame for the next build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **500W 12V DC motor** — sourced from eBay for £20, the primary drive unit
- **HiQuick batteries** — two 12V packs wired in series to provide 24V, previously assembled in video #999
- **Speed controller (motor controller)** — off-the-shelf unit, described as essentially plug-and-play
- **Twist throttle** — for speed control
- **Polarity-reversing switch** — switches motor terminal polarity for forward/reverse/neutral
- **Chain and rear sprocket** — chain drive from motor to rear axle
- **Scaffold pole** — used as a structural cross-member to create a stable square frame
- **Bamboo** — mentioned as a candidate material for a future build

## Techniques and Methods
- **Chain drive** — motor output connected to rear sprocket via chain
- **Series battery wiring** — two 12V packs connected in series to achieve 24V
- **Polarity reversal switching** — wiring a DPDT-style reversing switch between controller and motor to enable reverse without a reversing-capable controller
- **Shelf fabrication inside frame** — fitting a mounting platform under the seat for motor, controller, and batteries
- **Frame stabilisation with pipe** — a single length of pipe run between two crank positions to create a rigid square frame from the donor bike geometry
