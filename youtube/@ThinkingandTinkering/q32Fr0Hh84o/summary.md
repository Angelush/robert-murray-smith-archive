## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a graphite-based conductive ink at home using simple, safe ingredients. He explains the three-component framework of conductive inks (active material, binder, carrier) and walks through the full recipe and mixing process. The video concludes with a resistance measurement and comparison against a commercial conductive ink product.

## Key Topics
- The three-component structure of conductive inks: active material, binder, and carrier
- Recipe and preparation steps for a home-made graphite ink using gum arabic
- Importance of graphite particle quality and size for conductivity
- Heating and mixing techniques to ensure proper dispersion
- Measuring sheet resistance using a homemade square probe
- Normalizing conductivity readings to ohms per square per mil
- Comparison of the homemade ink vs. a commercial "Working Ink" product

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **5-micron flake graphite** — the conductive active material; available on eBay as lock lubricant
- **Gum arabic** — binder; same as used in watercolour paints; not waterproof
- **Water (hot)** — carrier solvent
- **Glycerin** — plasticizer added to the binder solution (3 ml per litre)
- **Oil of cloves** — preservative for the gum arabic solution (1 ml per litre)
- **Listerine** — alternative/additional preservative (~5 ml)
- **Polyurethane (varnish)** — mentioned as an alternative waterproof binder

## Techniques and Methods
- Dissolving gum arabic in hot water (100 g per litre, left for ~1 hour)
- Adding plasticizer (glycerin) and preservative (oil of cloves or Listerine) to the binder
- Heating the binder solution and stirring in graphite powder for ~3 minutes
- Wet grinding using an Indian wet spice grinder with an additional 500 ml water for 1 hour to achieve smooth dispersion
- Painting the ink onto paper and allowing it to dry
- Measuring ink film thickness with a paint thickness meter
- Square probe resistance measurement as a simplified alternative to the four-point probe method
- Calculating normalised sheet resistance: `(measured Ω ÷ 25 µm) × film thickness in µm` to give ohms per square per mil
