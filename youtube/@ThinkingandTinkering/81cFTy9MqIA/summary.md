## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses this video to demonstrate his process for developing DIY batteries, walking through the history from the Daniell cell (1836) to a modern rechargeable alkaline zinc/copper oxide cell. Using a 2021 research paper as a springboard, he builds an improved solid-state variant by incorporating Bird's historical plaster of paris separator technique, resulting in a rechargeable 1.1V cell that outperforms the original Daniell cell.

## Key Topics
- Robert's general methodology for battery research and invention
- History of the Daniell cell and its evolution (1836 to 1960s)
- The Landé cell and Edison's commercialisation of copper oxide batteries
- Using Google Scholar and patent databases for research
- The 2021 paper on rechargeable alkaline zinc/copper oxide batteries
- Role of bismuth trioxide as a performance-enhancing additive
- Bird's adaptation using plaster of paris as a separator/binder
- Construction of a solid-state briquette-style battery
- Bird's historical link to electroplating

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper** — electrode material (cut from water pipe)
- **Zinc** — electrode/anode material (powder and metal)
- **Copper sulfate** — electrolyte in the classic Daniell cell demonstration
- **Zinc sulfate** — electrolyte variant in the Daniell cell
- **Sulfuric acid** — original Daniell cell electrolyte (noted as hazardous)
- **Copper(I) oxide (Cu₂O)** — red powder, used as cathode material; Robert's chosen form
- **Copper(II) oxide (CuO)** — black powder, available as pottery glaze, also usable per the paper
- **Bismuth trioxide (Bi₂O₃)** — "wonder ingredient"; improves rechargeability and stability; also used in MnO₂ and lead-acid cells
- **Graphite** — added to cathode mix for conductivity
- **Zinc oxide (ZnO)** — 10 wt% in the anode mix
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — 45% solution used as alkaline electrolyte
- **Plaster of paris (calcium sulfate / gypsum)** — binder and separator material
- **Carbon black-filled HDPE** — current collector material
- **Aluminium sheet** — used as contact electrodes
- **Cellgard** — separator used in the original paper (not adopted by Robert)

## Techniques and Methods
- Historical literature review as a foundation for battery design
- Google Scholar search for current academic papers (targeting freely accessible supplementary information)
- Patent database searching (Google Patents, Espacenet) with critical interpretation
- Weight percentage formulation of electrode pastes (cathode: 55% CuO, 10% Bi₂O₃, 30% graphite; anode: 83% Zn powder, 10% ZnO, ~20% plaster of paris)
- Casting electrodes as paste onto carbon black-filled HDPE backing sheets
- Using plaster of paris as both a binder for briquette electrodes and as a KOH-impregnated solid separator layer
- Iterative optimisation of plaster of paris content by mechanical testing (strength vs. mass trade-off)
- Recharging at 1.2V per cell via external power supply

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Read the paper**: Robert repeatedly directs commenters to the source paper — *"Rechargeable Alkaline Zinc/Copper Oxide Batteries"*, DOI: `10.1021/acsaem.1c01133` — for in-depth detail rather than relying solely on the video.
- **Copper oxide substitution maths**: He explains the molar mass difference between Cu₂O (143 g/mol, 2 Cu ions) and CuO (79 g/mol, 1 Cu ion) and how to recalculate weight percentages when switching between the two forms.
- **Recharging**: Set a power supply to **1.2V per cell** to recharge.
- **Scaling current/voltage**: "For amps add mass — for volts connect in series."
- **Charging test result**: Noted approximately 12V and ~1A for a split second; energy density is design-specific and must be measured experimentally.
- **Dendrite suppression**: SDS (soap/surfactant) is a simple, effective option; lots of research exists on this.
- **Alternative materials**: Bio-based polymers and other materials could work as binders/separators.
- **The patent system**: Described as "basically broken."
- **Plaster of paris clarification**: It is calcium sulfate, also called gypsum.
- **Intent of the video**: Explicitly meant to encourage experimentation, not to deliver a finished product.
