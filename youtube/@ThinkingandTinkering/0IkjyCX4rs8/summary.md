## Overview
In this short philosophical video, Robert argues that 3D printers are simply another tool in the long history of human tool-making, no more threatening to craftsmanship than the circular saw or the power drill. He contends that creativity and craftsmanship reside in the person, not the tool, and that new tools only broaden the scope of what a person can express. He closes by introducing 3D scanning as the natural next step — bringing the physical world into the computer to complement 3D printing.

## Key Topics
- The history of tool development and humanity's recurring resistance to new tools (citing the Luddites)
- Survivorship bias in how we perceive the quality of historical craftsmanship — only well-made things survived
- The argument that craftsmanship is in the imagination and diligence of the maker, not in whether hand or machine tools are used
- 3D printers framed as democratised desktop tools, comparable in price (~£300–350) to a decent circular saw
- Parallel drawn to the music industry, where PC-based home recording caused a blossoming of garage bands
- Robert's own recent conversion to 3D printing enthusiasm
- 3D scanning as the complementary technology to 3D printing — getting the real world into the computer
- Practical use of 3D scanners by garage mechanics to virtually fit parts before fabrication

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (desktop FDM/resin, used as the central subject)
- 3D scanning (scanning physical objects, e.g. entire cars, to create digital models)
- Virtual fitting — scanning a car or object, designing parts in software, then printing/fabricating to guaranteed fit

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Photogrammetry:** Robert tried photogrammetry and was not particularly impressed with it as a scanning approach.
- **3D printing as a skilled craft:** He pushed back on the idea that 3D printing removes skill, stating he sees it as a tool that, like all tools, requires skill to use well — and that skill is part of the craft.
- **Skill reduction vs. democratisation:** In a longer reply, he argued that the apparent reduction in skill is actually a spreading of skill across a larger number of people — not a genuine decline. He noted that he could build a 3D printer from scratch and repair it fully, and that many young people share that ability. He suggested that professionals in the industry see more unskilled users approaching them simply because the total number of participants has grown, which skews perception.
- **Software and file formats:** He mentioned using TinkerCAD (supports STL, OBJ, and STG formats) and said he keeps meaning to move to Fusion 360 but hasn't got round to it yet. He expressed a "right to repair" philosophy.
- **Printer brand advice:** He said he has only ever used Elegoo printers and that, as far as he can tell, most desktop printers are "much of a muchness" — his advice is to use price as a guide and check reviews for ease of use and longevity.
- **Powdered metal printing:** He queried whether a commenter meant powdered metal printing for home use, noting it already exists in industry.
