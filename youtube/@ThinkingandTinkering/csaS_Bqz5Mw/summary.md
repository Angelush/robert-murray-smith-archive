## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to weld the electrode tabs on a homemade supercapacitor, following up on a previous battery-building video where viewers requested this detail. The device shown weighs 160 grams with six carbon plates and is expected to store around 20 watt-hours per kilogram. The process uses an inexpensive Chinese tab welder operated via a foot pedal, making the technique accessible to home experimenters.

## Key Topics
- Tab welding technique for supercapacitor/battery electrode assembly
- Supercapacitor construction overview (plates, materials, layering)
- Comparison between supercapacitor and battery plate construction
- Use of a low-cost tab welder as an accessible tool
- Kapton tape for alignment and protection during welding
- Next steps: adding electrolyte and performance testing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive plastic** — used as the structural/conductive plate material
- **Carbon foam felt** — electrode material layered on either side of the conductive plastic
- **Stainless steel** — embedded within the conductive plastic for conductivity (316 grade noted as preferable in comments)
- **Kapton tape** — used to hold tabs in alignment before welding
- **HDPE** — referenced approvingly alongside Kapton tape for protection
- **Electrolyte** — to be added after welding (details deferred to next video)
- **Lead acid** (referenced) — discussed only in contrast, as the subject of a prior rejuvenation video

## Techniques and Methods
- **Tab welding** — using a dual-station Chinese tab welder (purchased on eBay) with adjustable power, frequency settings, and a foot pedal trigger
- **Plate assembly** — layering carbon foam felt on either side of conductive plastic/stainless steel to build up electrode plates
- **Kapton tape alignment** — wrapping tabs before welding to keep them flush and orderly
- **Non-conductive work surface** — using stone to prevent burn-through during welding
- **Alternative joining method** (mentioned but discouraged) — punching a hole and using a bolt instead of welding

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Encouraging experimentation:** Robert clarifies he uses his own carbon materials in practice but deliberately uses commercially available components in his videos to make it easier for others to replicate and experiment.
- **Stainless steel grade:** He notes that 316 stainless is preferable, but since the assembly is encapsulated, aluminium could theoretically also be used.
- **Tab welder:** It's a generic cheapo tab welder from eBay — he suggests most types should work fine.
- **Prototype status:** He emphasises this is experimental/prototype work, not a finished product intended to replace commercial batteries. He acknowledges a glued top would be practical and would be done before electrolyte fill to avoid mess.
- **Electrolyte and graphic/paper separator:** Paper is fine as a separator; graphic (printed) paper will soak up electrolyte and become fragile quickly, so plain paper is preferable.
