## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a donated NEC plasma TV, walking through the internal components layer by layer. He explains the basic operating principle of plasma displays and focuses on identifying and recovering reusable components — particularly power electronics, aluminium sheet, speakers, and inductors — for future projects.

## Key Topics
- How plasma displays work (gas cells between glass layers excited by high voltage to produce light)
- Disassembly approach: methodically removing all visible screws
- Component identification across control boards, power boards, and signal boards
- Recovery of salvageable parts from discarded electronics
- Weight and structural characteristics of plasma TVs (~75 kg)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glass** — two printed layers sealed with epoxy forming the plasma cell, described as brittle
- **Aluminium sheet** — large backing plate behind the glass panel, recovered for future use
- **Rubber tape** — used to seal the glass to the aluminium frame (not epoxy as initially expected)
- **White reflective coating** — on the back of the protective glass layer
- **Epoxy** — used to seal the edges of the glass cell layers
- **Magnesium (possible)** — Robert notes one heat sink didn't ring like aluminium; a commenter suggested it may be magnesium cast, and Robert acknowledged this as plausible

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mechanical disassembly** — removing all visible screws systematically; tugging gently to detect missed fasteners
- **Cable connector removal** — depressing locking tab to release flat flex connectors
- **De-soldering via solder pot** — planned method to remove IGBTs/power transistors from boards
- **Component triage** — distinguishing reusable discrete components (transistors, inductors, magnets) from application-specific ICs not worth salvaging
