## Overview
Robert demonstrates how a salvaged 230V DC permanent magnet motor can be repurposed as a generator, explaining that motors and generators are electrically identical machines. Using the motor's nameplate specifications (150W, 230V, 0.6A, 4000 RPM), he walks through the calculations needed to determine torque requirements and expected power output, concluding that gearing is the key mechanical challenge to spinning the motor fast enough for useful generation.

## Key Topics
- Motors and generators as identical electrical machines
- Reading and interpreting motor nameplate ratings
- Voltage-to-RPM relationship (volts per revolution)
- Torque calculation using the formula: Watts × 9.55 ÷ RPM
- Wire gauge, voltage, and current trade-offs in motor windings
- Efficiency losses (~75–80%) when running a motor as a generator
- Gearing requirements to match human-powered input (arms ~60W, legs more) to motor speed
- Physical limits of the motor applying equally when used as a generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- DC permanent magnet motor (230V, 150W, 4000 RPM) salvaged from a lathe with a burned-out length control board

## Techniques and Methods
- Using motor nameplate data to predict generator performance
- Torque calculation: Power (W) × 9.55 ÷ RPM
- Volts-per-RPM calculation to estimate output voltage at low hand-cranking speeds (~80 RPM ≈ 5V)
- Gear chain design to step up low-RPM human input to high motor RPM
- Efficiency derating (~75%) to estimate real-world output power from rated input
