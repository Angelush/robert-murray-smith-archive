## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores two approaches to scaling up his "daisy" spoon turbine: physically enlarging it, or creating a "wind wall" — many small units arranged together. He 3D prints improved blade designs (incorporating a central cone, iterating through several versions with community input) and tests a single unit using a PC fan motor as a generator, achieving approximately 210 milliwatts. The plan is to build 100 such units and combine them into a wind wall.

## Key Topics
- Two strategies for scaling up the daisy/spoon turbine: larger single unit vs. wind wall of many small units
- Advantages of the wind wall approach: cost-effectiveness, redundancy, higher aggregate energy capture
- Iterative 3D-printed blade design process, culminating in a cone-centered final design
- Converting brushless DC PC fan motors into generators
- Live electrical output test using a hair dryer as wind source
- Ongoing 3D printer competition (video 1471)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Resin (absolute resin for 3D printing)
- PC fans (used as generator bases after blade removal)
- Teaspoons (original blade material, still considered a valid low-tech option)
- Brushless DC motor (from PC fans, used as generator)

## Techniques and Methods
- Resin MSLA 3D printing (Elegoo Mars 2 Pro P) for blade fabrication
- Snapping blades off PC fans and repurposing the motor hub as a generator
- Wiring multiple units in series (rows) and parallel (columns) with individual rectifiers per unit
- Testing output with a hair dryer as wind source, measuring volts and milliamps simultaneously with two meters
- Iterative design: cone → scale replica → larger-blade version → shuttlecock version → final cone-centered design

## Robert's Own Replies
Several substantive technical clarifications emerged from Robert's comments:

- **Wiring the wind wall**: Each unit has its own small rectifier; units are wired in series across rows and in parallel across columns.
- **The wall as a shroud/venturi**: The wind wall structure itself acts as an orifice plate — a "poor man's Venturi" — so adding individual cones to each unit in a wall context would yield only a tiny improvement not worth the cost or effort.
- **Encouragement to build on a small scale**: Robert repeatedly nudged commenters to just try ideas themselves with simple materials (teaspoons, cardboard, disposable cups, PC fans, glue) rather than waiting, noting the biggest barrier is rarely tools or space.
- **Series continuation**: The project will continue running in the background, though posting frequency is limited by viewer drop-off when one topic dominates the channel.
- **Community credit**: Robert acknowledged the community's suggestions (spiraling cone indentations, changing vane direction, etc.) as key contributors to the final design iteration.
