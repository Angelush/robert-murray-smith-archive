## Overview
Robert investigates whether a magnetic field can improve the conductivity of a transparent, flexible graphene-based solar cell by aligning the graphene flakes during deposition. He tests two samples dried on a printer's magnet at different orientations (parallel and perpendicular to the field lines) and finds a striking difference in output — 0.1 µA vs 5.6–7 µA — confirming that the diamagnetic nature of graphene causes it to orient under the field, significantly affecting charge transport between the electrodes.

## Key Topics
- Graphene's diamagnetic properties and their practical implications
- Magnetic field alignment of graphene flakes during solution drying
- Effect of graphene flake orientation on electrical conductivity in a thin-film solar cell
- Comparative testing of two electrode orientations (0° and 90° relative to field lines)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — active material in the transparent solar cell, noted as diamagnetic
- **Graphene solution** — applied thinly by drop-casting onto substrates
- **Copper strip** — used as one electrode
- **Aluminium strip** — used as the other electrode
- **Tape** — used to mask/contain the solution during deposition
- **Printer's magnet** — a multi-pole alignment magnet (used in typesetting) serving as the magnetic bed

## Techniques and Methods
- **Drop-casting** — graphene solution deposited onto substrates and allowed to dry naturally (no forced alignment in the control process)
- **Magnetic field-assisted deposition** — substrates taped to a printer's magnet during drying to align graphene flakes via diamagnetic response
- **Two-orientation comparative test** — one sample dried with electrodes parallel to field lines (0°), one perpendicular (90°), to isolate the orientation effect
- **Indoor photocurrent measurement** — samples tested under fluorescent light (~2 cm² active area) with current readings compared directly
