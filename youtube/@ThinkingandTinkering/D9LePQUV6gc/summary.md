## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the science behind liquid phase exfoliation as a method for producing graphene from graphite. He begins with an accessible chemistry primer on how dissolving works (ionic bonds, polar solvents, ligand bonds), then applies those principles to explain how non-polar organic solvents can disrupt the van der Waals forces between graphite sheets without breaking the covalent bonds within them. The video concludes with a practical demonstration showing a stable colloidal graphene suspension produced at home using a cheap 50W ultrasonic bath.

## Key Topics
- How dissolving works: ionic bonds, polar solvents, and ligand formation (using NaCl in water as an example)
- Graphene structure: the ab-axis (honeycomb), c-axis, van der Waals interlayer forces, and covalent/pi bonds
- Why non-polar organic solvents can exfoliate graphene without destroying it
- Using expanded (intercalated) graphite as a superior starting material vs. raw graphite
- Practical setup: glass jar, solvent, bath sonicator, 3-hour sonication process
- Results: stable colloidal suspension of few-layer graphene after 6 weeks

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — starting material; raw graphite and expanded/intercalated graphite both discussed
- **Expanded (exfoliated/intercalated) graphite** — preferred starting material (~200x expanded, sheets much further apart)
- **NMP (N-Methyl-2-pyrrolidone)** — first solvent historically used for liquid phase exfoliation
- **Ethanol (40% in water)** — mentioned as an alternative solvent with modest results
- **Type 10 uPVC cleaner** — Robert's preferred solvent; ~90% ethyl acetate, with traces of hexane, ethanol, and naphtha
- **Ethyl acetate** — primary active component of Type 10 cleaner
- **Hexane, naphtha** — minor components of the Type 10 cleaner solvent mix
- **Water** — used in the sonicator bath (not as the graphene solvent)
- **Sodium chloride (NaCl)** — used as a pedagogical example of ionic bonding

## Techniques and Methods
- **Liquid phase exfoliation (LPE)** — sonicating graphite in an organic solvent to peel off graphene sheets into stable colloidal suspension
- **Bath sonication** — 50W ultrasonic water bath, 8-minute cycle intervals, run for ~3 hours total
- **Intercalation/expansion of graphite** — pre-processing graphite to expand sheet spacing ~200x before exfoliation, reducing energy cost
- **Colloidal suspension characterisation** — visual inspection of suspension stability over 6 weeks as evidence of graphene presence
- **SEM and Raman spectroscopy** — mentioned in comments as the characterisation methods Robert uses to verify graphene
