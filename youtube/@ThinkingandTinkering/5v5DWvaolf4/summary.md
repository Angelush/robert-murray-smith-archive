## Overview
This video (part two of a two-part series, following video 1490) demonstrates Robert's DIY low-head water vortex turbine, built from a hyperbolic-curve bowl, an aluminium cover, and a PC fan motor. He tests both an impeller blade and a boat propeller to see which generates more electricity from the vortex flow, concluding that the impeller outperforms the boat propeller in this configuration.

## Key Topics
- Continuation of the vortex turbine project from video 1490
- How a hyperbolic curve bowl generates a water vortex
- Relative motion and frames of reference as applied to propellers vs. turbines
- Comparison of impeller blade vs. boat propeller performance
- Motor selection: low-torque/high-speed (PC fan) vs. high-torque/low-speed motors for water vs. wind applications
- Low-head, high-flow turbine design principles (analogous to Kaplan and whirlpool turbine designs)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Aluminium** — used as a cover/shroud around the hyperbolic bowl to contain water splash
- **PC fan motor** — used as the generator (noted as pre-characterised for volts/amps at specific speeds)
- **Drain pipe / drainpipe section** — added to complete the turbine structure
- **Isopon car resin (two-part polyurethane resin, sourced from Halfords)** — mentioned in comments as the resin used for the build (suitable for fibreglass work)
- **Boat propeller** — tested as an alternative blade
- **Impeller blade** — primary blade tested; performed better than the boat propeller

## Techniques and Methods
- Constructing a hyperbolic-curve vortex chamber to induce a spinning water column
- Attaching and orienting a propeller/impeller blade to a DC motor shaft for power generation
- Testing blade orientation to ensure correct interaction with vortex rotation direction
- Comparative testing of two blade types (impeller vs. boat propeller) under load
- Using a pre-characterised motor to reduce repeat measurement effort (known volts/speed and amps/torque relationships)
- Bucket-pour testing as a low-cost, DIY method to simulate water flow

## Robert's Own Replies
Several of Robert's comments clarify important design context:

- **Intended operating mode:** The turbine is designed for continuous full-flow, low-head conditions — the stop-start behaviour seen in the video is an artefact of using a bucket rather than a steady water supply. He emphasises this multiple times.
- **Motor suitability:** Acknowledged that the PC fan motor wasn't the ideal choice (low torque), but kept it for simplicity.
- **Resin used:** Isopon car resin (two-part polyurethane, from Halfords) was used in the build; he notes other resins would likely work too.
- **Wildlife/debris friendliness:** One notable advantage of this open vortex design is that solid objects — fish, stones, mud — pass straight through without being trapped or harmed, making it suitable for river deployment.
- **Tesla turbine comparison:** When asked, he noted a Tesla turbine would work very badly in this configuration, as it requires high pressure to operate well.
- **Tolerances:** Acknowledged the build tolerances aren't especially precise.
- **Toilet bowl as vortex former:** Expressed interest in the idea of using a salvaged toilet bowl as a vortex chamber, noting its geometry has been "worked out over centuries."
