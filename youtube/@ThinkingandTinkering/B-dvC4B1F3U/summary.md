## Overview
Robert explores the business potential of FDM 3D printing using the Elegoo Neptune printer, inspired by a seller who made over a million pounds printing and selling chess sets. He walks through the complete workflow — from finding a Doctor Who chess set on Thingiverse to slicing in Cura and printing — to demonstrate that a 3D printer can function as a "business in a box" for almost anyone.

## Key Topics
- 3D printing as a low-cost business opportunity
- Real-world example: a seller who sold 13,000 chess sets at £71 each via eBay using Thingiverse designs
- Step-by-step walkthrough of finding, downloading, and slicing a Doctor Who chess set
- Cura slicer settings for PLA printing (layer height, infill, speed, bed adhesion)
- Cost analysis: ~£3 per chess set (materials + ~18p electricity for a 10-hour print) vs £71 sale price
- Print time as "contact time" vs unattended time
- Robert's completed custom Doctor Who chess set with hand-picked character assignments

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA filament** — primary printing material used; noted to be odourless
- Printing temperature: **220°C** (nozzle), **60°C** (bed plate)

## Techniques and Methods
- Searching and downloading STL files from **Thingiverse** (with Creative Commons attribution licence)
- Importing and arranging models in **Cura** (Elegoo's bundled Cura port)
- Configuring slicer settings: layer height, **15% infill** (honeycomb/grid), print speed (40 mm/s), build plate adhesion (none used for chess pieces)
- Exporting as **G-code** to a micro SD card
- Loading filament through the filament sensor and using the printer's on-screen menu to start a print
- Z-axis levelling adjustment (noted as critical — skipping it caused his only failed print in 4 months)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Printer was free:** Elegoo sent him the printer to try out and make videos about — he admits he probably would never have bought one himself, describing himself as "a bit stubborn when it comes to adopting new things."
- **Print capacity:** The Max model can print around 6 chess sets per day, meaning one printer could theoretically produce 400+ sets over a period — he pushes back on a commenter who questioned the business maths.
- **Reliability:** Only one failed print in 4 months, and that was his own fault — he forgot the Z-axis adjustment after levelling.
- **No supports needed:** For the K9 model, the head and tail angles were sufficient that no support structures were required.
- **Slicer:** The Elegoo Cura slicer (a Cura port) comes bundled with the printer — no need to source separate software.
- **Licencing note:** He acknowledges that checking licences on Thingiverse designs is important before selling — it's the seller's responsibility.
- **FreeCAD:** He mentions he is looking at FreeCAD for designing his own models.
- **Shiny filament:** Confirmed it exists, in response to a commenter asking about aesthetics.
- **Business philosophy:** He emphasises that making a start — even imperfectly — is often more valuable than endlessly planning; many people never act on their ideas.
