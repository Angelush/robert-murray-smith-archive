## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the Poncelet wheel, a 19th-century undershot water wheel design featuring curved blades that eliminate the energy-wasting "bounce back" of traditional flat-blade wheels. The curved blades allow water to enter smoothly, rise briefly, and drain away slowly — extracting up to 80% of the water's kinetic energy compared to roughly 30% for conventional designs. Robert presents it as a compelling option for small-scale home power generation for anyone fortunate enough to have a stream on their property.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of traditional flat-blade water wheels (~30% efficiency due to bounce-back losses)
- History of water wheel improvement: Jean-Charles de Borda and Jean-Victor Poncelet in late 18th/early 19th century France
- How curved blades eliminate bounce-back and improve efficiency
- Comparison of wheel types: undershot, overshot, Poncelet, Pelton, Kaplan turbine, Sagebien, and Zuppinger wheels
- Practical manufacturability and cost advantages of the Poncelet wheel over Pelton/Kaplan designs
- Modern revival efforts, including a boom-arm mounted wheel by Naring Industries
- The Poncelet wheel at Groudle Glen on the Isle of Man as a surviving example

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Curved blade geometry to eliminate water bounce-back on blade impact
- Undershot wheel configuration (water passes beneath the wheel)
- Slotted gap at the wheel base to direct a focused slice of water onto the blades (Poncelet's refinement)
- Boom-arm mounting system for lowering/raising the wheel into a river current
- Overshot wheel method (gravity-fed buckets) noted for comparison
