## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a working model of a Nu Vinci continuously variable transmission (CVT) built from 3D-printed parts and ping-pong balls. He explains how friction-based machines exploit the varying radius of a sphere to achieve a continuously variable gear ratio, and shows the finished model achieving a gear range from 1:1 to 3:1.

## Key Topics
- Friction-based machines and the "slip" characteristic as a feature, not a flaw
- The Nu Vinci CVT operating principle: varying effective radius on a sphere changes gear ratio
- Analogy to great circle routes and pitch circle geometry to explain gear ratios
- Step-by-step assembly of a ping-pong ball CVT model
- Combining CVTs with planetary gearboxes to multiply gear range

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Ping-pong balls — act as the friction spheres at the heart of the CVT
- PLA (3D-printed parts) — main structural material; noted to slip too much without rubber coating
- O-rings (42 mm × 1.5 mm) — provide rubber friction surface on the input/output discs
- Spring (20 mm diameter × 1.2 mm wire thickness) — applies normal force to keep friction surfaces engaged
- Thrust bearings (25 mm ID × 47 mm OD × 11 mm thick) — two used to allow axial loading while permitting rotation
- Glue — used to attach axle stubs to ping-pong balls

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM/PLA) of all structural components and assembly jigs
- Jig-assisted axle alignment to ensure ping-pong ball axles run center-to-center
- Press-fit ball retention in a bevelled plate
- O-ring application to create friction contact surfaces
- Dual opposing spring/thrust-bearing preload to centre spheres and maintain friction engagement
- Rotating a control disc to tilt ball axles and shift gear ratio (manual lever or worm gear suggested)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Slip control is manual, not automatic** — shifting the lever is required; making it automatic would be no different in principle from a conventional automatic gearbox.
- **It is a model of a real product** — the Nu Vinci is an actual device in commercial use, so research exists to answer detailed performance questions.
- **Spring function is dual-purpose** — the springs both apply normal force (as one commenter suggested) *and* centre the spheres so they don't rub on the holder; a simple preload substitution alone would not address the centring role.
- **Friction fluid** — Robert noted he heard Nu Vinci were working with a friction fluid additive to improve drive efficiency.
- **Cross roller bearings preferred** — for any bearing modifications, cross roller bearings would be much better than standard bearings given the spring axial forces involved.
- **Evans CVT** — a related cone-and-disc CVT mentioned by a commenter is called the Evans CVT; Robert says he has covered it in a separate video.
- **Commercial adoption** — the Nu Vinci's path to cars is long; Robert notes it probably found its way into bicycles first for that reason.
