## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains Ohm's Law in conversation with a friend named Luke, arguing that the term "law" is misleading — scientific laws don't *govern* behavior like social laws do, they *describe* it. Using the formula V=IR and everyday analogies (hills, stones, toasters), he demonstrates how resistance is an intrinsic material property that naturally limits current, and briefly addresses the applicability of Ohm's Law to AC circuits.

## Key Topics
- The philosophical distinction between scientific "laws" (descriptions) and social laws (rules that govern behavior)
- Ohm's experimental method: measuring voltage, current, and resistance across wire segments
- The V=IR relationship and the VIR triangle mnemonic
- Resistance as an intrinsic material property
- How resistance naturally limits current (the "stone at the bottom of the hill" concept)
- Practical example: calculating required resistance for a 230V/5A toaster element
- Why Ohm's Law applies to DC but can be approximated for AC with purely resistive loads
- The distinction between resistance (DC) and impedance (AC)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wire (bits of wire used by Ohm in his experiments; toaster heating element wire)
- Manganese dioxide — mentioned in comments as having a *positive* thermal coefficient (becomes more resistive when hotter)
- Graphite — mentioned in comments as having a *negative* thermal coefficient (becomes less resistive when hotter)

## Techniques and Methods
- Measuring voltage, current, and resistance across wire segments of varying length (Ohm's original experimental method)
- Using the VIR triangle as a mnemonic to rearrange the Ohm's Law formula
- Static charge generation by rubbing a cat on a sofa (mentioned humorously as Ohm's original voltage source)
