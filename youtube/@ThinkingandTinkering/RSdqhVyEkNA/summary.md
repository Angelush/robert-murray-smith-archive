## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a discarded office paper shredder by salvaging its internal gearbox and reversing its mechanical advantage: the same gear train that converts high-speed/low-torque motor rotation into slow, powerful cutting action is run in reverse to multiply hand-crank input into high-speed output suitable for driving a generator. The modified gearbox, fitted with a fabricated handle and a small hand-blender motor as a generator, produces approximately 120V and 80mA (~9.6W) from hand cranking alone.

## Key Topics
- The relationship between generator speed, voltage, and power output (power proportional to the square of voltage)
- How paper shredder gearboxes invert speed and torque, and why this is useful in reverse
- Disassembly and inspection of a shredder's gear train and induction motor
- Selecting which components to keep and which to discard
- Fabricating a handle input shaft by welding a dome nut to bent steel pipe
- Differences between induction motors and DC motors for generator conversion
- Using a salvaged hand-blender motor as the generator head
- Measuring voltage and current output under resistive load

## Techniques and Methods
- Full disassembly of a paper shredder to extract the gear train and motor shaft
- Bearing removal using two stiff metal supports in a vise, struck with a hammer
- Drifting a shaft off using a hammer (press-fit removal)
- Cutting a sleeve bushing with a cutter/grinder
- Welding a dome nut onto a bolt/handle for a custom input shaft
- Reassembly of modified gearbox with cover plate
- Resistive-load electrical testing with voltmeter and ammeter
