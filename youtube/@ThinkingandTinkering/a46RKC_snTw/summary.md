## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith converts a broken 240V rotisserie oven into a 12V off-grid oven suitable for RVs and battery-powered setups by replacing the original cartridge heating elements with his conductive graphene ink painted onto the oven's interior walls. After adding ceramic insulation and connecting two 12V car batteries in series (24V total), the oven reaches over 200°C drawing only ~120 watts — a dramatic reduction from the original ~3 kilowatts. The video demonstrates that graphene ink-based resistive heating is a viable, low-power cooking solution for off-grid applications.

## Key Topics
- Converting a mains-powered (240V) oven to 12/24V DC operation
- Using conductive graphene ink as a resistive heating element
- Insulating a metal enclosure to allow application of conductive ink
- Wiring heating panels in parallel to increase current draw and heat output
- Power efficiency comparison: original ~3kW vs. modified ~120W
- Off-grid and RV cooking as a use case
- Prototype nature of the build and plans for a larger version

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene/conductive ink** ("Working Ink") — painted between copper tape strips to form the resistive heating element
- **Stove and barbecue enamel spray paint** — applied to the bare metal interior as a high-temperature electrical insulator (rated to 650°C) before ink application
- **Copper tape** — used as bus bars/electrodes on each painted panel, soldered with connection wires
- **Ceramic insulation blanket** — wrapped around the painted panels inside the oven; typically used for kiln work; significantly reduces heat loss
- **12V car batteries** (two, connected in series for 24V) — power source

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and cleaning of a commercial rotisserie oven
- Spraying stove enamel onto metal surfaces as a high-temperature dielectric layer
- Applying copper tape strips as electrodes, soldering lead wires
- Painting conductive ink between copper bus bars to form resistive heating panels (multiple coats, drying between each)
- Verifying electrical isolation between panels and metal case using a multimeter (checking for infinite resistance)
- Wrapping interior panels with ceramic fibre blanket for thermal insulation
- Connecting three painted panels (front, back, and base) in parallel across the supply

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Temperature units:** Confirmed the 270° reading was Celsius, not Fahrenheit.
- **Voltage clarification:** The build ended up running at 24V (two batteries in series), not 12V as originally intended; he acknowledged the confusion and noted that others have suggested ways to achieve true 12V operation, which he plans to explore in a larger version.
- **Why two batteries:** He explained that with one battery the oven only reached ~100°C; adding the second was the easiest fix rather than re-doing all the painted strips. He apologised for the confusion this caused.
- **Which sides were painted:** Front, back, and bottom (three sides total).
- **Thermostat note:** The original bimetallic strip thermostat relied on the whole uninsulated oven body getting hot to function; once insulation was added, it would likely no longer work — hence it was removed.
- **Insulation was added by him:** The original commercial oven had no insulation at all — a common finding when he takes these apart. The ceramic blanket he added is what makes the low wattage sufficient.
- **Efficiency explanation:** He clarified that the ink does not improve the electrical efficiency of heating (resistive heaters are ~99% efficient regardless), but it improves *heat transfer efficiency* through intimate surface contact and much better insulation, reducing the energy needed during the "soak" (hold-temperature) phase of cooking.
- **Future plans:** Intends to build a larger version, add proper temperature control (thermocouple + display/keypad), and potentially feature it on the "Memphis channel." He is also personally considering going off-grid at home and sees 12V cooking as a key step.
- **Ink temperature limits:** Noted the ink is rated to 400°C unprotected, and expressed curiosity about whether it could be pushed to 600°C.
- **Alternative substrate for lower-temperature heating applications:** Recommended PET film (laminating pouch material, as used in underfloor heating) for heat applications up to ~100°C.
- **Ink availability:** Directed viewers to the Working Ink shop (link in video description).
