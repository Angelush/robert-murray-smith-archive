## Overview
Robert demonstrates his "laser stacking" technique — cutting and simultaneously welding multiple acrylic sheets in a single laser-cutter pass — by building a functional air engine. The build serves as a direct practical comparison between laser cutting and 3D printing, showing that laser stacking produced all engine parts in roughly 30 minutes (vs. potentially days on a 3D printer). His conclusion is that neither tool is universally "better"; each suits different jobs, and laser cutters excel for layered, prismatic shapes.

## Key Topics
- Laser stacking technique: laying up multiple acrylic sheets and cutting/welding them in one pass
- Building a working air engine as a demonstration project
- Speed and practicality comparison: laser cutter vs. 3D printer
- Strengths and limitations of each fabrication method
- Component assembly: flywheel, cranks, piston, valve chest, piston chest

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Black acrylic sheet** — used in multiple layers to make the flywheel and other engine components
- **Cintra board / builders board** — used as the base for mounting the crank assembly
- **4mm bolt** — used to connect the flattened piston rod to the crank

## Techniques and Methods
- **Laser stacking** — stacking multiple acrylic sheets and cutting through all layers simultaneously; heat from the laser welds the layers together, producing finished multi-layer parts in one operation
- **Vacuum-driven air engine assembly** — piston and valve chest connected via a flattened, drilled piston rod; tested with a vacuum adapter
- **Mechanical fitting** — pressing out laser-cut parts and assembling without gluing (for laser-stacked parts) or with minimal gluing (for simpler flat-cut parts)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On his laser cutter**: It is a cheap 30W CO2 laser bought from eBay — a Chinese machine, described as "pretty clunky." He paid only £140 and recommends starting cheap.
- **On tool preference**: Despite owning both a laser cutter and a 3D printer, he admits he "hardly ever uses the 3D printer" and, if forced to choose one, would pick the laser cutter.
- **On the core argument**: He reiterates it is "horses for courses" — not which is better, but which is more suitable to the job, balanced against affordability (not everyone can own every machine).
- **On laser stacking mechanics**: No glue is needed — the laser melt welds the edges together and the bond is very strong. Recommends googling "laserstacking" for more resources.
- **On software used for designs**: He uses LibreOffice Draw (free, bundled with LibreOffice).
- **On high-end machine comparisons**: Notes that comparing cheap laser cutters to high-end 3D printers is "apples and oranges."
- **On hand tools**: Mentions he values hand-tool skill and often finds it the fastest method, though machines help those less experienced with hand tools.
- **Miscellaneous**: Mentions "annealing" in one reply (likely in response to a commenter's question about post-processing acrylic).
