## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith continues his "free energy power amplifier" series by demonstrating a modified solenoid piston enclosed in a steel tube, then unveils a new reciprocating piston mechanism driven by a rotating wheel. The video's main focus is sharing his concept for adding a flywheel and micro-switch to create a self-sustaining reciprocating action, with the goal of comparing energy input to the coil against the combined back-EMF and motional EMF output.

## Key Topics
- Enclosing the previous coil-and-piston assembly in a steel tube housing
- Design of a simple crank-driven reciprocating piston mechanism
- Flywheel concept: using stored mechanical energy to return the piston without electrical input
- Micro-switch timing: pulsing the coil on the upstroke and capturing coil output on the downstroke
- Back-EMF from collapsing magnetic field vs. motional EMF from magnet moving through coil
- Invitation for community feedback and suggestions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel tube (outer housing/enclosure)
- Steel bar (piston/push rod with magnetised end)
- Electromagnet coil (wound on the steel bar)
- Flywheel (planned addition; later built from lead with glued magnets per author comment)
- Capacitor / battery (for capturing recovered energy)
- Micro-switch (for timing the pulse)

## Techniques and Methods
- Enclosing a solenoid-type piston in a ferromagnetic steel housing to improve flux concentration
- Crank-wheel mechanism converting rotary motion to linear reciprocating motion via a pivoting joint
- Flywheel energy storage to provide the return stroke passively
- Micro-switch triggered pulsed driving (single high-voltage pulse per stroke)
- Energy recovery: routing coil output to a capacitor or battery during the unpowered stroke
- Finite element analysis (FEA) modelling of redesigned components (mentioned in author comments)

## Robert's Own Replies
- Acknowledges a commenter's idea positively ("that is a nice idea - thanks"), indicating openness to suggestions.
- Confirms he actually built and tested the flywheel: it was made from lead with magnets glued to the push rod, but spun so fast it flew apart.
- Reports he is rebuilding the assembly more robustly — all-steel construction with bolted joints.
- Mentions he has run finite element analysis (FEA) models on the new design to validate structural principles, though it is time-consuming.
- Reflects on the solenoid nature of the device in response to a commenter: acknowledges it is solenoid-like at heart but argues it is more efficient than standard designs; candidly admits he has stepped back from it as he suspects it may be a "free energy dead end," while affirming that failed experiments are still valuable learning experiences and that "constant experimentation" and constructive criticism are the right approach.
