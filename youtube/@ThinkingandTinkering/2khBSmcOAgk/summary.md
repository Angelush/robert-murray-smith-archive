## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a mains-powered plug-in drill to run on DC batteries by removing the AC switch and reconnecting the motor terminals with a capacitor across them. He shows the drill running successfully on two 12V car batteries in series (24V), then argues that this approach generalises to a wide range of power tools since they all share the same universal motor design.

## Key Topics
- Converting AC mains-powered tools to battery (DC) operation
- Internal anatomy of a drill: motor, commutator, brushes, armature, worm gear, fan
- Universal motors and why they can run on both AC and DC
- The role of the capacitor across the motor terminals
- Generalising the conversion to jigsaws, sanders, circular saws, and other power tools

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of drill casing (removal of 8 screws, case levering)
- Identifying and removing the AC switch and its wiring
- Retaining and reconnecting the capacitor across the motor's positive and negative terminals
- Connecting motor terminals directly to a DC power supply (two 12V car batteries in series = 24V)
- Bench-testing the motor with jury-rigged wiring before reassembly

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert explains *why* the conversion works: the drill uses a **universal motor**, a commutated series-wound motor whose stator field coils are wired in series with the rotor windings via a commutator. Because both the field coils and the armature reverse polarity together when AC is applied, the resulting rotational force is always in the same direction regardless of supply polarity — meaning the motor works equally well on DC. This is the fundamental property that makes mains power tools straightforward to convert to battery operation.
