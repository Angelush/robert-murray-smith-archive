## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith replicates Art Porter's "gapless motor" (gap motor) on a smaller, affordable scale suitable for home experimentation. Rather than using the large, expensive neodymium magnets from the original design, he substitutes small neo disc and bar magnets, and replaces the mechanical timing mechanism with a 555 astable timer circuit. The video demonstrates the motor running and concludes with ideas for improvements, including capturing back-spike energy and using the assembly as a linear alternator.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to Art Porter's gapless/gap motor design (via Patrick Kelly's free energy compendium)
- Scaled-down replication using small, affordable neodymium magnets
- Construction details: brass bar slider, plastic tube housing, soft iron core coil
- Replacing mechanical timing with a 555 astable timer and relay
- Mark-space ratio adjustment for timing optimisation
- Converting linear motion to rotary via a cam (Art Porter's approach vs. alternatives)
- Linear alternator concept as a potential direct output method
- Back-spike energy capture as a future improvement

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Neodymium (neo) bar magnet** — 5 cm × 1 cm, used as the moving head magnet seated in the plastic tube
- **Neodymium (neo) disc magnets** — 1 cm × 2 mm discs, stack of 15 used to replicate Art Porter's base magnet section
- **4 mm brass bar** — forms the slider shaft
- **6 mm plastic tube** — houses the bar magnet and fits over the brass bar
- **Soft iron core (8 mm)** — fabricated from a nail, filed flat, used as the coil core
- **26 gauge enamel wire** — wound thickly to form the coil
- **White electrician's tape** — used to wrap and secure the finished coil
- **100 µF electrolytic capacitor** — part of the 555 timer timing circuit (noted as too large; replacement with a lower-value cap planned)
- **12 V battery** — power supply for the demonstration

## Techniques and Methods
- **Coil winding** — 26 gauge enamel wire wound around a soft iron core until very thick, then taped
- **Core fabrication** — nail sawn down and filed flat, placed inside plastic tube with disc end-caps
- **Astable 555 timer circuit** — kit-built from Maplin (£5), used with a relay to electronically switch the coil on/off, replacing mechanical timing
- **Mark-space ratio adjustment** — two resistors on the 555 kit allow tuning of on-time and period; Art Porter recommends ~43% duty cycle
- **Magnet polarity arrangement** — south–south face opposition to create repulsion-driven linear motion
- **Linear alternator concept** — arranging north/south magnets in the moving arm and passing them through a coil for direct AC output without rotary conversion
- **Back-spike capture** — noted as a planned improvement to harvest inductive back-EMF from the coil
