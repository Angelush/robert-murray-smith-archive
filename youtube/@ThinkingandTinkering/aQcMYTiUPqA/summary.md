## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents an updated, improved version of the Hummers-based method (Thomas method) for synthesising graphene oxide, replacing his earlier lower-quality videos. The process is a three-stage temperature-controlled reaction — cold, medium, and high — that converts graphite powder into a filterable graphene oxide cake in approximately two hours. He emphasises that while the procedure is accessible to motivated home chemists, it involves genuinely dangerous chemicals and must be followed carefully to avoid explosive or runaway reactions.

## Key Topics
- Motivation for the update: improved method and better video/audio quality
- Overview of the three-stage synthesis: cold stage, medium temperature stage, high temperature stage
- Safety hazards, particularly the formation of manganese heptoxide (explosive) if temperature control is lost
- Practical tips for sourcing reagents (garden stores, online, hardware shops)
- Filtration and washing procedure to purify the final product
- Bath sonication to disperse the graphene oxide cake
- Discussion of graphite particle size and its effect on paste consistency and yield
- The role (and debated necessity) of sodium nitrate in the reaction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite powder (5 g)** — starting material; available from garden stores as fertiliser
- **Sodium nitrate / sodium nitrite (2.5 g)** — also called "nitrate of soda" or Chile saltpetre; available as fertiliser or food preservative; Robert notes the Tour group believes it contributes little, but he thinks it helps pre-oxidise the graphite
- **Sulfuric acid, 98% concentrated (115 mL)** — available as drain cleaner ("One Shot instant drain cleaner"); minimum usable concentration ~94%
- **Potassium permanganate (15 g)** — added slowly in powdered form during cold stage; forms explosive manganese heptoxide in solution if temperature exceeds ~55 °C
- **Water (230 mL + 400 mL, totalling ~630 mL)** — added in two separate stages
- **Hydrogen peroxide, 30–35% (50 mL)** — terminates the reaction; food-grade available online
- **Hydrochloric acid, 1 molar (~100 mL)** — used to wash out residual metal salts (potassium, sodium) from the filter cake; available as "spirit of salts" from builders' merchants
- **Deionised water (~800 mL)** — final wash to bring filtrate pH to ~6–7

## Techniques and Methods
- **Magnetic stirring** — used throughout; Robert previously made his own stirrer (covered in a separate video)
- **Ice bath** — used during cold stage to keep temperature below 20 °C while adding potassium permanganate
- **Controlled incremental reagent addition** — potassium permanganate added slowly via a paper fold to avoid runaway reaction; water added gradually to avoid quenching the oxidation
- **Three-stage temperature ramp**: cold (< 20 °C) → medium (35 °C, 30 min) → high (98 °C, 15 min)
- **Gravity filtration** — using a Buchner/suction funnel, cotton, and filter paper (Robert also mentions a vacuum filtration rig)
- **Sequential washing** — HCl wash first to remove metal salts, then repeated deionised water washes until filtrate pH ≈ 6–7
- **Bath sonication (~30 min)** — to disperse the dried graphene oxide cake into suspension
- **Drying and re-grinding** — for incomplete reactions, dry the product, powder in a coffee grinder, re-run the synthesis (acts as a pre-oxidation stage)
- **Raman spectroscopy and XRD** — mentioned as standard characterisation methods

## Robert's Own Replies
- **If a reaction runs away** (overheating, fuming): it is ruined and must be discarded — add crushed ice to the bath immediately, leave for an hour, then dispose; "run" is also an option he jokes about.
- **Role of sodium nitrate**: the Tour group consider it unnecessary; Robert believes it helps pre-oxidise the graphite before the main reaction; leaving it out and increasing potassium permanganate slightly is a viable alternative.
- **Graphite particle size**: he typically uses 45 microns but has used 150 micron and 5 micron; larger flakes suit liquid-crystal/electrospinning applications, smaller suit inkjet printing; he advises not going below 45 microns without testing.
- **Incomplete oxidation (greenish product)**: dry the entire batch, re-run the synthesis — the first run acts as a pre-oxidation stage and the second run reliably gives bright yellow GO.
- **Drying the product**: dry completely and gently (do not exceed 80 °C), grind in a coffee grinder, wash with DI water, then sonicate.
- **Scaling up**: 400 g potassium permanganate and 2 litres of sulfuric acid for a large batch; even then, graphite is tough and 100% yield is not expected.
- **Acid concentration**: minimum ~94% sulfuric acid; the process is sensitive to dissolved salt concentrations so substitutions are difficult.
- **Hydrogen peroxide concentration**: if only 50% is available, dilute it down; if 30% is used instead of 35%, add 5 mL extra.
- **Washing to neutral pH**: keep washing with DI water until pH 6–7; do not add alkali — it will irreversibly coagulate the GO.
- **Nomenclature**: Robert notes "graphene oxide" is arguably a poor name; the original term "graphitic acid" is more chemically accurate, as the material is a graphene basal plane decorated with carboxyl and hydroxyl groups rather than true oxides.
- **Characterisation**: Raman spectroscopy and XRD are the two basic standard tests.
- **Sourcing sodium nitrate in the US**: Harris Seeds sells it as "Nitrate of Soda" fertiliser for ~$14; also available from farm supply stores.
