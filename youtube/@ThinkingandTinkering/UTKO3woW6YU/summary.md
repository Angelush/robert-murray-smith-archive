## Overview
Robert demonstrates the use of a cheap, homemade graphene-water dispersion (first made in video 1933) as the mix water for standard concrete. Testing at a professional test house showed the resulting graphene concrete is 58% stronger than conventional concretes, and its 7-day cured strength exceeds the 28-day cured strength of all comparison mixes. The key takeaway is that this simple, low-cost method could allow using one-third less concrete globally, significantly reducing cement's environmental footprint.

## Key Topics
- Recap of graphene-water dispersion from video 1933 (7 mg/mL concentration)
- Two main problems with graphene concrete: dispersion and cost — both solved by this method
- Using the graphene dispersion directly as concrete mix water
- Standard concrete cube testing (4"×4"×4" and 6"×6"×6" steel moulds)
- Comparison of graphene concrete against five common cement types
- 7-day vs 28-day curing strength results
- Environmental implication: 33% less concrete needed by volume

## Techniques and Methods
- Graphene dispersed in water at 7 mg/mL (process detailed in video 1933)
- Graphene dispersion used directly as mix water instead of plain water
- Concrete cast in standard steel moulds (4"×4"×4" or 6"×6"×6")
- 7-day and 28-day water-bath curing
- Compressive strength testing at a professional test house using a constant-load test machine

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Method clarification (repeated many times):** "I made a 7 mg/mL graphene dispersion then used that instead of mix water and cured in a water bath."
- **Cement used:** Off-the-shelf Blue Circle ready-mix Portland cement.
- **Water ratio:** No reduction in total water — the dispersion simply replaces the plain mix water.
- **Volume savings:** The 33% reduction is by volume, justified purely by the strength increase.
- **Graphene properties:** The dispersion is not hydrophobic.
- **Superplasticiser:** Described as "basically just soap" — Robert doubts it would add much benefit.
