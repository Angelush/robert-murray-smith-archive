## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates that glycerine (glycerol), widely regarded as non-flammable and completely safe, can in fact be burned as a direct replacement fuel in diesel engines. Drawing on recent research papers, he shows crude vegetable glycerine — a cheap byproduct of biodiesel production — igniting readily, and argues it represents an overlooked, low-cost fuel source with real-world applications.

## Key Topics
- Glycerine's common uses and reputation as a non-flammable, non-toxic material
- Glycerine as a byproduct of biodiesel production (referenced from his video #1672)
- Burning glycerine as a direct diesel fuel replacement
- Required modifications: preheating to ~200°F and increased compression ratio
- Formula E's interest in glycerine-powered portable generators (via Aquafuel Research)
- Economic case for glycerine as a waste-stream fuel (~$3/gallon wholesale, crude)
- Broader circular-economy framing: plant → food → biodiesel → glycerine fuel

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glycerine / glycerol (vegetable-derived)** — the primary subject; demonstrated burning directly
- **Crude glycerine** — unrefined byproduct from his own biodiesel experiment, used in the demo
- **Polyethylene glycol** — mentioned as the other ~50% component of vape fluid
- **Biodiesel** — the production process that generates glycerine as a byproduct
- **Nitroglycerine** — briefly noted in comments as what glycerine becomes when treated with nitric acid (not pursued)
- **Water** — present in hygroscopic glycerine; noted as not preventing combustion; parallels drawn to hydro-diesel and WWII aircraft water injection

## Techniques and Methods
- Direct ignition of glycerine at ambient conditions (demonstration)
- Preheating glycerine to ~200°F prior to diesel injection
- Increasing diesel engine compression ratio to combust glycerine
- Atomisation of glycerine for flammability (mentioned in comments)
- Filtering and distillation of crude glycerine to remove impurities (noted as beneficial for engine longevity, not required for combustion)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Circular economy framing**: Robert repeatedly emphasises viewing glycerine not as "the product" but as a waste off-take from a chain: plant matter → digestion/food processing → biodiesel → glycerine as fuel. He finds the "glycerine as product" mindset limiting.
- **Pricing**: Wholesale crude glycerine is ~55–75 cents/kg (~$3/gallon USD), making it economically attractive; he distinguishes this from higher retail/refined prices.
- **Crude vs. refined**: The glycerine in his demo is crude (from his own biodiesel batch). Purification (filtering/distilling) is about protecting the engine, not a prerequisite for combustion.
- **Heating and atomisation**: You need to heat and atomise glycerine for it to be flammable — he stresses viewers should read the referenced research papers for full combustion analysis.
- **Mixing with diesel**: Glycerine does not mix with diesel (it naturally separates in biodiesel production); an emulsifier would be needed if blending were attempted.
- **Clean burning**: Research papers report glycerine burns very cleanly; he directs commenters to those papers for gas-emission analysis.
- **Cooking oil → biodiesel chain**: He notes that using already-cooked waste oil improves the overall economics and environmental case further.
- **Gunpowder engine aside**: Mentions he once made a gunpowder-powered engine video that YouTube removed and issued a strike over — offered as a lighthearted aside about platform restrictions.
