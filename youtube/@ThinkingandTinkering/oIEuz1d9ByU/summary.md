## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the troubled history of vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs) and argues they were abandoned too early. He outlines the serious economic and physical limitations now facing horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWTs), then presents two recent developments — the C2 Wind Turbine prototype and research showing VAWTs gain efficiency when clustered together — as evidence that VAWTs may represent the future of wind energy.

## Key Topics
- History of VAWTs from 9th-century Persia through the Darrieus designs of the 1920s
- Catalogue of catastrophic VAWT failures in the 1980s (Prince Edward Island, San Gorgonio Pass, England, Carmarthen Bay)
- Economic limitations of HAWTs: current cost ~$122/MWh, target <$50/MWh to compete with fossil fuels (~$38/MWh)
- Physical limits of HAWT technology: material constraints, 4.5% annual efficiency degradation, 80% failure probability after 8 years, high maintenance costs
- The C2 Wind Turbine (Twirl): S1 prototype (2015, 30 kW) and current 1 MW prototype for a 5-year trial
- Research finding that paired/clustered VAWTs benefit from each other's turbulence, yielding ~15% efficiency improvement
- The paradigm shift this combination of advances could represent

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel (~700 tons per 6 MW HAWT plant)
- Concrete (~5,000 tons per 6 MW HAWT plant)
- Fiberglass (VAWT blade material, cited in the 1986 Carmarthen Bay failure)

## Techniques and Methods
- Drag-based vs. aerodynamic lift-based VAWT design (Darrieus egg-beater and H-rotor/straight-bladed types)
- Small-scale prototype testing before utility-scale deployment (C2 S1 prototype)
- Clustered/paired VAWT array configurations to exploit inter-turbine turbulence
- Long-duration field trials (5-year trial for 1 MW C2 prototype)
