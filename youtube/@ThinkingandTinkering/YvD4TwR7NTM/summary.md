## Overview
In this fourth instalment of his "Exploding Water" series, Robert demonstrates two developments: a cylinder-and-plunger arrangement driven by his original spark-gap water explosion setup, and a significantly lower-energy version using photo flash capacitors. The episode concludes with a plan to combine both advances — a piston driven by the compact capacitor-based system — to be shown in a future update.

## Key Topics
- Using a spark-gap water explosion to physically drive a piston out of a cylinder
- Reducing the energy input required to produce the water-splitting/explosion effect
- Capacitor-based discharge as a lower-power alternative to the original high-energy setup
- Electrode spacing as a key variable for making the piston system work at lower energy
- Future plans: combining the piston cylinder with the capacitor circuit, and exploring a Cockcroft-Walton capacitor arrangement

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — the working medium, applied as a thin film on the plunger rather than sprayed into the cylinder
- **36MB05A full-wave bridge rectifier** — rated 1200 V, 35 A; used to produce DC for charging the capacitors
- **Photo flash capacitors (×3)** — 350 V each, ~100 µF each, wired in parallel; store energy for the spark discharge

## Techniques and Methods
- **Spark-gap discharge into water** — the core mechanism for rapidly vaporising water to drive a piston
- **Cylinder-and-plunger fabrication** — a machined cylinder fitted over the existing two-electrode arrangement with a close-fitting plunger
- **Capacitor bank (parallel)** — three photo flash capacitors in parallel to accumulate and release energy in a controlled pulse
- **Full-wave bridge rectification** — converting AC mains to DC for capacitor charging
- **Electrode gap optimisation** — reducing electrode separation inside the cylinder to make the lower-energy capacitor discharge effective
- **Planned: Cockcroft-Walton multiplier** — mentioned in comments as a next experimental step for the capacitor arrangement
