## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the "craziest clock in the world" — a historical escapement mechanism known variously as the Ignatz clock, flying foliot clock, or Da Vinci clock — by designing and 3D-printing his own version from scratch. He shares the STL files freely on Thingiverse, walking viewers through assembly and the considerable fine-tuning required to get the mechanism running. He ends with a candid observation: after testing, he suspects one key structural element (the T-section bar) may not actually be necessary, flagging it as a subject for future investigation.

## Key Topics
- History and naming of the escapement mechanism (attributed to Leonardo da Vinci c. 1600; also patented in 1883 by Clausen and Slater in Minneapolis)
- Alternative names: Ignatz clock, flying foliot clock, Da Vinci clock — "Ignatz" deriving from the *Krazy Kat* comic strip
- Designing the clock parts in Tinkercad
- 3D printing and assembling the mechanism
- Calibration and weight adjustment to achieve reliable operation
- Questioning whether the T-section escapement bar is truly necessary
- Sharing free STL files on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel rod (6 mm diameter, 250 mm long) — main structural rods
- Steel rod (6 mm diameter, 75 mm long) — top plate rods
- Aluminium or brass rod — noted as viable alternatives to steel
- M12 nut — used as pendulum bob weight
- Nuts and bolts (generic) — used to fill the weight housing
- Skateboard bearing (22 mm × 7 mm × 8 mm) — fitted to the top plate
- String — used to connect the weight to the drive mechanism
- Superglue / spot glue — for securing steel rods and clip components

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design using Tinkercad (basic shapes only)
- FDM 3D printing of all clock components
- Ratchet-and-bevel-gear drive assembly
- String-wound gravity weight drive (wound by hand via ratchet)
- Iterative weight and string-length tuning to balance pendulum swing amplitude
- Gluing printed sub-assemblies that could not be printed as single pieces
