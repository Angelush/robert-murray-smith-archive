## Overview
This video is a follow-up to video 1879, where Robert built a serpentine dual-coil motor. Here he explains how the blocking oscillator circuit driving it actually works, then demonstrates extracting usable electrical energy from the spinning rotor using a pickup coil from a microwave oven transformer, generating 10–13 volts AC and lighting a set of four lamps.

## Key Topics
- Explanation of the transistor as a switch (collector, emitter, base)
- How a blocking oscillator (pulse oscillator) works using a two-coil arrangement
- How the second (feedback) coil self-times the transistor switch, making the motor self-sustaining
- Efficiency advantages of transistor switching circuits (low duty cycle, zero-voltage switching)
- Mechanical energy extraction from the rotor using electromagnetic induction
- Applications of blocking/pulse oscillators (switch-mode power supplies, Joule thief, Slayer exciter)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium magnets — 36 magnets (1 cm × 2 mm) forming the rotor, plus 6 additional in N-S-N-S arrangement on top of the rotor for the generator pickup
- Transistor — used as the switching element in the blocking oscillator
- Coil from a microwave oven transformer — used as the pickup/generator coil
- Set of four LED/lamps — used to demonstrate usable power output

## Techniques and Methods
- Blocking oscillator (pulse oscillator) circuit using a two-coil + transistor arrangement
- Electromagnetic induction for energy extraction — a pickup coil held near spinning magnets acts as a mechanical transformer
- Manual spin-starting the motor to initiate oscillation
- Voltage and current measurement during operation (5 V supply, ~20 mA draw; 10–13 V output from pickup coil)
