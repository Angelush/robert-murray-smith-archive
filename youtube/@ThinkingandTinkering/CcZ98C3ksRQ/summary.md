## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses the analogy of restricted palettes in painting — as practiced by artists like Turner — to argue that having fewer, well-mastered tools produces better results than accumulating every tool available. He extends this philosophy across carpentry, chef knives, CAD software, and 3D printing, concluding that the single best "tool" anyone can have is simply the ability to get up and start doing.

## Key Topics
- The "restricted palette" principle in painting as a creativity-enhancing constraint
- Historical carpentry: skilled craftsmen using only a side axe and hand saw
- The psychological split between people who collect tools and people who actually make things
- Expensive vs. mid-range tools (e.g., the £400 Martinez "Venom" hammer vs. a hardware store hammer)
- CAD software debates and Robert's preference for Tinkercad due to its accessibility
- The importance of practice and doing over tool acquisition

## Techniques and Methods
- Restricted palette painting (limiting colours to red, yellow, blue)
- Traditional carpentry using a side axe (flat on one side, bevelled on the other) for planing, axe work, mortises, and chiselling
- 3D modelling using Tinkercad as a simple, free CAD tool
