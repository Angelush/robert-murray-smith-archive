## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history of the wristwatch from its WWI-era popularization to the revolutionary Bulova Accutron, which replaced traditional balance wheels with a transistor-driven tuning fork vibrating at 360 Hz. He then demonstrates a 3D-printed scale model of the Accutron's indexing mechanism that he designed himself, and highlights a freely available reference book ("Mechanical Movements, Devices and Appliances" by Hiscox) as an invaluable resource for mechanical designers.

## Key Topics
- History of the wristwatch and its rise to popularity during World War I
- Limitations of traditional mechanical clockwork (temperature, humidity, shock)
- The 1947 transistor and its impact on watch engineering
- Max Hetzel's 1957 invention of the transistor-driven tuning fork watch, commercialized as the Bulova Accutron in the 1960s
- How the Accutron mechanism works: tuning fork at 360 Hz, electromagnetic coils, indexing wheel, push rods
- Robert's 3D-printed model of the Accutron indexing mechanism (60-tooth version)
- The "engineer's Bible" — *Mechanical Movements, Devices and Appliances* by Hiscox — and its relevance to mechanism design
- Mention of "Nautilus gears" as another example of a mechanism documented in Hiscox long before it became trendy

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (via Tinkercad, per author comments) to fabricate the indexing mechanism components
- Assembly of printed parts: lever, push rods, indexing wheel, clips, and ratchet wedge
- Electromagnetic coil pulsing via transistor to sustain tuning fork resonance (described historically)
- Gluing ratchet into place to achieve a definite "click" per step

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief social responses ("cheers mate," "glad you liked it"), but a few contain useful substance:
- **On Tinkercad:** He uses it deliberately because his audience includes beginners; he recommends more advanced users stick with their preferred CAD software.
- **On the Accutron watch being purchasable:** He notes he believes you can still buy them.
- **On UK mains frequency:** He confirms UK mains is 50 Hz and suggests the mechanism could theoretically be driven from mains frequency.
- **On building the mechanism in metal:** He acknowledges it could be done but some parts might need to be made larger or replaced with metal components.
- **On the "click":** He personally enjoyed the click sound the ratchet produces — "I liked the click too lol."
