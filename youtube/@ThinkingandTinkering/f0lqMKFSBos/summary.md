## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a cheap, functional microwave kiln using a basic terracotta plant pot, graphite-based paint, and ceramic fiber blanket insulation. The video explains the science behind microwave kilns — how a susceptor material converts microwave energy into heat — and shows how to use the DIY kiln to fuse glass. It positions the build as a low-cost entry point for hobbyists wanting to try glass fusing or low-temperature ceramics before committing to commercial equipment.

## Key Topics
- History and purpose of microwave kilns (hobbyist glass fusing and ceramics)
- How susceptor materials convert microwave energy into heat
- Step-by-step construction of a DIY microwave kiln from a plant pot
- Insulation using ceramic fiber blanket
- Choosing better vessel materials for a more durable kiln
- Practical demonstration of fusing two layers of glass

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Terracotta plant pot** — used as the kiln body (cheap entry-level option, rated ~500–600°C)
- **Stoneware cup/bowl** — better alternative vessel, rated to ~800°C
- **Porcelain cup/bowl** — best alternative vessel, rated to ~1200°C
- **Alumina (aluminium oxide) brick** — recommended for a more permanent, professional-grade kiln body
- **Graphite/graphene paint** (specifically Rust-Oleum G-Shield cited as an example) — the susceptor coating; absorbs microwaves and re-emits heat
- **EMF/smog-shielding paints containing graphite** — general category of paints that work as susceptors
- **Water glass (sodium silicate)** — suggested as a permanent binder for graphite susceptor paint
- **Ceramic fiber blanket** — insulation wrapped around the outside of the kiln
- **Glass pieces** — the workpiece being fused in the demonstration

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting the interior of a vessel with graphite-based susceptor ink and allowing it to dry
- Wrapping the painted vessel in ceramic fiber blanket and securing with string to retain heat
- Placing a glass tray and a second piece of ceramic blanket inside the kiln cavity to support and cover the workpiece
- Running the microwave for 3–4 minutes and observing the red-hot glow through the door
- Allowing the kiln to cool naturally for ~30 minutes before opening
- Drilling a vent hole in denser vessels (stoneware, porcelain) using a tile drill bit
- Carving a shallow cavity into an alumina brick as the basis for an upgraded kiln
