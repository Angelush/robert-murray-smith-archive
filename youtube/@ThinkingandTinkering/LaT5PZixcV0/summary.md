## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to build a simple urea electrolysis cell for hydrogen production, adapted from a design by "Nighthawk Light." The key insight is that urea can be electrolyzed at just 0.37 volts — roughly 70% less than the 1.23 volts required for water electrolysis — making it a far more energy-efficient route to hydrogen. The video covers both the construction of the cell and the electrolyte recipe, and frames urea electrolysis as a way to extract value from a biological waste resource.

## Key Topics
- Urea electrolysis vs. water electrolysis: voltage comparison (0.37V vs 1.23V)
- Why urea electrolysis is more energy-efficient
- Adapting a water electrolysis cell design (by "Nighthawk Light") for urea
- Gas separation: collecting hydrogen at the cathode, venting nitrogen at the anode
- Electrolyte preparation (urea + potassium hydroxide in water)
- Operating voltage to ensure only urea (not water) is electrolyzed
- Replaceability and maintainability of the electrode assembly
- Safety considerations (caustic electrolyte, explosive hydrogen)
- Urea electrolysis for wastewater treatment and resource recovery

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Urea** — 80 g per 4 litres of water (≈ 1/3 mole); the fuel being electrolyzed
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — 1–5 M concentration; 1120 g per 4 litres used as the alkaline electrolyte
- **Water** — 4 litres; solvent for the electrolyte solution
- **Stainless steel pan scourer** — used as the high-surface-area electrode material
- **Stainless steel egg whisk** — structural housing for the scourer electrode
- **Solder** — used to attach the wire to the whisk/electrode
- **Hot glue** — used to seal the gas outlet hole in the bottle cap
- **Plastic (food container, drinks bottle)** — cell housing and gas-capture bottles

## Techniques and Methods
- Constructing large-surface-area electrodes by stuffing a stainless steel scourer into an egg whisk
- Modifying a plastic bottle (cutting the base, drilling the cap) to create an inverted gas-capture chamber over each electrode
- Wiring electrodes through a sealed cap to separate the gas collection from the electrolyte
- Running the cell at **1.4 V** to stay below the water electrolysis threshold and electrolyze urea selectively
- Monitoring current draw (~50 mA) to track urea consumption; near-zero current indicates exhausted urea
- Gas separation at source: hydrogen collected via tubing from the cathode bottle, nitrogen vented from the anode bottle
- Using a bubbler on the hydrogen outlet for safety
