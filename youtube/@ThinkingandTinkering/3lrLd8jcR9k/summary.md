## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed clamp built around a hypocycloid gear mechanism, following up on a previous video about hypocycloid gear ratios. The key insight is that hypocycloid gears are nearly impossible to back-drive, making them ideal for a clamp that requires little effort to close but holds with tremendous force. He also suggests the same design could serve as a non-slip drawing compass.

## Key Topics
- Hypocycloid gear mechanics and how extreme gear ratios are achieved via an eccentric
- Back-driving resistance as a functional property for locking mechanisms
- Practical application of hypocycloid gears in a 3D-printed clamp
- Comparison with the Archimedes drive (similar one-directional locking property)
- Secondary use case: a non-slip drawing compass
- Design files shared on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Glue (used to fix the eccentric position on the axle during assembly)
- 3D-printed PLA/filament (implied by the printing and Tinkercad design workflow)

## Techniques and Methods
- Hypocycloid gear design in Tinkercad
- FDM 3D printing of clamp components
- Eccentric-based gear assembly: marking and gluing the eccentric to the axle to prevent accidental bonding to the center
- Stacking and gluing hypocycloid discs with offset tooth counts (8 vs. 9 pins) to set gear ratio
- Clip-based axle retention for the assembled mechanism
