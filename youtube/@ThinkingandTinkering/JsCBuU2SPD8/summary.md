## Overview
This short, reflective video explores Neolithic "houses of the dead" — prehistoric burial mounds built to mirror the architecture of the living — as a lens for understanding how early humans conceptualised mortality, ancestry, and community. Robert draws on the example of Skara Brae and Orcadian cairns to argue that Neolithic people had a profoundly communal and animistic relationship with their dead, and concludes that those who came before us never truly leave: their memories and experiences persist in the landscape of our lives.

## Key Topics
- The span of human history vs. the brevity of individual lives (~80 years)
- Skara Brae, the Neolithic village in Orkney (~5,000 years ago)
- The Neolithic transition: from nomadic hunter-gatherers to settled farmers
- Burial mounds (cairns) built to replicate the floor plan of living houses
- The shift in attitude toward the dead: fear of the recently deceased vs. reverence for ancestral bones
- Neolithic animism: the belief that everything possesses spirit
- Ancestors as mediators between the living world and the spirit world
- The historical transition from collective/group ancestor worship to individual monuments
- Modern attitudes toward mortality and the concept of the individual
- The philosophical argument that our ancestors remain present within us

## Techniques and Methods
- Archaeological observation: comparing the internal architecture of burial mounds to Neolithic domestic dwellings (same niches, same layout)
- Covering a house-structured burial chamber with earth to "transmute" it from a house of the living into a house of the dead
