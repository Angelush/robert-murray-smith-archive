## Overview
This video is part of Robert Murray-Smith's ongoing series on building a recumbent bike/trike from paper tubes. He explains the three key geometric concepts required when fitting front forks — head tube angle, rake, and trail — using a normal bicycle as a visual reference before applying those principles to his own paper tube trike build.

## Key Topics
- Head tube angle definition and typical range (60–70 degrees)
- Rake: the offset of the fork, and its role in self-steering
- Trail: the distance between two reference lines through the fork, and its effect on handling
- The relationship between rake and trail (increasing one affects the other)
- Practical guidance on acceptable trail values (5–7.5 cm)
- Construction of the head tube assembly using paper tubes, support bars, stays, and spreaders
- Progress update on the trike build (short wheelbase recumbent design)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Paper tubes (hand-rolled, used as structural members throughout the build)
- Glue (referenced for tube joining)
- Strapping material (for reinforcing joints)

## Techniques and Methods
- Visual geometry demonstration using a reference bicycle to explain head angle, rake, and trail
- Cutting paper tubes to angle for the head tube fitting
- Gluing and strapping paper tubes together
- Using support bars, spreader bars, and stays to distribute fork forces into the frame
- Capping the head tube at the correct angle to achieve desired rake and trail
