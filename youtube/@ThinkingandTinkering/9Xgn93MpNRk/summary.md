## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a salvaged NEMA stepper motor (typically found in printers) into a low-cost high shear mixer. By removing the rotor bearings and magnets, drilling channels in the stator body, and sealing the housing, he creates a functional mixer that leverages the tight tolerances between rotor teeth and stator to shear liquid. The project is framed as a £450 alternative to commercial high shear blenders, with future potential as a frictional heater.

## Key Topics
- The heating effect of high shear blenders (frictional/kinetic energy transfer to liquid)
- Disassembly of a NEMA stepper motor to repurpose its tight-tolerance rotor/stator geometry
- Modification steps to convert the motor body into a flow-through shear mixer
- Testing the device as a mixer using titanium dioxide and water
- Future goal of running it at high RPM (10,000) to use as a frictional heater
- Cost comparison: DIY build vs. £450 commercial high shear blender

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **NEMA stepper motor** — salvaged from printers, used as the basis for the mixer
- **Neodymium ring magnets** — found inside the stepper motor rotor; removed during disassembly
- **Aluminium plate** — fabricated to seal the drilled holes in the stator body
- **Hot glue / resin** — mentioned as options for sealing internal channels
- **Titanium dioxide (TiO₂)** — used as a test substance to verify mixing performance
- **Water** — mixed with titanium dioxide during the test

## Techniques and Methods
- **Motor disassembly** — drifting the rotor out using a hammer and wood block to avoid deformation
- **Channel marking and drilling** — marking internal rotor channels on the stator exterior, then drilling holes to connect them to the outside
- **Housing modification** — bolting end caps back together, filling unwanted gaps (with hot glue, resin, or welding) to direct fluid flow through the rotor/stator gap
- **Aluminium plate fabrication** — covering drilled holes with a custom plate to control inlet/outlet flow
- **Functional testing** — running the assembly in a drill at ~700 RPM with a titanium dioxide/water slurry to verify mixing
