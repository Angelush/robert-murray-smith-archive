## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how commercially available carbon/graphite conductive paints — sold for EMF shielding and Faraday cage applications — can be repurposed to build a simple flat-panel resistive heater. He walks through painting MDF with the conductive paint, attaching copper tape electrodes, and powering the panel with 12V DC to achieve a 44°C surface temperature at 35W. The video positions this as a proof-of-concept, with a follow-up promised covering safety, heat control, and voltage calculations.

## Key Topics
- The electrosmog/EMF protection market as a source of useful conductive materials
- How Faraday cages work and why conductive paints exist commercially
- Carbon/graphite paint as a resistive heating element
- Flat-panel wall heater construction on MDF
- Voltage-based heat control (no complex thermostat needed at low voltages)
- Potential applications: ambient energy collection, radiant wall heating

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon/graphite conductive paint** — the core material; water-based, no metallic content, marketed for EMF shielding; ~£12 for 125ml or £30–50/litre
- **MDF (medium-density fibreboard)** — used as the substrate for the heater panel
- **Copper tape (slug tape)** — adhesive-backed copper used as electrode strips
- **Sticky-back plastic** — used to cover and protect the painted surface (book-covering film)
- **12V DC power supply** — used to drive the heater panel

## Techniques and Methods
- Laying adhesive copper tape strips along opposite edges of the substrate to form electrodes, with folded-over connection tabs
- Applying 2–3 coats of conductive paint between the copper strips, brushing in one consistent direction to align conductivity
- Checking resistance between the copper strips (~10 ohms target) with a multimeter before powering
- Covering the finished surface with sticky-back plastic to prevent paint abrasion
- Controlling heat output purely by adjusting supply voltage (no separate thermostat required at 12V)
