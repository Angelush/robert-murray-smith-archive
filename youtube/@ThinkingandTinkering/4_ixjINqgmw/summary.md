## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a functional drawing compass from scrap mahogany left over from an old coffee table, combined with brass hardware found at a local store. Motivated by the need to draw large circles for another project and frustrated by his flimsy shop-bought compass, he crafts a tool that is both practical and beautiful enough to hang on the wall as a display piece.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing scrap mahogany (remnants of an old coffee table used across multiple projects)
- Designing and cutting a two-legged compass with a fixed head from a single piece of wood
- Constructing a spring-action pencil holder with correct wood grain orientation
- Using found brass drawer handles as decorative and functional thumb screws
- The philosophy of making tools from minimal materials when a good commercial version isn't available

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Mahogany** — scrap offcuts from a recycled coffee table, used for the compass arms and pencil holder block
- **Brass-effect drawer handles** — sourced from a local store, repurposed as the thumb-grip screw
- **Brass nut and bolt** — used as the pivot/tension mechanism between the two arms
- **Stainless steel nail** — trimmed and set at an angle to serve as the compass point
- **Superglue** — used to secure the nail in the drilled foot
- **Oil** — applied as a wood finish

## Techniques and Methods
- Sawing down one side of the mahogany head and swivelling to cut the other side, creating two separate compass legs from one piece
- Carving the separation between legs using a carpet knife
- Sanding the arms to a smooth, rounded shape
- Drilling an angled hole in the foot to align the nail point correctly when the compass is closed
- Drilling a 7.5 mm hole in a separate mahogany block and cutting a slot to create a spring-action pencil clamp
- Drilling a 4 mm hole through the pencil clamp block for a tightening bolt
- Orienting wood grain correctly in the pencil clamp (along the length of the slot) to prevent splitting

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On rigidity:** Acknowledged a commenter's suggestion about adding more structural support, noting that in metal this would likely be essential, but the wood version was firm enough for his needs. Offered to do a follow-up on achieving the same from found materials if viewers were interested.
- **On minimal tooling:** Confirmed the entire project was completed with only a hand saw, drill, carpet knife, and sandpaper — consistent with his preference for doing as much as possible with minimal equipment.
- **On the beam compass alternative:** Mentioned he had considered making a beam compass but preferred this design.
- **On decorative utility:** Quoted the Latin phrase *"deuces et tutamen"* ("decorative and useful") — the motto on the first minted British pound coin — as a fitting description for the compass.
- **On historical context:** Noted that traditional compasses of this kind were made from boxwood.
- **On the finished piece:** Confirmed it is now hanging on his wall, and expressed genuine puzzlement that more people don't get into making their own tools given how poor commercially available alternatives can be.
