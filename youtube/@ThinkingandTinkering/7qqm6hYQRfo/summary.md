## Overview
Robert compares the energy output of a homemade lead carbon battery against a commercial CR2032 Panasonic lithium primary cell by logging simultaneous voltage and current readings, then calculating watt-seconds (joules) using Excel. Contrary to expectations, the lead carbon battery delivers approximately 9 joules versus 4 joules for the lithium cell — though it is considerably larger. Robert concludes this is a promising and surprising result, describing his "really bad battery" as performing quite capably against a commercial primary lithium cell.

## Key Topics
- Setting up BenchView software to simultaneously log voltage and current from two separate meters
- Discharging a lead carbon battery and a CR2032 lithium primary cell through a small motor to measure real-world power delivery
- Exporting logged data to Excel and multiplying voltage × current to compute power (watts) over time
- Comparing total energy (joules/watt-seconds) between the two battery chemistries
- Physical size comparison between the two battery formats
- Teasing an upcoming hemp battery test using the same methodology

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Lead carbon battery** — homemade, approximately 5 cm × 5 cm, ~0.1 mm thick; rechargeable; the primary subject under development
- **CR2032 Panasonic lithium primary cell** — commercial coin cell, ~3 mm thick, ~2 cm diameter; used as a benchmark reference; non-rechargeable
- **Zinc** — used as electrode plates sandwiching the CR2032 in the test rig

## Techniques and Methods
- **BenchView data logging** — software used to record voltage and current simultaneously from two multimeters, requiring a workaround to trick the meters into being recognised as different device types
- **Constant-load discharge test** — batteries run through a small motor until depleted, with voltage and milliamp draw logged throughout
- **Excel power calculation** — voltage and current readings exported and multiplied (V × A = W) column by column to generate a power curve
- **Watt-second integration** — summing the per-second watt values to obtain total energy in joules, used as the comparative metric
