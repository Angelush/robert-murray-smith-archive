## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith replicates a "Flynn Device" (a motor based on designs by someone named Flynn) using salvaged transformer cores instead of the fan motor internals typically used. He walks through the disassembly and preparation of a 1:1 isolation transformer, splitting it into two halves and fitting neodymium magnets to create a functional magnetic circuit, ready for electrical testing.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Flynn Device concept and existing replications
- Sourcing and selecting a 1:1 isolation transformer as an alternative core material
- Cutting technique for laminated iron cores without disturbing laminations
- Repairing scored coil wire and measuring coil parameters
- Magnet configuration (north-north / south-south) to direct flux out of the core ends
- Assembly of the completed device ahead of wiring and testing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 1:1 isolation transformer (salvaged from a TV or microwave oven)
- U-shaped laminated soft iron core
- Enamel wire coils (resistance ~2.4 ohms each, inductance ~1.7 millihenries each)
- Neodymium disc magnets (1 cm diameter × 2 mm thick, 24 per side, stacked in pairs)
- Super glue (applied to lamination ends to prevent separation during cutting)
- Red tape / insulation tape (to hold magnets together and re-insulate coil ends)
- Steel rule (used to demonstrate magnetic attraction)

## Techniques and Methods
- Desoldering a transformer from a PCB and cleaning it up
- Applying super glue to laminations before sawing to preserve integrity
- Cutting laminated iron cores with a fine-tooth saw and new blade, cutting parallel to the laminations (not across the edges) to avoid bending them out
- Unsoldering, removing a damaged outer coil layer, and resoldering to restore electrical integrity
- Balancing both coils by treating both the same way regardless of damage
- Stacking magnets in north-north / south-south orientation to force flux out through the core ends rather than containing it internally
