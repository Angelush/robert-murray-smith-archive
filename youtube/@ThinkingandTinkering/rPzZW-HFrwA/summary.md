## Overview
Robert corrects a mistake from video 1748, where he implied that an Ugrinsky wind turbine required 3D printing — that was only true of his twisted helix variant; the original Ugrinsky is a simple square-box design that can be made by hand from common materials. He builds one using 3D-printed parts as templates and stainless steel sheet for the curved infill panels, then tests it outdoors in a horizontal (rooftop) orientation. The video demonstrates that the turbine can spin under its own weight at low wind speeds (~0.8–1.3 m/s) even without a generator attached.

## Key Topics
- Correction/apology regarding the Ugrinsky turbine and 3D printing dependency
- Distinction between the original flat-blade Ugrinsky design and the twisted helix variant
- Using a 3D-printed part as a reusable cutting template for hand fabrication
- Recommended alternative materials (UPVC builders board, sheet aluminium, sheet plastic)
- Smoothing PLA with solvents (ethyl acetate, dichloromethane)
- Assembly with double-sided foam tape and M4 bolts
- Testing in horizontal orientation for rooftop and over-motorway deployment
- Future plans to add a generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA filament** — used to 3D print the blade/template parts (supplied by Alagoon)
- **ABS** — noted as preferable to PLA for outdoor use
- **UPVC builders board / foam-core fascia board** — Robert's preferred hand-cut alternative; UV-stable for ~25 years; sold by Eurocell
- **0.2 mm 306 stainless steel sheet** — used for the curved infill panels in this build
- **Sheet aluminium (~0.2–0.3 mm)** — suggested as an easier-to-work alternative to SS (less spring)
- **Sheet plastic** — general alternative for infill panels
- **Ethyl acetate** — solvent for smoothing PLA; sold as "Titan" UPVC cleaner
- **Dichloromethane** — alternative solvent for smoothing PLA; noted as hazardous
- **Double-sided foam tape** — used to hold infill panels during assembly
- **M4 bolts** — used to fasten the assembly together

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing blade forms in PLA to serve as reusable cutting templates
- Tracing template onto flat sheet material and hand-cutting blade profiles
- Bending stainless steel sheet by hand into an S-curve to fit between blade forms
- Taping infill panels in place with double-sided foam tape before final bolting
- Mounting turbine on a plastic U-bracket cradle in horizontal orientation
- Outdoor wind-speed testing using an anemometer (no generator attached)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Thingiverse file:** The 3D model is on Thingiverse — search "ugrinsky" and it will appear.
- **Sintra board** mentioned twice as another suitable material for the blades.
- **Sheet metal skill required:** Acknowledged that not everyone can handle sheet metal work.
- **Aluminium vs stainless steel:** Aluminium is easier to form (less spring) and would stay in shape better than SS.
- **Venturi modification:** Thinks it is certainly possible to bend the side plates to create a Venturi effect.
- **Balancing:** Agreed balancing is a good idea and said it is "pretty easy to do."
- **Electronic braking:** Shorting the generator output will act as a brake — "pretty simple to do."
- **Flywheel effect / orientation:** Heavier build gives up starting torque but gains even output; suspects overall performance difference between orientations will be small, but packing density will matter more.
- **Blade count:** Suggested 50–70 blades on thin steel seems more realistic than 10.
- **Rooftop context:** Reminded viewers that "roofs act as something of a wind guide."
- **Archimedes turbine:** Mentioned he was thinking about it as a related concept.
- **UPVC builders board availability:** Available locally in Europe at roughly the price of wood; harder and more expensive to source in the USA.
