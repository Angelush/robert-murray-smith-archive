## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith attempts to build a small wind turbine using a dried poppy head as the fan blade, aiming to generate enough power to light an LED. After two salvaged motors prove unsuitable — one too weak, one too torque-heavy — he solves the problem by hand-building a lightweight replacement commutator from cardboard, sticky tape, and neodymium magnets extracted from a Samsung phone case. The modified dishwasher motor successfully lights two LEDs from poppy-powered wind.

## Key Topics
- The challenge of matching a small, low-torque wind source (poppy head) to a suitable generator
- Diagnosing why the initial motor failed (insufficient voltage output: only 0.2V)
- Why the second (dishwasher pump) motor also failed (too much rotational resistance/torque)
- Design and construction of a custom lightweight magnet rotor from salvaged and household materials
- Final successful demonstration of poppy-powered LED lighting

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Poppy head (used as fan/turbine blade)
- Small DC motor salvaged from a bubble gun (~3V, ran on 2 AA batteries)
- Dishwasher pump motor (three-phase brushless)
- LEDs (at least two, wired across two phases of the brushless motor)
- Neodymium magnets (8 total, extracted from a leather Samsung phone case — 9 were found inside it)
- Cardboard (used to fabricate the rotor cylinder)
- Sticky tape (structural and magnetic attachment)
- Plastic rod from a fabric window blind (used as the rotor shaft)
- Hot glue (to attach the poppy head to the shaft)
- 2p coin (used as a sizing template for the cardboard circles)

## Techniques and Methods
- Salvaging and repurposing motors from consumer electronics and appliances
- Voltage measurement with a voltmeter to diagnose generator output
- Magnet polarity identification using a compass, marked red/white
- Fabricating a custom rotor by stacking cardboard circles (analogised to 3D printing layers) and wrapping with sticky tape
- Arranging magnets in alternating N-S-N-S polarity around the rotor circumference
- Using two LEDs wired in opposite directions (instead of a bridge rectifier) to capture both halves of the AC waveform from a brushless motor while minimising power loss
