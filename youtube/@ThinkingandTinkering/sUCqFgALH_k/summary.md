## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the conditions required to trigger thermal runaway in lithium-ion batteries, using thermite as an analogy for a heat-initiated chain reaction. After repeated attempts — nail puncture on both discharged and charged cells — he finds that significant abuse (including a blowtorch) is required to get any reaction, concluding that Li-ion batteries are far more robust than commonly feared.

## Key Topics
- Thermite reaction as an analogy for thermal runaway
- Three pathways to thermal runaway: thermal, mechanical (short circuit), and electrochemical
- Live experiments: nail puncture of discharged and charged Li-ion battery packs
- Why EV battery packs use steel enclosures and active cooling
- Practical safety assessment of Li-ion batteries in everyday use

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium — fuel component in thermite; also found inside Li-ion cells
- Metal oxide — oxidiser in thermite; also present in Li-ion cell chemistry
- Potassium permanganate — used as part of the thermite fuse
- Glycerol — combined with potassium permanganate to initiate thermite reaction
- Lithium-ion battery cells (manufactured 2016–2017)

## Techniques and Methods
- Nail-puncture test on a discharged Li-ion pack
- Nail-puncture test on a fully charged Li-ion pack
- Blowtorch application to force thermal runaway
- Short-circuit induction via mechanical puncture
