## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a cheap drone motor into a working speaker using a piece of cellophane as a vibration membrane. The build is extremely simple — the motor acts as an inductor/electromagnet that vibrates the cellophane diaphragm when connected directly to an amplifier output. The key insight is that any inductor (motor, ferrite, air core coil) can produce sound this way, making it essentially a DIY "sound bug."

## Key Topics
- Using drone motors (from cheap toy drones) as audio transducers
- The physics behind why inductors produce sound (magnetic field interaction)
- uPVC builders board ("Sintra board" in the US) as a cheap, versatile build material
- Cellophane as a heat-tensioned vibration membrane
- Connection directly to amplifier output — no special wiring required

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Drone motor** — the core transducer, sourced cheaply from toy drones (~4 for £5)
- **Cellophane** — flower-wrapping type; used as the vibration membrane; heat-shrinks taut when warmed
- **uPVC builders board (UPVC fascia/soffit board)** — foam-core PVC sheet used as the frame; sold as "Sintra board" in the US; main UK supplier is Eurocell
- **Double-sided tape / double-sided foam tape** — used to attach the motor to the cellophane membrane
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate / "crazy glue")** — used to bond uPVC board; bond is stronger than the material itself

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cutting uPVC board** — with a box knife or circular saw to produce a 15×15 cm square frame with a hole
- **Heat-tensioning cellophane** — applying a heat gun (or hair dryer) to shrink the cellophane drum-tight over the frame
- **Motor-to-membrane mounting** — attaching the motor to the centre of the cellophane using double-sided tape (no soldering to the membrane)
- **Direct amplifier connection** — clipping motor wires straight to amplifier output; polarity does not matter

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Wiring clarification:** The motor is not wired to the membrane — it is simply stuck on top of it with tape. The motor's own wires connect directly to the amplifier output, and polarity does not matter.
- **Bone conduction effect:** Holding the motor against your head anywhere will let you hear sound; it acts like a cheap bone conduction speaker, with the cellophane serving as the vibration membrane.
- **Why it works — inductor physics:** Any inductor will do this — ferrite cores, air-core coils, motors. Anything that creates a magnetic field in opposition to an existing field will produce sound. He equates them to commercial "sound bugs," which use Terfenol-D; this build is a cheap version exploiting the motor's inductance.
- **Power limitations:** He notes the output wouldn't have much power but encourages experimenting with different inductors.
- **TnT channel reference:** He directs viewers to his separate "TnT" channel where he builds a more capable full speaker.
- **Material clarification:** The foam core in the uPVC board is polyurethane (similar to Kingspan), not styrene.
