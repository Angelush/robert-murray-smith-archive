## Overview
This video is a detailed follow-up to Robert's previous video (1743) in which he built a rocket stove from plumbing fittings. Robert walks through the four main types of rocket stove designs (L, J, K, and V types), explains the specific pipe sizes and fittings needed for each, and covers costs, sourcing, and practical use — all while cooking a bacon sandwich and coffee on the stove to demonstrate it working.

## Key Topics
- Overview of the four rocket stove types: L-type, J-type, K-type, and V-type
- Specific pipe dimensions and fittings required for each type
- Cost breakdown for building each variant (approximately £50 for L-type, £90–100 for J-type)
- Where to source materials (plumbing suppliers like BES, metal cutting services like Metals for You)
- Using a trivet to support pots and pans on the stove
- Ash production (very low) and heat control (damper/baffle plate)
- Adapting the outdoor cooking stove for indoor use

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **100mm (4-inch) plumbing pipe** — main structural component for all stove types
- **Pipe elbow** — used in L-type and J-type configurations
- **Pipe T-fitting** — used in J-type configuration
- **Steel plate (~95mm × 200mm, 3–5mm thick)** — acts as the fuel shelf/air separator inside the stove
- **Silicate putty (Unibond brand)** — used as high-temperature adhesive/sealant; converts to a ceramic glass (~700°C); US equivalent is "stove cement"
- **Magnesium or calcium silicate** — the active compounds in the silicate putty
- **Nipples and feet fittings** — optional pipe fittings for adaptability and aesthetics
- **Steel cap/trivet** — used to support cooking vessels over the stove outlet
- **Sorrel cements** — mentioned as an alternative sealant (referenced from a previous video)

## Techniques and Methods
- Sliding and dry-fitting pipe sections before gluing
- Gluing pipe joints with silicate putty, then curing by heat (putty vitrifies into ceramic glass at ~700°C)
- Inserting a steel plate into the fuel chamber to separate fuel from airflow underneath
- Using a trivet (or improvised equivalent such as a drain cover) to support cookware
- Controlling burn rate via a damper or baffle plate on the air inlet or chimney draw
- Sourcing custom-cut steel plate from online metal cutting services (paying only for material used)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On insulation and "insane heat"**: Robert disagrees with viewers who suggest heavy insulation for maximum heat, arguing that excessive heat stresses the steel and shortens stove life — it's about balancing fuel cost against longevity.
- **On what makes a rocket stove**: He clarifies that to qualify as a true rocket stove, the design must gasify wood and burn the gasification products — which produces the characteristic roaring sound.
- **On operating temperature**: Confirms the stove reaches approximately 700°C, identifiable by the colour the steel turns.
- **On indoor adaptation**: Acknowledges he discussed this at the end of the video; controlling airflow (inlet damper or chimney baffle) is the key to adapting it for indoor heating.
- **On design philosophy**: States the entire ethos is simplicity — no tools required — which is why he hasn't added welded modifications, so anyone can replicate it.
- **On sourcing cheaply**: Recommends scrap yards as a way to cut material costs.
