## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed bevel gear assembly that achieves gear reduction using gears of identical size — something conventionally considered impossible. Building on a previous 2:1 ratio design, he adds a second module to achieve a 3:1 ratio, and explains that the effect arises from contra-rotation as the bevel gear orbits around the assembly. The device doubles as a satisfying fidget toy and the files are shared on Thingiverse for remixing.

## Key Topics
- The paradox of achieving gear reduction with same-sized gears
- Explanation of the 2:1 ratio mechanism from the previous design (contra-rotation of a bevel gear)
- Step-by-step assembly of a new module to extend the ratio to 3:1
- Back-drivability of the gear system
- Open invitation for the community to add further modules and explore higher ratios

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed PLA or similar filament (implied by printing process)
- Glue (used to secure specific components during assembly)

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in Tinkercad
- FDM 3D printing of gear components
- Press-fit and clip-based mechanical assembly
- Use of bevel gears in an epicycloidal arrangement to produce non-intuitive gear ratios
- Back-driveability testing by reversing the input
