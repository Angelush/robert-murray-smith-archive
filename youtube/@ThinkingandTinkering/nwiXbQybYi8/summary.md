## Overview
Video 1166 continues Robert's paper bicycle project from video 1165, providing background information on paper as a structural engineering material before construction begins. He explains the properties of paper composites, the importance of grain direction, and details the casein-based glue recipe he is using to make his paper sticks — arguing that paper's long history as a serious engineering material makes the bicycle project very viable.

## Key Topics
- Continuation of the paper bicycle project using rolled newspaper sticks
- BMX-style bike used as a physical model/reference for angles and geometry
- Historical uses of paper as a structural material (train wheels, cathedral domes, boats, canoes)
- Paper composites reinforced with various resins and glues
- Phenolic resin and its use in printed circuit boards
- Casein-based glue: recipe, properties, and historical applications
- Paper grain direction and its effect on mechanical properties (anisotropy)
- Strategy for aligning grain along the length of rolled sticks

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Newspaper/paper** — raw material for rolling into structural sticks
- **Casein** — protein used as the primary binder/glue (100 g per recipe)
- **Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime)** — combined with casein and water to activate the glue and add water resistance
- **Carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) / wallpaper paste** — noted as an alternative binder used by "Darcy," resulting in papier-mâché-like sticks
- **Phenolic resin (phenolic-formaldehyde)** — used in printed circuit boards and high-voltage insulation; cited as a precedent for paper composites
- **Polyurethane resin** — listed as one of many resins used in paper composites
- **Formaldehyde resin** — listed as one of many resins used in paper composites
- **Urea resin** — listed as one of many resins used in paper composites
- **Epoxy resin** — listed as one of many resins used in paper composites
- **Carbon black and oil** — clarified in comments as the composition of newspaper ink (not a toxin concern)

## Techniques and Methods
- Rolling newspaper sheets into tight cylindrical sticks using a stick-making machine
- Pre-coating paper with casein-based glue before rolling, to impregnate the composite
- Aligning paper grain along the tube axis to maximise longitudinal strength
- Using a physical BMX bike as a geometric reference to ensure correct angles during frame construction
- Mixing casein glue: 100 g casein + 150 g water, then separately 20 g calcium hydroxide + 50 g water, combined and stirred until a glue forms

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Grain direction vs. cross-ply**: Robert clarified that for tubes (where stresses run along the length), aligning the grain longitudinally is correct. Cross-ply would be better for panels that need stiffness in all directions, or for boxes/models where it matters less.
- **Rain resistance**: He is confident the finished bike will hold up in the rain.
- **Pressure during rolling**: He relies on the roller machine itself to compress the sticks tightly enough — no additional clamping pressure is applied separately.
- **Newspaper ink safety**: He confirmed the ink is simply carbon black and oil, dismissing toxicity concerns.
- **Fire risk**: He believes the paper sticks would burn similarly to wooden sticks.
- **Flour-and-water paste**: He advised against it for this project due to the need for water resistance on a bike, though he acknowledged it works well in other contexts.
- **Paper manufacturing history**: He clarified that bulk paper manufacturing is roughly 200 years old; before that it was hand-made, slow, and expensive.
- **General enthusiasm**: He repeatedly affirmed that "paper is just plain awesome as an engineered material."
