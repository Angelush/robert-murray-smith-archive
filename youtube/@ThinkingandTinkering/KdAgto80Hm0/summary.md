## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith helps his brother Dave build an intaglio (etching) press based on plans by Doug Forsythe, purchased for around £28. The build uses laser-cut steel parts, salvaged conveyor rollers, and basic welding to produce a press equivalent to a £3,000–5,000 commercial machine for roughly £250–300 in materials. The video demonstrates that with good plans, affordable laser-cutting services, and a modest amount of fabrication skill, high-quality equipment is within reach of most makers.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to Doug Forsythe's etching press plans and their value
- Using laser-cutting services as an accessible alternative to a fully equipped workshop
- Cost comparison: commercial press (£2,000–5,000) vs. self-built (~£250–300)
- Frame assembly from laser-cut quarter-inch steel side plates, bolted together with steel bar spacers
- Fitting salvaged conveyor rollers and matching them to pillow-block bearings
- Improvised lathe technique to reduce roller diameter using casters and an angle grinder
- Fabricating raising/lowering handles from threaded bar, nuts, and welded caps
- Fitting the press bed plate and completing the build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Quarter-inch (¼") steel plate — laser-cut side frames
- Steel bar/rod — spacers and handle stock
- Threaded bar — handle and adjustment mechanism
- Steel rollers — sourced secondhand (conveyor belt origin)
- Pillow-block bearings — roller mounting
- Steel nuts and bolts — frame assembly
- Concrete mold (repurposed) — used to produce the press feet

## Techniques and Methods
- Laser cutting (outsourced) — used to cut complex steel side plates from CAD drawings supplied with Doug Forsythe's plans
- MIG/arc welding — attaching feet, pinning handle nuts, capping handles
- Sawing steel bar to length — accounting for saw-blade kerf (approximately 3 mm) when sizing spacers
- Improvised cylindrical grinding — roller placed on four casters, driven by lathe belt, dressed down with an angle-grinder flap wheel to achieve correct bearing fit
- Bolted frame assembly — described as "Lego-like" construction
