## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the geometry behind cycloidal gear drives and demonstrates how to design a custom cycloid disc from scratch using Tinkercad, including its built-in Code Blocks scripting environment. The key insight is that gear ratios are fundamentally about distance traveled along a surface — not tooth count — and that a cycloid profile can be generated by iteratively "chiseling" small arcs from a disc using an eccentric cam shape. The video culminates in a printable cycloidal disc produced entirely within Tinkercad's free browser-based tools.

## Key Topics
- What cycloid drives are and why they are useful (compact, high gear ratio, wear-resistant)
- The fundamental principle of gear ratios as surface distance traveled, not tooth count
- How eccentric cams drive cycloid motion
- Step-by-step method for constructing a cycloidal profile by subtracting overlapping cam positions
- Using Tinkercad's Code Blocks (block-based scripting/macro tool) to automate repetitive geometry operations
- Mirroring and copying a half-tooth to build a complete cycloid disc
- Exporting the result as an STL for 3D printing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned

## Techniques and Methods
- Manual geometric construction: positioning circles, rotating and subtracting cam shapes at incremental angles to approximate the cycloid curve
- Eccentric cam design: offsetting a small circle to the edge of a larger circle to define cam throw
- Iterative subtraction / "chiseling": rotating the cam assembly in small angular steps (e.g., 18°, 9°, 4.5°, down to ~0.5°) and merging each position as a hole into the disc
- Mirroring a half-tooth and joining to form a full tooth, then rotationally copying around the disc (36° per tooth for a 10-tooth cycloid)
- Tinkercad Code Blocks scripting: writing a loop to repeat the subtraction operation 40 times automatically
- Exporting from Code Blocks as STL, reimporting into standard Tinkercad workspace for final assembly
- 3D printing and laser cutting (mentioned as proven fabrication methods for cycloids)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Eccentric cam rotation clarification:** In response to a viewer question, Robert explains that when constructing the profile you fix the cam rotation to 180° so it traces the correct path length. Setting it to 198° would make the path too long and break the geometry — the line is being *drawn* by this process, not pre-existing.
- **Spirograph connection:** Robert confirms a viewer's intuition that spirograph mechanisms were a major inspiration for his cycloid work, and mentions there is a follow-up video on the topic.
- He otherwise kept replies brief (acknowledgements and encouragement), with no additional technical content.
