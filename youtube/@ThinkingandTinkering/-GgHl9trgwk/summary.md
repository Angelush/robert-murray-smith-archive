## Overview
Robert adapts his rocket stove (previously used in multiple incarnations) to burn waste oil, building on lessons from video 1685 where he made a simpler oil burner from concrete blocks. The key innovation is a mineral wool basket made from stainless steel expanded metal, placed inside the rocket stove's firebox and fed by a simple drip-feed system. The result is a ferociously hot, remarkably simple burner, though Robert notes improvements are needed — a longer chimney and more air holes — to reduce smoke and improve combustion.

## Key Topics
- Adapting a rocket stove for waste oil burning
- Comparison with the simpler concrete block oil burner from video 1685
- Why spray nozzles don't work for waste oil (gumming/clogging) — drip feed is the correct approach
- Construction of a stainless steel expanded metal basket to hold mineral wool
- Lighting method: pre-soaking mineral wool in oil and using a firelighter
- Observations during the burn: heat output, smokiness, and duration (~1 hour from a bean can)
- Potential applications: sand battery, water heater, room heater, ducted air heating, mass heater
- Planned improvements: longer chimney, additional air holes at the base

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Waste oil** — fuel source, used as a drip feed
- **Mineral wool** — used as a wick/combustion medium inside the basket
- **Stainless steel expanded metal** — formed into a U-shaped basket to hold the mineral wool
- **Copper pipe** — inserted down the chimney to deliver the oil drip feed
- **Bean can** — used as the oil reservoir in the drip-feed system
- **Tank fitting / valve** — used as the flow control valve on the bean can reservoir
- **Compression fittings** — used throughout the oil feed assembly
- **Fire lighter** — used to ignite the mineral wool

## Techniques and Methods
- **Drip-feed oil delivery** — gravity-fed oil via copper pipe; avoids clogging problems of spray nozzles
- **Mineral wool basket fabrication** — stainless steel expanded metal crimped in a vice into a U-shape, inserted into the firebox
- **Rocket stove combustion** — tall chimney creates strong draft; burn tunnel length shown to be critical (shortening it worsens results)
- **Pre-soaking ignition** — mineral wool loaded with oil then lit with a firelighter

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Filtering waste oil**: Robert acknowledged a suggestion about filtering oil as a nice idea.
- **Flash-back safety concern**: Robert gave a detailed and reassuring explanation — waste oil will not flash back inside the copper pipe because there is no oxygen present. Combustion can only occur at the exit point of the drip. If the pipe got hot enough, ignition would progress gradually: first a burning drip, then a liquid jet burn, then a gas-like steady flame — essentially behaving like a gas burner. He noted the video footage inside the firebox shows the fuel jet going liquid and burning almost immediately upon exiting the pipe.
- **Burn tunnel length**: Robert confirmed from his own experiments that shortening the burn tunnel makes results worse — the length shown in the video was found to work well.
- **UK weather / freeze concern**: He acknowledged a viewer's point about cold-weather considerations (apparently irrelevant in the UK climate) and thanked them for raising it.
- **Red diesel**: A light-hearted comment expressing envy toward a viewer who has access to red diesel.
