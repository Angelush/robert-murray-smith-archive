## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores why the standard bicycle crank is mechanically inefficient and how the treadle mechanism from Singer sewing machines solved this problem by converting reciprocating motion into rotation with up to five times the torque. He traces the treadle's influence on the penny farthing bicycle and railway pump trolleys, then demonstrates building a simple hand-powered electromagnetic generator using a 3D-printed crank mechanism, a magnet disc, and a coil — successfully producing over 1.5V from a single coil.

## Key Topics
- The inefficiency of standard bicycle cranks at top dead center (pushing straight against the crank)
- The 1980s "biocycle" elliptical crank as an attempted fix
- The Singer sewing machine treadle and its torque advantage (up to 5× over a standard crank)
- The penny farthing's use of the Singer treadle crank mechanism, allowing the seat to be positioned further back
- Railway pump trolleys (also called pump carts, jiggers, or rail velocipedes) as another application
- 3D printing a treadle-based crank mechanism in TinkerCAD
- Using the mechanism to spin a magnet disc and generate electricity via electromagnetic induction
- Potential applications: wearable energy harvesting (e.g., shoe-mounted generators)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium-style magnets arranged in a north-south-north-south configuration around the edge of a 3D-printed disc
- 12mm × 6mm × 4mm bearings (four required; two per cradle)
- 6mm diameter steel bar (axle)
- 2mm diameter pins — 10cm and 50mm lengths (for pivots)
- Super glue (for fixing cranks to the bar)
- A coil (used to generate voltage near the spinning magnet disc)

## Techniques and Methods
- Designing parts in TinkerCAD for 3D printing
- Assembling a treadle-style crank mechanism with connecting rods and pivot pins
- Installing press-fit bearings into a printed cradle; drilling out holes to size with a 2.5mm drill if needed
- Setting crank arms at 90° offset (top dead center alignment) on the axle
- Demonstrating electromagnetic induction by holding a coil adjacent to the spinning magnet disc
- Measuring output voltage with a multimeter
