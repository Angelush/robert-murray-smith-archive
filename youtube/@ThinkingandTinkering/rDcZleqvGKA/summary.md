## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith attempts to grow carbon nanotubules at home by combining iron nanoparticles (made from iron chloride and green tea) with amorphous carbon (made from sugar and sulfuric acid), then heating the mixture in a microwave. He uses electrical conductivity as a proxy test for nanotube formation, since amorphous carbon is non-conductive while carbon nanotubes are. Though results are preliminary and imperfect, he observes a notable increase in conductivity after microwave heating, suggesting something chemically significant occurred.

## Key Topics
- Home synthesis of iron nanoparticles using green tea polyphenols as reducing/capping agents
- Production of amorphous carbon via the sulfuric acid + sugar "black snake" demonstration
- Using a microwave as a low-cost alternative to conventional high-temperature CVD nanotube growth
- Conductivity measurement as a functional test for nanotube formation
- Localized heating of indium tin oxide (ITO) film as a substrate for carbon nanotube growth
- Limitations of the experiment and directions for future work

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Iron(III) chloride (FeCl₃)** — iron salt source for nanoparticle synthesis
- **Green tea** — polyphenols act as reducing and capping agents to form iron nanoparticles in suspension
- **Table sugar (sucrose, ~50 g)** — precursor for amorphous carbon
- **Concentrated sulfuric acid (~20 ml)** — dehydrates sugar to produce amorphous carbon; also noted as drain cleaner
- **Amorphous carbon** — non-conductive carbon precursor for nanotube growth
- **Indium tin oxide (ITO) film** — conductive thin film (salvaged from an old television) used as a substrate; exploits localized microwave heating
- **Zirconium balls** — used in the ball mill to reduce contamination during grinding

## Techniques and Methods
- **Green tea reduction** — mixing green tea into an iron salt solution to precipitate iron nanoparticles in suspension
- **Sugar/sulfuric acid "black snake" reaction** — dehydration of sucrose with concentrated H₂SO₄ to produce expanded amorphous carbon
- **Ball milling** — grinding washed amorphous carbon lumps in water to produce a nanoparticulate suspension
- **Microwave heating** — combining iron nanoparticle and amorphous carbon suspensions (50/50 mix) and heating for ~5–10 minutes to drive nanotube growth
- **Resistance/conductivity measurement** — using a multimeter (up to 200 MΩ range) to test before and after microwave treatment as a proxy for nanotube formation
- **ITO substrate experiment** — depositing the iron/carbon mix onto an ITO film and microwaving; checking conductivity on the previously non-conductive sticky side as evidence of surface carbon nanotube growth
