## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to turn mushrooms into a functional supercapacitor by carbonizing them at 700°C, grinding the resulting carbon into a powder, and forming it into an electrode paste. The video is part of a broader series exploring the surprising utility of fungi and mushrooms. The supercapacitor successfully powers a small motor, achieving approximately 150 farads per gram as reported in the referenced research paper.

## Key Topics
- Carbonization of organic materials (specifically mushrooms) for use in energy storage
- Supercapacitor construction from bio-derived carbon
- Activation of carbon using potassium hydroxide (discussed but not performed)
- DIY home lab techniques for high-temperature pyrolysis
- The broader theme of mushrooms/fungi as a versatile material platform

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Mushrooms** — the primary feedstock; 250g raw yields ~5g of carbonized carbon
- **Activated carbon** — used as a protective burial medium during carbonization
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — discussed as an activation agent (4:1 ratio by weight), though not used in this demonstration
- **Acrylic binder** — art-grade binder added at 10–20% by weight to form the electrode paste
- **Deionised (DI) water** — used to make the paste
- **Graphite foil** — used as the current collector substrate for the electrodes
- **Kitchen towel** — used as the separator between the two electrode halves
- **Electrolyte** — briefly referenced when assembling the cell (specific electrolyte not named)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pyrolysis/carbonization** — mushrooms buried in activated carbon inside a steel container, heated to 700°C for 1 hour in a homemade kiln
- **Protective atmosphere substitution** — using activated carbon as a burial medium instead of argon gas, making the process accessible for a home lab
- **Mechanical grinding** — carbonized mushrooms ground into fine powder using a kitchen blender
- **Electrode paste preparation** — mixing carbon powder with acrylic binder and DI water
- **Supercapacitor assembly** — painting paste onto graphite foil, drying, cutting into two halves, sandwiching with a separator and electrolyte
- **Capacitor testing** — charging at 1.6V and discharging through a small DC motor to verify energy storage
