## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a variable-speed ball mill using a household drill, recycled desk legs as rollers, inline skate bearings, and a wooden cradle. He explains the two fundamental ball mill designs (central-axis and dual-roller), makes the case for the dual-roller design as the more practical homebuild, and walks through the complete construction from cutting timber to a working final assembly. The key takeaway is that a functional ball mill can be built in roughly two hours from scrap materials at zero cost.

## Key Topics
- Two approaches to ball mill design: central-axis spinning container vs. dual-roller drive
- Why the dual-roller design is preferred for home construction (no precise center-drilling required)
- Speed ratio mathematics: calculating drum RPM from roller and container circumference using π × diameter
- Motor options: household drill (0–2800 RPM with variable speed) vs. dedicated motor with pulley reduction
- Roller bearing installation and the importance of only contacting the inner bearing sleeve (using modified dome nuts as cones)
- Drum precession (axial drift) and how to correct it with electrical tape or a secondary roller bearing
- Grinding medium selection relative to drum speed — too fast causes centrifugal sticking; heavier media (steel ball bearings) required at higher RPMs

- **Aluminium/graphite composites** — example application (piezoelectric composites)
- **Nickel hydroxide-doped graphene** — stated as Robert's personal intended use for the mill

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dual-roller ball milling** — container rests on two parallel driven rollers; no center axis required
- **Speed ratio calculation** — circumference of drive roller divided by circumference of drum gives RPM reduction ratio
- **Bearing cone modification** — dome nuts sawn flat to create a cone that contacts only the inner race of the roller bearing, preventing binding
- **Paired-board drilling** — two side boards screwed together and drilled simultaneously to ensure aligned bearing holes
- **Precession correction** — electrical tape buildup on roller or addition of a flanking roller bearing to prevent axial drum drift
- **Drill-as-motor integration** — drill handle held in a U-cut slot in the cradle frame; drill chuck engages one roller directly
