## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith derives the parallel plate capacitor formula (C = εA/d) from first principles, using a common-sense approach alongside formal propositions from Gauss's law. The video is part of a mathematics series and applies the derived formula directly to supercapacitor design, showing how the three variables — permittivity, plate area, and plate separation — guide practical research decisions.

## Key Topics
- Mathematical propositions underlying capacitance (Gauss's law, charge/voltage definition)
- Building a physical model of a parallel plate capacitor
- Common-sense derivation of the capacitance formula C = εA/d
- Role of permittivity (absolute and relative) in energy storage
- How each variable (ε, A, d) affects capacitance
- Practical strategies for maximising supercapacitor capacitance
- Mathematics as a language describing relationships between sizes

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon** — used for its high surface area in supercapacitor electrodes
- **Barium titanate** — noted for extremely high relative permittivity (in the millions)
- **Aluminium oxide** — dielectric layer in electrolytic capacitors; relative permittivity in the tens-to-twenties range
- **Acetonitrile** — mentioned in author comments as the typical solvent used in supercapacitors

## Techniques and Methods
- Parallel plate capacitor modelling as the basis for formula derivation
- Common-sense / proportional reasoning to establish relationships between C, ε, A, and d
- Physical testing: separating or compressing plates to observe capacitance change
- Minimising dielectric thickness (e.g. oxide layer in electrolytics) to increase capacitance
- Surface roughening and plate rolling to maximise electrode area in a compact volume
- Using high-surface-area carbon structures to dramatically increase effective plate area

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Ion double-layer capacitance:** At the relevant scale, capacitance is formed by the double-layering of ions — a key mechanism distinct from simple dielectric polarisation.
- **Supercapacitor solvent:** Supercapacitors typically use acetonitrile as the solvent, not water.
- **Power formula:** Power is W = VI minus losses; a viewer's related conclusion broadly follows from that.
- **Energy storage vs. self-discharge:** Supercapacitors do not lose their energy quicker than batteries — they simply do not store as much energy to begin with.
- **DIY testing encouraged:** Robert challenged a sceptical commenter to test permittivity themselves by placing different materials between two pieces of kitchen foil, charging it, and measuring how long it lights an LED.
