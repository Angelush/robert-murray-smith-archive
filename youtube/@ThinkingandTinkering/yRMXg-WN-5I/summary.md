## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores whether it's possible to build a DIY flameless catalytic heater as a cheaper alternative to expensive commercial platinum-based units. He references a 1965 US Defense Department document by Victor Shikov outlining a multi-oxide catalyst formulation using potassium dichromate, manganese sulfide, and cobalt nitrate. He demonstrates the process on carbon felt, concluding it works but recommends mineral wool as a better substrate.

## Key Topics
- How catalytic heaters work (flameless oxidation, lowered activation energy)
- The high cost of commercial catalytic heaters due to platinum catalysts
- The 1965 declassified Shikov defense document as a cheap catalyst recipe
- Safety hazards of potassium dichromate (hexavalent chromium) as a carcinogen
- Conversion of dichromate salts to safe chromium(III) oxide via heat
- Carbon felt vs. mineral wool as substrate materials
- How commercial catalytic heaters ignite and sustain reaction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium dichromate** — primary catalyst precursor; noted as a serious carcinogen in salt form
- **Ammonium dichromate** — mentioned as an alternative dichromate form
- **Chromium(III) oxide** — the safe green oxide product formed after heating dichromate
- **Manganese sulfate** (referred to as "manganese sulfide" in speech) — catalyst component
- **Cobalt nitrate** — catalyst component
- **Carbon felt / carbon foam** — substrate used in the demonstration
- **Mineral wool** — recommended alternative substrate, rated to 1600°C
- **Paraffin (kerosene)** — hydrocarbon fuel used to douse and ignite the catalyst
- **Platinum** — the catalyst used in expensive commercial heaters; reason for catalytic converter thefts

## Techniques and Methods
- Dissolving dichromate, manganese, and cobalt salts in solution to ensure intimate contact and small particle size
- Impregnating the substrate (carbon felt) by dipping it into the mixed salt solution
- Calcination: heating the impregnated substrate to convert soluble toxic salts into stable metal oxides (target: ~400°C for 3–4 hours per Shikov; demonstrated with a blowtorch)
- Ignition procedure: dousing the catalyst with hydrocarbon fuel, lighting it, and allowing the flame to self-extinguish once operating temperature is reached
- Commercial method noted: using a heating element to reach operating temperature before fuel takes over
