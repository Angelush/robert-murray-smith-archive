## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a self-limiting conductive ink based on Positive Temperature Coefficient (PTC) behaviour, applied to fabric as a flexible heating element that regulates its own temperature without any control electronics. The material works by expanding when hot — pushing conductive particles apart to increase resistance and halt heating — then contracting and conducting again as it cools. Beyond heating, he also shows its use as a pressure sensor, opening up applications in smart flooring and position detection.

## Key Topics
- Explanation of Positive Temperature Coefficient (PTC) behaviour in conductive materials
- PTC ink applied to fabric as a self-regulating, electronically-passive heating mat
- Demonstration at 24 V / 12 W with even surface heating (~39°C)
- Dual-use as a pressure sensor (resistance drops dramatically under pressure)
- Concept for an underfloor position-detection grid using the same ink
- Applications in heated clothing and smart floor sensors
- Philosophy of problem-solving rather than following set recipes

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PTC conductive ink (KTC 810)** — the self-limiting ink applied to fabric; described as flexible, waterproof when dry, and resistant (~40–50 Ω/square)
- **Volatile organic solvents** — used in the ink formulation; noted as unpleasant to work with ("very stinky") but safe once dried and applied
- **Supermarket bed sheet / curtain fabric** — used as the substrate for ink application

## Techniques and Methods
- Applying PTC ink to fabric substrate (printing/coating)
- Resistance measurement per square (sheet resistance characterisation)
- Thermal imaging to verify even surface heating
- Pressure sensing via resistance change measurement (~400 Ω at rest, ~4 Ω under light pressure)
- Conceptual crosshatch grid layout (PTC ink in one axis, conductive ink in the other) to create a position-sensing matrix analogous to a touchpad
