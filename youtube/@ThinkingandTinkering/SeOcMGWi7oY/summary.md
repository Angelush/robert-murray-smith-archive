## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a working Scotch yoke mechanism — a device that converts rotary motion into linear reciprocating motion — using hardwood cutting boards sourced cheaply from a local supermarket. The video is part of his "Mechanical Alphabet" series and serves as both a practical build tutorial and an appreciation of elegant mechanical design. He demonstrates the finished model and notes that the same mechanism is found inside everyday jigsaws.

## Key Topics
- The Scotch yoke as a mechanism for converting rotary motion to reciprocating (linear) motion
- Sourcing inexpensive exotic hardwoods from supermarket cutting boards and chopping boards
- Step-by-step construction of the Scotch yoke model from timber
- The mechanism's role in Robert's ongoing "Mechanical Alphabet" series
- Real-world applications of the Scotch yoke (e.g., jigsaw blades)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Beech wood — cutting board blocks purchased for £2.50, used for the main components
- Olive wood (suspected) — another cutting board block purchased for £2.00, noted for attractive colour and grain

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting timber pieces to shape from chopping board stock
- Assembly of a Scotch yoke comprising: a handle/crank, a wheel with an off-centre peg, a slotted yoke, and slide guides
- Hand woodworking to produce a functional mechanical model
