## Overview
This is Part 7 of Robert Murray-Smith's DIY gasification and generator build series, covering the final assembly steps before first firing. Robert installs the cooling unit with a condensation trap (inspired by viewer comments), discusses sealing weld joints with Gun Gum, and outlines the last two remaining tasks — sealing and adding a blower unit — before the system is ready to run a generator.

## Key Topics
- Cooling unit assembly using table leg pipes welded to box sections
- Condensation trap design using a glass water bottle with a stainless steel top
- The role of a blower unit to create initial downdraft in the gasifier
- Sealing imperfect welds with Gun Gum (automotive exhaust sealant)
- Optional bubbler unit as a final gas cleaner and flame arrester
- The value of community comments in improving the build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Table leg pipes and box sections** — used to construct the cooling unit
- **Glass water bottle with stainless steel top** — repurposed as a condensation trap
- **Gun Gum** — automotive exhaust sealant used to seal weld joints; described as gas-impermeable and rock-hard when set
- **Silicone sealant / RTV silicone** — used for sealing unit connections; Robert notes some grades tolerate high heat
- **12-volt battery** — power source for the startup blower
- **Syngas (producer gas)** — the product of the gasification process being routed through the cooling system

## Techniques and Methods
- Welding thin pipe with spot welds to avoid burn-through, then sealing with Gun Gum
- Drilled-and-welded bottle modification to create a transparent condensation trap
- Modular "slot-on" assembly approach — each unit slots onto the main machine frame
- Using engine vacuum to sustain gas flow once running (blower only needed for startup)
- Bubbler/water-bucket method as an optional alternative for gas cooling, cleaning, and flame arresting

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Cooling rationale:** The cooler collapses the gas for higher energy density — "the cooler the gas the higher the energy density." He's not focused on water extraction specifically, just some cooling effect.
- **Sealing materials:** He notes that RTV silicone grades vary considerably in heat tolerance — some can handle quite high temperatures.
- **Water injection:** Pushes back on a claim about water injection and power, noting water injection at the manifold was historically used (including by the Air Force) to increase power.
- **Rubber crumb as feedstock:** Speculates you would need to carbonise rubber crumb first before using it as gasifier fuel, based on prior experiments.
- **Flame arrester / bubbler:** Clarifies these are not normally part of a standard gasifier design, but he's including both anyway.
- **Heat recovery:** Flagged as an upgrade for after first run — also liked a commenter's CO detector suggestion.
- **FEMA handbook:** Repeatedly credits the FEMA handbook as the basis for the burner sizing and overall design.
- **Aesthetics:** "I know it is made from scrap but I don't see why it has to look like it was made from scrap."
- **Engine self-regulation:** Notes the burner is sized to the engine per FEMA specs and the engine vacuum provides a degree of self-regulation.
