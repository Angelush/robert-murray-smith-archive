## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith highlights the environmental problem of toothbrush waste — 200 million discarded annually in the UK alone — and explains why mixed-material construction makes them nearly impossible to recycle economically. He then demonstrates an inventive upcycling solution: using toothbrush heads as the bristle elements in a 3D-printed ratchet/freewheel mechanism, turning a landfill-bound object into a silent, surprisingly forceful mechanical component.

## Key Topics
- Scale of plastic toothbrush waste and its environmental impact (landfill, ocean pollution)
- Why most toothbrushes cannot be economically recycled (multi-material construction)
- Design of a 3D-printed ratchet cog system in Tinkercad
- How toothbrush bristles naturally function as ratchet teeth (one-directional flex)
- Potential applications for the resulting freewheel mechanism
- "Third-order recycling": separating handles (recyclable into filament) from heads (upcycled into the ratchet)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polypropylene** — toothbrush body/handle
- **Polyethylene** — coloured grip sections
- **Silicone** — flex/grip elements on some brushes
- **Acrylic** — transparent sections
- **Nylon** — bristles (~2,800 per head); also noted as invented in 1938 by DuPont
- **Metal staples** — hold bristles into the brush head
- **8 mm bar** — used to seat the ratchet into its printed cradle

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in **Tinkercad** (ratchet cog with upstands, matching ratchet-tooth cog, and a cradle)
- **FDM 3D printing** of all structural parts
- **Mechanical separation**: sawing/chopping toothbrush heads from handles
- **Adhesive bonding**: gluing brush heads onto printed cog upstands, bristles pointing outward
- **Filament recycling**: polypropylene handles can be granulated and extruded back into 3D-printing filament
