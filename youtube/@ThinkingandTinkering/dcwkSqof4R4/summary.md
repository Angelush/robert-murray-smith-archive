## Overview
This short video is an update on Robert Murray-Smith's ongoing work developing a home-made bulletproof plastic, previously demonstrated being shot at. The presenter shows that the plastic is extraordinarily hard — hard enough to snap the tip of a tempered knife blade during routine mold cleaning — and also notes that it bonds strongly to metal without any surface preparation and exhibits measurable electrical conductivity.

## Key Topics
- Progress and iterative improvement of the bulletproof plastic formulation
- Hardness demonstration: the plastic broke the tip of a tempered knife blade
- Strong adhesion of the plastic to bare metal (no surface treatment required)
- Electrical conductivity of the plastic, measured with a two-probe resistance test
- Potential applications beyond ballistic protection (conductive coatings, metal cladding)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bulletproof plastic (unspecified polymer)** — the central subject; pressed from powder in a mold; Robert deliberately keeps the polymer identity secret
- **Metal** — used as a substrate; the plastic bonds to it readily without any pre-treatment

## Techniques and Methods
- **Compression moulding** — plastic powder pressed in a reusable metal mold
- **Knife/blade hardness test** — a tempered blade used to clean mold residue; the plastic snapped the blade tip
- **Two-probe resistance measurement** — probes placed ~1 inch apart on the plastic surface to measure conductivity (~5.5 ohms)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Polymer kept secret:** Robert explicitly refuses to name the polymer, but encourages experimenters to find a better one themselves: *"your job — should you choose to accept it — is to find a better one."*
- **Project paused:** The bulletproof plastic work had to be set aside due to time constraints; he directed followers to another project (a road trip/crowdfunding campaign).
- **Thickness claim:** At 1 inch thickness, he suggests it would stop "just about anything."
- **Mixing is the key challenge:** For those trying to add additives (e.g., conductive fillers), Robert notes that thorough dispersion is the main difficulty.
- **Manufacturing form:** The material is made as a powder before pressing.
- **Moulding ideas:** He mentions he would be more likely to use a one-piece mold and is open to experimenting with gluing pieces together.
- **Can't be purchased:** Confirmed the plastic is not commercially available.
