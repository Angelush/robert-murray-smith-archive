## Overview
This is the second part of Robert's homemade ukulele (banjo-ukulele) build series, focusing on shaping and finishing the neck. He walks through how he shaped an oak neck using workshop tools, installed improvised copper frets, made a fiberglass nut, and previews the tin-can body he found at a pound store — teasing the final assembly video.

## Key Topics
- Shaping the ukulele neck from oak floorboard
- Installing frets using copper electrical wire instead of commercial fret wire
- Making a nut from fiberglass offcut
- String retainer/tuning head recap from earlier video
- Using a 6-inch tin can as the banjo-ukulele body
- Preview of the final assembly to come

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oak floorboard** — used for the neck
- **Copper wire (2.5 mm cross-sectional area)** — used as improvised fret wire; the type used for socket ring mains, available from B&Q, Home Depot, or electrical stores
- **4 mm fiberglass sheet** — used to make the nut
- **Super glue** — used to seat frets and nut
- **Teak oil** — applied as a finish to make the neck look shiny
- **6-inch tin can (from a pound store)** — to be used as the banjo body

## Techniques and Methods
- Neck shaping with a **belt sander**, **disc sander**, **chisel**, and **wood rasp**
- Cutting an angled heel using chisel then belt sander
- Cutting **fret slots** and pressing in copper wire frets, secured with super glue and a clamp
- **Dressing frets level** with a file
- Cutting a nut slot with a **hacksaw**, gluing in fiberglass nut, filing string slots
- Drilling and screwing the string retainer plate
