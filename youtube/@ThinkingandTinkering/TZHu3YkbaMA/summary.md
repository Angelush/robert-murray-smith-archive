## Overview
Robert builds a rubber band heat engine mounted on the magnetic floating bearing system introduced in the previous video (1428). The engine exploits rubber's unusual thermomechanical property — contracting rather than expanding when heated — to generate rotation. The result works in principle but runs at an extremely slow speed (~2 rotations per day with only four rubber bands).

## Key Topics
- Rubber band heat engines and their thermomechanical principle
- Integration with the magnetic levitation bearing from video 1428
- Low-friction machines as a motivation for the magnetic bearing system
- Balancing a rotating disc using blue tack
- Historical context: half-horsepower and planned five-horsepower versions of rubber engines

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Rubber bands — the working medium of the heat engine; contract on heating rather than expanding
- Plastic disc — cut into a ring to serve as the rotor
- Transformer core laminations (small metal pieces) — used as stress spreaders/spacers in the notches of the disc to prevent rubber bands from pulling through the plastic
- Blue tack — used as a balancing mass on the disc

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a plastic disc into a ring to form the rotor
- Notching the rotor rim to seat rubber bands and distribute tension
- Spanning rubber bands across the disc diameter to create an off-centre pull when heated
- Static balancing of the rotor by hand (placing the disc on an axle and adding counterweight until it holds any position)
- Time-lapse filming to capture the very slow rotation
