## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces electrostatic motors — simple devices powered by high voltage — and explores the idea of harvesting atmospheric electricity to run them. The video explains how the Earth's electric field creates a voltage gradient of roughly 100V per meter of altitude, and highlights recent developments (including a motor powerful enough to drill) that suggest atmospheric energy may be far more practical than once thought.

## Key Topics
- How electrostatic motors work (like-charge repulsion / unlike-charge attraction)
- The Benjamin Franklin wheel as an early example of an electrostatic motor
- Earth's atmospheric electric field: ~100V per meter of altitude, extending ~50 km
- Methods for harvesting atmospheric electricity (wire on drone, balloon, etc.)
- Historical and contemporary researchers working on atmospheric-electricity motors
- The C-Motive electrostatic motor (stator/rotor plate design, three-phase sine wave drive)
- Torque limitations of earlier atmospheric motors vs. recent breakthroughs
- Broader optimism about energy solutions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper (fins on the 3D-printed rotor)
- 3D-printed plastic (rotor body)

## Techniques and Methods
- Connecting a high-voltage supply (positive and negative terminals) to an electrostatic motor rotor
- Lifting a wire via drone or balloon to exploit the atmospheric voltage gradient
- Grounding one terminal of the motor while the elevated wire serves as the high-voltage terminal
- C-Motive plate-based motor: applying a three-phase sine-wave voltage to stator plates to induce rotor rotation
- Varying supply voltage to increase torque output
