## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a proof-of-concept CVT (Continuously Variable Transmission) designed for bicycles, built around a sliding gear and mechanical rectifier mechanism introduced in his previous video (2382). The design philosophy centres on keeping costs competitive with standard bicycle gearing (~£40), prioritising affordability over engineering elegance. The prototype demonstrates working variable-ratio transmission by combining a rack-and-pinion rectifier with a variable-pivot crank (Whitworth mechanism), with 3D-printed parts available on Thingiverse.

## Key Topics
- Engineering philosophy: cost-focused vs. engineering-focused design (pen vs. pencil allegory)
- Market constraints for a mass-market bicycle CVT (~£40 target price)
- Mechanical rectification as the core enabling technology (from video 2382)
- Sliding gear mechanism for CVT ratio variation
- Whitworth (quick-return) mechanism as the variable-stroke crank driver
- Known design limitation: uneven output speed due to the Whitworth mechanism's asymmetric motion
- Scotch yoke mechanism as a potential alternative
- Prototype assembly walkthrough and live ratio demonstration
- Next steps: metal prototype and real-world testing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Pre-cut steel plates (for low-cost fabrication of the final version)
- Off-the-shelf gears
- 3D-printed parts (PLA or similar — prototype only)
- Steel rod (used as the movable pivot pin)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing for rapid prototyping
- Mechanical rectification (rack-and-pinion with one-way gears, converting linear motion to unidirectional rotation)
- Variable-throw crank design (pivot point moved up/down to change stroke length)
- Whitworth quick-return mechanism (used here as the variable-stroke driver)
- Manual assembly with pegs, sliding rack, center plate, and fork linkage
- Comparative ratio demonstration (selector arm at low vs. high position)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Target gear ratio**: For a bicycle, Robert suggests a range of roughly **3:1 to 9:1** as a reasonable target.
- **3D printer recommendation**: Recommends the **Elegoo Neptune 3** (~£130) as a cheap, easy-to-use machine for those wanting to print the files.
- **Chain drive suggestion**: A commenter suggested driving the mechanism with a chain and tensioner — Robert liked the idea and said it hadn't occurred to him.
- **Theory vs. practice**: Acknowledges that in theory the output variation might be a problem, but in practice "you don't need to go infinitesimally small to achieve a result indistinguishable from theory in use."
- **Pen/pencil allegory**: Repeatedly deflects debate about the historical accuracy of the story, clarifying it is "just a silly story / allegory to illustrate a point."
- **Previous attempts**: Confirms he made "a few tries" before arriving at this design.
