## Overview
In this short meta-video, Robert Murray-Smith outlines the community guidelines for his YouTube channel, explaining why he moderates comments and what kinds of behaviour will result in deletion or banning. The core message is that the channel is a supportive, exploratory space — not an open forum — and that rudeness, off-topic posts, and attempts to "hijack" the discussion will not be tolerated. Robert emphasises that disagreement is welcome, but manner and tone matter enormously.

## Key Topics
- Why Robert felt compelled to make this video (high volume of comments on his work)
- The channel being a moderated, not open, forum
- Rules against rude or snarky comments — towards Robert or other commenters
- Staying on-topic and understanding the "spirit" of each video
- Discouraging "armchair experts" who post without reviewing previous videos, prior comments, or referenced papers
- The distinction between disagreeing respectfully versus attempting to hijack or dominate the channel
- Jargon corrections being unwelcome — communicating the basic idea matters more than precision of terminology
- Channel housekeeping: banning trolls keeps the comments useful as a resource

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's replies in the comments are largely warm, brief acknowledgements ("cheers mate", "bless your heart mate") rather than substantive clarifications. A few notable points emerge:

- He confirms the policy works: *"I think most of the comments on the channel are pretty good."*
- He gives a concrete example of a banned comment: someone complained about his hair and teeth, which he describes as simply rude with no justification — he banned them immediately.
- He expresses frustration that a small minority of bad actors spoil things for everyone: *"I don't think that should be spoiled by idiots."*
- He reassures a commenter that the videos are staying up: *"I don't think so mate — the videos stay."*
- He mentions in passing that he found auto-generated transcripts of his videos to be poor quality (*"I once saw the transcripts — wow what rubbish"*) and jokes about trying closed captions (CCS).
