## Overview
This short-format video introduces a low thermal differential (LTD) Stirling engine that can run on very small heat sources such as a cup of tea (40–50°C). Robert explains the three design features that make it effective, then demonstrates how removing the mechanical link between the displacer and power piston — and replacing it with magnetic coupling — creates a "ring bomb" (magnetic Stirling) engine capable of operating on a temperature difference as small as 0.5–1°C.

## Key Topics
- LTD (Low Temperature Differential) Stirling engine principles
- Three key design criteria: large displacer volume relative to power piston, short power piston stroke, and large heat exchange surface area
- Conversion from a standard LTD Stirling to a magnetic Stirling (ring bomb) engine
- Use of magnets to couple the displacer and power piston without a physical link
- Ultra-low temperature differential operation (down to 0.5–1°C)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium magnets (used for magnetic coupling between displacer and power piston; noted in comments that the Curie point is not a concern in this application)

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing the mechanical link between the displacer and power piston to form a ring bomb engine configuration
- Magnetic coupling: attaching one magnet to the power piston and one to the displacer so they interact via magnetic force rather than a rigid connection
- Heat exchange surface area optimisation for low-grade heat sources

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Efficiency note:** Robert clarifies that the engine's ability to run on a smaller heat difference indicates higher efficiency, but he has not formally measured it.
- **Work output caveat:** Available work is a function of available power — with only half a degree of difference there is very little power, so a bigger engine and larger temperature differential would be needed for meaningful work output.
- **Curie point:** Acknowledges that technically, with neodymium magnets in an engine of this kind, the Curie point will never be reached — so it is not a practical concern.
