## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores building a wind generator from a discarded household fan (a "taran"), inspired by the concept of the "wind wall" — an array of small turbines originally conceived by New York designer Joe Duet. He argues that accessibility and the ability to build from recycled materials matters more than peak efficiency, and demonstrates a working prototype using salvaged fan components paired with a cheap stepper motor.

## Key Topics
- The "wind wall" concept and its inspiration from designer Joe Duet
- Critique of efficiency-centric thinking in DIY energy projects (photosynthesis analogy)
- Salvaging a broken oscillating fan for wind generation components
- Using a squirrel-cage induction motor rotor as a wind turbine element
- Converting induction motors into generators by adding magnets
- Proof-of-concept demonstration lighting an LED and a 6V bulb
- Scaling potential: assembling multiple units into a wind wall array
- Sourcing cross-flow/tangential fan rotors from AliExpress as an alternative to salvage

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Broken oscillating desk/floor fan (primary salvage source, ~£30 new)
- Squirrel-cage induction motor (8-pole, extracted from fan)
- Stepper motor (17HS8-104S, ~£2 each on eBay) — used as the generator
- Neodymium/permanent magnets — mentioned as a method to convert induction motors into generators by drilling and gluing into the rotor
- LED (used as initial load indicator)
- 6V incandescent bulb (resistive load for voltage/current indication)
- Cross-flow / tangential fan rotors (available on AliExpress, ~£8 each)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of consumer fans to extract usable components
- Identifying and salvaging motors, switches, bearings, and cabling from discarded appliances
- Coupling a salvaged fan rotor to a stepper motor to form a simple generator
- Using a hair dryer as a controlled wind source for bench testing
- Converting squirrel-cage induction motors into permanent-magnet generators by drilling the rotor and gluing in magnets
- Arraying multiple rotor-generator units to form a wind wall
