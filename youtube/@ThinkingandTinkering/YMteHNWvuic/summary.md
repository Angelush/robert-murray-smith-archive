## Overview
Robert revisits a Newman motor he built roughly a year earlier from salvaged microwave oven transformer coils and permanent magnets, testing it as an AC generator. He demonstrates it lighting a 6V bulb by hand-spinning and then by drill, producing ~0.25 A — compared to only ~13–14 mA from a commercially purchased 12V/120W wind turbine generator under the same conditions. His conclusion is that a scrappy, cordless homemade generator built from scrap parts outperforms a £30 shop-bought unit.

## Key Topics
- The Newman motor design and its prior life as a DC motor with a single-transistor commutator
- Repurposing the Newman motor as an AC generator
- Coil wiring configuration: the importance of cross-connecting the two coils (input-to-input, output bridged) to get output rather than cancellation
- Hands-on demonstration lighting a 6V bulb by hand and by drill
- Direct comparison against a commercial 12V/120W shop-bought generator
- Discussion of prime movers (drill used as a stand-in for wind turbine, water wheel, etc.)
- Cost argument: homemade (~£3 in parts from scrap) vs. shop-bought (~£30)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Microwave oven transformer coils (two salvaged units used as the stator coils)
- Permanent magnets (N35 neodymium, arranged North/South on a rotating axle)
- 6V incandescent filament light bulb (resistive load for comparison)
- Shop-bought 12V/120W generator (likely using ceramic/ferrite magnets)
- Single transistor (used previously as electronic commutator for DC motor operation)

## Techniques and Methods
- Salvaging transformer coils from microwave ovens and mounting them on a board
- Gluing permanent magnets onto a central axle to create a spinning rotor
- Cross-wiring two opposing coils (same-side terminals connected, outputs bridged) to achieve additive rather than cancelling EMF
- Using trial-and-error connection testing: spin and check for output to confirm correct wiring
- Using a handheld electric drill as a prime mover for consistent spin speed (~300–400 RPM)
- Current measurement in series with a bulb using a multimeter on amp reading
- Side-by-side comparison of homemade vs. commercial generator under identical load conditions

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Magnet quality is key**: The most significant factor in the Newman generator's superiority is magnet field strength — Robert used N35 neodymium magnets, while the commercial unit almost certainly used weaker ceramic/ferrite magnets.
- **Fair comparison defence**: The load was identical (same 6V filament bulb, resistive load), and the drill applied roughly the same torque (~300–400 RPM). The brightness difference was visually obvious without needing wattage figures.
- **Lorentz law governs all generators**: When challenged, Robert points sceptics to EMF = BLV·sinθ — the Newman generator follows the same physics; stronger B-field means more output for the same velocity.
- **No magic overunity claim**: Robert is clear the design is just a practical, cheap generator — ~£3 of scrap versus £30 shop-bought, better output for the same input.
- **Cogging trade-off**: Adding a steel armature (as someone suggested) would improve output but introduce cogging, harming low-speed performance — the air-core design avoids this.
- **Voltage regulation**: Advises using a single-chip voltage regulator if regulated output is needed.
- **Further ideas welcomed**: Mentions the possibility of using a coil pulled from a standard DC motor as a "tickler coil" variant.
- **Cost is the real story**: Repeatedly summarised as "£30 versus £3 — and I put the same input in — roughly."
