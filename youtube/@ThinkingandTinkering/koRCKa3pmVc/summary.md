## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores biomimicry as applied to Vertical Axis Wind Turbine (VAWT) design, focusing on serrated trailing edges inspired by owl wings — a concept commercialised by Siemens as "DinoTails." He redesigns and 3D-prints an updated version of his earlier VAWT (from video 210), attaching owl-inspired tails, and demonstrates that the modification makes the turbine self-starting and slightly increases RPM.

## Key Topics
- Biomimicry as a design principle for wind turbines
- Owl wing anatomy as inspiration for blade trailing edges
- Siemens "DinoTails" v1 and v2 — serrated trailing edges with added comb structures for noise reduction
- Other companies working on owl-inspired blade designs (SE Airbag, Biome Renewables)
- University research on biomimetic wind turbine blades
- Redesign and 3D-printed upgrade of his own VAWT from video 210
- Observed effects: self-starting behaviour and marginally higher RPM

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Rubber tape — used to attach the 3D-printed tails to the turbine, allowing angle adjustment

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of turbine blade tails and cubicle structures (files shared on Thingiverse)
- Modular two-part design (blade body + separate tail) to allow easy retrofitting
- RPM measurement of the running turbine (results: ~165 RPM rising to ~204 RPM)
- Attempted decibel/noise measurement (inconclusive due to environment)
- Attachment of serrated trailing edge tails using rubber tape
