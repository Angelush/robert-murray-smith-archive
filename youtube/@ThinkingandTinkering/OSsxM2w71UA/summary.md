## Overview
Robert continues his Singer sewing machine generator project (referenced as build #1918) by adding a two-stage 3D-printed gear train to multiply rotor speed. Each gear stage provides a 3:1 step-up ratio, yielding a combined 9:1 ratio, which produces approximately nine times the voltage compared to the ungeared version. The video demonstrates the assembled gear chain in operation and discusses further improvements such as adding a freewheel mechanism and a full serpentine coil.

## Key Topics
- Upgrading a Singer sewing machine generator with a gear train
- Calculating gear ratios and their effect on output voltage and torque
- 3D-printed gear chain design (TinkerCAD files shared)
- Measured voltage improvement (~9× over baseline)
- Discussion of further upgrades: freewheel, serpentine coil, additional gear stages

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 60-tooth cog (replacement for the original sewing machine wheel)
- 62-tooth gear and 20-tooth gear (forming each 3:1 stage)
- 3D-printed plastic parts (designed in TinkerCAD)
- Wire coil (placed on the generator in preparation for measurement)

## Techniques and Methods
- Gear ratio calculation (compound gear train: 3:1 × 3:1 = 9:1 overall)
- Swapping the original wheel for a larger cog to integrate the gear chain
- 3D printing a bracket/holder for the gear assembly
- Heat-gun bending of a printed part to clear the cog profile
- Voltage measurement with a multimeter while manually operating the pedal mechanism

## Robert's Own Replies
The author comments are mostly brief social acknowledgements ("cheers mate", "lol"), but a few contain useful insights:
- **Power potential:** Robert estimates the device could plausibly deliver "half a kilowatt easily for just about anyone," suggesting real practical energy-harvesting potential.
- **Tricycle adaptation:** He mentions a delta tricycle (not tadpole) as a natural mechanical adaptation of this pedal-generator concept.
