## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith takes on the restoration of a badly neglected potter's wheel that had been sitting outside for roughly 10 years, covered in rust, moss, and snails. He disassembles it, cleans the metal frame, replaces rotten wooden components, and reassembles it with a working motor — demonstrating that even seemingly ruined equipment can be fully recovered. The video's closing moral is a characteristic Robert note: don't be too quick to give up on things, even when they look fit for the bin.

## Key Topics
- Assessing and disassembling a heavily corroded potter's wheel
- Rust and dirt removal from a metal frame
- Replacing a rotten baseboard with new blockboard using the old piece as a template
- Filling splits in weathered timber using sawdust-and-resin filler
- Sanding and refinishing aged wood
- Reassembling the drive mechanism (pulley, fork selector, foot pedal, switch)
- Motor refurbishment (covered in a separate companion video)
- Re-wiring and testing the wheel
- Brief discussion of future use — pottery sessions open to locals

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Vinegar, salt, and washing-up liquid** — Robert's go-to "miracle cleaning solution" for rust and grime removal
- **Blockboard** — used to replace the rotten baseboard
- **Sawdust mixed with resin and hardener** — used as a wood filler for splits in the timber top
- **Cast iron** — the large flywheel and turntable
- **Bronze** — chunky fittings noted as high quality
- **Wood** (species uncertain — possibly teak; Robert notes it's "a bit red for teak") — original timber top, survived 10 years outdoors

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and condition assessment before cleaning
- Wet scrubbing with vinegar/salt/washing-up liquid solution to remove rust and surface dirt
- Repainting the cleaned metal frame
- Templating from the old rotten baseboard to cut a replacement piece
- Sanding weathered timber surfaces
- Mixing sawdust with resin and hardener to fill timber splits
- Photographing the inside of the switch before disassembly to aid re-wiring
- Motor testing prior to final assembly
- Reassembly and functional testing of the full drive mechanism

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed the restoration took **significantly longer than it appears** on screen — about a week of work.
- Clarified the real-world **value of the wheel**: a student-quality wheel costs around $200; a professional wheel capable of handling larger amounts of clay runs $1,500–$2,000. He emphasises this is "beside the point" — the satisfaction of restoration matters more.
- Confirmed the **foot pedal controls speed** (in response to a viewer question).
- Noted the **cleaning solution is really just vinegar and salt** (with a little washing-up liquid) — reiterating how simple and effective it is.
- On the wood finish choice: he opted for something **more waterproof** because pottery is a wet environment (clay work uses a lot of water), acknowledging a viewer's alternative suggestion would also work well and allow for wood movement.
- Agreed that a **concrete flywheel** (referencing an earlier project) would have been well-suited for a potter's wheel.
- Invited local viewers to come and **have a go on the wheel**, offering tea if they bring biscuits.
