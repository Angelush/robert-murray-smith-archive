## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a zinc-iodine battery that he considers among the simplest batteries anyone can build at home, using only water, iodine, zinc foil, and a copper mesh electrode coated in conductive plastic. The battery self-forms as iodine reacts with zinc, turns purple when charged, and is rechargeable at 1.2V. The video is framed as a direct contrast to fraudulent "supercapacitor" DIY videos — this one actually works.

## Key Topics
- Critique of a viral fake supercapacitor DIY video as inspiration
- Construction of a minimal zinc-iodine battery from household/lab materials
- The electrochemical reaction between zinc and iodine as the energy source
- Visual indicator of charge state (purple coloration from triiodide)
- Speeding up formation with potassium iodide (salt additive)
- Rechargeability after initial formation cycle
- Self-discharge mechanism and how to mitigate it by removing the zinc electrode
- Limitations: high ESR and physical plate separation reducing efficiency

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Iodine** — the active oxidant; electrolyte component; causes purple coloration when forming zinc iodide/triiodide
- **Zinc foil** — anode material; consumed during discharge
- **Copper mesh** — cathode current collector
- **Conductive plastic** — coating on copper mesh, noted to protect copper from iodine corrosion (per author reply)
- **Water** — solvent/electrolyte base
- **Potassium iodide** — optional salt additive to accelerate the formation reaction
- **Salt (general reference)** — mentioned as equivalent shorthand for potassium iodide in context

## Techniques and Methods
- Assembling a simple open-cell battery in a jar with no specialist equipment
- Layering conductive plastic-coated copper mesh as the cathode
- Inserting zinc foil as the removable anode
- Adding iodine solution to initiate the electrochemical reaction
- Using potassium iodide to increase ionic conductivity and speed formation
- Observing color change (clear → purple → clear) as a charge-state indicator
- Recharging at 1.2V after the initial formation cycle
- Removing the zinc electrode to halt self-discharge when not in use
- Scaling up by connecting multiple cells in series
