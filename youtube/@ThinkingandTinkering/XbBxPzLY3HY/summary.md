## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a hand blender motor to explain how it works as a generator, then demonstrates a flywheel generator using 46 magnets arranged in a north-south configuration around a spinning case. He measures the output of a single coreless coil (~1.2W) and concludes that the real secret to generator performance is rotational speed, not exotic magnetic or coil arrangements.

## Key Topics
- Internal anatomy of a brushed DC motor (commutator segments, coils, pole pieces, permanent magnets)
- Converting a motor into a generator by spinning the magnet array past fixed coils
- Frames of reference: spinning the outer magnet case vs. spinning the rotor are equivalent
- Using 46 magnets in a north-south alternating ring as a "gearing" effect (23× pole-pair equivalent)
- Coreless vs. cored coils: trade-off between output power and cogging
- Single-coil output measurement (~28 mA, ~1.2 W) under resistive load (soldering iron)
- Scaling to multiple coils around the flywheel
- Speed as the dominant factor in generator output (power ∝ voltage², voltage ∝ speed)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Hand blender motor (brushed DC, ~£5)
- 46 permanent magnets arranged in a north-south alternating ring
- Turntable motor synchronous coils (coreless, many turns of thin wire)
- Single open-core (coreless) test coil
- Soldering iron used as a resistive load

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of brushed DC motor (press-pin removal of end cap)
- Flywheel generator construction: outer rotating magnet ring + fixed inner coils
- Coreless coil winding for reduced cogging
- Output measurement under resistive load using an ammeter and voltmeter
- Frames-of-reference analysis to explain rotating-magnet vs. rotating-rotor equivalence
