## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to convert a PC fan into a hand-crank generator by building a simple gear system from everyday materials. The key insight is not the generator itself, but the cog-making technique: cutting circles from builders board (PVC-coated foam board) and gluing toothed belt around the edges to create functional gears with controllable gear ratios. The finished device produces measurable AC voltage (~2.6V) just from hand-cranking.

## Key Topics
- Converting a PC brushless fan motor into a hand-crank generator
- Building functional gear systems without precision machinery
- Using toothed belt (timing belt) as a gear tooth surface on DIY cogs
- Gear ratios and speed multiplication (large cog driving small cog)
- Reference to the book *Mechanical Movements, Devices and Appliances* by Henry T. Brown (Hiscox, 1899)
- Follow-up to a previous video on wind generator electronics and bridge rectifiers

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PC fan** — used as the generator (brushless motor core)
- **Builders board / PVC-coated foam board** — roofline fascia/soffit material, used as the structural base for cog discs
- **Toothed belt (timing belt)** — various pitches; large-pitch belt chosen deliberately for better mesh tolerance when wrapping around circles
- **Superglue** — used to assemble the entire frame and glue belt to cog discs

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting circles from PVC foam board to form cog blanks
- Wrapping and gluing toothed belt around the perimeter of cut circles to create toothed gears
- Assembling a multi-cog gear train to step up rotational speed from hand-crank to generator shaft
- Drilling axle mounts into the foam board frame
- Testing AC output voltage directly (no bridge rectifier) with a voltmeter while hand-cranking

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Large-pitch belt was a deliberate choice**: the coarser tooth pitch provides enough slack/tolerance to mesh properly when the belt is wrapped around a curved disc, unlike small-pitch belt which would be too tight.
- **Toothed belt vs. 3D-printed gears**: Robert acknowledges 3D-printed gears are a valid alternative but notes the belt method is ideal for people without a 3D printer and is accessible on a budget.
- **The *Mechanical Movements* book**: Robert confirms he has owned his copy for a long time and used to make automata as a hobby — the book was invaluable for that work.
- **Casting cogs in resin or aluminium**: In response to a suggestion, Robert floats the idea of using the foam/plastic cogs as patterns for casting in resin or aluminium (noting a kiln reaching ~600°C would be needed for Ali).
- **Flywheels**: Robert engages positively with flywheel suggestions from commenters, calling them "awesome" and a "great solution in some places."
- **Purpose of the gear demonstration**: Clarifies he deliberately built the gear train (rather than just a pulley) specifically to show the gear-making technique — the point of the video was the method, not just the generator output.
