## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to make a magnetic silly putty at home using PVA glue, borax solution, and magnetite powder — a safe, accessible alternative to the siloxane-based commercial silly putty. The resulting material exhibits the classic non-Newtonian properties (stretches slowly, snaps quickly, bounces) and is strongly attracted to a magnet, eventually "swallowing" it.

## Key Topics
- Why commercial silly putty (siloxane-based) is hard to replicate at home and the rationale for using PVA instead
- The chemistry of PVA: polyvinyl acetate vs. polyvinyl alcohol and their roles
- Cross-linking PVA with borax to create a polymer network
- Incorporating magnetic powder into the putty for magnetic responsiveness
- Non-Newtonian behaviour: slow stretch vs. fast snap, bouncing, spreading
- Tips for tuning the recipe (PVA concentration, borax concentration, magnetic powder amount)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVA (polyvinyl acetate / polyvinyl alcohol)** — the base polymer, used as a 50/50 mix with water; any white/school/wood glue works
- **Borax** — cross-linking agent; ~1 teaspoon in 50 ml water (saturated); available at hardware stores or online; "borax substitute" is the UK alternative
- **Magnetite powder** — ~30–40 g; the magnetic filler; available on eBay; iron filings or magnetic nanoparticles can be substituted
- **Water** — used to dilute PVA and dissolve borax
- **Siloxane / cyclohexane monomer** — mentioned only as the basis of commercial silly putty; noted as toxic and not recommended for home use

## Techniques and Methods
- Preparing a 50/50 PVA-to-water solution
- Preparing a saturated borax solution (teaspoon of borax in 50 ml water, with residual undissolved salt as concentration indicator)
- Mixing magnetite powder thoroughly into the PVA solution *before* adding borax (to ensure even distribution prior to gelation)
- Triggering cross-linking by adding borax solution; gelation is nearly immediate
- Stirring until the mixture thickens and pulls away from the container
- Storing finished putty in an airtight container to prevent drying (mitigating the water-based limitation)
- Demonstrating magnetic attraction by holding a magnet near the putty

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Where to buy magnetite:** Available on eBay as a powder; he also mentions having made his own in a previous video.
- **Substitute magnetic materials:** Toner from old photocopiers/laser printers (iron oxide based) may work if it tests as magnetic; new toner (carbon black) is likely not magnetic.
- **Safety reassurance:** Firmly dismisses safety concerns — the ingredients are white glue and borax (a washing powder), standard primary-school materials.
- **Permanent magnetisation:** Believes the putty will not permanently magnetise because magnetic domains are held in a loose matrix; they would realign but remain loose. He mentions expanded polyurethane foam might work better for permanent alignment.
- **Nanoparticle note:** At small particle sizes, magnetite becomes ferromagnetic (rather than ferrimagnetic) because particle size falls below the magnetic domain size.
- **General sourcing:** Most materials available at a hardware store; nanoparticles can be bought online or made following his other videos.
