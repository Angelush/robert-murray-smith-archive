## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a concise history of mechanical timekeeping — from water clocks and escapements to the balance wheel — before explaining the tourbillon: a rotating cage invented by Abraham-Louis Breguet in 1801 to counteract the effect of gravity on a pocket watch's balance wheel and spring. He then walks through his own 3D-printed tourbillon design, assembles it on camera, and connects it to the spring motor he built in video 2381 to demonstrate a complete wind-up clock mechanism. The video is explicitly framed as a teaching aid to help viewers understand the principles, not as a blueprint to copy exactly.

## Key Topics
- Brief history of timekeeping: water clocks, candle/sand clocks, mechanical escapements, pendulum clocks (Huygens, 1656)
- Spring-driven vs. weight-driven movements; balance wheel and spring (Huygens & Hooke, 1675)
- The problem of gravity affecting pocket watch accuracy
- Invention of the tourbillon by Breguet (patented 1801) and how it works
- Whether tourbillons actually improve accuracy (ongoing debate; largely prized as a demonstration of craft)
- Step-by-step assembly of a 3D-printed tourbillon design
- Tick rate, regulator concept, and how spring mass/length affect frequency
- Integration with the spring motor from video 2381 to form a complete wind-up clock

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed filament parts (black filament used for aesthetic finish, printed on an Elegoo Centauri)
- Standard skate bearings — 22 mm × 7 mm × 8 mm
- Smaller bearings — 12 mm × 6 mm × 4 mm
- Adhesive/glue (used to fix a gear component into the rest assembly)

## Techniques and Methods
- FDM 3D printing (filament printer); long-axis printing orientation used for a stress-bearing pin to improve strength
- Tight-fit press assembly for bearings and cage components
- "Distressed metallic" surface finishing (painting/weathering) applied to the printed parts for aesthetics
- Balance wheel and hairspring oscillator assembly inside a rotating cage
- Escapement/pallet-fork mechanism integration with a driving gear
- Spring regulator concept explained (sliding arm to shorten effective spring length and tune tick rate), though not implemented in this build

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Printer choice:** Any filament printer works; he used an Elegoo Centauri but considers it largely interchangeable.
- **Pronunciation:** Acknowledged he didn't know how to pronounce "tourbillon" correctly and thanked commenters for corrections.
- **Design philosophy (key insight):** Explicitly explained why he used a plastic spring and omitted the regulator — the video is intended as a *teaching aid*, not a finished model to replicate. His goal is for viewers to understand the underlying principles well enough to design their own mechanisms, choose better materials, and improve on his design. He hopes people will add their own regulator rather than copy his omission.
- **Favourite personal project:** Mentioned in passing that his favourite thing he's ever made is a hand-cranked phone charger from a few years ago, though that video was not widely viewed.
