## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how a cordless electric drill can be converted into a hand-cranked generator, showing that both devices share the same four core components. He compares a commercial hand-crank charger (£20) with a DIY version built from a free old drill plus a £1 voltage regulation circuit board, concluding both produce comparable output (up to ~18 watts) at dramatically different costs.

## Key Topics
- Internal components of a hand-crank generator vs. an electric drill
- Sun-and-planet (epicyclic) gearbox mechanics
- DC permanent magnet motors used in reverse as generators
- Voltage regulation/stabilization circuits
- DIY assembly of a hand-crank generator from salvaged drill parts
- Cost comparison: commercial charger vs. DIY build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Permanent magnet DC motor (from both the commercial charger and a cordless drill)
- Sun-and-planet gearbox (from the drill)
- Voltage regulation/control circuit board (3–30 V input, up to 5 A, adjustable output 3–24 V via potentiometer)
- 28 LEDs (7×4 array, used as a load demonstration)
- 10 mm nut (used as a handle attachment)
- Steel stock (for the crank handle)
- Kapton tape (mentioned by Robert in comments as a future material to try)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of a cordless drill (removing chuck screw, unscrewing chuck, removing body screws)
- Identifying and reusing gearbox and motor components from salvaged drills
- Soldering wires to voltage regulation circuit board terminals (OUT+, OUT−, IN+, IN−)
- Setting output voltage by adjusting a brass potentiometer screw on the regulator board
- Measuring output current and voltage with a multimeter (readings called out by a helper)
