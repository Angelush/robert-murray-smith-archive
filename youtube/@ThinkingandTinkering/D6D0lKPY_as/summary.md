## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple all-carbon battery inspired by the "secret" Japanese carbon battery and the principles of the Karpen cell. By painting one side of a piece of photocopy paper with graphite and the other with amorphous carbon (from ground-up charcoal briquette), then immersing it in a sodium carbonate electrolyte, he generates a measurable voltage — reaching around 62–66 millivolts. The video argues that the Japanese "secret process" is little more than an application of Karpen's principle: two different forms of carbon separated by an electrolyte-soaked medium.

## Key Topics
- The Karpen cell concept (two dissimilar carbons or carbon/metal electrodes generating voltage)
- Connection to a Japanese carbon battery design
- Construction of an all-carbon, all-paper battery
- Voltage generation from two forms of carbon in an electrolyte
- Suggestions for improving the design (current collectors)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Photocopy paper** — used as the separator/substrate between the two carbon electrodes
- **Graphite** — painted on one side of the paper as one carbon electrode
- **Amorphous carbon** — ground from a charcoal briquette, painted on the other side as the second electrode
- **Polyvinyl acetate (PVA / white glue)** — used as the binder to make the carbon paints
- **Sodium carbonate (washing soda)** — used as the electrolyte solution
- **Copper strips** — suggested (not used in demo) as current collectors to reduce internal resistance

## Techniques and Methods
- Grinding charcoal briquette to produce amorphous carbon powder
- Mixing carbon powders with polyvinyl acetate to form paintable electrode slurries
- Painting each side of paper with a different carbon form to create a two-electrode sandwich
- Immersing the painted paper in electrolyte and measuring voltage with a voltmeter (millivolt range)
- Wicking of electrolyte through the paper to activate the cell

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Pore clogging limit**: Increasing carbon loading only helps to a degree — beyond a certain point, pore clogging reduces current output.
- **Asymmetric capacitors**: The same principles apply; use a metal oxide on the cathode for asymmetric caps (directs readers to Google Scholar for details).
- **Binder clarification**: Polyvinyl acetate is simply white glue — the recipe is genuinely that simple.
- **Practicality**: To make this useful in the real world, further development work is needed — but that is true of any technology with real utility.
- **Current status**: Robert notes he has another cell drying, suggesting active ongoing experimentation at the time.
- **General tone**: Enthusiastic and encouraging — invites followers to try experiments along these lines themselves, emphasising this is "now" technology, not future tech.
