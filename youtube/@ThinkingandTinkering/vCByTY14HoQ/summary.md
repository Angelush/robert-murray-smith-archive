## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the physics behind designing resistive (ohmic/Joule) electric heaters, focusing on how to calculate how much power is needed to heat a given mass of water or air. Using specific heat capacity, Ohm's law, and simple arithmetic, he shows that heater design is fundamentally a straightforward energy calculation — and that the choice of heating element material is irrelevant as long as the wattage is right.

## Key Topics
- Definition and naming of resistive heaters: ohmic heaters and Joule heaters
- Specific heat capacity: what it is, units (kJ/kg/°C), and how to look it up
- Worked example: calculating time for a 1 kW kettle to boil 2.5 L of water from 0–100 °C (~17 min), and why most kettles are 3 kW (~5–6 min)
- Worked example: calculating energy needed to raise 100 m³ of air (~125 kg) by 10 °C
- Demonstration that heating element material is irrelevant — only watts matter (nichrome wire vs. carbon fiber, both cutting polystyrene at 4 W)
- Role of insulation in closing the gap between theoretical and real-world energy requirements
- Specific heat capacity of the human body (~3.47 kJ/kg/°C)
- Efficiency of electric resistance heaters (~100% conversion of electrical energy to heat)
- Practical takeaway: no magic heater product improves on physics; insulate and heat smaller spaces instead

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Nichrome wire** — thin resistance wire (sourced from Crazy Wire, vape grade); used in cookers, kilns, kettles, and vapes as a standard heating element
- **Carbon fiber** — bog-standard structural carbon fiber (same used in racing boats and car panels); demonstrated as an alternative heating element
- **Polystyrene** — used as a test material to verify 4 W heat output from both elements

## Techniques and Methods
- Applying Ohm's law to size a heating element
- Using the specific heat capacity formula: Energy (J) = specific heat capacity × mass (kg) × ΔT (°C)
- Converting joules to watts using the relationship 1 W = 1 J/s to determine heater power and heat-up time
- Looking up specific heat capacity values from Wikipedia (water: 4.2 kJ/kg/°C; air: 1.006 kJ/kg/°C)
- Deriving air mass in a room from volume × density
- Bench demonstration using a DC bench supply to drive nichrome wire and carbon fiber to equal wattage, verifying material-independence
