## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple but effective laboratory balance scale, inspired by an idea from his Australian friend Pete. The balance uses water as a known reference weight (1 g/mL) to measure unknown quantities, and is constructed primarily from PVC foam board ("builder's board"). The finished balance can measure up to approximately 50 g with an accuracy of around ±0.1 g.

## Key Topics
- The concept of using water as a calibrated reference weight (1 g per mL)
- Construction of a balance scale from inexpensive, accessible materials
- Balancing and calibrating the finished instrument using sliding lead weights
- Aesthetic finishing techniques (shaping, sanding, drilling decorative holes)
- Reading the balance using a central needle and pencil-marked indicator board

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Builder's board (PVC foam board / foam-cored PVC)** — primary structural material; dimensionally stable, easy to cut and sand; Robert links to Eurocell soffit board as the specific product he used
- **Lead sheet / lead weights** — used for the counterweights on the balance arms and as adjustable trimming weights
- **Aluminium** — small shoe piece fitted to the fulcrum point
- **Hooks and eyes** — used to hang the balance pans
- **Water** — used as the reference/known weight (1 g/mL)

## Techniques and Methods
- Scoring and snapping PVC foam board with a carpet knife
- Using a manufactured square edge of the board as a precision reference for layout
- Drilling decorative holes and cutting scroll shapes for aesthetics
- Sanding the cut pieces smooth
- Gluing components together (implied PVC-compatible adhesive)
- Filing a notch in the aluminium shoe with a triangular file for the fulcrum edge
- Crimping lead weights onto the arms for fine balance adjustment
- Calibrating the balance by sliding trimming weights along the arms until the needle centres on the indicator marks
