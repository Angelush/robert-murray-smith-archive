## Overview
Robert submits a competition entry featuring a wearable chemical battery powered by human sweat, constructed as a necklace using salvaged carbon rods and purchased magnesium ribbon as electrodes. The battery is paired with a jewel thief boost circuit to drive an LED. The experiment reveals that sweat alone was insufficient to light the LED brightly, but calcium carbonate-rich groundwater worked, demonstrating that electrolyte mineral content is critical.

## Key Topics
- Sweat-powered galvanic battery as a competition entry
- Electrode construction using salvaged and purchased materials
- Use of a jewel thief (boost converter) circuit to amplify low voltage output
- Comparison of different electrolytes: sweat, mineral-rich groundwater, and rainwater
- Wearable battery design in necklace form

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Magnesium ribbon** — used as the anode electrode
- **Carbon rods** — salvaged from old C cell batteries found in the woods, used as the cathode
- **Human sweat** — intended electrolyte for the battery
- **Calcium carbonate** — present in the groundwater used for testing; successfully powered the LED
- **Rainwater** — used as a control; too low in minerals to power the LED
- **Hot glue** — used to secure electrical connections
- **Yucca leaves** — twisted into cordage to insulate part of the necklace structure

## Techniques and Methods
- Salvaging carbon rods by disassembling old C cell batteries
- Constructing a galvanic cell with dissimilar metal electrodes (Mg / C)
- Using a jewel thief (air-core boost converter) circuit to step up low cell voltage
- Twisting plant fibers (yucca leaves) into cordage for structural insulation
- Physical exertion (carrying firewood) to generate sweat as a natural electrolyte
- Comparative electrolyte testing across sweat, mineral groundwater, and rainwater
