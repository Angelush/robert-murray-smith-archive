## Overview
This video is a follow-up to video 2118 on flux switching, where Robert builds a practical, 3D-printed switchable magnet device from just three printed parts, six M12 steel nuts, and four neodymium magnets. By rotating a central section 180°, the magnetic fields either reinforce or cancel at the surface, turning the holding force on and off. The design is shared on Thingiverse under a Creative Commons licence for anyone to adapt.

## Key Topics
- Flux switching principle applied to a functional handheld device
- 3D-printed housing design with a sliding channel for magnets
- Magnet orientation (same pole direction) as the key to switchable behaviour
- Scalability: using stronger or more magnets to increase holding force
- Practical applications: door locks, work-holding, welding clamps

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **N35 neodymium magnets** — 1 cm diameter, 5 mm deep; four used as the active magnetic elements
- **M12 steel nuts** — six used; act as flux conductors/pole pieces within the assembly
- **Super glue** — used to attach the handle to the body

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing the three-part housing (files on Thingiverse, Creative Commons)
- Drilling out printed parts to 12 mm to accept the M12 nuts
- Press-fitting nuts into recesses so all components maintain contact
- Inserting magnets with identical pole orientation into the sliding cylindrical channels
- Assembling the two halves so they find a natural rest position
- Gluing the handle on top with super glue
- Rotating the inner section 180° to switch between on and off states
