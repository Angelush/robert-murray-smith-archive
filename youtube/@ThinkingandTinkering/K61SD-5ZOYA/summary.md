## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the history of 3D printing from its earliest conceptual roots in 1945 science fiction through to modern consumer-grade machines, arguing that the technology has a much longer and richer lineage than is commonly credited. He highlights the shift from filament-based FDM printing back toward stereolithographic (resin/MSLA) printing as prices have collapsed. The video doubles as a plug for an ongoing competition to win an Elegoo Mars Pro 2 MSLA printer.

## Key Topics
- Early science fiction precursors to 3D printing (1945–1950 short stories)
- The disputed history of who "invented" 3D printing (Gottwald 1971, Masters 1984, Hull 1984)
- Stereolithography (SLA) vs. filament/FDM printing — origins and dominance shifts
- Selective laser sintering and metal powder sintering
- The concept of "additive manufacture" vs. subtractive CNC machining
- The dramatic fall in price of resin printers (from ~£300,000 to ~£200)
- Exotic 3D printing materials (clay, chocolate, paper, gypsum/powder binder)
- Multi-material and electronics-integrated 3D printing
- Research at MIT, UC Berkeley, and Yale toward Star Trek-style replicators
- 3D printing in STEM education (China as an example)
- Competition announcement: water wheel that lights an LED (closes 12 May), prize: Elegoo Mars Pro 2

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Photo-curable liquid polymer** — used in stereolithography; cured by UV light to build parts layer by layer
- **Metal powders** — sintered via laser (selective laser sintering / SLS)
- **Filament (FDM material / fusible deposition material)** — plastic filament used in desktop printers like MakerBot
- **Clay** — used as an alternative 3D printing medium
- **Chocolate** — cited as an exotic printable material
- **Layered paper** — used in paper-based 3D printing processes
- **Powdered binder / gypsum** — powder deposition with binder sprayed in to create gypsum models
- **Liquid metal** — referenced in Gottwald's 1971 "liquid metal recorder" patent

## Techniques and Methods
- **Stereolithography (SLA)** — UV/laser curing of a liquid photopolymer resin, layer by layer
- **MSLA (Masked SLA / UV resin printing)** — uses a masked screen to cure an entire layer at once; represented by the Elegoo Mars Pro 2
- **Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM)** — extruding and layering thermoplastic filament
- **Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)** — fusing metal or polymer powders with a laser
- **Powder binder jetting** — spraying a binder into a powder bed to form gypsum or similar models
- **Multi-material / embedded electronics printing** — printing with mixed materials including electronic components
- **Photogrammetry** — mentioned briefly in comments as a related scanning/capture process

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief social exchanges, but a few contain useful substance:
- He acknowledges the video was condensed (7–10 minutes) and that a lot of history was necessarily left out.
- He recommends the **Filastruder** open-source project (available as a kit, plans, or finished unit) to someone asking about building their own filament extruder.
- He suggests **Chitubox** slicing software as the easiest on-ramp for new resin printer owners, noting it has about a five-minute learning curve and that learning CAD can come much later.
- He references his own **windmill videos** as relevant follow-up content.
- He advises a commenter whose prints are failing to compare against published research papers on long-term testing to identify what is going wrong.
- He expresses genuine enthusiasm about getting into 3D printing himself alongside the audience.
