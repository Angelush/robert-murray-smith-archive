## Overview
In this short follow-up video (part of his DIY heater series), Robert tests a range of alternative fuels in the alcohol burner he built in video 1702. He finds that lighter alcohols perform best, while heavier oils (paraffin, diesel, vegetable/sunflower oil) can be made to burn but produce sooty, yellow flames and are poorly suited for indoor room heating.

## Key Topics
- Follow-up to the DIY alcohol burner/room heater from video 1702
- Testing multiple fuel types in the stainless steel mesh mantle burner
- Comparison of flame quality, sootiness, and heat output across fuels
- Suitability of each fuel for indoor vs. garage use
- Techniques for starting heavier fuels (ethanol priming)
- Teaser for a future video on modifications to better handle heavy oils

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — original fuel used in video 1702, the reference baseline
- **Ethanol** — tested as a fuel; also used as a primer/starter for heavier fuels
- **Isopropanol (isopropyl alcohol)** — listed for testing (lighter alcohols deferred to next video)
- **Paraffin** — burns with a yellow, sooty flame; requires ethanol to start
- **Diesel** — similar performance to paraffin; slightly more controlled but sooty
- **Glycerin / Vegetable glycerin** — listed for testing
- **Sunflower oil (vegetable oil)** — very difficult to ignite; required ethanol dosing and raising the wick 5 mm; produced a very sooty flame
- **Stainless steel mesh** — used as the burner mantle

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ethanol priming** — pouring ethanol around the rim/wick to pre-heat and ignite heavier fuels that won't light directly
- **Wick height adjustment** — raising the wick by 5 mm to increase airflow when burning viscous oils
- **Smothering to extinguish** — lifting the mantle off or covering with a tin to put out the flame
- **Chimney method** — briefly demonstrated as an alternative extinguishing approach
- **New mantle between tests** — swapping mantles to avoid cross-contamination of soot between fuels
