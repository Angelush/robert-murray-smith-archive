## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to repurpose a microwave oven transformer (MOT) into a functioning electromagnetic speaker. He salvages the primary coil and iron core laminate sections, combines them with neodymium magnets and a hacksaw blade reed, and attaches a cellophane diaphragm to produce audible sound. The result is a working—if slightly tinny—speaker built entirely from salvaged and household materials.

## Key Topics
- Disassembly methods for microwave oven transformers
- Electromagnetic actuation using a coil, iron core, and neodymium magnets
- Building a mechanical reed/driver from transformer laminate and a hacksaw blade
- Constructing a diaphragm from cellophane or shrink-wrap film
- Connecting the driver to the diaphragm to produce sound

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Microwave oven transformer (MOT)** — primary coil and iron core laminates salvaged for the speaker
- **Hacksaw blade** — snapped in half, used as a spring/reed to mount the vibrating iron segment
- **Neodymium magnets** — placed in opposing polarity around the iron to create the magnetic field for actuation
- **Builder's board** — used as the base for the assembly
- **Black acrylic plastic** — cut with a slot to hold the hacksaw blade reed firmly
- **Cellophane / shrink-wrap (drinks pack wrapper)** — used as the speaker diaphragm; heat-shrunk tight with a hairdryer
- **Crazy glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to bond iron laminates to the hacksaw blade
- **Double-sided tape** — used to attach cellophane to the acrylic frame and to couple the driver rod to the diaphragm
- **Bent brass wire** — used as the mechanical link connecting the driver to the diaphragm centre
- **6-volt battery** — used for initial static deflection test of the coil/magnet assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- Sawing through the MOT weld bead (hacksaw or angle grinder) to separate the core sections
- Splitting iron laminate stacks by inserting a trimming knife blade and striking with a hammer (exploiting the weak resin bond)
- Snapping a hacksaw blade cleanly in a vice by striking it (exploiting its brittleness)
- Heat-shrinking cellophane/shrink-wrap with a hairdryer to form a taut diaphragm
- Static deflection test with a 6 V battery to verify coil/magnet polarity and actuation before assembly
- Mechanical coupling of driver to diaphragm centre via a drilled button and bent brass wire
