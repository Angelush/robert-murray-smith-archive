## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces and demonstrates his graphene-based conductive ink, highlighting a packaging change from plastic bottles to recyclable tins for environmental reasons. The ink combines graphene with graphite to achieve excellent conductivity at a practical price point, and is suitable for applications including electroplating, EMF shielding, and silkscreen printing. The main takeaway is that this thin, conforming ink preserves surface detail while delivering high conductivity.

## Key Topics
- Packaging redesign from plastic bottles to recyclable metal tins
- Composition and conductivity properties of the graphene/graphite ink
- Practical use cases: electroplating, EMF shielding, silkscreen printing
- Pricing rationale for mixing graphene with graphite
- Distribution and retail opportunities

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — primary conductive component; responsible for high conductivity but costly pure
- **Graphite** — mixed with graphene to reduce cost while maintaining conductivity; particle sizes of 40 microns and 5 microns
- **Conductive ink (graphene-based)** — the finished product; conductivity quoted as approximately constant ohms per square per mil

## Techniques and Methods
- **Silkscreen printing** — the ink's particle size (5–40 microns) makes it compatible with silkscreen application
- **Electroplating prep coating** — thin ink conforms tightly to surface detail, preserving features before plating
- **EMF shielding by painting** — ink can be brush-applied to plastics to create electromagnetic shielding
