## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents an improved, simpler method for synthesizing graphene oxide (GO) at home, addressing complaints that his previous method was too complex and required strict temperature control. The new approach — essentially the Tour/Marcano method — requires only about 5 minutes of preparation, no heating, and just 3–4 days of passive stirring, with an approximate 100% yield of graphene oxide.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of the previous graphene oxide synthesis method (modified Hummer's)
- Step-by-step Tour method for GO synthesis using sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid, and potassium permanganate
- Visual indicators of reaction progress (green → brown → yellow)
- Quenching the reaction with hydrogen peroxide
- Post-reaction washing and pH adjustment
- Safety precautions when working with concentrated acids and pressurized vessels
- Characterization of GO via UV-vis spectroscopy and XRD (in author replies)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — starting material; 3.2 g used; purity/crystal size affects yield quality
- **Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), 98% concentrated** — 320 mL; primary oxidizing solvent
- **Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄), 75%** — 80 mL; co-solvent in the Tour method
- **Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄)** — 18 g; oxidizing agent, added slowly over 5–10 minutes; produces green color and purple fumes
- **Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), 30%** — ~100 mL total; used to quench/terminate the reaction; causes bright yellow coloration
- **Hydrochloric acid (HCl), 1 molar** — used in washing step to remove sulfates and metal ion contaminants
- **Water (hot)** — added after H₂O₂ quench; also used in washing steps

## Techniques and Methods
- **Modified Tour method (Marcano et al.)** — mixing H₂SO₄/H₃PO₄ with graphite, then slowly adding KMnO₄; stir or leave static for 3–4 days at room temperature
- **Passive oxidation without stirring** — tested as a simplification; reaction still proceeds to completion over 4 days
- **Reaction quenching with H₂O₂** — terminates oxidation; bright yellow color confirms successful GO formation
- **Washing with 1M HCl** — removes sulfate residues and free metal ions
- **Water washing to pH 4–5** — repeated washes until neutralized
- **Centrifugation** — mentioned as preferred separation method; filtration also viable
- **UV-vis spectroscopy** (author reply) — characteristic peaks at 230 nm (π–π*) and 310 nm (n–π*) used to identify GO
- **XRD** (author reply) — used to determine interlayer spacing; GO shows ~0.6–0.7 nm vs. 0.335 nm for pristine graphite
- **AFM** (author reply) — used to measure layer thickness and count layers in GO samples

## Robert's Own Replies
- **This is the Tour method**: Robert confirms the procedure is based on Marcano et al.'s *"Improved Synthesis of Graphene Oxide"* (Tour group); links to the paper.
- **UV-vis characterization**: Detailed technical note on GO identification — π–π* peak at 230 nm and n–π* shoulder at 310 nm; AFM layer thickness values given (~0.57 nm monolayer, interlayer spacing 0.6–0.7 nm vs. 0.335 nm for graphite).
- **XRD guidance**: Notes limitations of XRD for GO characterization and correct sample preparation procedures.
- **Safety**: Neither the Tour method nor classic Hummer's is truly "safe"; suggests ball milling graphite with CO₂ as a safer alternative for GO production.
- **Temperature critical**: Must not exceed 55°C during reaction — above that it becomes explosive.
- **Graphite quality matters**: Better crystal size and higher carbon content give better results; pencil leads (6B or 8B) can be used as a substitute.
- **H₂O₂ and HCl concentrations**: 30% H₂O₂; 1 molar HCl for washing.
- **pH affects dispersion**: Too alkaline and GO will never disperse properly.
- **Washing rationale**: HCl converts sulfate residues and free metal ions into soluble salts that can then be removed by water washing — tedious but necessary.
- **GO applications**: Can be used to coat glass, make conductive inks, build supercapacitors, and unzipped for graphene; used in his later videos.
- **Yellow color** confirms purity of the product.
