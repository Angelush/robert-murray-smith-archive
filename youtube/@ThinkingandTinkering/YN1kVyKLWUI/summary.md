## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a cheap, battery-powered portable soldering iron using an automotive glow plug as the heating element. He walks through the minimal assembly required — attaching electrical contacts and a handle — and shows it successfully soldering two wires at just 4 volts. The key insight is that voltage directly controls heat output, making it simple to regulate without electronics.

## Key Topics
- Using automotive glow plugs as cheap, robust heating elements
- Rethreading the glow plug's machine thread to accept a standard M10 bolt
- Creating positive and negative electrical contact points
- Building an insulating handle from fire brick ceramic
- Attaching a copper nail as a soldering tip via a terminal block connector
- Voltage-based heat control (operates across 2–12V range)
- Optional improvements: thermal cutout switch, high-temperature sealant handle

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Automotive glow plug** — the heating element; rated for 12V, built to withstand combustion cylinder environments; possibly coated with platinum or iridium
- **M10 bolt and washers** — used to form the negative contact point after rethreading
- **Brass terminal blocks** (3A and 5A) — used for positive contact and to hold the copper nail tip
- **Copper nail** — cut down to serve as the soldering tip
- **Fire brick / kiln ceramic** — drilled out as an insulating handle
- **Kapton tape** — heat-resistant tape (rated to 400°C) used for insulation
- **High-temperature sealant** — rated to 1200°C, offered as an alternative handle material
- **Ceramic blanket** — used around the handle area (left over from a previous kiln build)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Rethreading** — using an M10 × 1.5 tap to recut the glow plug's thread to accept a standard M10 bolt
- **Terminal block disassembly** — splitting plastic terminal block housing to extract the bare brass connector
- **Drilling ceramic** — boring a 9mm hole through fire brick to create an insulating handle sleeve
- **Voltage-regulated heating** — controlling temperature by varying supply voltage rather than using active electronics
- **Wire clamping contacts** — securing leads by trapping wire between M10 bolts/washers and inside terminal block screws
