## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to build wind turbine blades entirely from bamboo, splitting a 4-meter pole into three blade sections using basic hand tools. He mounts the finished blades on a radiator fan motor atop a bamboo pole and tests the turbine outdoors, producing around half a watt in very light winds. The video argues that bamboo is an ideal, genuinely renewable material for DIY wind turbine construction.

## Key Topics
- Robert's current obsession with bamboo as a building material and why he considers it superior to typical Western perceptions of it
- The sustainability problem with commercial wind turbine blades (fiberglass/resin cores vs. truly renewable alternatives)
- Splitting and shaping bamboo into aerodynamic turbine blades using minimal tools
- Assembling the blades onto a radiator fan motor (reused from a previous "easiest wind generator" project)
- Outdoor testing and power output in light breeze conditions
- Cost and sourcing of large-diameter bamboo in the UK

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bamboo** — 4-meter pole, ~120 mm diameter at the base, used for both the turbine blades and the mounting pole
- **Bar iron with a wedge** — used as a splitting tool
- **Balsa wood** — mentioned as the core material in commercial wind turbine blades (cited as a sustainability concern)
- **Fiberglass and resin** — mentioned as the problematic non-renewable components of commercial blades
- **Soil pipe (PVC)** — referenced as the material used in a previous wind turbine blade video, for comparison

## Techniques and Methods
- **Splitting bamboo with a wedge/bar iron** — placing the wedge at the center of the cut end and driving it down to produce clean halves
- **Node cleaning** — sanding or chiselling the internal nodes flat after splitting
- **Blade profile cutting** — narrowing the blade to half its width one-third of the way along its length, cutting downward to avoid splitting
- **Sanding** — finishing the blade surfaces after cutting
- **Bolting blades to a triangular hub** — attaching the three blades to a metal triangle bracket connected to the radiator fan motor shaft
- **Field testing** — hoisting the assembled turbine on a 4-meter bamboo pole and measuring voltage/power output in ambient wind

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Blade orientation / "back to front" myth:** Robert clarifies at length that there is no true "back" or "front" to the blades — if mounted the "wrong way round," the turbine will simply spin driven by wind from the other side. The leading edge position determines clockwise vs. anticlockwise rotation only.
- **Lift vs. drag type:** He confirms this is a **lift-type turbine** in its standard orientation; reversed, it would act as a drag type.
- **Bamboo splitting on bolting:** He states it is "a myth that bamboo will split on bolting."
- **Bamboo sourcing and cost:** He paid **£18 for a 4-meter, 120 mm-diameter pole from UKBamboo**; notes this is cheaper than equivalent pressure-treated pine (£24). He recommends searching for "UKBamboo" or "bamboo UK."
- **Tools used:** Hand axe, handsaw, and sander — emphasising the minimal tool requirement as one of bamboo's key advantages.
- **Build time:** "It took me a couple of hours from pole to blade."
- **Blade design credit:** He acknowledges the blade profile is copied from a commercial wind turbine design, not his own original design.
- **Power output:** He states he doesn't know the exact output and "doesn't really care" — the point of the video is demonstrating that bamboo can produce blades comparable to commercial designs.
