## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents his completed "Crystal Antikythera" — a laser-cut replica of the ancient Greek Antikythera mechanism, which is a geocentric orrery (celestial computer) dating from roughly 150–87 BC. Built using the xTool P2 CO2 laser cutter in just three days, the model comprises approximately 890 parts and demonstrates the planetary motions as seen from Earth, including epicyclic movement and lunar phases. A full step-by-step build video and STL files on Thingiverse are promised as follow-ups.

## Key Topics
- Background on the original Antikythera mechanism: its discovery in 1901 in a shipwreck near the island of Antikythera, and its dating to the classical period (c. 150–87 BC)
- Geocentric vs. heliocentric models of the solar system, and why the Antikythera uses a geocentric model
- Epicyclic (epicycle) planetary motion and how it can be represented with meshing gears
- The classical planets (the only ones known in antiquity) as displayed by the mechanism
- Use of the xTool P2 CO2 laser cutter to fabricate the ~890 flat parts quickly and accurately
- Comparison with 3D printing (Bambu lamp 3D printer used for curved/twisty parts in an earlier project)
- Calibration and operation of the finished model — aligning arms, turning the handle, observing relative planetary motion
- Moon representation: dual-axis swivel to show lunar phases
- Fluorescent acrylic chosen for aesthetic effect
- Plans for a decorative top ring (date ring, zodiac/star signs) still to be laser-engraved
- Reference to the famous brass replica by "Clickspring" as prior art

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Fluorescent acrylic sheet (used for all cut parts, chosen for its visual glow)

## Techniques and Methods
- CO2 laser cutting (xTool P2) — primary fabrication method for flat gear and structural parts
- FDM 3D printing (Bambu lamp printer) — used in the earlier related orrery build for complex curved parts
- Laser engraving — used (for the first time by Robert on this machine) to mark planet names and symbols on the indicator arrows
- Gear meshing to produce epicyclic (geocentric) planetary motion
- Calibration by aligning all indicator arms to the same start position before operation
- Layer-by-layer CT/X-ray scanning of the original artifact (referenced as the research method used by prior scholars to reverse-engineer the mechanism)
