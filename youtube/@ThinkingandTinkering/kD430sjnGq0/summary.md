## Overview
This video demonstrates how FWG's water-resistant conductive ink can be used to make non-metallic surfaces electroplatable with copper, using minion erasers as the demonstration object. Presented by Mandy from FWG with a technical segment by Robert, the video walks through the full process from surface preparation to a finished, polished copper-coated minion. The key takeaway is that low current density applied slowly over a long period (two days at 50mA) produces the best results.

## Key Topics
- FWG water-resistant conductive ink for enabling electroplating on non-metallic objects
- Copper electroplating chemistry and bath setup
- Importance of current density in achieving a smooth, salmon-pink copper finish
- Surface preparation for porous or oily objects
- Anode construction from copper pipe
- Use of saccharin as a plating brightener

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper sulfate** — primary electrolyte; 125g dissolved in 500ml deionized water
- **Copper formate / copper acetate** — mentioned as alternative copper(II) ion sources
- **Deionized water** — solvent for the electrolyte bath
- **FWG water-resistant conductive ink** — applied to the minion to make it electrically conductive
- **Acetone** — used to clean the minion before inking
- **Saccharin** — added to the copper sulfate solution as a brightener
- **Brass screw** — embedded in the minion as a highly conductive cathode contact point
- **Copper pipe** — flattened and filed to form the anode

## Techniques and Methods
- Cleaning non-metallic objects in acetone to remove oils before applying ink
- Applying 2–3 coats of conductive ink; drying naturally (~1 hour) or with a hairdryer on low heat for 5–10 minutes
- Resistance testing of the inked surface with a multimeter (~450–500 ohms achieved)
- Flattening copper pipe with a hammer and filing edges to fabricate the anode
- Electroplating setup with red/black alligator clips (red = anode/positive, black = cathode/negative)
- Using a DC power supply at 0.8V / 50mA for slow, even deposition over two days
- Rotating the object 90° every 30–60 minutes for uniform coverage (or using an aquarium pump for continuous ion movement)
- Polishing the finished piece with a Dremel tool

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments add several useful technical clarifications:

- **Any copper(II) salt works**: pretty much any Cu²⁺ ion source will electroplate correctly; most have a characteristic blue colour in solution.
- **Inkjet printing not possible**: the ink particle size is too large (~too big for a 45-micrometre nozzle); reducing particle size to ~5 micrometres would lower conductivity by raising the critical transition temperature.
- **Anode/cathode terminology**: Robert clarifies the confusion — think of it in terms of what you want the ions to do: positive metal ions are attracted to the negatively charged object, so make the object to be plated the negative electrode.
- **The wiring problem**: the original failed attempt used magnet wire whose varnish coating hadn't been removed, preventing conduction; they switched to stranded wire from a kettle flex.
- **Battery vs. power supply**: the battery suggestion in the video is for casual experimentation; serious platers need a proper current-limited supply.
- **Bath formulation for dendrite suppression**: saccharin and many other additives suppress dendrites; Lancaster University has done significant work on deep eutectic solvents in electroplating for those wanting to go deeper.
- **Credit**: the water-resistant conductive ink was Steve's work, not Robert's own.
- **Repeatability caveat**: home electroplating results will vary with individual skill rather than kit contents — if it works at all, that's a success.
