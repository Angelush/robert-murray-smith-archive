## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith addresses the perennial question of which wind turbine design is "best," arguing that efficiency — while easy to calculate — is too narrow a metric. He contends that "best" is a broader, context-dependent concept shaped by personal values, living situation, budget, and priorities. The key takeaway is that the best turbine is the best one *for you*, and doing something is better than waiting for a perfect solution.

## Key Topics
- Historical context of wind turbines: horizontal axis (ancient Greece, Europe, Americas) vs. vertical axis (Nashtifan, Iran)
- The ongoing debate between horizontal and vertical axis wind turbine designs
- Why efficiency is an oversimplified measure of "best"
- Environmental problems with modern wind turbines (concrete CO2, non-decomposing blades)
- The role of personal values and beliefs in defining what "best" means
- Practical factors: living situation, DIY vs. buying, cost, upcycling concerns
- The diversity of wind turbine designs as answers to diverse problems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Concrete (referenced in the context of CO2 emissions from turbine construction)
- Turbine blades (noted as non-biodegradable/non-decomposing)
