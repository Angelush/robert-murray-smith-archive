## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith upcycles a second-hand pine desk (purchased for £20 from a charity shop) into a handmade wooden doll's house as a Christmas gift for his granddaughter. The finished toy is modular and collapsible, packing back into its own base box, and was built as a deliberate alternative to commercial doll's houses costing £350–£500. Beyond the build itself, Robert uses the project to make a broader point: practicing craft skills on any project — however humble — is essential preparation for more ambitious making endeavors.

## Key Topics
- Upcycling and repurposing charity-shop furniture into legacy toys
- Designing and building a modular, collapsible wooden doll's house
- The value of practicing transferable woodworking skills
- Cost comparison: handmade (£20 + labour) vs. commercial equivalent (£350–£500)
- Woodworking as a skill-building activity applicable to larger projects (engines, turbines, etc.)
- Making gifts as labors of love

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Pine desk (sourced from a charity shop, ~£20) — primary timber source
- Door frame offcut — laminated up to form the staircases
- Wooden dowels — used for joints and locating pegs
- Wood glue — for assembling modules
- Varnish — mentioned as a finish that makes the dowel joints visible
- 1:12 scale dolls' house furniture — added to the finished toy

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of flat-pack/screw-and-glue furniture to recover usable timber
- Cutting a timber cutting list from a hand-drawn design
- Modular box construction (four interlocking boxes forming floors and roof)
- Jigsaw work for curved decorative details and window/door cut-outs
- Extensive sanding to round all edges (safety requirement for a toddler's toy)
- Dowel joinery — pegs and corresponding drilled holes for locating and assembly
- Clamping during glue-up
- Laminating door-frame offcuts to form staircase blocks
- Sawing and sanding stair profiles from laminated blanks
- Balcony construction from a plank with dowel balusters
