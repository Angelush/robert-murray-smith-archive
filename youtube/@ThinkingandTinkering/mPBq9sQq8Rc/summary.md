## Overview
Robert demonstrates a homemade magnetic hot stirrer plate built primarily from a salvaged VCR motor and a conductive ink heater element (made with "our ring" ink from ag-ink.com), stacked in a layered acrylic enclosure. The device operates at 24V, uses a microcomputer temperature controller (up to 120°C) and a PWM motor speed controller, and costs a fraction of a commercial equivalent (£120–140). Testing showed the hotplate could raise a cornflour solution from 12°C to 34°C in just nine minutes.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: building cheap lab equipment vs. buying commercial magnetic stirrer hotplates (£120–140)
- Two-design iteration process; the final stacked/layered acrylic design
- Live demonstration of stirring a cornflour-in-water solution
- Temperature rise measurements taken in 6-minute increments
- Plans for a follow-up build video and a members-only tutorial

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Conductive ink (from ag-ink.com / "our ring") — used to fabricate the heating element
- Acrylic (clear acrylic sheet) — used for the enclosure/body
- Cornflour (corn starch) dissolved in water — used as a demo stirring solution

## Techniques and Methods
- Salvaging a motor from a VCR for use as the stirrer drive
- Printing/applying conductive ink to form the hotplate heating element
- Stacking acrylic layers to create the device enclosure
- PWM speed control for motor RPM adjustment
- Microcomputer-based PID/temperature controller for hotplate regulation
- Temperature logging in fixed 6-minute increments to characterize heat-up rate
