## Overview
Robert revisits his earlier rough comparison of the toroidal propeller as a potential wind turbine generator blade, prompted by criticism that his first test was unfair. Using a larger toroidal propeller mounted on a NEMA stepper motor with a rectified output and a programmable electronic load, he conducts a more rigorous measurement. The results confirm his original suspicion: the toroidal propeller performs poorly as a wind generator blade, producing only ~3.6 milliwatts at 3 m/s wind speed against an expected ~200–230 milliwatts.

## Key Topics
- Background on the toroidal propeller (origins in marine use for cavitation reduction)
- Critique of his own earlier "rough comparison" test and motivation to retest fairly
- Testing methodology: fixed resistive load, simultaneous voltage and current measurement
- Measured output vs. expected output for the blade size and wind speed
- Conclusion that swept area is the fundamental limiting factor, and the toroidal design offers no advantage over a standard wind turbine blade

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Mounting a toroidal propeller on a NEMA stepper motor used as a generator
- Rectifying the AC output of the stepper motor for DC measurement
- Using a BK Precision 8500 programmable electronic load set to 100 Ω fixed resistance
- Simultaneous voltage and current (power) measurement under load
- Wind speed measurement with an anemometer at the blade face
