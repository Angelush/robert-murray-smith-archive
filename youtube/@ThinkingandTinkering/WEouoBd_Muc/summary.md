## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a cheap and easy DIY ethanol stove using a repurposed cast iron concrete test mold as the base, a tin can as the fuel reservoir, and an optional steel tube chimney to boost heat output. The main takeaway is that for around £25 total, anyone can create a functional room heater or decorative fire without tools, flues, or professional installation — a practical solution in the context of UK energy costs and potential blackouts.

## Key Topics
- Using secondhand cast iron concrete test molds as fireplace/stove bases
- How ethanol biofire heaters work and their popularity (~17 million sold in the USA)
- Converting a gentle decorative ethanol flame into a high-output "rocket stove" by adding a steel tube chimney
- Safety: ventilation requirements, lighting technique, and extinguishing the flame with a steel plate
- Cost comparison vs. professionally installed log burners (£2,000+)
- UK fuel regulations (Eco Design 2022 / Ecoguard 2022) and permitted fuels

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ethanol / methylated spirits** — primary fuel; methylated spirits in the UK is ethanol with pink dye and bittering agent
- **Methanol** — alternative fuel
- **Isopropanol (isopropyl alcohol)** — alternative fuel
- **Waste oil / used oil / paraffin** — noted as combustible in the stove but restricted by Ecoguard 2022 legislation
- **Cast iron concrete test molds** — used as the stove base (100 mm and 150 mm square sizes)
- **Tin can (e.g., bean tin)** — fuel reservoir placed inside the mold
- **Steel tube (300 mm × 4-inch square section, from Metals for You)** — chimney to create rocket stove effect
- **5×5 inch steel plate** — used to extinguish the flame by starving it of oxygen
- **Vinegar, table salt, washing-up liquid** — DIY cleaner for the secondhand mold
- **Wire wool** — used with the cleaner for scrubbing

## Techniques and Methods
- Cleaning secondhand cast iron with a vinegar/salt/washing-up liquid solution and wire wool
- Filling a tin can with alcohol fuel and lighting with a paper spill (not directly with a lighter)
- Placing a steel tube chimney over the flame to induce a rocket stove effect (audible roar, significantly increased heat output)
- Extinguishing by sliding a steel plate over the opening to starve the flame of oxygen
- Using a trivet (from a previous rocket stove build) to support a cooking vessel above the flame

## Robert's Own Replies
- **The stove will burn almost anything** (waste oil, used oil, paraffin, etc.), but Robert repeatedly stresses that **ethanol, methanol, and isopropyl alcohol are the only legally permitted fuels** under **Ecoguard / Eco Design 2022** legislation in the UK. Coal is specifically banned in any burner.
- **Adding wick material (wool, hemp, ceramic) does not improve efficiency** — it reduces fuel capacity because the wick takes up space in the can, so the stove burns for less time. Any decorative benefit is purely aesthetic.
- **A fuel feed system** is something Robert says is on his mind as a future improvement.
- **Mantle heaters vs. this design**: mantle heaters convert flame to radiant heat; this design primarily heats the air — a different mode of operation.
- **UK context**: Robert flags the threat of winter blackouts and rising energy costs as motivation, and notes a **£1,000 fine** for burning prohibited fuels.
- Robert confirms he **has already done a video** on converting a purchased stove to ethanol, and references earlier videos on rocket stoves and concrete testing.
