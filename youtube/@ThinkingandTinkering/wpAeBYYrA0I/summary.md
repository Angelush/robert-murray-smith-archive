## Overview
This video is Part 3 of Robert Murray-Smith's series on building a Van de Graaff generator, focusing on the base structure and the completed tower assembly. Robert walks through the construction of the base platform, the threaded rod support system, and explains how the design allows for belt alignment correction. The key takeaway is that careful attention to surface finishing (removing burrs) and a tilt-adjustment mechanism are critical for the generator to function efficiently.

## Key Topics
- Overview of the completed basic structure including the steel gazing ball toroid top
- Relationship between the toroid radius and maximum voltage output
- Sources of charge leakage and how to minimise them (especially around the bottom hole of the sphere)
- Construction of the base from builder's board
- M12 threaded rod leg system with square plate washers and dome nuts
- Tilt/alignment correction mechanism for belt tracking

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel gazing ball (used as the toroid top terminal)
- Builder's board (MDF/chipboard, ~175 mm wide)
- M12 threaded rod (45 cm lengths)
- Square plate washers
- Dome nuts
- Sandpaper / emery paper (for deburring)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a hole in the steel gazing ball using a Dremel with a circular cutting disc (~20 minutes)
- Deburring and smoothing the cut hole with a Dremel grinding head and sandpaper to reduce charge leakage
- Joining two pieces of builder's board with cross-bracing underneath for rigidity
- Drilling 16 mm holes (oversized from the 12 mm rod) to allow controlled tilt for belt alignment
- Clamping rods rigid using square plate washers above and below the base board tightened with dome nuts
- Using a plumb bob to assist in aligning the upper and lower belt drums
