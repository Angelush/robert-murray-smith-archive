## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a functional water clock by adapting the water motor he created in video 2170. The device uses the motor's regular oscillating motion (one rock every 4 seconds) to drive a ratchet-and-gear mechanism, ultimately turning clock hands at the correct minute and hour rates. The result is a working, if imprecise, 3D-printed water clock requiring only a steady drip of water to run.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the water motor mechanism from video 2170 and its regular oscillation
- Using a constant drip rate to produce a predictable 4-second rocking cycle
- Converting oscillating/reciprocating motion into unidirectional rotation via a ratchet
- Gear train design to produce correct minute-hand (1/60th turn per minute) and hour-hand (1/12th turn per hour) motion
- Assembly details: pivot points, brackets, linkage arm, counterbalance weight
- Accuracy limitations and potential improvements (adding bearings)
- Water supply options for continuous operation (recirculating pump, drip tank, tapped hose)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bamboo** — used as the rocker arm / pendulum beam
- **Brass rod (3 mm)** — used as the push rod connecting the rocker to the ratchet; chosen for strength over 3D-printed plastic
- **Dome nut** — glued on as a counterbalance weight to re-balance the rocker after adding the push rod
- **Super glue** — used throughout for assembly of printed parts
- **Tap water** — used as the working fluid for testing
- **3D-printed PLA/plastic parts** — gears, ratchet wheel, brackets, clock face support, clock hands (STL files on Thingiverse)

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — all structural parts (arc, ratchet, gears, brackets, carriers, clock face support, hands) printed and assembled
- **Ratchet mechanism** — large ratchet wheel with a small central gear; push rod advances it one click per 4-second rock cycle; a second ratchet pawl prevents back-travel
- **Gear train calculation** — 60-tooth and 52-tooth gears combined with a 16-tooth gear to achieve 1/60th rotation per minute for the minute hand; additional two-gear stage produces 1/12th rotation per hour for the hour hand
- **Flow-rate tuning** — adjusting the water drip rate via a tap or valve to calibrate timekeeping speed
- **Counterbalancing** — adding a dome nut opposite the push rod to restore balance of the rocker arm
- **Pivot and bearing assembly** — pivot points on bamboo rocker; option noted to add bearings for improved accuracy and smoothness
