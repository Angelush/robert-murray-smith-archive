## Overview
This is the fifth and final installment of Robert's series on converting a rooftop rotating ventilator into a wind-powered generator. Robert completes the assembly — attaching the coil ring to the supporting stays and fitting the fan blade — then tests the device outdoors in a light breeze, measuring approximately 2–3 volts and 2–3 amps (~6–9 watts). The key takeaway is a proof of concept: placing the generator on the circumference of a slowly rotating structure, rather than at the center, provides a meaningful leverage and speed advantage for harvesting low-torque wind energy.

## Key Topics
- Final mechanical assembly of the ventilator generator (coil ring, magnet ring, fan blade, stays)
- Ring construction technique: staggering the two ring splits 180° apart to equalise tension (analogous to a scarf joint or plywood layering)
- Tracking coil winding direction to ensure all coils are connected in phase
- Fan blade attachment: removable, weight-bearing carried by stays rather than the blade joint
- Parallel wiring of coils for higher current output
- Outdoor power generation test in light wind conditions
- Circumference vs. centre placement for generators: the leverage argument for rim-mounted designs
- Comparison with axial flux generators
- Broader concept of harvesting low-torque, low-speed rotational energy at scale

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ferrite magnets** — used in the magnet ring; chosen for low cost
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to close a finger cut sustained during handling; Robert notes it was originally developed for field surgery during the Vietnam War
- **12 mm extension bolt and 12 mm bar** — used to extend the central axle after the stays were repositioned
- **Plastic ring** — structural component of the assembly
- **Short screws** — used to reinforce the structure after stay relocation

## Techniques and Methods
- **Offset ring splits**: the two segments forming the outer ring were joined with their splits 180° apart to distribute tension evenly around the circumference
- **Coil winding direction tracking**: each coil's start point and winding direction were marked before mounting to prevent phase cancellation on connection
- **Parallel coil wiring**: coils connected in parallel to maximise current output at low rotational speed
- **Separate voltage and current measurement**: volts and amps measured independently (not under simultaneous load) as a field expedient
- **Removable fan blade design**: fan blade attached weakly and without structural load, allowing easy removal or reversal to test wind-direction effects
- **Outdoor testing**: device mounted and tested in natural light breeze to verify rotation and power generation

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Fan blade effect**: Robert noted he "didn't really see much of a difference" with the fan blade fitted.
- **Magnet orientation**: Confirmed the magnets are arranged NSNS (alternating poles).
- **Build cost**: Outside of the ventilator itself, total cost was approximately **£20**, achieved by scavenging most components and using cheap ferrite rather than neodymium magnets.
- **Centering problems**: Confirmed he had significant trouble getting the ring to run true, and if rebuilding would **stiffen the stays** considerably.
- **Power management thinking**: In a longer reply, Robert shared his current thinking on actually using the generated power — he favours a **capacitor bank as a buffer/reservoir** between the generator and the load, so that variable loads draw from the bank and the generator sees a roughly constant load. He noted he hasn't done a video on this yet as he is still working it through.
- **Self-assessment**: Repeatedly characterised the project as answering one specific question — *"can it be done?"* — and acknowledged there are many practical improvements still to be made.
- **Future project**: Mentioned he is now looking out for a **front-loading washing machine with direct drive** as a source of a large-diameter rim generator for future experiments.
- **Venting**: Suggested to a commenter that any flat plate added to the design should be vented to allow airflow through.
