## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a brief update on his "thermal forever battery" experiment — a simple electrochemical cell made from plastic sheets painted with graphene/conductive ink and submerged in a copper chloride solution. The device generates a measurable voltage and current at room temperature, and he notes performance improves with higher copper chloride concentration or elevated temperature. He frames it as a fascinating aside before returning to his main "SWAG" project thread.

## Key Topics
- Construction of a simple electrochemical cell using graphene conductive ink on plastic strips
- Voltage and current output measurements across a resistive load
- Effect of copper chloride concentration and temperature on power output
- Brief mention of the Chinese origin of the underlying idea
- Relationship to the "Maxwell's Demon" concept (thermal/atomic motion as energy source)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Plastic sheet** — used as the substrate for each cell panel
- **Copper strips** — applied to the plastic as electrodes
- **FWG conductive (graphene) ink** — painted onto the plastic strips to form the active surface
- **Copper chloride (CuCl₂, copper II)** — electrolyte solution (100 g in 15 L of water)
- **Water** — solvent for the electrolyte
- **Supercritical CO₂** — mentioned in a comment as a solvent for graphite

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting plastic substrate with graphene conductive ink to create active electrode sheets
- Stacking 10 painted sheets into a tank electrolyte bath
- Measuring output voltage across a 10 Ω resistor to derive current (~54–55 mA at ~4.5–7 V)
- Passive (unstirred) operation relying on room-temperature atomic/thermal motion of the liquid
