## Overview
Robert demonstrates how conductive ink, applied freehand with a pen to a flat piece of scrap glass and then heated in a microwave, can cut complex curved shapes cleanly and easily. The video is a direct response to a viewer question (from Luke Turner) asking whether the technique works on flat glass. Robert concludes that this method makes traditional glass cutters effectively obsolete for hobby glasswork.

## Key Topics
- Using conductive ink as a glass-cutting tool on flat sheet glass
- Cutting freehand curved and complex shapes (not just straight lines)
- Microwave-heating the ink to thermally fracture the glass along the drawn line
- Potential hobby applications, including stained glass and Tiffany lamp-making

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Scrap flat glass** — the workpiece being cut
- **Conductive ink** (applied via a pen/marker) — drawn along the desired cut line; heats resistively in the microwave to create a thermal fracture

## Techniques and Methods
- **Freehand drawing with conductive ink pen** — tracing the desired cut curve directly onto glass
- **Microwave curing/heating** — placing the inked glass in a microwave to heat the ink and cause the glass to snap along the line
- **Thermal fracture cutting** — glass separates cleanly along the heated ink trace without a traditional glass cutter

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Microwave safety**: Robert acknowledges uncertainty about whether the technique could damage a microwave, noting it hasn't blown his up — but can't guarantee it for others.
- **Microwave only**: He clarifies the technique only works with a microwave, not a door or other heat source.
- **Plexiglass/acrylic**: He suggests that for plexi, connecting a battery across the ink trace (using it as a resistance heater) would probably work well as an alternative to microwave heating.
- **Intended scale**: He notes this is aimed at hobby glass work, not large-scale applications — most hobbyists won't have a piece too large for a domestic microwave.
- **Stained glass inspiration**: Robert mentions his wife saw the video and wants to try stained glass with the technique; he also independently thought it would be great for making stained glass windows and Tiffany lamps.
