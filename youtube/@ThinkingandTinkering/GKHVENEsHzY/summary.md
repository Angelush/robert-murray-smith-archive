## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents the near-completed rebuild of his sub-£50 DIY wind turbine, walking through the final assembly of the coil and rectifier arrangement and demonstrating it spinning indoors on a blower. He connects the output to microwave oven capacitors and a 12V battery to begin charging, explaining that real-world battery charging over a full day is the most meaningful performance test before a planned roof-mounted trial.

## Key Topics
- Total build cost breakdown (approximately £50 or under)
- Turbine dimensions and theoretical maximum power output
- Betz's Law and its 59.3% efficiency cap on wind energy extraction
- Coil and bridge rectifier arrangement (38 coils, parallel connection, four arc segments)
- Capacitor-buffered battery charging circuit with blocking diode
- Indoor blower demonstration as a substitute for outdoor wind testing
- Plans for a real-world roof-mounted charging test

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium magnets (~50 units, ~£20)
- Bearings (£3–4)
- Donated copper coils (38 units)
- Bridge rectifiers (£1 per 10, ~£3.80 total)
- Microwave oven capacitors (donated)
- 12V battery (used as energy store)
- Blocking diode (on positive output line)
- Welding rods (minor consumable cost)

## Techniques and Methods
- Parallel connection of individual coil outputs, each with its own bridge rectifier
- Arrangement of coils in four arc segments fixed with screws
- Capacitor buffering between turbine output and battery
- Blocking diode to prevent back-discharge from battery to turbine
- Theoretical power curve calculation adjusted for the Betz coefficient
- Battery voltage monitoring as a proxy for energy generation measurement
- Indoor blower spin test prior to outdoor deployment
