## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores asymmetric capacitors — specifically the kind where one plate is much larger than the other — and demonstrates that a high-voltage asymmetric field can generate thrust and move air without requiring ion wind. Using a coffee can, a 9B pencil-drawn circuit, and crisp packet foil, he builds simple electrostatic devices that produce audible sound and physical movement, arguing the effect is field-based rather than ion-based.

## Key Topics
- Definition and distinction between two types of asymmetric capacitors (supercapacitor sense vs. geometry sense)
- Ion wind lifters vs. pure electrostatic field thrust
- Asymmetric electrostatic fields as a source of propulsion
- NASA's interest in field-based thrust for space travel
- Electrostatic speakers as a practical application
- DIY demonstration with household materials

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium (used as capacitor plate material)
- Manganese dioxide (mentioned as an example material in asymmetric supercapacitors)
- Coffee can (large metal plate for the asymmetric capacitor demo)
- 9B graphite pencil (used to draw a capacitor structure on paper)
- Crisp packet / aluminized Mylar foil (aluminium layer sandwiched between two plastic films, used as a floating plate)
- Window film (used as an alternative dielectric/plate in a second demo)

## Techniques and Methods
- Winding a thin wire around a coffee can to create a large-plate / small-plate asymmetric capacitor
- Driving the capacitor with an audio signal via a Yamaha amplifier fed into an automotive ignition coil (2000:1 step-up ratio, ~1.5–2 kV output)
- Drawing an asymmetric capacitor geometry directly on paper with a high-graphite pencil to create conductive traces
- Placing aluminized crisp-packet foil or window film over the drawn capacitor and applying high voltage to observe electrostatic attraction/repulsion
- Using breakdown voltage of air (~1 kV/mm or ~1 kV/cm) as a guide for safe plate separation
