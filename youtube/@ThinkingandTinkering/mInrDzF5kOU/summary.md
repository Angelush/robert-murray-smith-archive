## Overview
This video demonstrates how to build a DIY power resistor from scratch as a cost-saving alternative to commercial units. The presenter constructs a 35-ohm resistor using kanthal wire wound around a mica core, encased in plaster of Paris. The finished resistor measures 35.1 ohms, closely matching the target resistance.

## Key Topics
- Why commercial power resistors are expensive (£11.53 cited for a 35Ω aluminium-housed unit)
- Selecting appropriate resistive wire length to hit a target resistance
- Choosing a non-conductive core material
- Winding wire without turn-to-turn shorts
- Creating a mould and casting the resistor body
- Adding crimp terminals for reliable connections

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Kanthal (resistance wire)** — wound to form the resistive element, cut to ~36Ω before losses
- **Ferrite/ferro rod** — initially considered as the core but abandoned (found to be magnetic)
- **Mica sheet** — used as the final core; kanthal wire seats into it easily without touching
- **Plaster of Paris** — used as the casting compound for the casing (mixed to a yogurt-like consistency)
- **PET sheet plastic** — rolled into a cylindrical mould for the plaster casting
- **Crimp terminals** — added to the wire ends for reliable electrical contact

## Techniques and Methods
- Measuring wire resistance with a multimeter before winding
- Tightly winding kanthal wire around a core while keeping turns spaced to avoid bridging
- Rolling PET sheet into a cylinder and taping it as a disposable casting mould
- Mixing plaster of Paris to yogurt consistency and pouring into the mould
- Crimping terminals onto the lead wires after the plaster has set
- Final resistance verification with a multimeter
