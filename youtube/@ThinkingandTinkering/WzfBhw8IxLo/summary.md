## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple air engine using two sets of bellows connected to a handmade rotary valve chest machined from acrylic. The engine requires minimal tools and loose tolerances, making it highly accessible to home builders. While not a powerhouse, the project showcases the versatility of bellows as a construction platform and introduces a clever valve design adaptable to compressed air, steam, vacuum, and Stirling engine applications.

## Key Topics
- Building a reciprocating air motor from two paired bellows units
- Constructing a rotary valve chest from acrylic sheet salvaged from LCD screens
- How the valve alternately pressurises and exhausts each bellows to produce motion
- Testing the engine using a domestic vacuum cleaner as the air source
- The broader versatility of bellows for DIY motors, pumps, and generators
- Availability of a TinkerCAD/Thingiverse version of the valve for 3D printing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bellows** — two sets used as the motor cylinders
- **Acrylic block** — salvaged from disassembled LCD screens, used to make the three-part valve chest
- **12 mm pipe** — used as port entry tubes in the valve chest
- **Skater bearing (8 mm)** — pressed into the centre of the valve wheel to allow smooth rotation
- **Jar with drilled hole** — used as an improvised air inlet port fitting
- **Guide/runner rods** — used to constrain and guide the bellows travel

## Techniques and Methods
- Pairing two bellows sets on a common frame so they fill and empty alternately
- Laminating three acrylic blocks, bolting them in registration, then drilling port holes in precise angular positions (centre, ±45°) with a hole saw
- Sequential disassembly and re-drilling to produce the correct hole pattern on each of the three plates (inlet/outlet plate, valve plate, backing plate)
- Routing an arc-shaped exhaust slot in the valve disc to allow continuous exhaust sweep
- Press-fitting a skater bearing into the valve disc centre for low-friction rotation
- Using a push rod off the piston shaft to actuate the valve automatically
- Designing the same valve in TinkerCAD and publishing to Thingiverse for 3D-printed reproduction
