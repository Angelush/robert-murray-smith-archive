## Overview
Robert explores wave energy conversion using a 3D-printed bevel gear mechanism that translates the linear up-and-down motion of ocean waves into continuous unidirectional rotation suitable for driving a generator. He contrasts wave power (wind-driven) with tidal power (moon-driven), briefly surveys existing commercial solutions like the Pelamis sea snake and Wave Dragon, and then walks through the assembly of his own ratchet-based design. The key takeaway is that opposing ratchets on a bevel gear cradle ensure the output shaft always spins the same direction regardless of whether the wave arm moves up or down.

## Key Topics
- Difference between wave power (wind-driven) and tidal power (moon-driven)
- Wave characteristics: amplitude (height) and wavelength (period)
- Existing wave energy converters: Pelamis/sea snake and Wave Dragon
- Bevel gear differential-style mechanism for motion rectification
- Ratchet pairs facing opposite directions to produce unidirectional output
- Standardised component philosophy (22 mm skater bearings, 8 mm bar)
- One-way clutch/sprag bearings as a more robust alternative to printed ratchets
- Connecting rotational output directly to a generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **22 mm skater bearings** — standard bearing used throughout the assembly
- **8 mm steel bar** — axle stock (100 mm and 120 mm lengths used)
- **Super glue** — bonding ratchet arms to ratchet bodies
- **One-way clutch bearing** — mentioned as a preferable alternative to printed ratchets (used previously in a freewheel generator build)
- **Sprag bearings** — flagged as superior to ratchets; Robert states he is building an upgraded version using them

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — all structural parts designed in TinkerCAD and printed; files freely available via description link
- **Bevel gear assembly** — interlocking bevel gears arranged so either input direction drives the output gear the same way
- **Dual opposing ratchet mechanism** — two ratchets oriented in opposite directions on a shared bar so upward and downward strokes each engage one ratchet, always driving the output in one direction
- **Press-fit bearing installation** — bearings pressed into printed housings at each end of the cradle and output shaft
- **Float/buoy coupling** — the rocking arm is designed to be attached to a floating object (e.g. a bottle) that rises and falls with waves

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Generator output is AC:** Clarifies that the generator automatically produces AC; DC requires a rectifier.
- **Sprag bearings preferred:** Confirms he is actively building an upgraded version using sprag bearings, which he considers significantly better than printed ratchets, though harder to implement correctly.
- **The concept is not new:** Acknowledges the idea is old and has predominantly been applied with Wells turbines, despite often being presented as novel.
- **Multiple valid approaches exist:** Notes that wave energy can be captured via compressed air, linkages, gears, or linear generators — none is universally best; suitability depends on skills, location, and budget.
- **Wells turbines recommendation:** Directs a commenter asking about oscillating water columns to look into Wells turbines, which are used in exactly that application.
- **Float doubles as counterweight:** Confirms that the buoy/float attached to the arm also acts as a counterweight.
- **Australian blowhole generator:** References it as comparable to a suggestion made in the comments about air-compression-based capture.
