## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith cuts through the noise around home heating debates by returning to first principles: the Second Law of Thermodynamics (heat flows from hot to cold), the relationship between mass/volume and energy required, and the distinction between efficiency and effectiveness. His key takeaway is that "efficiency" claims in heating advertising are mostly meaningless — all electric heaters are 100% efficient — and that "effectiveness" (how well a heating approach suits your actual needs) is the concept that should guide decisions, alongside basic measures like insulation and reducing heated space.

## Key Topics
- The Second Law of Thermodynamics explained in plain terms: heat always moves from hot to cold
- Why insulation is the single most impactful thing for home heating
- The relationship between mass/volume and energy required to heat it
- Mass heaters debunked: more thermal mass requires more energy input
- All electric heaters are 100% energy-efficient — the type of heater makes no efficiency difference
- Cost of electricity varies greatly by location (UK vs Canada example)
- Efficiency vs. Effectiveness: why "effectiveness" is the more useful concept
- Practical effectiveness tips: door draft excluders, room size management, dehumidification

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Calcium chloride — mentioned as a dehumidifying agent; reducing moisture in a room can lower heating energy needs by ~5%

## Techniques and Methods
- Insulation (cavity wall, triple glazing) to slow heat loss to the outside
- Reducing the size of the heated space to reduce energy demand
- Using radiant/infrared heaters (quartz, halogen) for localized personal warmth rather than whole-room heating
- Using a fan to circulate warm air more evenly around a room (trades faster whole-room average warmth for slower localized warmth)
- Draft exclusion (door sausages, closing doors between rooms)
- Dehumidifying rooms with calcium chloride to reduce the energy cost of heating moist air
