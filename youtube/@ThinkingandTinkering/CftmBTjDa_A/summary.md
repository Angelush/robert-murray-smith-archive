## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith, filming from Sri Lanka, demonstrates how to process diamagnetic vein graphite flakes into a fine powder and bind it into a highly conductive ink using a liquid binder. He paints the ink onto glass, burnishes it, and tests its conductivity — finding it surpasses even silver ink. The video is an early-stage exploration of the material's electrical properties rather than a finished application.

## Key Topics
- Properties of diamagnetic vein graphite (flexible, draws like a pencil, floats on magnets)
- Processing bulk flakes into ultra-fine graphite powder
- Formulating a graphite-based conductive ink
- Painting and burnishing technique on glass substrate
- Conductivity measurement and comparison to silver

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Diamagnetic vein graphite** — mined (not synthetic), the primary material; forms the conductive and diamagnetic element of the ink
- **Water** — used as processing medium for blending and settling
- **Gum arabic** (referred to as "gabic" in transcript) — used as a liquid binder to form the ink
- **Glass** — used as the substrate for painting the ink

## Techniques and Methods
- **Food blender milling** — graphite flakes blended with water to break down into fine particles
- **Sieving** — to separate coarse from fine fractions
- **Gravitational settling / decantation** — large chunks allowed to sink over ~5 hours; fine suspension poured off and dried
- **Ink formulation** — fine powder mixed with gum arabic binder
- **Brush painting** — ink painted onto glass in a defined shape
- **Burnishing / surface smoothing** — rubbing on a flat table to compact and smooth the deposited layer
- **Two-probe conductivity testing** — meter probes placed ~1 cm apart on the dried ink trace

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Source of graphite**: Clarifies it is **mined vein graphite**, not synthetic — responding to a question about its origin.
- **Inkjet printing**: Considers it unlikely to work without breaking the structure down to the nanometre level; notes diamagnetism is influenced by sheet size, and defects (not smoothness) affect performance; conformality and minimum interconnects are the key challenges.
- **Heat pressing**: Doubts it would work unless at very high temperature and pressure, but encourages experimentation.
- **Glass substrate**: Firmly clarifies the glass is **plain window glass from Sri Lanka — not ITO-coated** — correcting a viewer assumption.
- **Lead content**: States there is **no lead whatsoever** — the material is pure graphite, confirmed by assay.
- **Future directions**: His personal interest is in **homopolar motors** from this material; also planning future work on **AZO coating glass** (zinc acetate prep work already done).
- **Plating organic substrates**: Mentions he has experience with conductive inks and platinum seeding for electroless deposition, and plans a video on plating organics.
- **Solvent suggestions**: Suggests trying **acetone** (lighter VOC) for better substrate adhesion; also floats **ethyl acetate in polyethylene glycol** as a possible ink formulation.
- **General stance**: Describes the work as early-stage property characterisation, not applications yet — explicitly hopes the community will "pick it up and run with it."
