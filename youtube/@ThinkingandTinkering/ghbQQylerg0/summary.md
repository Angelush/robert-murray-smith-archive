## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a clean geometric proof of the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²). He constructs a large square from four identical right-angled triangles arranged around a smaller central square, then equates two different expressions for the same total area to derive the theorem algebraically.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Pythagorean theorem and the relationship a² + b² = c²
- Why the theorem is true, not just what it states
- A geometric proof using a large square subdivided into four triangles and one inner square
- Algebraic expansion of (a + b)² and cancellation to isolate c² = a² + b²

None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Geometric construction: arranging four congruent right-angled triangles around a central tilted square to form a larger square of side (a + b)
- Algebraic expansion: expanding (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
- Area comparison: computing the same square's area two ways, then equating and cancelling the 2ab terms to prove the theorem
