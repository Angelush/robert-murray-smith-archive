## Overview
Robert builds a simple DIY multi-fuel heater from two food tins (a peas tin nested inside a tomatoes tin), carbon felt as a wick, and methanol as fuel — responding to the 2022 UK energy crisis and rising solid fuel costs. The design is essentially a homemade alcohol stove combined with a thermal mass heat radiator, producing clean combustion (CO2, no CO) and significant radiant heat from a steel block placed on top. Robert frames this as part one of a series, with a finished version to follow.

## Key Topics
- DIY heater construction from everyday food tins
- The UK 2022 energy/fuel cost crisis as motivation
- Using a steel (or copper) thermal mass block to store and radiate heat
- Clean-burning alcohol fuels as an accessible, storable alternative to solid fuel
- Methanol heaters as an established marine technology

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — primary fuel used; burns cleanly, producing CO2 with no CO; Robert notes it costs ~£1.55/litre on Amazon and is historically used in racing cars and marine heaters
- **Ethanol** — mentioned as an equally viable alternative fuel
- **Carbon felt (100% carbon, no plastic)** — used as wicking material inside the inner tin; must be pure carbon to avoid melting; available on Amazon
- **Steel block** — placed on top of the stove as a thermal mass to absorb and radiate heat after the flame dies
- **Copper** — mentioned as a potential alternative to steel for the thermal mass plate
- Two food tins: **tomato tin** (outer) and **peas tin** (inner, ~1 inch taller)
- **10p coin** — used as a jet regulator/cover over the fill hole

## Techniques and Methods
- Nesting two food tins of slightly different heights to create a double-wall alcohol stove
- Punching four holes in the bottom of the inner tin for airflow
- Drilling a 22 mm hole (via hole saw) in the inner tin as a fill port (alternative: remove bottom with can opener)
- Lining the inner tin with carbon felt as a passive wick
- Priming the stove by placing a small amount of fuel in the rim lip before ignition
- Placing a heavy thermal mass (steel block) on a tripod stand above the flame to capture and radiate heat
- Using a fire blanket or tin lid to extinguish the flame safely

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Carbon felt sourcing:** Available on Amazon; must be labelled "pure carbon felt" with no plastic additives. He linked a specific Amazon listing.
- **Methanol sourcing and cost:** Available on Amazon at ~£1.55/litre; linked a specific 99.85% purity product. Methanol is the correct fuel (not ethanol), and it's already used in marine heaters and historically in racing cars.
- **Burn time:** Approximately 1 hour per 100 mL of methanol; estimated 8–10 hours for a larger version.
- **Extinguishing:** Drop a fire blanket over it, or use a tin lid — any cover works.
- **Safety note:** Oxygen enters from the sides of the burner, not the centre, so pressure build-up is not a concern with this design.
- **This is part of a series:** He confirmed a final version was coming "today or tomorrow," hinting at a more refined design.
- **Alternative designs:** Acknowledged copper coil versions exist but chose this design for its simplicity.
