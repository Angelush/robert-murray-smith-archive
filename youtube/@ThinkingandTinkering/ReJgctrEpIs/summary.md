## Overview
Robert shares his philosophy on design, sparked by a conversation with Luke about designing a computer with a distinctive feel. His central argument is that good design provides just enough information to suggest a style — no more, no less — allowing the viewer to mentally complete the image and become engaged. He illustrates this with steampunk PC examples and his own project recreating "Bessie" (an Edwardian-style car) on a lawn mower.

## Key Topics
- The principle of "sufficient information" in visual design
- Viewer engagement through implied rather than exhaustive detail — letting people "fill in the blanks"
- How over-designing can alienate rather than attract
- Choosing a style (steampunk, gothic, Art Nouveau, etc.) and researching it visually
- Using internet image research to distil the essential elements of a style
- The role of materials vs. aesthetics — paint on plastic can be as valid as real brass and mahogany
- His ongoing "Carbesi" project: a lawn mower styled after the Edwardian Citroën "Bessie"

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Brass** — used in quality steampunk builds; also available cheaply as brass paint
- **Mahogany** — cited as a key material in authentic steampunk aesthetics
- **Plastic** — noted as a common base material for steampunk builds
- **Paint** — highlighted as the primary tool in most steampunk projects, sufficient to create the aesthetic

## Techniques and Methods
- Visual research via internet image search to gather style references
- Distilling a large set of reference images down to the minimal defining elements of a style
- Caricature-like design: selecting only the features that most strongly communicate the intended aesthetic
- Applying a chosen style as an overlay/aesthetic onto a functional object (e.g., a lawn mower)
