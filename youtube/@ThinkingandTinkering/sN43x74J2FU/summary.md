## Overview
Robert tests whether homemade biodiesel (made in a previous video, #1672) can be used as a fuel in a standard primer-type kerosene/paraffin stove. After a lengthy startup process of 3–4 minutes instead of the stated 30 seconds, the biodiesel successfully vaporizes and burns, demonstrating that with patience it can work in existing kerosene equipment. The main takeaway is that biodiesel's higher viscosity requires significantly more heat to vaporize, but once it does, the self-sustaining combustion works well.

## Key Topics
- Compatibility of homemade biodiesel with existing kerosene/paraffin stoves and lamps
- The vaporization challenge posed by heavier fuels (vegetable oils, diesel, motor oil) vs. kerosene
- The primer/pressure stove mechanism and how it heats and vaporizes fuel
- Soot and incomplete combustion (yellow, sooty flame) due to insufficient oxygen
- Future idea: building a rocket stove burner capable of running on biodiesel, vegetable oil, or motor oil

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Biodiesel** — homemade, tested as the primary fuel in the stove
- **Kerosene / paraffin** — the fuel the stove was designed for; used as a reference
- **Bioethanol** — used as the priming fuel to initially heat the stove's vaporizing pipe
- **Vegetable oil** — mentioned as a comparable heavy fuel that faces the same vaporization challenge
- **Motor oil** — mentioned as another heavy fuel that could potentially be burned with sufficient heat

## Techniques and Methods
- **Primer/pressure stove operation** — pumping up pressure, using a priming cup with bioethanol to pre-heat the vaporizing pipe before introducing the main fuel
- **Fuel vaporization** — heating heavy fuel until it vaporizes to sustain combustion; identified as the critical step for non-standard fuels
- **Visual combustion assessment** — observing flame colour (yellow/sooty vs. clean) and bowl glow to evaluate burn quality

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Viscosity and wicking**: Robert notes biodiesel is likely too viscous to wick in a wick-based lamp, but acknowledges it is worth trying.
- **Sooting and clogging**: He confirms the sooty flame observed is a real concern and believes improvements to the burner design would be needed.
- **Plastic cup mishap**: He admits he melted the small plastic priming cup on the stove — he was too focused on the main stove to notice, crediting a commenter for spotting it.
- **Rushed experiment**: Acknowledges he rushed the test for the video and that a commenter pointing out procedural issues was probably right.
- **Used cooking oil supply**: Mentions his neighbour sells him used oil for 5p per litre.
- **Babington burner / drip cup**: Suggests these as alternative arrangements for burning viscous fuels more safely.
- **Fractional distillation**: Notes that separating fuel components properly would require a fractional distillation column.
- **General tone**: Characteristically casual and community-engaged; pushes back firmly on one critic, telling them to make their own video rather than complain.
