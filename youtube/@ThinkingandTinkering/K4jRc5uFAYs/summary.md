## Overview
Robert uses the story of Rolls-Royce rear axles lasting longer due to mirror-polished surfaces to introduce the underlying principle: smoother surfaces reduce stress crack formation and friction. He then applies this insight to 3D printed bevel gears, sharing a technique suggested by a commenter named Mark — using T-Cut automotive polish to lap gear teeth in place, dramatically reducing noise and stiffness.

## Key Topics
- Why Rolls-Royce rear axles outlast standard axles (mirror finishing and stress crack theory)
- Surface roughness at a microscopic level and how it causes mechanical failure
- Bevel gear mechanism producing two counter-rotating outputs from a single input
- Application of bevel gear design to axial flux generators
- Community-sourced technique: using T-Cut abrasive compound to lap 3D printed gear teeth in situ
- The broader applicability of in-situ lapping to both printed and machined gears

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **T-Cut** — automotive cutting/polishing compound used as a lapping agent on 3D printed gear teeth
- **Silicone oil** — applied after lapping to lubricate the gears
- **Water** — used to wash off T-Cut residue after lapping
- **Toothpaste (powder form)** — suggested by Robert in comments as a possible alternative abrasive
- **Cerium oxide** — mentioned by Robert in comments as another candidate lapping compound
- **Sand** — suggested by a community member for tumble polishing (Robert was less enthusiastic)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mirror finishing** — polishing metal axle surfaces to eliminate stress-concentration points and extend fatigue life
- **In-situ gear lapping** — applying T-Cut to assembled 3D printed gear teeth, spinning the mechanism to self-polish mating surfaces, then washing clean
- **Post-lapping lubrication** — applying silicone oil after the lapping and washing step
- **Counter-rotating bevel gear arrangement** — using a central bevel gear to drive two outputs in opposite directions simultaneously

## Robert's Own Replies
- Robert suggested **toothpaste powder** (not gel/tube) and **cerium oxide** as potentially interesting alternative lapping compounds to T-Cut, inviting the community to test and report back.
- He was open to the tumble-polishing-with-sand idea but preferred something **less aggressive** than sand.
- He repeatedly emphasised that 3D printing is mainly a **prototyping/ideation tool** for him — he wouldn't necessarily put a printed gear into a finished machine, but the lapping trick is still useful at the development stage.
- He credited Mark warmly and explicitly, noting he made the video specifically so Mark's comment wouldn't get **buried and lost** in a large comment thread.
- He encouraged multiple community members to try various alternative abrasives and share their results, positioning the video as a **starting point for further experimentation**.
