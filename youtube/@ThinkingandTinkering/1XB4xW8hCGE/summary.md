## Overview
Robert converts a low thermal difference Stirling engine (purchased from Amazon, previously featured in video 1616) into a small electrical generator by attaching magnets around the rim and positioning a solenoid coil nearby. He demonstrates the principle by generating ~0.44V from a hot cup of coffee and successfully lighting an LED using a tea light candle, then reflects on why Stirling engines remain underused in home energy applications.

## Key Topics
- Follow-up to video 1616: converting a Stirling engine from demonstrator to generator
- Rim-mounted magnet arrangement for achieving higher rotational speed at the coil
- Three factors governing electricity generation: magnetic field strength, speed of changing magnetic field, and number of coil turns
- Circuit design: coil → rectifier → capacitor → LED
- Voltage output comparison: coffee cup (~0.44V) vs. tea light (~0.7V)
- Why Stirling engines are not widely adopted for domestic use (maintenance, "love affair with solid state")
- Potential for scaling the design up

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Low thermal difference Stirling engine (Amazon product)
- Permanent magnets (rim-mounted, north/south alternating)
- Solenoid coil (used as the generator coil)
- Rectifier (bridge rectifier implied)
- Capacitor
- LED
- Hot coffee / hot water (heat source)
- Tea light candle (higher-temperature heat source)

## Techniques and Methods
- Attaching magnets around the outer rim of the Stirling engine's rotating disc to maximize peripheral speed
- Positioning a solenoid coil adjacent to the rim magnets to form a generator
- Rectifying the AC output before storing in a capacitor
- Measuring output voltage directly across the capacitor
- Using a tea light on magnet "legs" to elevate and stabilise it under the engine

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Power limit clarification**: The ultimate power source is the temperature difference between the coffee and ambient air — the engine can extract at most ~37% of that, as it is a heat engine (Carnot-limited).
- **Voltage figures confirmed**: ~0.7V with the tea light, ~0.4V with the coffee cup.
- **Rotating coil vs. rotating magnet**: It doesn't matter which moves — the system only "sees" a rotating magnetic field; the choice is made purely for ease of construction.
- **Joule thief**: Mentioned in reply to a comment, suggesting it as a way to use the low voltage output more effectively.
- **Scaling up**: He confirms it is straightforward in concept — the main additional requirement is a counterweight on the displacer.
- **Heat pipes**: He notes he was already thinking about heat pipes in conjunction with Stirling engines.
- **Related video**: Points viewers to video 1612 for further context.
- **Commercial availability**: Finds suitable units expensive (cheapest found was ~US$45) and hard to source as consumer products.
- **Encouragement**: Repeatedly encourages viewers not to wait for him — to try building and scaling their own versions and share results.
