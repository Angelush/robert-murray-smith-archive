## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a step-by-step tutorial on building a Tesla turbine configured as an air-powered generator, using everyday materials centered around DVD discs. He walks through each fabrication stage from cutting the acrylic drive plate to assembling the disc pack, adding generator components (magnets, ferrite rods, and coils), and housing everything in a repurposed DVD spindle case. The video concludes with a live spin test using a hand pump to demonstrate the device in motion.

## Key Topics
- Purpose and context: response to viewer requests for a clear, step-by-step Tesla generator tutorial
- Tesla turbine disc-pack construction using DVD discs
- "Phoenix design" — Tesla's concept of outer-ring features to improve air capture and exhaust
- Acrylic drive plate fabrication and bearing hub construction
- Generator vs. turbine distinction: this build includes magnets, ferrite rods, and coils for electricity generation
- Repurposing the DVD spindle case as the device housing
- Initial spin test with a hand pump

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **DVD writable/readable discs** — 25-pack used as the turbine disc stack; spindle case repurposed as the housing
- **4 mm acrylic sheet** (greenhouse-grade, from local store) — used to cut the drive plate and bearing hub layers
- **M4 washers** (1000 for ~£3) — used as both spacers and locators between discs
- **Bolts and spacers** — used to assemble the disc pack and outer Phoenix-design ring features
- **Acrylic/cyano glue** ("siren acrylic glue") — bonds stacked acrylic discs for the bearing hub; stated to be stronger than the plastic itself
- **Bearings** — pressed into the drilled acrylic hub block to support the axle
- **Permanent magnets** — fixed into the back of the drive plate for generator action
- **Ferrite rods** — positioned close to the magnets through the stator plates to improve magnetic pickup
- **Coils** — wound onto the ferrite rods; form the generating element

## Techniques and Methods
- **Template drilling**: a 12-line radial template printed in Word is used to mark and align hole positions on both discs and acrylic plates
- **Stacking and gluing acrylic discs**: five 4 mm layers are glued with acrylic adhesive to form a ~20 mm bearing hub block, which is then drilled out to accept bearings
- **Countersinking**: backing plate bolts are countersunk to ensure a flat rotor surface
- **Simultaneous plate drilling**: two stator support plates are clamped and drilled together at 6 mm to guarantee alignment of axle and exhaust/coil holes
- **Exhaust port construction**: cut and built up directly in the acrylic backing plate
- **Disc-pack assembly**: DVDs are stacked onto the drive plate using M4 washers as spacers and located with screws through matched holes
- **Case fabrication**: a section is sawn from the DVD spindle case and fitted with seals to form the turbine casing with a directed air slot
- **Spin testing**: hand-operated air pump used for initial no-load spin verification

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Managing expectations on home power use**: Robert explicitly cautions that connecting the device to a house is not imminent — control electronics would be needed, there is no guarantee it would work, and it is presented as "just an interesting idea" with significant development work remaining.
- **How it generates electricity**: He describes it as functionally equivalent to a wind turbine in terms of how output would be harvested.
- **Spiral-cutting the DVDs**: When asked, he suggests that cutting the discs in a spiral aligned with the direction of airflow could be beneficial.
- **Channel etiquette**: Robert warns that rudeness in comments results in removal and a ban, and he encourages viewers to try making their own versions before criticising his methods.
