## Overview
This video is a community competition entry submitted by Alen Grignon in response to a challenge announced by Robert Murray-Smith to improve his "rain fuel cell" design. After extensive experimentation with various triboelectric cell configurations failed to light an LED, Alen pivoted to building a practical water motor from salvaged parts — a hoverboard motor and a microwave hood fan — which successfully generated approximately 2 watts of electricity. The video documents both the journey of failure and the pragmatic solution that ultimately worked.

## Key Topics
- Robert Murray-Smith's rain fuel cell competition
- Triboelectric series and its application to energy harvesting cell design
- Iterative experimentation with rain fuel cell variants
- A patent-pending combined rain and solar cell design by Alen Grignon
- Construction of a water-jet motor from salvaged components
- Practical electricity generation from water flow

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Verithin (or similar conductive coating)** — used to paint portions of rain fuel cells
- **Aluminum oxide sandpaper** — used as a triboelectric material in a custom cell
- **Silicon** — paired with aluminum oxide sandpaper in a cell variant
- **Hoverboard motor** — repurposed as a generator
- **Microwave hood fan assembly** — used as the turbine/blade component

## Techniques and Methods
- Replication of Robert Murray-Smith's original rain fuel cell design as a baseline
- Iterative cell modification: adding electrodes, varying painted surface area with conductive coating
- Materials selection using the triboelectric series to identify candidate cell materials
- Assembly of a water-jet turbine generator by coupling a fan blade assembly to a BLDC motor
- Directing a water jet across turbine blades to drive the motor and generate AC/DC output
