## Overview

Robert demonstrates the use of Rustin's G-Shield graphite paint (a commercially available conductive coating) as a low-cost material for building thermoelectric generators (TEGs). He explains the Seebeck effect — the principle behind TEGs — and constructs a simple aluminium-graphite thermocouple by painting a strip of G-Shield on card and attaching aluminium foil, successfully generating a measurable millivolt output from a hair dryer's heat. The key takeaway is that cheap, printable graphite-based TEGs could become a viable route to low-cost electricity generation.
