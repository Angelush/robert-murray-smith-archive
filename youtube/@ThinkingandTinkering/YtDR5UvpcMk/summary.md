## Overview
Robert demonstrates the use of Rustin's G-Shield graphite paint (a commercially available conductive coating) as a low-cost material for building thermoelectric generators (TEGs). He explains the Seebeck effect — the principle behind TEGs — and constructs a simple aluminium-graphite thermocouple by painting a strip of G-Shield on card and attaching aluminium foil, successfully generating a measurable millivolt output from a hair dryer's heat. The key takeaway is that cheap, printable graphite-based TEGs could become a viable route to low-cost electricity generation.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Rustin's G-Shield graphite conductive paint (10 Ω/sq at 100 µm)
- Thermoelectric generators (TEGs) and how they work
- The Seebeck effect and its discovery by Thomas Johann Seebeck
- Material options for thermocouples (bismuth telluride, lead telluride, graphite-based pairs)
- Applications of TEGs: space probes, remote power, waste heat recovery
- Advantages of TEGs: no moving parts, low maintenance, long lifespan
- Challenges: low conversion efficiency and high material cost for premium TEGs
- Live demonstration of a graphite/aluminium thermocouple generating voltage

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Rustin's G-Shield graphite paint** — conductive coating, 10 Ω/sq at 100 µm, used as the graphite electrode
- **Graphite** — described as naturally exfoliated in G-Shield; the primary conductive material
- **Aluminium foil** — used as the second conductor in the thermocouple junction
- **Bismuth telluride** — mentioned as a common high-efficiency TEG material (not used here)
- **Lead telluride** — mentioned as another conventional TEG semiconductor (not used here)
- **Zinc** — noted as a candidate alternative to aluminium for the foil electrode
- **Nickel** — noted as another candidate foil material for future experiments
- **Bronze, copper** — mentioned as possible graphite pairing materials

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting a strip of graphite conductive ink onto card to form one leg of a thermocouple
- Attaching aluminium foil to form the second leg of the thermocouple
- Connecting the junction to a voltmeter and applying heat via a hair dryer to demonstrate the Seebeck effect
- Qualitative assessment of output: observing voltage rise and fall with heat application and removal
