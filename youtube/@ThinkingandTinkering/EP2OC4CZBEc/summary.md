## Overview
This video traces the largely forgotten history of solar thermal energy, from early pioneers in the 19th century through the post-WWII housing boom in America. Robert argues that solar thermal was a viable, widely-adopted technology in the 1950s — with roughly 80% of Florida homes using it — until electrical utility companies (GE, Westinghouse) actively suppressed it through advertising campaigns and backroom deals with mass homebuilder William Levitt. The core takeaway is that today's renewed interest in solar is a rediscovery of something that was deliberately killed for commercial reasons.

## Key Topics
- The history of solar thermal energy vs. photovoltaic solar
- Early energy crises and deforestation during the Industrial Revolution
- Frank Shuman's parabolic mirror solar engine (1913, Egypt)
- Augustine Mouchot's French Academy-funded solar generator (1871)
- William Levitt and the mass production of post-WWII suburban housing
- Maria Telkes and her solar thermal research at MIT
- The electric utility industry's campaign to suppress solar thermal
- Current energy infrastructure pressures and the renewed case for solar

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glauber's salt (sodium sulfate)** — used by Maria Telkes as a phase-change material in solar thermal heat stores; melted by hot air to store solar energy
- **Wire-reinforced safety glass** — Shuman's commercial invention that funded his solar work
- **Coal** — dominant energy source of the Industrial Revolution, associated with severe pollution

## Techniques and Methods
- **Parabolic mirror solar concentration** — Shuman's arrangement of parabolic mirrors around pipes to boil water and generate low-pressure steam for a "sun engine"
- **Solar thermal heat storage via phase-change materials** — Telkes's method of using Glauber's salt to store heat energy released by molten-to-solid phase transition
- **Passive solar heating** — hot air blown over phase-change salt medium to capture and store solar energy for domestic use
- **Solar distillation** — portable solar distillation packs developed by Telkes, issued to airmen in WWII
- **Mass-production housing construction** — Levitt's 26-step modular build process (relevant context for how solar was locked out of new housing stock)
