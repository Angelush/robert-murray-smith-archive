## Overview
In this video (part of a series), Robert Murray-Smith takes the DIY push wheelchair he built in video 1198 — constructed from a car seat and shopping trolley — and motorises it using a salvaged e-scooter drive system. The result is somewhere between a powered wheelchair and a "crazy cart," and Robert tests it with a friend, finding it fun but tricky to control. He closes by explaining how a simple modification (welding the rear casters) could turn it into either a more conventional motorised wheelchair or a full crazy cart.

## Key Topics
- Converting a DIY push wheelchair into a motorised vehicle
- Reusing a salvaged e-scooter drive system (motor, wheel, belt drive, controller)
- Tiller steering design using chopped bicycle forks
- Twist throttle and plug-and-play motor controller wiring
- Handling characteristics and control challenges
- The design decision: motorised wheelchair vs. crazy cart
- Fixing rear casters via spot weld to improve straight-line tracking

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Car seat (from the previous build)
- Shopping trolley frame (from the previous build)
- E-scooter drive system (motor, wheel, toothed belt drive — ~120W DC motor)
- Bicycle forks and steering column (chopped and repurposed for tiller steering)
- E-scooter motor controller
- Batteries (mounted in a tray under the seat)
- Twist throttle (from the scooter)
- Retaining/returning pin (for the tiller arm)

## Techniques and Methods
- Metal cutting and welding (fabricating the steering column mount, attaching fork remains)
- Tiller steering fabrication from recycled bicycle components
- Repurposing an e-scooter drive train as a single steerable front drive unit
- Plug-and-play motor controller wiring (following labelled connectors on the controller)
- Battery tray integration under the seat
- Proposed caster modification: spot-welding rear casters to lock them in a straight line for better control

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Control fix:** Robert clarifies that he deliberately didn't want tight control, and the intended solution is to weld the rear casters so they trail in a single track — a simple, low-cost fix.
- **Motor specs and cost:** The motor is a 120W DC motor with a toothed belt drive; he estimates the entire drivetrain (motor, wheel, controller) could be sourced from an online scooter store for around £40.
- **Philosophy on disability aids:** Robert pushes back on a commenter who criticised the lack of robustness, saying people with disabilities are people too and deserve fun — a simple lever to lock the rear wheels would give the user the choice between "fun mode" and "normal wheelchair mode."
- **Mark II teaser:** He repeatedly hints that a more powerful Mark II version is coming.
- **General tone:** Most replies are brief and jovial, reinforcing that the point of the video is fun and accessible DIY making, not engineering perfection.
