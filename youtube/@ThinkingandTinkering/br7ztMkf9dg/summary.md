## Overview
This short update video documents Robert Murray-Smith's experiment with diamagnetic graphite sourced in Sri Lanka, specifically addressing a practical problem: the material arrives as awkward chips. He demonstrates that grinding the chips in a food blender with water, drying, and pressing the result into a flat disc preserves the diamagnetic property, with the compressed chip floating visibly above magnets.

## Key Topics
- Diamagnetic graphite and its levitation behaviour
- Processing raw graphite chips into a more usable flat form
- Confirming that mechanical grinding does not destroy diamagnetic properties
- Practical tips for hobbyists struggling with oddly shaped chips

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Diamagnetic graphite** — the subject material, sourced in Sri Lanka, discussed in chip and compressed disc form
- **Water** — used as a medium during blending to help consolidate the ground graphite

## Techniques and Methods
- Grinding graphite chips in a household food blender with water to produce a fine slurry
- Drying the slurry and pressing it with a spoon inside folded paper to form a flat compressed disc
- Levitation testing over permanent magnets to verify retained diamagnetic properties
