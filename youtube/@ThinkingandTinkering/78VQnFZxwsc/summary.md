## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores whether the flat disc geometry of a traditional Tesla turbine is necessary, proposing a variant using stacked cones instead of discs for wind power applications. He 3D prints a cone-based prototype and tests it with a hair dryer, confirming that the boundary-layer friction principle still works provided airflow enters at an angle. He concludes that geometry is flexible as long as laminar flow layers with small separations are maintained, and hints at hyperbolic funnels as a further refinement.

## Key Topics
- Explanation of how a Tesla turbine works (bladeless, friction-driven, disc stack)
- The "Solar Wind Turbine" as an existing Tesla-turbine-based wind device
- Proposal to replace flat discs with stacked cones for a unidirectional wind turbine
- 3D printed cone-stack prototype design and assembly
- Importance of angle of attack — airflow must enter at an angle, not straight on
- Hair dryer testing of the cone prototype
- Discussion of hyperbolic funnels as a potential geometry improvement
- Need for torque measurement (Prony brake) to evaluate power output
- Applicability to water as well as air
