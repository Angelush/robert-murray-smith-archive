## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores small-scale hydroelectric generation by replicating a miniature version of the Turbulent Hydro vortex turbine — a Belgian design claimed to be environmentally friendly, fish-safe, and capable of powering ~300 homes from a low water fall. He builds and tests two prototypes combining an involute water channel with his previously designed "Rose" turbine blade, measuring output voltage as a proxy for performance, and concludes the approach is promising and worthy of further community experimentation.

## Key Topics
- The Turbulent Hydro Belgian vortex turbine as inspiration
- The "Rose" turbine — a prior wind turbine design adapted for water use
- Involute/scroll channel design to create a water vortex
- Modifying the Rose turbine to eliminate the flat back plane (replacing it with a full cone)
- Testing the modified Rose turbine in wind before water trials
- Testing vortex water flow through the involute with the Rose attached
- Experimenting with hole position (top dead center vs. offset) to vary water entry point
- Using voltage as an internal relative measure of turbine performance
- Pelton wheel-style jet-on-blade as a possible alternative approach
- Wave energy as a speculative future test
- Encouraging community participation in the project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Perspex (acrylic sheet)** — used as the base for the first prototype housing
- **Aluminium sheet** — cut and shaped into the first involute/scroll
- **Hot glue** — used to attach the involute to the base
- **Foamboard (builder's board)** — used for the second involute housing
- **Plastic strip** — bent into the involute spiral for the second version
- **PC cooling fan** — repurposed as a generator/turbine rotor base

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling and screwing a fan blade assembly to a Perspex disc
- Cutting and bending aluminium into an involute (scroll/spiral) channel shape
- Hot-glue bonding of components
- Cutting rose-petal turbine blades and wrapping them around a cone to eliminate the flat back plane
- Snap-removing fan blades and gluing the fan hub as a central mount for the Rose assembly
- Hair dryer and wind testing to verify spin before water trials
- Gravity-fed water reservoir testing through the involute channel
- Repositioning the outlet hole (top-center vs. offset) to test vortex entry angles
- Voltage measurement as a relative performance indicator for turbine speed
