## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a DIY omnidirectional vibrating speaker from a salvaged speaker and a few pennies' worth of materials, as an alternative to commercial products like "Vibe" that sold for £40–50. By removing the speaker cone and attaching the voice coil to a flat plate or surface, the vibrating assembly turns any surface — a desktop, glass panel, or acoustic ceiling tile — into the speaker itself. The core takeaway is that the commercial versions are wildly overpriced for what is a trivially simple build.

## Key Topics
- What omnidirectional/vibration speakers are and how they work
- Disassembly of a standard speaker to expose the voice coil
- Building a vibration speaker from scrap components
- Testing on different surfaces (desktop, glass laptop screen, acoustic ceiling tile)
- Comparison to overpriced commercial products (e.g., "Vibe", ~£40–50)
- Credit to Tech Ingredients for the acoustic ceiling tile idea

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Salvaged/scrap speaker (voice coil, diaphragm/spring, magnet, housing)
- 20 mm plastic pipe (matched to the 20 mm voice coil diameter)
- Corriboard / builder's board (flat plate base)
- Superglue (for bonding pipe to board and voice coil)
- Glass (laptop screen, used as a test surface)
- Acoustic ceiling tiles (noted as excellent flat-panel speaker surfaces)

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing the rubber surround, paper cone, and central dust cap from a salvaged speaker to expose the voice coil
- Measuring the voice coil diameter (20 mm) to select a matching pipe
- Gluing pipe to flat board, then bonding the assembly rigidly to the voice coil so mechanical motion transfers directly to the plate
- Placing the flat plate against various surfaces to test acoustic transmission
- Using the surface itself as the speaker diaphragm (exciter/transducer principle)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Commercial overpricing analogy:** Robert draws a parallel to Apple once selling a powerless plastic horn amplifier for ~$50, comparing it to people who've never seen a gramophone — a wry dig at premium pricing for simple technology.
- **Road-mounted transducer idea:** Robert expresses genuine interest in a commenter's suggestion about embedding these in roads — specifically excited by the reverse concept: if vibration produces sound, could road resonance produce electricity? He frames it as a "road-mountable transducer" and says it aligns with his core interest in *generation* (energy harvesting) rather than just audio.
- **Surface ideas for other projects:** He mentions seed trays, sprouting mats, and heating plates as useful surfaces for applying vibration/ink in related projects, and references a prior "sound lens" video.
- **Audio quality disputes:** Several commenters reported sound issues; Robert responds that he didn't experience them and gently pushes back on criticism of his build method, noting it "clearly works just fine."
- **Speaker aesthetics:** Robert agrees with a commenter that conventional speakers are ugly, suggesting he finds flat-panel and surface-exciter designs more elegant.
