## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make zinc iodide solution, the electrolyte for a simple DIY battery he introduced in video 1793. He covers both a quick informal method (no measuring required) and a precise stoichiometric approach for those who need a known concentration. The main takeaway is that this battery chemistry is remarkably accessible — scalable to any size and solvable for self-discharge by simply removing the zinc electrode when not in use.

## Key Topics
- Quick, unmeasured synthesis of zinc iodide from zinc powder, iodine, and water
- Stoichiometric calculation for making a precise 1 molar zinc iodide solution
- Visual cues during the reaction (purple → clear, frothing)
- Scaling the battery to any size using a bucket and rod electrodes
- Solving self-discharge by removing the zinc electrode from solution
- Sourcing zinc (boat sacrificial anodes) and iodine (iodine pearls)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zinc powder** — the reducing agent and negative electrode material
- **Iodine (pearls)** — the oxidant; a halogen, noted as a skin irritant/stain
- **Water** — the solvent for the electrolyte
- **Zinc iodide (ZnI₂)** — the resulting electrolyte, molecular mass 319 g/mol

## Techniques and Methods
- Direct mixing of zinc powder, iodine, and water with no measurement (informal method)
- Stoichiometric batching: 65 g zinc + 254 g iodine per mole of ZnI₂; scaled down (6.5 g Zn + 25.4 g I₂ in 100 mL) for a 1 M solution
- Adding a slight excess of zinc to ensure all iodine reacts, confirmed by the solution turning clear
- Removing the zinc electrode from solution to halt self-discharge during storage
