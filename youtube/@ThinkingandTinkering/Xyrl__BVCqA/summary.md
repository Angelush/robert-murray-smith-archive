## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to create a homemade plastic by combining PVA glue (a mix of polyvinyl acetate and polyvinyl alcohol), borax solution, glycerol as a plasticizer, and an inert filler such as talcum powder. The borax cross-links the polymer chains, producing a moldable putty that hardens into a tough, white plastic as it dries. The video is an exploratory experiment inspired by a friend's question about magnetic silly putty, showing that different fillers yield dramatically different material properties.

## Key Topics
- The chemistry of PVA: polyvinyl acetate vs. polyvinyl alcohol and how commercial PVA is typically a mix of both
- How borax cross-links PVA to form slime or putty depending on concentration
- The role of a three-component plastic system: polymer, plasticizer, and filler
- Controlling hardness by varying borax concentration and PVA ratio
- Comparison of different fillers (talcum powder, sand, clay) and their effects on the final material
- Properties of the dried product: toughness, low shrinkage, moldability, and limited water resistance

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVA (polyvinyl acetate / polyvinyl alcohol)** — the polymer base; commercial white builders' glue used as-is
- **Borax** — cross-linking agent; used as a 4% aqueous solution (4 g borax per 96 g water)
- **Water** — used to dilute PVA to a 50/50 mix and to prepare the borax solution
- **Glycerol (glycerine)** — plasticizer; available in supermarket home-baking sections
- **Talcum powder (Johnson's baby talc)** — inert filler used in the main demonstration
- **Sand** — alternative filler; produces a very hard, imitation-rock-like material
- **Clay** — alternative filler; causes significant shrinkage on drying
- **Magnetite** — mentioned as a filler used in a prior experiment to make magnetic silly putty

## Techniques and Methods
- Diluting commercial PVA 50/50 with water to create a workable base
- Adding glycerol as a plasticizer before cross-linking
- Incorporating dry filler (talcum powder) into the PVA mix *before* adding borax, since the material becomes too stiff to accept filler after cross-linking begins
- Adding 4% borax solution drop-by-drop while stirring vigorously to control the cross-linking reaction
- Allowing the moldable putty to air-dry into a hard plastic
- Casting and molding the wet material into desired shapes before drying

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Water content is the key variable**: if results are too wet or too sloppy, the PVA is too dilute — switch to concentrated builders' PVA or dry it out before use.
- **US sourcing tip**: Elmer's white wood glue is the equivalent of UK builders' PVA; larger gallon tubs are available cheaply at Home Depot, Target, or Walmart.
- **Filler choice is open-ended**: almost anything works — sand, clay, talc, fibers, metal powders, cement, plaster. Each filler changes the properties of the finished material significantly.
- **Machinability depends on filler**: talc-filled versions sand and machine well; sand-filled versions are very poor for machining.
- **Waterproofing**: painting with varnish or clear coat is the practical solution; dedicated waterproofing additives exist but are expensive and hard to source.
- **Colorants**: Robert confirms colorants can be added to the mix.
- **Not waterproof**: when wetted, the dried plastic re-wets, swells, and eventually breaks apart.
- **Borax/PVA safety**: LD50 is in the kilogram range; both ingredients are low-toxicity (borax used in laundry, PVA is white glue) — checking the MSDS via Google is recommended if concerned.
- **This is a base for experimentation, not a fixed recipe**: Robert repeatedly encourages viewers to try different ratios and fillers and share their results.
- **Related chemistry tip (UV cure)**: Robert mentions experimenting with gelatin + ammonium ferric citrate (green, food-additive grade) as a UV-curable alternative.
- **Graphene oxide tangent**: several replies reveal Robert was concurrently trying to incorporate graphene oxide into materials but was stuck on dispersing it in water, lacking a sonicator.
