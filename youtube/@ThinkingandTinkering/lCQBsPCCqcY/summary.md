## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith walks through the construction of a basic laboratory kiln suitable for temperatures up to 1200°C, explaining the three core components: the kiln body, the electronics, and the outer enclosure. The video focuses on building the brick-and-ceramic-blanket body assembly, emphasising that using properly rated commercial materials (rather than DIY mixes like perlite or vermiculite) eliminates long drying times and guesswork. The main takeaway is that a functional, properly-rated lab kiln can be built cheaply and easily using fire bricks, ceramic fiber blanket, and high-temperature silicone putty.

## Key Topics
- The three-part structure of a kiln: body, electronics, and enclosure/case
- Kiln insulation theory — balancing heat input against heat loss
- Temperature ratings and why commercial refractory materials outperform homemade alternatives
- Cutting and shaping insulating fire bricks with ordinary hand tools
- Carving U-shaped channels in bricks to house the heating coil
- Assembling the brick body with high-temperature silicone putty
- Reinforcing the assembly with metal angle brackets and hose clips
- Wrapping the body in ceramic fiber blanket for improved insulation
- Using repurposed objects (a stainless steel bathroom cabinet) as the outer enclosure
- Preview of upcoming parts: electronics and door construction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Insulating fire bricks** (SWP140-26 grade, 230×114×64mm) — rated to 1260°C; form the structural kiln body; soft enough to cut and carve with hand tools
- **Ceramic fiber blanket** — rated to 1400°C; wrapped around the brick body as the primary insulation layer
- **High-temperature silicone/silicate putty** — rated to 1200°C; used to glue brick sections together; searched online as "high temp silicate"
- **Resistive heating wire** — used to wind the heating coil; described as very cheap and available online
- **15mm copper pipe** (with angled cut end) — repurposed as a gouging tool for carving coil channels in bricks
- **Perlite, vermiculite, sand, water glass** — mentioned as common DIY insulation alternatives; Robert advises against them as they spall, melt, or splinter at high temperatures
- **Stainless steel bathroom cabinet** — repurposed as the kiln enclosure
- **Pink zircon** (mentioned in comments) — kiln floor coating to prevent sticking; does not raise the temperature rating of the kiln

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting insulating fire bricks in half lengthwise using an ordinary panel saw
- Marking and carving a 15mm-diameter U-shaped groove in side bricks using a copper pipe as a gouge (patient, gentle strokes)
- Scoring coil channels with a pad saw as an alternative to gouging
- Drilling wire passage holes through end bricks by aligning matched side bricks and marking through
- Gluing the brick assembly together with high-temperature silicone putty
- Clamping and reinforcing the glued assembly using metal angle brackets at the four corners secured with hose clips
- Wrapping the completed brick body in ceramic fiber blanket for added insulation
- Fitting the insulated body into a repurposed metal cabinet enclosure, using brackets to achieve the correct height

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Silicone putty sourcing:** It is a silicate putty; he found it by searching "high temp silicate" online.
- **Against perlite alternatives:** For a workhorse piece of kit, skipping ceramic fibre blanket in favour of perlite mixes is a false economy — "you will probably regret that decision sooner or later."
- **Higher temperature kilns:** Construction method is identical; simply buy higher-rated insulation. Exceeding 1200°C is rarely needed in his work, but it is achievable with appropriate materials.
- **Fire brick pricing:** Available on eBay from approximately £1.75 each.
- **Ceramic fiber blanket safety:** Hazard warnings apply primarily to daily, heavy occupational exposure. For occasional use, a mask and gloves are sufficient; once the blanket is sandwiched between the bricks and the outer case, there is no need to seal it further.
- **Pumice as alternative:** Will spall — not suitable.
- **Red house brick as kiln lining:** Will spall after a few firings unless thickly coated; recommends using the correct insulating fire brick, with a hard kiln shelf to spread the load.
- **Pink zircon coating:** Prevents materials sticking to the kiln floor but does not raise the temperature capability — that requires properly rated bricks.
- **Reconditioning second-hand kilns:** Strongly recommended. Robert bought a used kiln for £250 (new price £7,000) because the electrics and coils were failed; knowing kiln electronics makes reconditiong straightforward.
- **Asbestos suggestion (from a commenter):** Acknowledged some valid points but noted the cultural resistance to asbestos is too entrenched for the suggestion to be practical.
- **Using a torch to fire the kiln:** A propane torch burns at ~800°C and already damages the material; it cannot withstand repeated use at 1200°C.
