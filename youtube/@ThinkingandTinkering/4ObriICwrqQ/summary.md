## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a quick DIY low-friction bearing made from graphite-coated HDPE (high-density polyethylene), motivated by excessive friction in a small wind turbine generator he had been building. By rubbing graphite into both sides of a cut HDPE disc and placing it between rotating surfaces, he achieves surprisingly smooth, sustained rotation. The technique is presented as an accessible solution for prototyping and model-making.

## Key Topics
- Friction problem with a DIY wind turbine generator
- HDPE as a bearing material
- Graphite as a dry lubricant / surface treatment
- Practical assembly using CD cases as a test platform
- Suitability of the method for prototyping and Meccano-style models

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — sourced from document wallets, food containers, or damp-proof course material; used as the bearing disc
- **Graphite** — rubbed into both surfaces of the HDPE disc to reduce friction; forms an electrostatic bond with the plastic and goes shiny/metallic
- **CD cases and spare CDs** — used as the test platform and spacers to provide a flat, bump-free surface

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a circular disc from an HDPE document wallet
- Punching a centre hole using a centre punch (or knife) over a glass surface
- Rubbing graphite (from a graphite-and-adhesive tissue) firmly into both sides of the HDPE until it bonds, goes shiny, and no longer transfers to the finger
- Stacking CDs inside the CD case as spacers to eliminate surface bumps
- Testing rotation with a hairdryer to demonstrate sustained spin after airflow is removed
