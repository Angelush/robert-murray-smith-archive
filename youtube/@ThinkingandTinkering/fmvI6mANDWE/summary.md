## Overview
Robert salvages the squirrel cage blower motor from a scrap Nissan Micra and tests it as a rotor for a DIY flat wind generator, following a suggestion from viewers of his earlier flat wind generator series. He experiments with mounting it on a flat board and adding a concrete slump-test cone in front to increase the swept area of wind capture, getting the rotor to spin under both no-load and short-circuit (loaded) conditions. The video also addresses viewer criticism of vertical wind turbines, making a broader argument about cost-appropriateness and situational suitability over raw efficiency.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging a squirrel cage blower and pancake motor from a scrap Nissan Micra
- Testing the squirrel cage as a flat wall-mounted wind generator rotor
- Using a metal cone concentrator to increase effective swept area
- UK planning permission constraints that motivate wall-mounted (below-roofline) wind generation
- Philosophy of vertical vs. horizontal wind turbines and when each is appropriate

- Short-circuit (under-load) testing of the motor/rotor assembly

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned

- **Pancake (brushed DC) motor** — 12V, ~18W no-load, ~25W under load; also salvaged from the Nissan Micra blower assembly
- **Car heat exchanger / AC radiator** — recovered alongside the blower; noted as a potentially useful component
- **Metal slump-test cone** — normally used for concrete slump testing; repurposed as a wind concentrator/shroud to increase captured swept area
- **Large flat board ("burger board")** — used to simulate a flat wall surface during testing
- **CD disc** — referenced as a size comparison for the squirrel cage (~same diameter)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Automotive parts salvage** — removing HVAC blower assembly from a donor vehicle (three bolts, several screws/clips)
- **Flat-board wall simulation** — mounting the rotor on a large flat board to approximate real-world wall installation
- **Fan-driven wind testing** — using a large room fan as a wind source (estimated 20–40 mph airflow)
- **Cone concentrator** — placing a conical shroud in front of the rotor inlet to funnel a larger air column into the smaller rotor opening, increasing effective swept area
- **Short-circuit load test** — briefly short-circuiting the motor leads to verify the rotor can still turn under electrical load
- **Iterative low-cost prototyping** — building and testing small-scale versions before committing to a larger unit
