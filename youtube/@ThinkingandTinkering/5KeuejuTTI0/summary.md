## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a simplified replication of the O-Wind turbine concept using a cuboctahedron shape, inspired by a visit to his brother Anthony who was using the geometry for AI research. The cuboctahedron is constructed from hardboard salvaged from old whiteboards, slotted together into a Cartesian frame and fitted with skate bearings on a steel rod axle. Testing with a hair dryer demonstrates the device spins in response to airflow from any direction, validating the multi-directional wind-capture principle of the O-Wind concept.

## Key Topics
- The O-Wind turbine concept — a spherical wind generator designed for urban environments with variable wind direction
- The cuboctahedron as an accessible geometric form suitable for replicating O-Wind behaviour
- Construction of a Cartesian three-plane frame from three interlocking squares
- Determining face dimensions using the Pythagorean theorem
- Testing three different pivot/axle orientations to find the best spinning behaviour
- Multi-directional wind response demonstrated with a hair dryer

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Hardboard (plastic-covered, salvaged from old broken whiteboards) — main structural material for the cuboctahedron faces
- Fibreglass rod — used as the main axle shaft
- Aluminium tube (10 mm outside diameter) — bottom bearing housing
- 8 mm steel rod — axle inserted through the aluminium tube
- Skate bearings (8 mm) — used as the rotation bearings for the axle
- Glue — used to fix the interlocking frame and faces together

## Techniques and Methods
- Slot-together Cartesian frame construction: three squares with interlocking half-depth cuts assembled into an XYZ frame
- Pythagorean calculation to derive diagonal face dimensions (30 cm sides → 21.21 cm diagonals)
- Cutting and gluing six square faces onto the frame vertices to complete the cuboctahedron
- Pivot orientation testing: three positions trialled (centre of square face, edge vertex, centre of triangular face) to identify optimal spin axis
- Hair dryer simulation of wind for indoor testing
