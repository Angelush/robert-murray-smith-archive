## Overview
Robert discovers two squirrel cage rotor fans discarded outside a supermarket during an air conditioning unit replacement, and repurposes them to build a horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) rotor. He disassembles the shaded pole motors, cleans the aluminium squirrel cage rotors, and assembles them onto a threaded rod axle with skate bearings. The video concludes with a successful outdoor test showing the rotor spinning freely in light wind, with the key takeaway that no single wind turbine design is universally optimal — different designs suit different circumstances.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging squirrel cage rotor fans from discarded air conditioning units
- Disassembly of shaded pole motors to extract components (axle, stator, rotor)
- Building a horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) rotor from reclaimed parts
- Comparison of horizontal vs. vertical axis wind turbine designs
- Testing the rotor in real wind conditions
- Philosophy of using context-appropriate wind turbine designs rather than a single "best" solution

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium squirrel cage rotors (extracted from salvaged fans)
- 13 mm diameter bearings (skate bearings)
- 14 mm pipe (used as central spacer after drilling out to 14 mm)
- 10 mm threaded rod (used as central axle)
- Glue (used to temporarily bond rotor fins)
- Resin (mentioned as planned finishing material for balancing)
- Magnets and coils (mentioned as planned additions for power generation)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of squirrel cage fans by drilling out pop rivets and undoing bolts
- Extraction and cleaning of squirrel cage aluminium rotors
- Drilling out central spacer hole from 13 mm to 14 mm to accept pipe
- Turning axle ends to accept standard skate bearings
- Temporary assembly using threaded rod and clamping bolt
- Balancing assessment by spinning and observing wobble
- Outdoor wind testing using a anemometer (wind speed 1.1–2.6 m/s recorded)
