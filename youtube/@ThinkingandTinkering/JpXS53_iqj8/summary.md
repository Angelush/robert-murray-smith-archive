## Overview
In video 1964 of his wind turbine series, Robert demonstrates a 3D-printed stacking planetary gearbox he designed in TinkerCAD to dramatically increase the output of his DIY generator. By assembling three 3:1 planetary stages for a combined 27:1 ratio, he achieves a startling 104 volts from hand-cranking alone. The project targets a portable, collapsible wind turbine capable of outperforming a $400 commercial unit on a fraction of the budget.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the generator build from video 1963
- Design and redesign of a stacking planetary gear system (TinkerCAD)
- Gear ratio multiplication: 3:1 → 9:1 → 27:1 per added stage
- Assembly of planet carrier, sun gear, ring gear, and bearing components
- Hand-crank voltage test yielding 104 V
- Comparison to a $400 commercial portable wind turbine (~800 mW at 3.5 m/s)
- Next step: fabricating a blade adapter for the folding wind turbine

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA/plastic** — all gearbox components are 3D-printed for prototype testing
- **Metal pinions** — suggested as an upgrade for greater durability
- **Bearings (22 mm × 7 mm × 8 mm)** — fitted at the sun gear center to allow free spinning on the axle
- **Bolt and hex nut** — used to clamp the stacked planetary stages together

## Techniques and Methods
- **Stacking planetary gearing** — multiple 3:1 stages stacked axially to multiply gear ratio
- **TinkerCAD design** — full gearbox modelled and files made freely available in video description
- **3D printing** — planet carrier, sun gear, ring gear, and fixing plate all printed; ring gear done in sections to allow ratio adjustment
- **Series wiring** — generator coils connected in series to sum output voltages
- **Hand-crank voltage testing** — used as a quick proxy for wind-driven output before blade adapter is made

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On output limits:** Estimates roughly 60 W is achievable with human arm strength alone.
- **On being conservative in testing:** Acknowledges he was "being a bit over gentle" and confirms his minimum goal is simply to beat the $400 commercial turbine — which he states has already been exceeded in lower wind-speed tests.
- **On drag:** Admits the gearbox has notable drag but says this is acceptable at this stage; improving the gears (tighter tolerances, more grease, and adding bearings — notably absent in the current prototype) is the clearest path to improvement.
- **On modifiability:** Notes he designed the ring gear in sections specifically so he can easily swap in different gear ratios and iterate on the hoof.
- **On print size:** Confirms the design was intentionally sized to fit a small-format 3D printer.
- **On the commercial competition:** Reiterates the benchmark — a best-selling folding turbine rated at 40 W in a 28 mph (12.5 m/s) wind, sold for $400 — and is confident this build will match or beat it at a far lower cost.
- **On patentability:** Doesn't believe the design is patentable.
