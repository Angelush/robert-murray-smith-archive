## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores shape memory materials — starting with the well-known metal alloy nitinol and revealing that ordinary PLA 3D printing filament also exhibits shape memory behaviour. He demonstrates PLA recovering its printed shape when dipped in warm water, then discusses the potential and limitations of nitinol-based heat engines, concluding that PLA's shape memory opens exciting possibilities for low-thermal-difference engines and novel 3D printing applications.

## Key Topics
- What nitinol is: a 50/50 nickel-titanium alloy with shape memory properties
- History of nitinol: discovered at the National Ordnance Laboratory (1959), shape memory noticed in 1961
- History of shape memory alloys more broadly, dating to 1932
- The Banks Engine (1973) — first engine made using nitinol
- Shape memory in non-metals, particularly PLA plastic
- Demonstration of PLA recovering its printed shape in warm water
- The McDonald Douglas nitinol engine project (1980) and its 55 MW proof-of-concept
- Why nitinol engines haven't been widely adopted (low ~16% efficiency, economics)
- Nitinol's niche suitability for scavenging waste heat and low thermal difference applications
- Potential for 3D-printed PLA shape memory engines powered by solar heat

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Nitinol** — nickel-titanium alloy (50/50 ratio); also variants Nitinol 55 and Nitinol 60
- **Nickel** — component of nitinol
- **Titanium** — component of nitinol
- **Gold-cadmium** — early shape memory alloy (noted from 1932 research)
- **Iron and palladium** — mentioned in context of early shape memory alloys
- **Copper, aluminium, and nickel alloy** — cheaper alternative shape memory alloy
- **Brass (copper-zinc)** — noted as a recognised shape memory alloy
- **PLA (polylactic acid)** — standard 3D printer filament shown to exhibit shape memory
- **ABS (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene)** — noted as NOT having shape memory

## Techniques and Methods
- Bending PLA cold into a coil or spring shape, then immersing in warm water to trigger shape recovery
- Straightening a PLA spring by force, then recovering original shape via warm water immersion
- Nitinol spring stretching and contraction via temperature differential to drive a pulley
- Using nitinol to shift a wheel's centre of gravity (Banks Engine approach)
- Draping heat-activated PLA over a former to create compound curves (mentioned as a fabrication technique)
