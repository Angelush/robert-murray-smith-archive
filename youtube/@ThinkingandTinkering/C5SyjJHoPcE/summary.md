## Overview
Filmed at the Manston RAF Museum during a nighttime WWII recreation event, Robert uses a stunning GE J79 turbojet engine as a springboard to explore what "perfection" really means in engineering. He argues that "good enough" — defined by appropriate tolerance — is functionally identical to perfection, and illustrates this with a back-of-the-envelope calculation about storing wind energy inside street lampposts using a falling weight. His central takeaway is that chasing an idealized mental image of perfection leads to disappointment, while understanding tolerance leads to better, more practical engineering.

## Key Topics
- The concept of perfection vs. "good enough" in engineering
- The GE J79 turbojet engine as an example of beautiful, high-tolerance manufacture
- Weld quality as a real-world measure of perfection
- Engineering tolerance: definition, importance, and cost implications
- The M16 rifle as a cautionary tale of over-tight tolerances
- Gravitational potential energy storage: theoretical use of a falling weight inside a lamppost to power street lights
- 3D-printed mechanical mechanisms (mechanical rectifier, wind energy storage concept)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned

- **Steel** — proposed material for the theoretical energy-storage weight inside a lamppost (calculated as ~14 kg, later corrected to ~14,000 kg — see author replies)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Welding** — examined on the J79 engine as a benchmark of real-world "best possible" quality, complete with minor surface bubbles and grind marks
- **3D printing** — used to fabricate a mechanical rectifier and a wind-energy/weight-drop mechanism for concept demonstration
- **Gravitational energy storage** — theoretical method: wind turbine raises a heavy weight up inside a hollow lamppost; the falling weight powers an LED street light
- **Back-of-the-envelope energy calculation** — Watt-hours → joules → required mass via potential energy formula (mgh), performed live and approximately

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Math error acknowledged**: Robert admits he made a significant arithmetic mistake in the lamppost energy storage calculation. He explains his working — 195 Wh → 702,000 joules → divided by 5 m height → ~140,000 N → ~14,000 kg — meaning the actual required weight is **14,000 kg, not 14 kg**, making the concept impractical. He chose not to edit the video since it wasn't central to the main point.
- **Perfection vs. the energy crisis**: He notes the conflation of "good enough" with "not good enough unless perfect" is something he's strongly against, and that it "crosses into lots of things including the energy crisis."
- **Computer modelling vs. reality**: He expresses agreement with comments about people who model things digitally and then expect direct real-world application — a pet concern of his.
- **Battery storage context**: He notes energy losses in battery storage are around 30%, which sets a relevant baseline for comparing alternative storage methods.
- **The J79's appeal**: He pushes back on one commenter who found the engine unattractive, insisting it's "one sexy machine" while acknowledging he never had to maintain it.
