## Overview
Robert demonstrates a fast, efficient method for removing tannins from acorns using a 40% ethanol-water solution, based on a published research paper. The process takes only three hours compared to traditional methods that can take days or weeks, opening the door to using acorns — an abundant, globally-produced resource — as both food and a non-food-crop source of bioethanol.

## Key Topics
- Nutritional profile of acorns (starch, fat, protein, vitamins, minerals; classified as a superfood)
- Historical and global use of acorns as food (Native American, Korean, English traditions)
- The problem of tannins in acorns — taste, toxicity, and the burden of removal
- Traditional tannin-removal methods: cold leaching, hot leaching, lye treatment
- Ethanol-water extraction method from a research paper (40% ethanol, 60°C, 3 hours)
- Recovery and commercial value of tannin as calcium tannate
- Potential for acorns as a biofuel feedstock (fermentation → bioethanol)
- Circular process: using bioethanol produced from acorns as the extraction solvent

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acorns** (English/pedunculate oak) — the subject material, ~3–4% tannin content
- **Ethanol** — extraction solvent; 40% by volume in water
- **Methylated spirits** — source of ethanol used in the experiment (96% ethanol, 3% isopropanol, 3% methyl ethyl ketone, trace methyl violet dye; EU-regulated formulation)
- **Deionized water** — used in the 40% ethanol-water solution (300 mL water + 200 mL ethanol)
- **Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime)** — added to the tannin-rich filtrate to precipitate tannin
- **Calcium tannate** — insoluble precipitate formed; a commercially traded product
- **Calcium carbonate (limestone)** / **Calcium oxide (quicklime)** — mentioned as precursors to calcium hydroxide
- **Brewer's yeast** — for fermenting acorn starch to alcohol
- **Vodka** — suggested as a food-safe alternative solvent (40% ABV)
- **Tannic acid** — the predominant form of tannin in acorns

## Techniques and Methods
- **Shelling acorns** with a trimming knife — splitting and peeling the nut from the shell
- **Grinding** shelled acorns in a food processor/blender
- **Ethanol-water extraction**: 1:20 ratio (g acorn : mL fluid), 40% ethanol, 60°C, 3 hours with stirring
- **Vacuum/gravity filtration** to separate treated acorn meal from the tannin-containing liquid
- **Tannin precipitation**: adding calcium hydroxide to the filtrate to form insoluble calcium tannate
- **Fermentation** (described as follow-on step): acorn mash + brewer's yeast in water, body temperature, ~2 weeks → strong beer
- **Distillation** (described conceptually): fermenting the beer to produce bioethanol

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Tannin kills yeast**: Confirmed that tannin must be removed before fermentation — acorns produce high tannin as a defence against insects and yeasts; the tannin is predominantly tannic acid, which is why adding an alkaline material (calcium hydroxide) is effective.
- **Ethanol solvent reuse**: Emphasised that the ethanol can be recovered and reused, making the process circular.
- **Methylated spirits vs. methanol**: Clarified that UK methylated spirits follows EU regulation (ethanol + IPA + MEK), and is *not* the more toxic methanol-based US formulation. Still recommends vodka for food use.
- **40% ethanol is optimal**: Confirmed the research specifies 40% as the ideal concentration.
- **Species matters**: Notes that different oak species have different tannin levels; English oak is "a bit bitter — not as bad as red oak." One extraction pass was sufficient in his test, though he suspects species is the key variable.
- **Acorn oil**: Acknowledged interest from commenters; suggested pressing acorns first to extract oil before the ethanol process.
- **Commercial viability**: Pointed out that acorn processing is already done commercially in China and Korea; tannin itself is a recovered commodity increasingly used as a formaldehyde substitute in urea-formaldehyde glues and plastics.
- **Horse chestnut vs. sweet chestnut**: Expressed interest in exploring horse chestnut as a comparison after a commenter raised it.
- **Leftover mash**: Agreed that the post-fermentation mash would make a good animal feedstock.
