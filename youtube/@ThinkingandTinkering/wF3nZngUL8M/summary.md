## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple homemade battery built from five US copper pennies, aluminum foil, and vinegar-and-salt potato chips as the electrolyte. Each cell produces nearly half a volt, and stacking five cells yields around 2.4V — enough to light a white or red LED. The project uses only cheap household materials and serves as a fun entry into a competition around low-cost battery construction.

## Key Topics
- Building a voltaic pile (stacked cell battery) from everyday materials
- Using potato chips as a combined electrolyte/separator
- Achieving usable voltage (2.4V) from five stacked cells
- Lighting LEDs with the resulting battery

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper pennies (US, older)** — used as the positive electrode in each cell
- **Aluminum tape (furnace ductwork type)** — used as the negative electrode, wrapped around plastic spacers
- **Vinegar and salt potato chips** — serve as the electrolyte/separator (soaked in water to soften)
- **Orange plastic cut from a vinegar jug** — used as the substrate for the aluminum foil spacers
- **Copper strip** — used as a negative contact at the bottom of the stack
- **White and red LEDs** — used to demonstrate the battery output
- **Paper towel** — mentioned as an alternative separator with straight vinegar

## Techniques and Methods
- Soaking potato chips in water to soften them for cutting
- Using a hole punch to cut uniform discs from softened chips
- Wrapping aluminum tape around plastic squares so it contacts both faces (forming a bipolar plate)
- Cleaning penny surfaces to remove corrosion before assembly
- Stacking cells in series: aluminum/plastic → chip disc → penny, repeated five times
- Measuring voltage with a multimeter across the stack
