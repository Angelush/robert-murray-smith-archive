## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores whether solar tracking systems are genuinely worth the cost, examining both traditional and emerging low-cost tracking technologies. He concludes that the answer depends on individual circumstances, but highlights exciting innovations — particularly bellows-driven and soft robotic actuators — that could make solar tracking far more economical and practical.

## Key Topics
- How solar energy is composed of direct, diffuse, and reflected light, and why direct light matters for tracking
- Single-axis vs. dual-axis solar tracking, and the trade-offs in complexity, cost, and energy gain
- Why tracking gains (~25% for single-axis) are offset by the ~25% cost of tracking systems
- The drop in solar panel prices and how it changes the economics of tracking
- Sun Folding's bellows-driven pneumatic solar tracker as a low-maintenance commercial solution
- Liam Doran's soft robotic actuator inspired by heliotropism (sunflower-like sun-following)
- A passive heliotropism concept (attributed to Nighthawk / NASA) using solar cell power to drive a motor
- How tracking reduces battery bank requirements for off-grid systems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ethanol** — mixed with carbon and silicon to create the soft robotic actuator material
- **Carbon** — component of Liam Doran's heliotropic material
- **Silicon** — component of Liam Doran's heliotropic material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Single-axis solar tracking** — panel rotates to follow the sun's daily east–west arc; yields ~25% more power
- **Dual-axis solar tracking** — adds seasonal north–south adjustment (±23°); yields only ~2–3% additional gain over single-axis
- **Bellows/pneumatic actuation (Sun Folding)** — uses bellows bladders to move panels; mechanically simple, low maintenance, low cost
- **Soft robotic actuation (Liam Doran)** — ethanol/carbon/silicon composite swells when heated by sunlight, passively tracking the sun with no electronics
- **Passive heliotropism via solar-powered motor** — solar cell generates enough power to drive a motor when sunlight hits, requiring no external power source
