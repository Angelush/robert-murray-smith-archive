## Overview
Robert examines the WWII-era GN44 (GM44) hand-cranked military field generator, explaining how its alternator, commutators, and electromechanical voltage regulator worked together to produce dual voltage outputs for field radios. He then replicates the concept using a modified exercise bike as the mechanical frame, fitting neodymium magnets to the flywheel and a salvaged microwave oven fan coil to demonstrate DIY electricity generation.

## Key Topics
- History and use of the GM44 military hand-cranked generator (D-Day landings, ~50,000 units deployed)
- Internal workings: rotor, field windings, brushed commutators, dual voltage outputs (6V filament supply and 500V plate supply)
- Electromechanical voltage regulation via relay-based closed-loop feedback
- Passive filter system (choke and capacitors) for smoothing DC output
- DIY replication using a modified exercise bike
- Flywheel-as-generator concept vs. belt-driven alternator approach
- Scaling output by adding more coils in series or parallel

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Neodymium magnets (1 cm × 30 cm × 3 mm) — glued to the steel flywheel rim as the magnetic rotor
- Superglue — used to assist magnet adhesion to the steel flywheel
- Fiberglass resin — used to fix the stator coil in place
- Cardboard — used as a temporary spacer when setting the coil gap
- Microwave oven fan coil — repurposed as the stator winding
- Carbon brushes — part of the original GM44 commutator assembly
- Electrolytic capacitor — low-voltage side filter in the GM44
- Paper capacitor (large) — high-voltage side filter in the GM44
- Choke (inductor) — part of the GM44 filter system

## Techniques and Methods
- Modifying an exercise bike frame (cutting off pedals, welding handles) to serve as a hand-crank generator stand
- Gluing alternating-polarity neodymium magnets (N-S-N-S) around a steel flywheel to create a permanent-magnet rotor
- Embedding a salvaged coil in fiberglass resin against the flywheel rim to form a stator
- Using a gear-up mechanism (inherent in the exercise bike's flywheel ratio) to increase rotational speed from arm cadence
- Closed-loop electromechanical voltage regulation: relay pulls resistor in series with field coils, then shorts field coils if voltage still exceeds threshold
- Scaling power output by adding multiple coils around the flywheel and connecting in series or parallel
