## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces a new series on conductive plastics, presenting a graphene-coated BOPET (biaxially oriented polyethylene terephthalate) film created by electrostatically bonding graphene and other 2D/carbon powders onto the plastic surface. The material achieves very low sheet resistance (~10 ohms) while retaining the mechanical integrity that carbon-filled bulk conductive plastics sacrifice. The video demonstrates copper electroplating onto the material as one of many planned applications.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of conventional carbon-filled conductive plastics (strength vs. conductivity trade-off)
- Electrostatic surface bonding of graphene onto BOPET film
- Sheet resistance measurement and normalization to ohms per square per mil
- Copper electroplating demonstration on the graphene-coated film
- Potential applications: heating mats, EMI shielding/Faraday cages, metamaterials, energy harvesting, capacitive touch screens, antennas, IR reflectors, thin-film battery electrodes

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — electrostatically bonded onto BOPET as the primary conductive layer
- **Other 2D powders / carbon powders** — co-deposited with graphene on the film surface
- **BOPET (biaxially oriented PET film)** — the plastic substrate
- **Copper sulfate (1 molar solution)** — electrolyte used in the electroplating demonstration
- **Copper strip** — used as the anode in the electroplating setup

## Techniques and Methods
- **Electrostatic surface bonding** — adhering graphene/carbon powder directly onto the BOPET film surface
- **Sheet resistance measurement** — using probes and a multimeter; normalization to ohms per square per mil
- **Copper electroplating** — depositing copper onto the graphene film at 1.6 V using a copper sulfate solution
- **Thermal cutoff integration** — built into the heating mat prototype to cap temperature at 40°C
