## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how a simple full-bridge rectifier circuit, when combined with a variable capacitor made from two metal plates and a plastic sheet, acts as an "electron pump" that scavenges and traps electrical energy. He shows that varying the capacitance — by changing plate separation or area — drives electrons through the diode circuit into a storage capacitor from which they cannot escape. The video argues this principle is a low-cost, tabletop-scale entry point into energy harvesting research.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a disassembled turntable motor as a hand-cranked AC generator
- Full-bridge wave rectifier as an electron trap
- Variable capacitors as electron pumps
- Energy scavenging, free energy, and energy harvesting concepts
- Scaling from small tabletop experiments to larger investigations
- False earth (large metal object as electron sink)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Turntable motor (coil and permanent magnet, separated and remounted)
- Plastic U-frame with 3 mm drilled holes
- Bearings (mounted in frame uprights)
- Diodes × 4 (wired as a full-bridge rectifier)
- Capacitor (storage, across rectifier output)
- Metal plates (used as capacitor electrodes)
- Plastic sheet (dielectric between plates)
- Metal drum (used as a false earth / electron sink)
- Rubber balloon coated with conductive paint (mentioned as an example variable capacitor)
- Conductive paint (mentioned for balloon electrode application)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a turntable motor and remounting components on a custom plastic frame to create a hand-cranked generator
- Building a full-bridge wave rectifier from four diodes
- Constructing a parallel-plate variable capacitor using metal plates, a plastic dielectric, and a false earth
- Varying capacitance by altering plate separation or plate area to drive electron pumping
- Using a large metal object (drum, radiator, bench) as a false earth in lieu of a true ground connection
