## Overview
Robert revisits the Elegoo Neptune 3 Pro 3D printer (pre-release, ~£200) to explore its potential for fabricating vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) components, specifically printing a Gorlov/Gorlovinsky-type helical turbine downloaded from Thingiverse. He prints five sections of the double-helix turbine, noting the printer's quiet operation and large build volume make it practical for producing complex, large-scale parts that would be very difficult to make by hand, with a view to assembling them into a "wind wall" array for testing.

## Key Topics
- Review of the Elegoo Neptune 3 Pro FDM printer (pre-release unit, ~£200/~$230)
- Vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs) and the concept of a "wind wall"
- Gorlov (Gorlovinsky) helical turbine vs. Savonius-type turbines
- Printing complex double-curvature turbine blades in PLA
- Surface texture (print ridges/dimples) and aerodynamic asymmetry
- Squirrel-cage rotor turbines and their potential for highway wind energy generation
- Plans to assemble printed turbines into a stacked wind wall for testing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA (Polylactic Acid)** — primary filament used for printing; Robert notes he switched from ABS to PLA because Elegoo supplied him a large quantity
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — his previous filament of choice before this project
- **Crazy glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to assemble the printed turbine sections; Robert notes a specialist glue for PLA also exists

## Techniques and Methods
- **FDM/FFF 3D printing** — using the Elegoo Neptune 3 Pro with a large build volume to print full-size turbine blade sections
- **40% infill setting** in PLA for turbine blades
- **150% scaling** applied to the Thingiverse model to match desired size
- **Stacking/bolting sections** — turbine sections designed with bolt holes; Robert assembles by stacking and gluing with crazy glue
- **Deliberate surface retention** — print ridges left in place intentionally to exploit aerodynamic asymmetry (analogous to golf ball dimples)
- **Using printed parts as molds** — mentioned as an option for casting multiple copies at scale

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Printing time and cost:** Each section took approximately a day to print (days total for all five). Cost was effectively zero as Elegoo supplied the printer and filament.
- **Filament choice:** He was using ABS previously but switched to PLA solely because Elegoo sent him a supply; either works.
- **Assembly method:** Used crazy glue; mentions a specialist PLA glue exists but hadn't tried it at time of posting.
- **Source file:** Repeatedly clarifies it is simply a Thingiverse download — the Thingiverse page screenshot is shown in the video around the 2-minute mark. He intentionally avoids posting a direct link to encourage people to find it themselves, reasoning that if someone can't be bothered to look it up they probably won't build it.
- **Scaling:** Confirms he scaled the model up by 150%.
- **Mold idea:** Finds the suggestion of printing one as a mold blank for casting multiple copies "interesting" and worth considering, especially for a garden fence-scale installation.
- **Surface dimples:** Finds it genuinely fascinating that dimples improve aerodynamics and agrees with the principle.
- **Highway VAWT plans:** Acknowledges there are real proposals to install VAWTs along highways and agrees the squirrel-cage/Gorlov designs are likely candidates.
- **Staggered stacking:** Notes that in the original patent the sections are shown staggered, and finds the suggestion to stagger them "a good thought."
- **Future plans:** Intends to build a small wind wall, test it, and may also experiment with making his own filament extruder.
- **3D printing in general:** Reflects that 3D printing has crossed a threshold from "toy" to genuinely useful tool, and was surprised himself by how much he now values it.
