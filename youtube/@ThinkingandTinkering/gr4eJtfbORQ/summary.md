## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a homemade balanced armature speaker, built from a previous electromagnetic reed device by adding a bent brass arm connected to a styrofoam bowl cone on an acrylic frame. He explains the historical significance of the design (popular from the 1920s–1950s, still used in naval sound-powered telephones) and honestly critiques the design flaws in his own implementation while suggesting improvements.

## Key Topics
- Balanced armature speaker operating principle (vibrating reed deflected between two magnets)
- Historical context: balanced armature speakers in the 1920s–1950s and modern naval sound-powered telephones
- Live audio demonstration playing music through the device
- Self-critique of design decisions and their acoustic/efficiency trade-offs
- Proposed improvements for a future iteration

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Laminated steel reed
- Permanent magnets
- Bent brass arm (connecting reed to cone)
- Styrofoam (expanded polystyrene) bowl — used as speaker cone
- Acrylic sheet — used for the frame
- Rubber band strips — used as spacers to prevent magnets snapping onto the reed
- Iron bar (core of the coil)

## Techniques and Methods
- Electromagnetic actuation: passing current through a coil around a laminated steel reed to deflect it between two magnets
- Mechanical coupling of a vibrating reed to a cone via a bent brass linkage arm
- Gluing an acrylic frame rigid to the base
- Using rubber-band offcuts as compliant spacers between magnets and reed
