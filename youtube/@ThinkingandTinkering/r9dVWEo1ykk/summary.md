## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits Oxford Street in London — where up to half a million people walk daily — to explore the concept of footfall energy harvesting. He reviews existing commercial implementations (notably Pavegen's electromechanical pavement tiles) and then presents his own 3D-printed electromechanical energy harvester design, which uses an oscillating spring-and-gear mechanism to generate voltage from impacts. The video argues that while footfall harvesting is still early-stage, dismissing it for low output mirrors short-sighted criticism of early electric motors.

## Key Topics
- Oxford Street as a case study for high-footfall energy potential
- Pavegen's real-world pavement energy harvesting installations (Bird Street, Finsbury Square Avenue)
- Three main approaches to footfall energy harvesting: piezoelectric, electrostatic, and electromechanical
- Robert's own 3D-printed electromechanical harvester design (files on Thingiverse)
- The spring-oscillation mechanism as an energy buffer between footfalls
- Adaptability of the design: pavement tiles, shoe generators, wind-driven flag/tree generators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Recycled tires — used in Pavegen's pavement surface
- 0.2 mm diameter copper wire — wound as a serpentine coil on the harvester plates

## Techniques and Methods
- Electromechanical generation (moving coil and magnet, analogous to speaker internals)
- Serpentine coil winding on flat plates around nodule spacers
- Gear-ratio mechanism using two side gears to constrain motion to oscillation (preventing energy loss to vertical bounce) and amplify torque
- Central spring integrated into a modulus-1.5 gear cog to store kinetic energy between impacts
- 3D design in Tinkercad, files shared on Thingiverse
- Voltage output measured with a voltmeter during hand-oscillation testing
