## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith briefly documents large solar cookers in use at Dingboche, Nepal, a high-altitude village on the Everest trekking route. The cookers are being used primarily to heat water, and Robert is surprised by both their size and prevalence. His takeaway is that this remote location makes effective use of its abundant strong sunlight through multiple solar thermal installations.

## Key Topics
- Solar cookers used for water heating at high altitude in Nepal
- Size and scale of the installations (~1.5 metres)
- Community sharing of the cookers among local lodge operators
- Renewable energy use in remote Himalayan villages (solar and wind)

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert clarified that the cookers were about 1.5 m in size, and that lodges in the area had between 4–6 each across roughly three lodges; they were effectively open for communal use. He noted that a large amount of water was boiled during his stay, though he personally didn't use them as he was on "hard tack" (basic rations). He also highlighted that the area is very windy and that small wind generators were also common — but that he was most impressed by the sheer number and size of the solar cookers.
