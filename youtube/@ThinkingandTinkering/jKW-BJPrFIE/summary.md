## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a biodegradable plastic composite made from casein (milk protein), hemp fibers, and tea — developed as a viable alternative to conventional petroleum-based plastics. The material solves key historical problems with milk-based plastics (water sensitivity, formaldehyde curing) by using tea as a curing agent and graphene as an additive, resulting in a waterproof, thermoplastic, hemp-reinforced material he is actively seeking commercial partners to develop.

## Key Topics
- History of casein (milk-based) plastic and its traditional formaldehyde curing process
- The environmental motivation: microplastics pollution and the need for biodegradable alternatives
- Using waste milk (~50% of milk produced is discarded) as a feedstock
- Tea as a replacement curing agent for formaldehyde
- Hemp fiber as a reinforcing agent (analogous to fibreglass in resin composites)
- Graphene as an additive enabling edge-material remoulding
- Material properties: waterproof, thermoplastic, granular/powder form suitable for extrusion
- Density (~0.95 g/cm³) and comparison to nylon, HDPE, and wood
- Full biodegradability: composts in approximately two weeks
- Commercial interest: Robert is seeking partners to licence or jointly develop the technology

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Skimmed milk / casein** — primary plastic matrix material
- **Baking soda** — used in initial milk preparation
- **Lemon juice or vinegar** — acid used to precipitate casein curds
- **Tea** — used as a curing agent in place of formaldehyde
- **Hemp fibers** — reinforcing agent to reduce brittleness, analogous to fibreglass
- **Formaldehyde** — historical curing agent (discussed but explicitly replaced)
- **Graphene** — additive that enables thermal remoulding of scrap/edge material and produces the characteristic dark colour
- **Water** — used in 24-hour boiling waterproofness test

## Techniques and Methods
- Acid precipitation of casein from skimmed milk (using lemon juice or vinegar after baking soda treatment)
- Separating and discarding the fat/sugar-rich whey liquid
- Pressing and squeezing curds to form a blank
- Tea-based curing of casein blanks (replacing traditional formaldehyde soaking)
- Compounding with hemp fibers to create a fibre-reinforced composite
- Addition of graphene to improve reprocessability
- Hot pressing granular/powder material into final form
- Extrusion processing (noted as feasible due to granular/powder form)
- Boiling waterproofness test (100°C, 24 hours)
- Process condition variation to tune material properties from rubbery to hard/brittle

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Thermoplastic nature confirmed:** Robert confirms the material is thermoplastic and speculates it could potentially be used in 3D printing, though he hasn't tried it himself.
- **No colour options:** Due to the graphene and tea, the material cures to a deep brown/black — "any colour you like as long as it's black."
- **No quantified strength data:** He acknowledges not having formal mechanical test numbers yet but intends to do that work in the future.
- **Durability unknown:** Lifespan is unknown — "ages if you look after it, no time at all if you abuse it" is his best estimate.
- **Dairy industry potential:** He agrees with commenters that using waste milk could benefit the dairy industry.
- **Contact for collaboration:** Directs interested parties to email him at robertmurraysmith64@gmail.com.
- **General tone:** Characteristically informal and self-deprecating — acknowledges the technology has been sitting unused for years and he's only one person.
