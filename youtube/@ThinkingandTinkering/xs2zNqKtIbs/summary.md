## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a parabolic solar reflector using a spokeless bicycle wheel and a space blanket (Mylar). He walks through the underlying parabola mathematics to determine focal length, surveys several construction methods, and ultimately uses a rubber sheet inflated with a tire pump (inspired by "Nighthawk in Light") to form the parabolic shape — producing a bright, functional mirror he plans to use with a Stirling engine or flash steam boiler.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: building a solar reflector for potential use with a Stirling engine or steam boiler
- Parabola geometry and the mathematical relationship between radius, depth, and focal length (`r² = 4 × focal point × depth`)
- How to find/draw a parabola using a flashlight (conic sections demonstrated visually)
- Overview of multiple parabolic mirror construction methods
- Detailed build using a bicycle wheel, Mylar space blanket, rubber sheet, and tire valve inflation
- Brief mention of future plans involving copper nanoparticle flash steam boilers

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bicycle wheel (spokeless)** — used as the rigid outer rim/frame
- **Space blanket (Mylar / aluminised Mylar)** — the reflective mirror surface
- **Super glue** — bonding the Mylar to the bicycle wheel rim
- **Gorilla Tape** — securing and tensioning the Mylar to the work surface
- **Rubber sheet** — used as the inflatable membrane to form the parabolic shape
- **Tire valve** — glued to the rubber sheet to allow pump inflation
- **Fiberglass** — applied to fix/hold the inflated parabolic form
- **Copper nanoparticles** (mentioned in passing) — MIT research context for flash steam boilers; not used in this build

## Techniques and Methods
- **Inflation method** (Nighthawk in Light's technique): rubber sheet with tire valve inflated by pump to form a dome approximating a parabola, then set with fiberglass
- **Mylar tensioning**: taping Mylar flat to a surface at corners and center before bonding to the rim
- **Flashlight parabola tracing**: shining a torch at an angle onto a board so the light beam touches the rim edges and desired depth point, tracing the resulting parabolic shadow
- **Mathematical focal length calculation**: using `r² = 4·f·d` to determine focal point from wheel radius and chosen depth
- **Cut-and-fold method** (described as alternative): cutting sections in a circular flat sheet and folding up to approximate a parabola
- **Structural parabolic curve method** (described as alternative): drawing true parabolic curves on a rigid structure and forcing reflective material into it
