## Overview
Robert demonstrates that LEDs can operate in reverse — shining light on them generates a small voltage and current, just as spinning a motor generates electricity. He pulls apart broken LED lighting units (torch, floodlight, undercabinet strip) to harvest their LED arrays and tests them as improvised solar cells, successfully charging a capacitor. His main takeaway is that COB LEDs salvaged from scrap make a surprisingly capable and essentially free DIY solar panel.

## Key Topics
- The semiconductor physics of LEDs and reverse photovoltaic operation
- Disassembling broken LED products to harvest usable LED arrays
- Comparing three LED form factors: through-hole, surface-mount strips, and COB (chip-on-board)
- Removing current-limiting resistors to allow power output rather than input
- Measuring voltage output from an LED panel under a torch beam
- Charging a capacitor to demonstrate real power generation
- Cost comparison between commercial solar cells and salvaged LED panels

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **LED (light-emitting diode)** — the core component used as a photovoltaic cell
- **YAG phosphor (terbium aluminium oxide doped with cerium)** — the yellow epoxy coating on white LEDs that shifts blue emission to produce white light
- **Silicon semiconductor** — the base material of the LED junction
- **Electrolytic capacitor (1000 µF, 50 V)** — used as a storage device to confirm real power output
- **Commercial silicon solar cell (from Radio Shack, ~£84)** — used as a reference benchmark
- **COB LEDs (chip-on-board)** — preferred salvage type, described as cheapest and easiest to use

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of broken consumer LED lighting products
- Identifying and snipping the current-limiting resistor on LED boards to enable power output
- Connecting LED arrays to a multimeter to measure photovoltaic voltage
- Shining a torch at varying distances to test light-intensity vs. voltage response
- Connecting an LED panel in parallel with a capacitor to demonstrate charge accumulation
- Back-of-envelope efficiency calculations comparing LED panels to commercial solar cells
