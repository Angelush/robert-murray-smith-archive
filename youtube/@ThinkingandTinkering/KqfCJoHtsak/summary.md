## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple pinwheel-style wind generator from recycled LCD screen plastic (or paper/card), a repurposed PC fan motor, and PVC builders' board. The project is presented as a quick, cheap, and educational homeschool activity covering basic principles of wind and electricity generation. Even in near-calm conditions, the finished generator produces around half a volt.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing LCD screen diffuser plastic as blade material
- Converting a brushless PC fan into a generator
- Pinwheel windmill design and construction
- Wind energy generation principles for educational/homeschool use
- Building a simple stand from PVC builders' board

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bright white plastic from LCD screens** — used as the windmill blade material
- **PC fan (brushless motor)** — converted into a generator by identifying and soldering to the coil ends
- **PVC-coated foam board ("builders' board")** — used for the stand; described as easy to cut and glue
- **Cyanoacrylate ("crazy glue")** — used to assemble the stand and attach the windmill

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pinwheel blade fabrication** — cutting a square sheet corner-to-corner, marking ~5 cm from centre, cutting to the edge to create a Maltese Cross shape, then folding alternating corners to the centre and gluing
- **PC fan motor conversion** — removing the fan blades, identifying the two ends of the brushless motor coil, and soldering on output wires
- **Stand construction** — four pieces of builders' board glued upright, sized so the base clears the spinning windmill
- **Hub attachment** — optional plastic peg or circle of builders' board glued to the motor hub to stiffen the blade assembly

## Robert's Own Replies
- Points viewers to a related video (`https://youtu.be/8RFQG4dglSs`) for further reference (likely the PC fan motor conversion tutorial).
- Clarifies that for some builds he adds resistors, but it depends on his interest at the time — sometimes he just wants to confirm the principle works rather than measure output precisely.
- Describes this as a "toy / science fair project" and says he is considering a more robust sheet-metal version where he would then properly measure power output.
- Acknowledges that adding features (e.g., extra components) would create drag and cost extra energy.
- Notes the project is a solo effort and he only has so much time, in response to suggestions about expanding the content.
- Encourages commenters who want a more robust version to "build one — it's not that hard."
