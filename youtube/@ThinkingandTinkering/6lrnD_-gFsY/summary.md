## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a small electromagnetic generator powered by the rubber band motor from his previous video (2297), capable of lighting a light bulb from the stored energy of a single twisted rubber band. The generator is constructed using a serpentine coil cassette and a dual-disc neodymium magnet rotor, producing approximately 12 volts open-circuit. Robert concludes that while the prototype is basic, it proves the concept and suggests practical improvements such as using a supercapacitor to smooth and extend the power output.

## Key Topics
- Building a generator to pair with the rubber band motor (video 2297)
- Serpentine coil cassette design and construction
- Neodymium magnet rotor disc assembly
- Gear/cog coupling between motor and generator
- Voltage measurement with a multimeter (~12V open circuit)
- Suggestions for improvement: supercapacitor buffering, ratchet mechanism, crank handle
- All parts designed in TinkerCAD and available on Thingiverse

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Neodymium magnets** — 10 mm × 1 mm disc magnets, 18 per rotor ring, arranged in alternating north-south polarity
- **Skater bearings** — 22 × 8 × 7 mm, two fitted inside the coil cassette
- **Rubber band** — single band providing the mechanical energy input
- **Light bulb** — small electric lamp used to demonstrate electricity generation
- **Supercapacitor** — mentioned as a recommended improvement to store and smooth the discharge
- **3D-printed PLA/plastic parts** — cassette body, magnet carrier discs, gear, spacers, axle blocks (implied by TinkerCAD/Thingiverse workflow)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Serpentine coil winding** — same method used across Robert's generator series; coil is wound, inserted into the cassette, and capped
- **Cassette assembly** — coil holder, base, and cap glued together; bearings pressed in
- **Magnet disc assembly** — 18 magnets seated in moulded indentations on two discs, alternating polarity
- **Gear coupling** — generator cog meshes with the rubber band motor cog to transfer mechanical energy
- **Open-circuit voltage measurement** — Ranglé multimeter used to measure output voltage during a run
- **3D design and fabrication** — all parts modelled in TinkerCAD; STL files published to Thingiverse

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On serpentine coil length calculation**: Robert explains the method — wrap a piece of string inside the form, measure it, then increase that length by 15% to get the correct wire length.
- **On generator terminology**: He acknowledges being "a little loose and free" with the term "generator," noting there are many ways to build one.
- **On rubber band variations**: He encourages experimentation — longer bands, doubled-up bands — and says he looks forward to seeing what viewers come up with.
- **On a similar prior project**: He notes he covered a comparable concept in video 1871, where everything was 3D printed, similar in spirit to a lasersaber design.
- **On future coverage**: He confirms he will cover additional related topics in a forthcoming video (released the following Monday), and points viewers to his Thingiverse page.
