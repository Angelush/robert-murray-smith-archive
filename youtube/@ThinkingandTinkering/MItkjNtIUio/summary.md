## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith designs and builds a 3D-printed micro-generator intended to fit inside a standard UK rainwater downpipe, harvesting energy from falling rainwater. Extending a previous water-based generator experiment, he assembles a six-part printed turbine with wound coils and neodymium magnets, demonstrating it generating 1.6V when blown on and 5V during a water test. He makes the design files freely available on Tinkercad and outlines clear paths for improvement.

## Key Topics
- Concept of harvesting energy from rainwater downpipes
- 3D-printed axial-flux generator design split into six printable parts
- Assembly of rotor, stator, magnet ring, and thrust bearing
- Coil winding and basic waterproofing
- Live voltage testing (breath test and water test)
- EMF formula and how more wire would increase output
- Open-source release of design files on Tinkercad

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- N35 neodymium magnets — 3 mm × 10 mm circular, 8 used, arranged alternating N-S around the rotor
- Copper wire — thin gauge, wound as the stator coil (~few hundred turns)
- Candle wax — suggested for filling the cap as a simple waterproofing measure
- Hot glue — used to secure wire routing and finish sections
- Thrust bearing — 35 mm OD, 20 mm ID, 2.5 mm thick (ball-race type, sourced from Amazon)
- Skate bearings — used in the rotor section

## Techniques and Methods
- FDM 3D printing — six-part split design, printed without supports
- Serpentine coil winding around the stator
- Axial-flux generator assembly (magnet rotor above wound stator)
- Waterproofing with candle wax fill
- Resistance testing of coil before closure (measured 32 Ω)
- Voltmeter testing under two conditions: breath (low flow) and running water
