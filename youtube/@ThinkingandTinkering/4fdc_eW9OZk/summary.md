## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores ammonia as an alternative fuel, tracing its history from powering the hypersonic X-15 rocket plane and wartime Belgian buses to its modern revival as a viable car fuel. GAC (a Chinese state-owned manufacturer) in partnership with Toyota has produced the first production ammonia-powered saloon car, claiming up to 90% emissions reduction. Robert presents ammonia as a serious contender to electric vehicles, though stops short of declaring it their definitive replacement.

## Key Topics
- History of ammonia as a fuel (X-15 aircraft, WWII Belgian buses, University of Michigan cross-country drive)
- Ammonia's combustion properties: burns to nitrogen and water only, no CO2
- The need for a combustion "promoter" (liquid oxygen or ~3% hydrogen addition) to make ammonia burn at useful speeds
- Hazards of concentrated ammonia (attacks zinc, brass, plastics; forms caustic ammonium hydroxide with moisture)
- GAC/Toyota production ammonia car: 2L, 4-cylinder, 161 bhp, ~90% emissions reduction claimed
- Ammonia's existing production and distribution infrastructure (fertiliser industry)
- Ammonia vs. electric vehicles: range anxiety, charging infrastructure, battery disposal, fire risk
- Australia solar-to-ammonia-to-hydrogen energy transport concept
- Ammonia's lower energy density (~1/3 to 1/2 that of hydrocarbons) requiring larger tanks
- Ammonia use in heavy industry, power generation, and shipping

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ammonia (NH₃)** — primary fuel subject; burns to nitrogen and water
- **Ammonium hydroxide** — formed when ammonia gas contacts moisture; highly caustic
- **Liquid oxygen** — used as combustion promoter in the X-15 engines
- **Hydrogen** — ~3% addition used as a combustion promoter; also the product of electrolyzing ammonia
- **Zinc** — material attacked/corroded by ammonia
- **Brass** — material attacked/corroded by ammonia
- **Hydrocarbons** — conventional fuel comparison baseline; mentioned as a small-percentage promoter option

## Techniques and Methods
- Use of a combustion promoter (liquid oxygen or hydrogen) to raise ammonia's burn rate to match conventional fuels
- Electrolysis of ammonia to recover hydrogen (for use as a safe hydrogen transport medium)
- Solar energy conversion to ammonia synthesis (proposed Australian project)
- Adaptation of internal combustion engines to run on ammonia (referenced as a solved engineering problem by GAC/Toyota)
- Ammonia production via existing fertiliser industry infrastructure repurposed for fuel distribution

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Zinc chloral hydrate battery:** An earlier ammonia-adjacent vehicle technology didn't succeed because the 1970s fuel crisis ended the conditions that made it viable.
- **Engine modification:** Robert expects the GAC/Toyota ammonia engine is essentially a modified conventional engine.
- **Emissions debate around hybrids:** He acknowledges ammonia would cut point-of-use emissions but notes hybrids still consume petrol, and that grid electricity in many countries still comes from burning fossil fuels — so the emissions debate is ongoing.
- **Safety/chemistry clarification:** Pushes back on a commenter conflating ammonia with methamphetamine precursors — "ammonia doesn't contain methamphetamine, it's just ammonia."
- **Tone:** Several replies are brief affirmations or polite disagreements, maintaining his characteristically direct but good-humoured style.
