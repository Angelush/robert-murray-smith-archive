## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates three bright metal plating solutions he developed — zinc, nickel, and copper — which produce shiny, finished-quality coatings with minimal post-processing. The video covers both wand/brush plating and bath plating techniques, highlighting how the solutions work robustly across a wide voltage range (2–20V) and deposit bright, adherent metal in seconds. Robert's main takeaway is that these solutions dramatically simplify electroplating, making it accessible without the usual expertise or finishing work required.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to three bright plating solutions: zinc, nickel, and copper
- Comparison of brush/wand plating vs. bath plating methods
- Voltage and current density in electroplating (10–30 mA/cm²)
- Why bright finishes are difficult to achieve conventionally (many variables: concentration, temperature, current density)
- Demonstration of nickel bath plating on copper in ~10 seconds
- Safety considerations for nickel salts (carcinogenicity)
- Solution economy — very little solution consumed per job
- Commercial availability of the three solutions through Robert's shop

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zinc** — used as donor metal and plating solution for zinc electroplating
- **Nickel** — donor metal and nickel salt solution; noted as carcinogenic, handle with care
- **Copper** — used as the substrate in the nickel plating demo; copper plating solution also available
- **Acetone** — recommended for surface cleaning before plating
- **Brasso** — occasionally used for a final light polish (optional)
- **Conductive plastic** (carbon black-filled polyethylene, supplied by Caplinq) — used as plating substrate
- **Aluminium** — used as a backing/connection point for the conductive plastic

## Techniques and Methods
- **Wand/brush plating** — negative connected to workpiece (or conductive backing), positive to a homemade wand (donor metal wrapped in cloth/sponge), solution applied by rubbing
- **Bath plating** — workpiece submerged in plating solution with donor metal anode; ~10-second immersion demonstrated for nickel
- **Surface preparation** — wipe with acetone and optional light sanding before plating
- **Current density control** — targeting 10–30 mA/cm²; power supply set to ~2V for slow plating, up to 20V for fast plating
- **Post-plate finishing** — optional light rub with Brasso; no significant finishing required with these solutions
