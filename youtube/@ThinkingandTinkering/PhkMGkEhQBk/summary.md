## Overview
In this video, Mandy (a colleague at Robert Murray-Smith's lab) demonstrates how to make a Harry Potter-style wand using a chopstick, string, and a hot glue gun, then copper-plate the handle using electroplating. The key enabling material is a water-resistant conductive ink developed by the team, which makes the non-conductive wand surface ready for electrodeposition. The finished wand receives a shiny copper-plated handle after approximately seven hours in the electroplating bath.

## Key Topics
- Constructing a decorative wand from low-cost craft materials
- Coating the wand with varnish to seal the substrate before applying conductive ink
- Measuring and verifying the electrical conductivity of the conductive ink (resistance testing and normalization)
- Setting up and running an electroplating bath to copper-plate the wand handle
- Lessons learned from prior copper-plating attempts (dog figurine, minion, golf ball)
- Post-plating finishing: polishing and varnishing to prevent oxidation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Chopstick** — wand base/shaft
- **String** — wound around the handle to create texture
- **Hot glue** — applied around the chopstick end to build up the handle shape
- **Yacht varnish** — two coats applied to seal the wand before conductive ink; also recommended after plating to prevent oxidation
- **Water-resistant conductive ink** (graphite-based, from "Working Ink" / the team's own product) — painted onto the handle to make the surface electrically conductive
- **Copper sulfate** — 250 g dissolved in 1 litre of water; used as the electroplating electrolyte
- **Copper wire** — stripped from a cable; used to make the electrical connection to the wand (cathode lead); noted that shellac-coated wire from a roll caused problems in a prior experiment
- **Copper piping** (flattened) — used as the anode in the electroplating bath
- **Shellac** — mentioned as a coating on commercially wound copper wire that interfered with an earlier plating attempt

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hot-glue sculpting** — building up an irregular, characterful handle shape on a chopstick
- **String wrapping** — winding string around the handle to add surface texture before gluing
- **Varnishing (yacht varnish, two coats, ~16 hours between coats)** — sealing the substrate to prevent absorption and ensure ink adhesion
- **Conductive ink application (two thin coats, stippling technique)** — painting graphite-based ink into all surface crevices; drying with a hair dryer (~10 min per coat); buffing between coats to remove loose graphite
- **Resistance testing and normalization** — measuring sheet resistance with a multimeter and calculating normalized resistivity using micrometer thickness measurement
- **Electroplating (copper electrodeposition)** — 0.2 V DC, copper sulfate solution, copper pipe anode, copper wire cathode connection to the wand; periodic agitation of the bath; suspending the wand mid-process to avoid dendrite formation
- **Post-plating polishing** — fine sandpaper (or Dremel) to buff the copper surface
- **Final varnish coat** — applied after plating to protect against oxidation
