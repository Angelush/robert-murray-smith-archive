## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple, low-cost method for making water-filled lenses of any size or focal length using an airtight box, clear plastic film (OHP acetate), and a heat gun. By heating the plastic until pliable and then blowing air through a tube to form a dome shape, two matching lens halves are created, joined together, and filled with water to produce a functional converging lens. The video concludes with a demonstration focusing LEDs from a torch at approximately one metre focal length.

## Key Topics
- Building an airtight wooden/MDF box as a lens-forming jig
- Selecting aperture size to control lens diameter
- Thermoforming plastic film into a spherical dome using breath pressure
- Assembling two dome halves into a biconvex water lens
- Controlling focal length by varying blow pressure (or vacuum level)
- Potential applications including telescopes and low-cost optics
- Scaling and repeatability using a vacuum gauge instead of breath

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **OHP acetate (overhead projector acetate / polyethylene terephthalate film)** — used as the flexible membrane to form the lens surfaces; ~15 cm square sheets
- **Water** — lens-filling medium, injected via syringe; clean water noted as important
- **Silicone sealant** — used to seal the box edges and secure the plastic film
- **Double-sided tape** — used to join the two lens halves with a small gap
- **Rubber sheet/gasket material** — used as seals between box and lid
- **Silicone oil** — mentioned in comments as a potential alternative fill fluid
- **Glycerol** — mentioned by Robert in comments as another fill fluid he intends to try
- **Clear cast resin** — mentioned in comments as a possible material for making permanent solid lenses

## Techniques and Methods
- **Airtight box construction** — timber frame around MDF with drilled aperture hole; edges sealed with silicone sealant
- **Thermoforming / blow moulding** — heating acetate film with a heat gun/hair dryer until soft, then inflating with breath through a tube to form a hemispherical dome
- **Vacuum moulding** — discussed as an alternative to blowing, allowing repeatable curvature control via vacuum gauge
- **Water-filled lens assembly** — two domes joined face-to-face with double-sided tape, leaving a small fill port; water injected by syringe
- **Focal length testing** — demonstrated by focusing an LED torch beam to a point, measuring focal length at approximately 1 metre

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments section was active and contained several useful follow-up insights:

- **Material clarification**: Confirmed the plastic is **OHP (overhead projector) acetate**, still available on Amazon.
- **Lens shape**: Believes the dome is **spherical**, reasoning that air pressure is applied evenly across the surface.
- **Focal length control**: Suggested that with enough control over blow pressure or vacuum, consistent focal lengths should be achievable; recommended **blow-moulding into a controlled mould** rather than free-air forming if precise curvature is needed.
- **Alternative fill fluids**: Expressed interest in trying **silicone oil** and **glycerol** as alternatives to water; was unaware of what fluid was used in a historical reference and speculated it might be an oil.
- **Solid lenses**: Open to experimenting with **clear cast resin** to make permanent lenses; acknowledged challenges of degassing and heat during curing but thought these could be overcome.
- **Telescope project**: Repeatedly confirmed his intention to **build a telescope using water lenses**, calling it a firm plan.
- **Community inspiration**: Noted he was reading through the comments and found many great ideas from viewers, including using a **space blanket** (which he promptly ordered) and other optical suggestions.
- **"Litre of Light" connection**: Acknowledged the link to the humanitarian **Litre of Light** project (bottle lenses used for room illumination), noting light collection is the key limitation.
