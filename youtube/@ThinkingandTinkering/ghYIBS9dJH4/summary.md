## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates that his 3D-printed vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) can be converted into a horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) with a simple modification — replacing the thrust bearing with a spacer. He calls this the "Swiss army knife" of wind generators, as it resolves the VAWT vs HAWT debate by being both. The design is based on Mike Waters' turbine and is built in a modular, interchangeable format to make it accessible and easy to replicate.

## Key Topics
- Vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs) vs horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWTs)
- Converting a VAWT to a HAWT by swapping the thrust bearing for a spacer
- Modular, 3D-printed turbine design for easy component swapping
- Design philosophy prioritising accessibility, cheapness, and repeatability
- Updated conical blade profile (replacing the earlier flat profile)
- Thrust bearings vs maglev bearings: trade-offs and reasoning
- Serpentine coil design rationale
- Credit to Mike Waters' original turbine design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Thrust bearing — used as the primary bearing for VAWT operation; removed when converting to HAWT
- Spacer — 3D-printed component that replaces the thrust bearing to enable HAWT mode
- Rotor bearings — two bearings within the rotor that take load in HAWT configuration

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM, up to 420×420×500mm build volume) for all turbine components
- Modular design approach allowing interchangeable printed parts across design iterations
- Conical blade profile shaping (updated from flat profile; STL files on Tinkercad)
- Serpentine coil winding for the generator section
- Tail/fin attachment for wind direction tracking in HAWT mode
- Pole mounting for outdoor real-world testing

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Orientation doesn't matter much:** Robert doesn't think it will matter which way round the turbine is oriented.
- **Maglev is possible but tricky:** Confirmed that a horizontal maglev version is possible, though the standard design uses thrust bearings for simplicity.
- **Scaling:** The design is "pretty easy to scale" — users can make it bigger or stack multiples.
- **3-phase considered:** Robert has been thinking about 3-phase output but chose single-phase as the easier-to-replicate option.
- **Cost:** Priced at approximately £9 per unit (UK).
- **Measurements philosophy:** He deliberately limits measurement videos as they make for "super dull" content; he typically measures at the start and end of a series, with voltage as a mid-series speed indicator.
- **Flywheel effect:** Acknowledged a viewer's point about rotor mass acting as a flywheel — called it "horses for courses" and worth experimenting with.
- **Coil winding:** Directed viewers to a separate video he made on making the serpentine coil.
- **Blades in lift mode:** Confirmed blades operate in lift mode, not drag mode.
- **Location:** Based in Canterbury, Kent, and welcomes visitors.
- **Explorations, not projects:** Described his work as "explorations" — "projects come out of them but the exploration never stops."
