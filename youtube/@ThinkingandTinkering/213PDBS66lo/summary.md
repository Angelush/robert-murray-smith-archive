## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith issues a correction and apology for an error in his earlier Faulhaber coil video, pointed out by viewer Keith Reynolds. The error concerned how the coil winding cancels itself out when wound continuously across magnetically opposing regions, and he explains the correct winding technique using a center-tapped approach with two separate wire segments. He also clarifies why litz wire is used in this project — not for its high-frequency properties, but for the practical advantage of winding multiple strands in parallel to increase amperage output.

## Key Topics
- Correction of a winding error in the original Faulhaber coil video
- How opposing magnet poles (N-S-N-S arrangement) interact with coil windings
- Why a continuous winding across opposing magnetic regions causes cancellation
- Center-tapping the coil using two separate wire segments joined at the correct ends
- How to verify correct coil phasing using a magnet and continuity check
- Series vs. parallel winding: voltage increase (series) vs. amperage increase (parallel)
- Why litz wire is used — for parallel winding efficiency, not high-frequency performance

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Litz wire** — multi-strand insulated wire used to wind multiple coils simultaneously in parallel, improving amperage output
- **Magnets** — arranged in N-S-N-S alternating polarity on a wooden base for the generator
- **Plexiglas** — used as a reference surface with drawn magnet positions to guide winding boundaries
- **Copper pipe** — mentioned as an analogy for what stranded (non-litz) wire is effectively equivalent to

## Techniques and Methods
- **Center-tapped coil winding** — splitting the winding into two separate wire segments to avoid magnetic cancellation across opposing pole regions
- **Phasing verification** — using a magnet swept over the coil with a meter/clip to confirm the correct ends are joined (generates signal = correct, no signal = wrong ends)
- **Parallel winding with litz wire** — winding 20–50 strands simultaneously and connecting ends together in parallel to boost current output
- **Series vs. parallel connection** — choosing between higher voltage (series, individual windings) or higher amperage (parallel, litz wire)
