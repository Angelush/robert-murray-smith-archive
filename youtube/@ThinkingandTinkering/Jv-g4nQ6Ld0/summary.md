## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the growing problem of decommissioned wind turbine blades — made from durable epoxy and fiberglass composites — which are increasingly difficult to dispose of as landfill bans spread across Europe. He surveys emerging recycling approaches (thermal separation, chemical dissolution) before demonstrating his own hands-on solution: fabricating working wind turbine blades from raw bamboo, arguing that sustainable materials are a better long-term answer than end-of-life recycling.

## Key Topics
- Why wind turbine blades are the hard recycling problem (not the steel/copper tower)
- Traditional disposal methods: cement kilns, road fill, landfill
- Landfill bans in Germany and the Netherlands driving urgency
- Scale of the problem: up to 43 million tons of blade waste projected
- University of Strathclyde's Project Progress (thermal/hot-airstream separation)
- Vestas's chemical dissolution approach for blade recycling
- Repurposing blades as playgrounds and bike sheds (local-scale solutions)
- Voodin's all-wood turbine blade research
- Cambridge/Davis bamboo blade research
- Robert's hands-on build: bamboo wind turbine blades from scratch
- Bamboo as a renewable, biodegradable structural material

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Epoxy resin** — matrix material in conventional turbine blades, makes them hard to recycle
- **Fiberglass** — reinforcing material in conventional blades; can be recovered via thermal separation
- **Balsa wood** — used as the core in some conventional blades (not the structural shell)
- **Bamboo** — featured as a renewable, fast-growing alternative blade material; Robert uses a species with 130 mm diameter culms
- **Steel, copper, aluminum** — turbine tower/nacelle materials (~80–85% of turbine mass), already recyclable
- **Bar iron (wedge tool)** — used for splitting bamboo (tooling material, not blade material)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Thermal/hot-airstream separation** (University of Strathclyde, Project Progress) — blades shredded and fed into a hot airstream to separate glass fiber from resin for reuse
- **Chemical dissolution** (Vestas) — liquid chemicals break down blade composites at low temperature using widely available reagents; claimed 475 blades recycled by end of 2022
- **Mechanical repurposing** — whole or cut blades reused as playground equipment, bike shelters, etc.
- **Bamboo splitting** — using a bar-iron wedge and mallet to split culms lengthwise rather than sawing, exploiting bamboo's natural grain
- **Node cleaning and sanding** — flattening the nodes flush so blade surfaces are smooth
- **Blade profile shaping** — cutting a taper starting one-third down the blade, narrowing to half-width, with the tip cut to 1/20th of blade length
- **Assembly and field test** — blades bolted to a triangular hub on a radiator fan motor, mounted atop a 4 m bamboo pole and tested in low-wind conditions

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Bamboo sourcing**: available from garden stores (that's where he bought his).
- **Reusing old blades**: implies reused blades develop microcracks and become a safety hazard, suggesting direct reuse isn't viable.
- **Bamboo for VAWTs**: notes there are also experiments using bamboo for vertical-axis wind turbines (VAWTs), not just horizontal-axis designs.
