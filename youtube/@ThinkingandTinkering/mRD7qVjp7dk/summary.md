## Overview
Robert presents a novel mechanical differential based on Archimedean spirals, inspired by his earlier video on the Torsen differential. The core insight is that Archimedean spirals share the non-back-drivability property of worm gears, enabling them to serve as the locking mechanism in a differential. He designs, 3D prints, and assembles a working prototype that achieves the same functional properties as a Torsen differential but with significantly fewer parts.

## Key Topics
- How differentials work (splitting/combining power, allowing wheels to turn at different speeds)
- The Torsen differential and its reliance on worm gear non-back-drivability
- Archimedean spirals and their non-back-drivable property
- Design rationale: replicating Torsen function with fewer, simpler parts
- 3D printing and assembly of the Archimedes Differential prototype
- Applications: vehicles, dual-rotor wind turbines, power combining/splitting

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar 3D printing filament (implied by printing process)
- Glue (used to fix P-shaped housings and caps)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D modelling in Tinkercad
- FDM 3D printing of all custom parts
- Mechanical assembly: press-fit axles, clips, glued components, bevel gear input
- Gear engagement via Archimedean spiral meshing with spur gear teeth
- Torque distribution across multiple spiral/spur gear sets (three used; six suggested for higher torque)
