## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith follows up on his previous vortex stove build (video 1736) by adding a stainless steel mesh mantle, an idea suggested by viewers and inspired by his earlier methanol heater project (video 1702). He demonstrates the mantle's dramatic effect on the flame, producing an impressively intense vortex fire, while acknowledging it needs better fuel control for practical use as a space heater.

## Key Topics
- Adding a mesh mantle to an existing vortex stove design
- Joining sections of stainless steel wire mesh to form a taller mantle sleeve
- Live fire test of the mantle-equipped vortex stove
- Discussion of suitable fuels (wood, pellets, used motor oil) for continuous operation
- Safety considerations during the burn test
- Future plans for heat extraction and flame control

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Stainless steel wire mesh (1 mm openings, sold as soffit/eave pest guard, available in ~100 m rolls)
- Sticks/wood (used as fuel for the test burn)
- Pellets (mentioned as preferred continuous fuel)
- Used motor oil (mentioned as an alternative liquid fuel)
- Hose clamp (used to secure the mantle to the pipe)
- Sugar container lid (repurposed as the mantle top cap, 110 mm diameter)

## Techniques and Methods
- Folding and interlocking mesh sections to join pieces into a longer sleeve (L-bend fold-over method)
- Using a clamp or hammer to tighten mesh joins
- Fitting and securing the mantle around the stove pipe with a hose clamp
