## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a home chemistry synthesis of graphene oxide (GO) using the modified Hummers method, working through the low-, mid-, and high-temperature stages of the process. He documents the full procedure from mixing graphite with concentrated sulfuric acid and oxidizers through washing, centrifuging, and obtaining a golden-brown GO suspension. The video concludes with the GO in solution and a preview of upcoming reduction steps to convert it into graphene.

## Key Topics
- The "jam jar chemistry" philosophy — doing serious science with accessible materials
- Why graphene is interesting (unusual electrical properties; Robert's interest in conductivity for a capacitor)
- Three-stage synthesis overview: low-temperature oxidation, mid-temperature reaction, high-temperature dilution/quench
- Converting graphite → graphite oxide → graphene oxide → (future) graphene
- Separation and washing of the product via centrifugation
- Reduction methods teased: LightScribe DVD drive laser, camera photo-flash
- Safety warnings for working with strong acids and oxidizers

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Deionized/distilled water** — solvent and wash medium
- **Hydrochloric acid (HCl)** — "spirit of salts," used for washing the product (1 molar solution)
- **Concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄, ~98%)** — drain cleaner; primary reaction medium (150 mL)
- **Hydrogen peroxide (9%)** — used to quench/kill the reaction (150 mL added with 600 mL water)
- **Sodium nitrate (NaNO₃)** — fertilizer/"nitrate of soda"; oxidizer (2.5 g)
- **Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄)** — strong oxidizer added gradually (15 g over 1 hour)
- **Graphite powder** — starting material purchased online (5 g)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ice-bath temperature control** — keeping the low-temperature stage below 5 °C using a tin filled with ice
- **Magnetic stirring** — homemade stirrer used throughout (2+ hours total stirring)
- **Gradual reagent addition** — potassium permanganate added slowly over 1 hour to control exotherm
- **Water-bath heating** — mixing hot and cold water to maintain ~45 °C for the mid-temperature stage
- **Reaction quench** — adding dilute hydrogen peroxide solution to terminate the oxidation
- **Centrifugation** — homemade drill-based centrifuge used to pellet the product
- **Repeated washing** — three cycles of centrifugation + wash in 1 M HCl to remove impurities
- **Gravitational settling** — allowing larger graphite particles to sink; decanting the golden-brown GO supernatant
- **Drop casting / painting** — mentioned as a method to deposit GO onto substrates (paper, plastic)
- **Sonication** — identified as the next needed step; Robert notes commercial sonic baths (~$30) can disperse GO

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Washing video**: Robert confirms he made a separate dedicated video on washing graphene oxide, and directs commenters to it.
- **Safety of final product**: The resulting golden-brown GO-in-water suspension is not dangerous — by the end of the process, the sulfuric acid and potassium permanganate have been removed through washing.
- **Sludge separation**: The dark sludge is a mix of GO and unreacted graphite. Once cleaned, GO dissolves in DI water while graphite does not — keep the dissolved fraction and discard the graphite sediment. Yield is low and a lot of GO is lost in earlier washes, but producing any GO at home is an achievement.
- **Centrifuge speed**: Use the homemade drill centrifuge as fast as it will go until the brown material pellets; the supernatant can be kept or allowed to settle further.
- **Conductivity / bonding**: Graphene oxide film does not itself conduct well enough to bond directly — conductive silver paint is needed as an interface.
- **Dispersion solvents**: GO can be dispersed in a wide range of solvents (water, ethanol, PVP, toluene, etc.) but it does not truly dissolve — sonication is unavoidable. A cheap ~$30 sonic bath works; Robert credits friend Eric Goeken's channel for demonstrating this.
- **Oxidizing agents**: Potassium permanganate is preferred, but hydrogen peroxide, perchlorates, and nitrates can also work.
- **Reduction methods**: Flash reduction with a xenon lamp is reported but with limited success; low-temperature drying is needed for even film formation.
- **Yield estimate**: ~5 g graphite gives roughly 1 litre of GO solution at ~4 mg/mL — enough to coat on the order of 50 m² of substrate.
- **Source papers**: Robert cites four peer-reviewed references underlying the procedure, including Lim & Huang (Int. J. Nanomedicine, 2011) and Zhu, Murali et al. (Carbon, 2010).
- **Ongoing work**: At time of commenting, Robert was having samples tested and was redoing the entire graphene series as an organised course with better audio and video.
