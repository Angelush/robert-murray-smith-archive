## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith and his colleagues Steve build a DIY hotwire cutter (dubbed the "Megameltatron") using Kanthal resistive wire and salvaged parts from an old heater-stirrer. The video demonstrates the full assembly process and confirms the cutter works effectively on polystyrene, cutting clean circles for an ongoing project.

## Key Topics
- What Kanthal wire is and why it's suited for hotwire cutting
- Sourcing and recycling parts from old lab equipment
- Assembling the cutter armature, brass mounting points, and wiring
- Electrical insulation using Kapton tape and silicone
- Practical demonstration cutting polystyrene circles to specific dimensions
- Brief mention of cutting acrylic as well

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Kanthal wire** — resistive wire made of chromium, aluminium, and iron; aluminium oxide layer allows heating in air without fouling
- **Nickel wire** — ordered alongside Kanthal from the Crazy Wire Company (not used in this build)
- **Polystyrene (polyarene/polystyrene foam)** — primary material being cut
- **Acrylic** — also tested with the cutter
- **Brass** — used as the wire mounting points/terminals
- **Kapton tape** — used to electrically insulate the frame from the brass mount
- **Silicone** — used to secure the Kapton tape when screwing in the mount

## Techniques and Methods
- Salvaging and cleaning parts from decommissioned lab equipment (heater-stirrer)
- Tensioning Kanthal wire between two brass mounting points on an adjustable armature
- Electrical insulation check using continuity testing
- Strain relief via cable ties at the junction box
- Template-guided freehand cutting of polystyrene circles with the hotwire

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Toaster wire vs. Kanthal**: Toaster wire (NiChrome) works fine as a substitute — Kanthal was used simply because they received it as a free sample from the Crazy Wire Company.
- **Power supply**: A standard 12V wall wart at approximately 1 amp is sufficient; the length of wire can be adjusted to suit whatever supply is available.
- **Acknowledges prior art**: Robert notes he is aware that similar professional/school-grade hotwire cutters exist — this video is aimed at those who want to build their own.
