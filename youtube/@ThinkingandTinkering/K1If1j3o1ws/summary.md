## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a DIY hot plastic stapler for repairing cracked plastic — particularly car bumpers and light clusters — using a salvaged microwave oven transformer (MOT). He walks through the full build, from preparing the transformer and fabricating the electrode connectors to testing the device on scrap plastic, concluding that it works remarkably well and is a practical, low-cost alternative to expensive professional repairs.

## Key Topics
- Motivation for the build: costly plastic bumper and light cluster repairs (Robert mentions a £600 car light cluster replacement)
- Repurposing a microwave oven transformer (MOT) as the power source
- Fabricating the electrode connectors from copper and brass tubing
- Making DIY staples from steel wire vs. buying pre-made staples
- Safety considerations: the output side is ~0.8 V (safe), the mains side is 240 V (dangerous)
- Live demonstration on scrap plastic

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Microwave oven transformer (MOT)** — primary coil retained, secondary coil removed to create a step-down transformer
- **Jumper cable wire** — used as a single-turn secondary winding
- **Copper tubing (8 mm micro bore)** — used to fabricate electrode connector lugs
- **Rubber hose** — used as insulating sleeve over copper tube connectors
- **Brass tube (two sizes)** — used to reduce connector diameter to fit the staples
- **Steel wire** — bent into DIY staples using needle-nose pliers
- **Washing-up liquid** — used as lubricant to slide rubber hose onto copper tube
- **Zip ties** — used to strap the transformer assembly together

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing the secondary coil from an MOT (referenced to a separate video)
- Winding a single turn of heavy jumper cable wire as the new secondary
- Cold-crimping copper tube lugs by inserting wire and hammering flat (or using a vice)
- Nested brass tube crimping to reduce electrode tip diameter to match staple gauge
- Wiring a momentary switch into the mains circuit for safe operation
- Hot stapling: pressing a steel staple across a plastic crack and applying current to embed it
