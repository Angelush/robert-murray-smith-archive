## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to make a fluorescent jelly from cheap highlighter pen ink and gelatin, following up on video 1664 where a fluorescent ink-soaked t-shirt improved solar cell output. He extracts the water-soluble dye, sets it into a gelatin mold, and places the shaped jelly over a solar cell, achieving a measurable improvement. The experiment is presented as a low-cost proof-of-concept for fluorescent light-guiding structures that could enhance solar cells.

## Key Topics
- Extracting fluorescent dye from highlighter pens using deionized water
- Making a fluorescent gelatin jelly as a light-modifying material
- Testing the jelly's fluorescence under UV light
- Placing the jelly over a solar cell to improve output
- Comparing DIY jelly to commercial fluorescent acrylic sheets
- Potential applications including Fresnel lenses and solar tracking elimination

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Highlighter pen ink** — water-soluble fluorescent dye; the ink reservoir is cellulose acetoacetate surrounded by cellulose acetate plastic (the same material used in cigarette filters)
- **Deionized water** — 200 ml used to extract the dye
- **Gelatin** — 1 teaspoon per 200 ml of dyed water, used to set the jelly
- **Reflective paper** — placed behind the jelly triangle to direct light onto the solar cell
- **Fluorescent acrylic sheets** — commercial alternative mentioned for comparison (£10 each vs. 4 highlighters for £1)
- **Cooking oil** — used to grease the mold for easy release

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a highlighter pen to retrieve the felt ink reservoir
- Soaking the felt in deionized water to produce a fluorescent dye solution
- Blooming and dissolving gelatin in the dye solution with heat
- Pouring into an oiled plastic tray mold and allowing to set
- Cutting the set jelly into a triangular prism shape
- Backing the jelly with reflective paper and positioning it over a solar cell
- UV light testing to verify fluorescence

## Robert's Own Replies
Several comments contain meaningful technical clarifications and follow-up thinking:

- **Prism vs. fluorescence effect:** Robert noted that the triangular gel shape is likely acting as a **prism** rather than purely a fluorescent concentrator, and that a fairer test would use a prism with a reflector to isolate the effect.
- **Light guide, not cover:** He clarified the jelly is intended as a **light guide** rather than a simple cover, and his main interest is whether it performs well from multiple angles — if so, it could eliminate the need for mechanical solar tracking systems.
- **Geometry is key:** He repeatedly emphasized that making this practical will require significant experimentation with geometry, which can be expensive unless cheap test methods are found.
- **Long-term limitations of jelly:** Acknowledged practical problems — drying out, rot, and insects — but stressed the jelly is for **experimentation**, not a finished product.
- **Dye durability:** Marker pen dyes fade quickly; he noted that long-lived pigments and dyes do exist for more permanent implementations.
- **Resin alternative:** For a resin-based version, a **solvent-soluble dye** would be needed, as highlighter dyes are water-based and incompatible with resin.
- **IR interaction:** Suggested the solar improvement may partly involve **infrared interaction**, not just visible fluorescence.
- **Gelatin as electrolyte:** Mentioned that gelatin makes a good **pseudo solid electrolyte**, hinting at broader electrochemical applications.
- **Future curiosity:** Expressed interest in turning the jelly into a **cryogen** to see what happens, and noted he had ordered some **LIT** (a material mentioned by a commenter) for further experimentation.
