## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to repurpose an old hard disk drive as a functional speaker by extracting the voice coil and magnet assembly from the read/write head mechanism. He builds a simple frame from foamboard and acrylic, constructs a cellophane diaphragm, and couples it to the HDD armature to produce sound. The result is a working (if tinny) speaker, with the suggestion that a salvaged speaker cone would yield better audio reproduction.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging and repurposing old hard disk drives
- How a hard drive's voice coil and magnet assembly can function as a speaker motor
- Identifying the correct voice coil pins on the HDD connector using resistance measurements
- Building a diaphragm from acrylic and cellophane
- Using heat (hairdryer) to tension the cellophane membrane
- Coupling the HDD armature to the diaphragm via a brass rod and disc

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Old hard disk drive (internal or external)
- Acrylic sheet (yellow mentioned as example)
- Foamboard / builder's board (known as Foam-X, Sintra board, or Surf board in Europe/UK)
- Cellophane (used as diaphragm membrane)
- Double-sided sticky tape
- Bent brass rod (used as coupling linkage)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling a hard drive to extract the read/write head armature and voice coil
- Using a multimeter to identify voice coil pins by resistance (4–14 ohms vs. kilohm/megaohm for other pins)
- Soldering wires onto the identified connector pins
- Cutting and mounting foamboard/acrylic to create a speaker frame
- Stretching cellophane over an acrylic ring and heat-shrinking it taut with a hairdryer
- Coupling the armature to the diaphragm with a small disc glued to a brass rod and held with double-sided tape
