## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the Geneva Drive, a mechanism invented by Swiss clockmakers in the 17th or 18th century that converts continuous rotation into precise intermittent steps. He traces its historical journey from clock over-winding prevention to film projection and banknote counting, then walks through a hands-on build of a 3D-printed multi-digit turn counter using cascaded Geneva Drive number drums.

## Key Topics
- Origins of the Geneva Drive in Swiss clockmaking
- How the Geneva Drive mechanism works (intermittent rotation with positive locking)
- Backlash in ordinary gears vs. the precision of the Geneva Drive
- Use of the Geneva Drive in early cinema film projectors (Oscar Mester, 1896)
- Comparison with Edison's Vitascope and the earlier egg-beater mechanism (Georgie Demeny, 1893)
- Variants: external, internal, and spherical Geneva drives; the "Maltese cross" naming
- Slot count choices and their effect on step angle
- Building a cascaded multi-digit mechanical counter (counting to 9,999)
- Practical application as a coil-winding turn counter

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of Geneva Drive components (number drums, drive cogs, Geneva mechanism plates)
- Cascading Geneva Drive stages with interlocking cogs at a 1:1 ratio to carry digits
- Assembling a mechanical counter on a shared bar/axle with a bearing
- Interfacing the counter to a motor for automated coil-winding turn counting
