## Overview
Robert continues from video 1800, introducing Faulhaber (star) coils as an improvement over serpentine coils for coreless motor/generator designs. He explains the trade-offs involved and demonstrates how to wind a pancake-format Faulhaber coil on an acrylic disc, including making homemade Litz wire from standard magnet wire. The goal is building a low-cogging-torque generator suited to the UK's characteristically low average wind speeds.

## Key Topics
- Recap of serpentine coils and their limitation (excess copper outside the magnetic field)
- Faulhaber (star) coil geometry: zigzag/triangular winding pattern that keeps all copper within the magnetic field
- Trade-offs of Faulhaber coils vs. serpentine coils (power density vs. field efficiency)
- Coreless motor/generator design and the elimination of cogging torque
- Application of Faulhaber coils in drone motors (high RPM, low weight)
- Pancake vs. cylindrical Faulhaber coil formats
- Making homemade Litz wire from magnet wire
- Single-phase vs. multi-phase winding strategies
- Encapsulating the finished coil in resin
- Spinning coil vs. spinning magnets — design trade-offs (slip rings, mass)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic disc** (3 mm thick) — used as the coil former
- **Wooden disc** — used as a template to mark magnet positions
- **Magnet wire (enamel-coated)** — used to wind the Litz wire bundles and the coil itself
- **Litz wire (homemade)** — bundled thin insulated wires twisted together to reduce skin effect at high frequencies
- **Car resin (polyester/epoxy)** — used to encapsulate and fix the finished coil
- **Plastic (acrylic offcut disc)** — reinserted as the coil's central section for spinning
- **Zip ties** — used to bundle wire during Litz wire fabrication

## Techniques and Methods
- **Faulhaber/star winding** — triangular zigzag pattern wound between notches on a flat acrylic disc former
- **Pancake coil format** — flat disc adaptation of the normally cylindrical Faulhaber winding
- **Homemade Litz wire fabrication** — running magnet wire back and forth between two mole grips over a 5 m bench, bundling with zip ties, then twisting with a drill
- **Hole-saw cutting** — used to cut the acrylic disc and its central ring
- **Notch-guided winding** — notches cut at magnet boundaries to align and anchor wire passes
- **Resin encapsulation** — pouring car resin over the wound coil to fix the geometry
- **Tapping/flattening** — using a block of wood to flatten the wound coil before resin application
- **Multi-phase winding** — described as dividing the disc into thirds for 1-, 2-, or 3-phase outputs
