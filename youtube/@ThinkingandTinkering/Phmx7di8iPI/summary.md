## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reviews the Vevor tent stove (a budget-friendly ~£150 outdoor wood stove) and demonstrates how to convert it into a safe indoor heater by using a homemade two-tin alcohol burner as the fuel source instead of wood. The key takeaway is that any enclosed stove can serve as an emergency indoor heater when paired with an ethanol/methanol burner, since those fuels don't require venting, making the setup compliant with UK legislation for unvented indoor use.

## Key Topics
- Review of the Vevor tent stove: assembly, construction, and value for money compared to alternatives
- The fundamental problem: converting an outdoor chimney stove for safe indoor use without a chimney
- Building a two-tin alcohol burner from recycled food cans and wicking material
- Safe indoor use of ethanol/methanol fuels vs. fuels that require venting
- Non-combustible surface requirements under UK fire safety guidelines
- Practical heat management: fuel-to-heat ratio and swapping out burners

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel** — material the Vevor stove is constructed from
- **Carbon felt** — preferred wicking material for the alcohol burner; described as long-lasting
- **Ethanol / methanol (methylated spirits)** — the approved indoor fuel; does not require venting
- **Cotton / old jeans / bandage / sock** — alternative wicking materials
- **Hand sanitiser** — mentioned in comments by Robert as a cheap ethanol source
- **Recycled food tins (tomato can, sweetcorn can)** — used as the burner body; chosen because they fit together

## Techniques and Methods
- **Two-tin alcohol burner construction**: nesting two food cans (one slightly smaller), drilling/punching four air holes near the bottom of the outer can before removing its base, lining the inner can with wicking material, and filling the gap between cans with fuel
- **Wicking**: lining and wrapping cans with carbon felt or textile scraps to draw fuel to the flame
- **Stove assembly**: unfolding legs, inserting the grate and ash pan, fitting chimney sections of varying diameters (70 mm or 80 mm) and lengths
- **Safe fuel swapping**: using gloves or pliers to remove hot spent burner tins and replace them, analogous to adding a log to a fire
- **Non-combustible base preparation**: placing the stove on a tile of at least 12.5 mm thickness with a clearance of ≥100 mm, per UK guidelines
