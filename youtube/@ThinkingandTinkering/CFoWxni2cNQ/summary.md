## Overview
This is a memorial tribute video posted by Robert Murray-Smith in memory of his wife Patti (Patti Murray-Smith), who passed away shortly before the video's publication. It features existing footage of Patti — a teacher by profession — demonstrating a simple bottle rocket experiment for home use, using the acid-base reaction between vinegar and bicarbonate of soda to propel a corked bottle into the air. The video serves both as a science tutorial aimed at encouraging children and families to experiment at home, and as a loving remembrance of Patti's warmth and talent as an educator.

## Key Topics
- Memorial tribute to Patti Murray-Smith, Robert's wife and a teacher
- Simple home science experiment: acid-base bottle rocket
- Chemistry of the vinegar-bicarbonate of soda reaction (producing CO₂ gas)
- Building a stable tripod launch stand from everyday materials
- Safety practices when handling chemicals at home
- Encouraging experimentation and hands-on science for young people

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Empty plastic water/drinks bottle** — serves as the rocket body; recycling encouraged
- **Vinegar (acetic acid)** — weak acid; bottle filled halfway; noted that brown vinegar makes more mess and premium vinegars are unnecessary
- **Bicarbonate of soda (baking soda / sodium bicarbonate)** — alkaline reactant; approximately one tablespoon used; explicitly distinguished from baking powder
- **Tissue paper / tracing paper** — used to wrap the bicarbonate of soda into a packet; tracing paper found to work best through experimentation
- **Cork or rubber bung** — seals the bottle; a chemistry flask bung used as substitute for a wine cork
- **Masking tape** — preferred over cellophane tape for ease of handling; used to secure tripod legs
- **Three pens, pencils, or sticks** — form the tripod launch stand
- **Small funnel** — from a basic home lab kit; used to pour vinegar
- **Safety glasses** — recommended whenever working with any chemicals
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — the gas produced by the reaction, which propels the rocket
- **Water and salt** — also noted as byproducts of the acid-base reaction

## Techniques and Methods
- **Tripod construction:** Three pens/sticks taped evenly around the bottle neck at approximately 120° apart (measured with a protractor) to create a stable inverted launch stand
- **Bicarb packet preparation:** Wrapping bicarbonate of soda in tracing paper, twisting one end closed, adding roughly one tablespoon of bicarb, then folding (not twisting) the other end
- **Rocket assembly and launch:** Dropping the bicarb packet into the vinegar-filled bottle, inserting the cork firmly, shaking, then inverting the bottle to initiate the reaction and launch

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert responded personally to a very large number of condolence comments. The key points he expressed across these replies are:

- **Who Patti was:** He repeatedly describes her as *"a teacher and wonderful person and the love of my life"*, *"the best half of me"*, and *"the better part of me."* He says she *"was the inspiration for me."*
- **His grief:** He was candid about still being *"in a bit of a fog"* shortly after her passing, and confirmed *"I cry a lot and think of her all the time."* He described the loss as *"like losing half of your soul."*
- **His purpose in posting the video:** He said repeatedly, *"I want her to be remembered and for a short glimpse of the truly wonderful person she was to be seen."* He wanted people who never knew her to catch a glimpse of who she truly was.
- **On her reluctance to be filmed:** He noted that *"she didn't like being videoed much even though I told her she was a natural — it was just the kind of person she was."*
- **On the community's support:** He expressed genuine gratitude for every message, prayers, and kind words, saying repeatedly that *"it really does make a difference."*
- **Looking ahead:** While clearly still in early grief, he indicated he would try to carry on, replying *"I will try"* and *"right now I just miss her."*
