## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith replicates an MIT experiment using a two-layer solar steam generator made from carbonised Oasis floral foam coated with exfoliated graphite. The device wicks water up through the hydrophilic carbon foam structure to the graphite surface, where absorbed infrared/solar energy converts it to steam — enabling low-cost water desalination. The experiment is demonstrated under an infrared lamp and shown to produce visible steam within minutes.

## Key Topics
- Replication of MIT's solar steam/desalination foam technology
- Carbonisation of Oasis (phenolic floral) foam to create a hydrophilic carbon scaffold
- Arc-exfoliation of graphite to produce exfoliated graphite/graphene worms
- Two-layer device assembly: carbon foam base + exfoliated graphite top layer
- Steam generation via infrared/solar heating for water desalination
- Potential extensions: steam-driven engine, off-grid power generation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oasis foam** (phenolic wet floral foam) — hydrophilic scaffold; carbonised to form the wicking base layer
- **Exfoliated graphite / graphene worms** — top absorber layer; produced by arc-exfoliation of graphite; highly hydrophilic and infrared-absorptive
- **Water (saline/general)** — feedstock for desalination demonstration
- **Graphite** (source material for exfoliation via arc welder)
- **Acid** — used to acid-wash graphite prior to arc exfoliation

## Techniques and Methods
- **Carbonisation of foam** — Oasis foam is pyrolysed to produce a lightweight, structured carbon scaffold (causes ~20% linear shrinkage, e.g. 1 cm → 0.8 cm)
- **Arc exfoliation of graphite** — acid-washed graphite placed on a metal plate and subjected to an arc welder to produce exfoliated graphite worms
- **Two-layer device assembly** — carbonised foam base with exfoliated graphite pressed/flattened onto the top surface
- **Capillary wicking** — water drawn up through the porous carbon foam structure to the heated surface
- **Infrared lamp heating** (proxy for solar illumination in the UK winter) — focused through a Fresnel lens onto the device surface to generate steam

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Carbonised foam acts as a preheater:** Robert clarifies that the carbonised foam layer functions as a preheater, and that he tried the device without carbonising the foam and it did not work.
- **Orientation:** The graphite layer sits on top only; the device should be horizontal.
- **MIT attribution:** Robert is candid that he largely glanced at the experimental section of the MIT paper rather than reading it fully, and may have misread some of the claims — he notes it was initially encountered via a news article.
- **Scalability:** He gave the idea away freely and suggests others can determine whether it is viable at industrial scale.
- **Collecting steam:** Confirmed that collecting and condensing the steam for pure water is straightforward and not much of a challenge.
- **Oasis foam identity:** Quotes Wikipedia to clarify that Oasis is a trademarked phenolic foam used in floral arranging, with capillary action similar to plant tissue.
- **Follow-up video:** Hints that the next video in the series is "truly astounding."
- **Polypropylene as alternative:** Raises the possibility of polypropylene as an alternative foam material.
