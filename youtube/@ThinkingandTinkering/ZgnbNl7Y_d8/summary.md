## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a DIY spin coating machine from a repurposed magnetic stirrer and a chocolate tin, then uses it to apply graphene oxide (GO) to glass substrates. The video shows the full process from construction through coating, resulting in a thin, transparent, electrically conductive graphene layer — confirmed by measuring approximately 200 ohms resistance across the coated glass.

## Key Topics
- Converting a magnetic stirrer into a spin coater using a Celebrations chocolate tin
- Attaching glass substrates to the spinning magnets using double-sided tape (or wax for a better hold)
- Applying graphene oxide suspension via dropper to the spinning substrate
- Controlling coating thickness through spin speed and application time
- Demonstrating electrical conductivity of the resulting graphene film
- Comparing thin vs. thick coatings (short vs. ~30-minute coating sessions)
- Post-deposition reduction of graphene oxide to graphene (referenced via later videos)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide (GO) in water** — the coating suspension applied via spin coating
- **Glass** — the substrate being coated
- **Aluminium strips** — used as electrodes on either side of the glass to measure conductivity
- **Double-sided tape** — used to attach the metal rod to the glass (wax recommended as better alternative)
- **Wax** — recommended for more firmly mounting the substrate

## Techniques and Methods
- **Spin coating** — depositing a thin film by dropping liquid material onto a spinning substrate; centrifugal force spreads it into a uniform coat
- **DIY spin coater construction** — repurposing a magnetic stirrer with a plastic shield (Celebrations tin) to contain splatter
- **Substrate mounting** — attaching glass to a metal rod magnetically coupled to the stirrer
- **Resistance measurement** — using a multimeter across aluminium strip electrodes (~198–200 Ω) to verify conductivity
- **Thermal treatment (baking at 200°C)** — used to brown/reduce the coating enough to visually confirm it and convert GO to graphene
- **Extended spin coating (~30 minutes)** — to build up a thicker, more visible but still transparent coating

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Graphene oxide suspension**: Confirmed the coating material is graphene oxide in water.
- **Reduction step**: He reduced the GO to graphene after application — details in later videos.
- **Supercapacitor use**: Noted that an ultra-thin graphene coating is actually *bad* for supercapacitors — a relatively thick coating is needed due to how supercapacitors store charge.
- **Theoretical vs. achievable performance**: Graphene theoretically offers ~500 F/g capacitance but practically achieves ~90 F/g — "pretty rubbish really but that's the way it is."
- **Conductivity vs. resistivity**: He measures resistivity (198 Ω confirmed) and uses it as a guide for conductivity since he can't directly measure conductivity.
- **Coating durability**: The coating withstands rubbing but not scouring.
- **Substrate size**: 6.5 cm glass.
- **ITO comparison**: Explained that ITO (indium tin oxide) coatings are expensive because indium is rare — graphene is a viable alternative.
- **Graphene ink**: Mentioned that graphene ink already exists commercially ("vor-ink") and expressed the goal of making a graphene ink better than copper.
- **Future work**: Mentioned plans to make carbon nanotubes and mix them with graphene; also considering graphitic carbon nitride.
- **Risk philosophy**: Characteristically dismissed safety concerns — "suggesting I be careful is like telling the sun not to set mate — there is no invention without risk."
