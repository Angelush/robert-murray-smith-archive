## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith walks through the basics of building a touch-sensitive lamp circuit, demonstrating how to assemble a lamp base using MDF and a bayonet batten holder, wire it up to a touch switch circuit built around a 555 timer and NPN transistor, and connect a conductive ink touch pad. A key finding demonstrated in the video is that the conductive ink touch pad continues to function correctly even after being over-painted with ordinary emulsion paint.

## Key Topics
- Overview of the touch-lamp circuit (555 timer in astable mode with NPN transistor)
- Building a lamp base from MDF with a bayonet batten holder and door-handle feet
- Wiring up the lamp holder: neutral, line, lamp live, and supply live terminals
- Creating a conductive ink touch pad on paper using copper tape as a connection point
- Demonstrating touch switching and brightness control with an LED lamp
- Testing whether conductive ink remains functional when over-painted with emulsion paint

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- MDF (medium-density fibreboard) — used as the lamp base
- Bayonet batten holder (European bayonet fitting) — lamp socket
- Conductive ink — painted onto paper to form the touch pad
- Copper tape/copper strip — used to make a solid connection between the wire and the ink pad
- Emulsion paint — used to over-paint the conductive ink to test if touch sensitivity is retained
- Varnish — previously applied over the ink before the emulsion test
- LED lamp — used as the light source in the demonstration
- NPN transistor (likely BC547/2N3904 type; specified as "thing 1 1 5 3") — part of the touch switch circuit
- 555 timer IC (astable mode) — core of the touch switch circuit

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling MDF to fit the batten holder and feet
- Wiring terminal blocks in a lamp holder (connecting neutral, line, lamp live, supply live)
- Fitting a cable clamp to the base for strain relief
- Painting conductive ink onto paper to form a touch-sensitive pad
- Using copper tape to bond wire to the conductive ink pad
- Over-varnishing and over-painting (emulsion) the conductive ink pad to test conductivity retention
- Crocodile clip connections for prototyping/testing
