## Overview
Robert revisits his SWAG (Solid-state Wideband... energy generation) project, demonstrating improved power output by switching to aluminium chloride as the electrolyte salt. He then explores separating the magnetically-aligned diamagnetic and magnetic carbon regions of SWAG ink to dramatically boost current output, extrapolating the results to suggest a potential of ~240 W/m² — exceeding commercial solar cells — if the technique is scaled up properly.

## Key Topics
- SWAG plate construction: copper and aluminium strips printed with SWAG ink on a printer's block
- Effect of higher-charge ions (Al³⁺ from AlCl₃) on SWAG output current and voltage
- Magnetic alignment of carbon constituents in SWAG ink (diamagnetic vs. magnetic carbon separation)
- Scraping/separating dark and gray carbon regions to isolate electrode zones
- Power density calculations and comparison to solar cell efficiency (~150 W/m²)
- Plans to systematically scrape all lines using a magnifying glass and pin for a future update

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **SWAG ink** — a homemade graphene-based ink applied to printer's blocks to form the active layer
- **Copper** — used as the positive electrode strip and as connectors to separated carbon regions
- **Aluminium** — used as the negative electrode strip
- **Aluminium chloride (AlCl₃)** — electrolyte solution; chosen because Al³⁺ carries a +3 charge, increasing charge-separation effect
- **Copper oxide (CuO)** — used as electrolyte additive in the separated-carbon experiment (Cu²⁺ ion chosen to match copper electrodes)
- **Deep Eutectic Solvent (DES)** — used as the solvent/electrolyte medium in the small separated-carbon experiments
- **Neodymium (neo) magnet** — used to align carbon in ink on a larger plate for separation experiments

## Techniques and Methods
- Printing SWAG ink onto plates aligned by a printer's block with embedded magnets
- Using a neodymium magnet on a plate to produce larger magnetically-aligned carbon regions (central dark square + surrounding gray ring)
- Physically scraping carbon regions apart with a blade to separate diamagnetic and magnetic carbon zones
- Attaching separate copper electrodes to each scraped region for independent electrical measurement
- Measuring output with a multimeter on both µA and mA scales depending on experiment scale
- Normalising measured power output per unit area (mm² → m²) to compare with solar cell benchmarks

## Robert's Own Replies
- **SWAG ink sourcing:** The printer's blocks were bought on eBay for typesetting; the SWAG ink itself was homemade from graphene — he directed people to his earlier ink-making video as a starting point.
- **Status of the project:** He acknowledged going quiet on SWAG for a while due to other work, but intended to return to it.
- **Oscilloscope:** He does not own one, limiting some measurement options suggested by commenters.
- **Safety awareness:** He cautioned that some aspects of the experiments border on dangerous and he would not share certain steps to avoid accidents.
- **Recognition vs. knowledge:** He noted the effect "is known, just not recognized — because it doesn't come out of a university."
- **Electrode separation method:** Clarified he scraped away the interface between light and dark ink areas and attached a separate electrode to each region.
- **Practical scaling via DES:** Suggested a real-world deployment might pump DES with AlCl₃ over the material (e.g., driven by wave/sea energy) rather than dipping.
- **Too experimental for sharing full details:** Described the work as too early-stage to document comprehensively for public replication.
