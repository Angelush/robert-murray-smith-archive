## Overview
This video is a competition entry by a viewer who attempted to replicate and improve upon Robert Murray-Smith's "Easiest Wind Turbine You'll Ever Make" project. The creator documents their iterative process of building pipe-blade wind turbines from salvaged car fans, detailing the mistakes made in version one and the improvements that led to a working version two. The key takeaway is that blade design and geometry are the critical factor in getting the generator to produce usable power.

## Key Topics
- Sourcing automotive cooling fans from a scrapyard as cheap generator cores
- First-attempt (v1) blade fabrication mistakes and lessons learned
- Second-attempt (v2) blade improvements and design principles
- Aerofoil blade geometry: leading edge rounding, trailing edge sharpening, taper, and twist
- Testing at a hilltop park vs. at home
- Portable/collapsible storage design for the turbine

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Salvaged automotive cooling fans (used as generator cores)
- PVC or similar pipe (quartered for blade blanks)
- LEDs (for output testing)
- Dual-diode bridge rectifier ("jewel teef" / dual teeth) — used to allow LEDs to light from AC output

## Techniques and Methods
- Using a multimeter to test salvaged fans before purchase
- Coarse grinding disc for shaping (v1 — noted as ineffective)
- Thin cutting wheel for straight, clean cuts (v2 improvement)
- Rounding the leading edge and sharpening the trailing edge to approximate an aerofoil profile
- Tapering blades toward the tips to reduce width
- Applying a decreasing pitch angle from hub to tip (with an accidental but correct twist)
- Re-drilling hub mounting holes to achieve a shallower blade angle
- Collapsing blades inside the backing pipe for storage and transport
