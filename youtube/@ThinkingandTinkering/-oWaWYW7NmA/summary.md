## Overview
Robert shares his astonishment at discovering the Elegoo 3D printer is now available for just $127.99, using it as a springboard to address community concerns about 3D printing being financially out of reach. He systematically refutes objections around machine cost, filament price, software, electricity, and the learning curve, while honestly conceding that laser cutters are a different matter — and commits to demonstrating that laser-cut projects can be replicated with traditional hand tools.

## Key Topics
- The surprisingly low current price of Elegoo 3D printers (~$127.99 / ~£127)
- Rebuttal of cost-barrier arguments against 3D printing adoption
- Comparison of filament cost versus the rising cost of timber
- Free CAD software options, particularly TinkerCAD
- Electricity consumption of a 3D printer vs. other workshop tools
- The 3D printing learning curve and why Robert considers it manageable
- Laser cutter costs and why they are a more legitimate accessibility concern
- Plans to recreate a crystal generator project using only traditional hand tools, as a counterpoint to laser cutting dependency

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA filament** — the most common FDM printing material; Robert notes it is not oil-based, countering an environmental concern raised in comments
- **Acrylic** — the material used in the crystal generator project, and the planned medium for the hand-tool remake
- **Resin** — mentioned briefly; Robert avoids resin printers because of the smell and the fact that his printers are inside his home

## Techniques and Methods
- **FDM (filament) 3D printing** — primary fabrication method discussed throughout
- **Laser cutting** — used to produce the original crystal generator; acknowledged as a more expensive and less accessible tool
- **Traditional hand-tool fabrication** — Robert commits to remaking the crystal generator using conventional tools to demonstrate the laser cutter is not strictly necessary
- **Fretsaw work** — specifically mentioned as the skilled hand equivalent of laser cutting; noted as genuinely challenging to learn

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was active in the comments and added several substantive points:

- **On his workshop situation**: He revealed he can no longer afford a traditional workshop (£1,800/month to rent the building alone) and has shifted to 3D printing out of necessity — *"it's more like I have swapped cake for bread."* This gives important personal context to his advocacy.
- **On accessibility and priorities**: He found a Creality Ender 5 for NZ$80 on eBay New Zealand and pushed back on affordability claims, noting people often make these comments while typing on phones or computers that cost more — *"I think it's about priorities more than anything."*
- **On laser cutting by hand**: Confirmed it is entirely possible — *"anything done on a laser cutter can be done by hand — there are only two real differences: you don't need the saw skills — and good fretsaw skills are a challenge to learn — and you don't need the time."*
- **On software**: Recommends TinkerCAD as a genuine starting point before attempting Fusion 360; agrees Blender has a steep learning curve but is good once learned.
- **On resin printing**: Avoids it because it's too "stinky" and his printers live inside his home; uses filament only.
- **On PLA and the environment**: Argues 3D printing *decreases* waste relative to traditional methods, and since PLA is not oil-based, environmental objections reflect personal bias rather than fact.
- **On keeping up with technology**: *"If they really want empowerment — they should keep up — not sit in the past and whine about it."*
- **Directed viewers to video 2293** for the already-completed laser-vs-hand-tools comparison.
- **On NiFe batteries**: Acknowledged he made them and they are not difficult, but has moved on to avoid endlessly repeating content — *"if you don't move on you don't grow."*
