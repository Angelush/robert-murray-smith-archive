## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a 5 kW solid fuel wood-burning stove into an alcohol burner using a simple bean tin filled with liquid fuel. The conversion is motivated by European Ecodesign 2022 regulations, the high cost of installing a fixed flued stove, and the desire for a portable, vent-free heating solution. The key takeaway is that this is an extremely low-cost, accessible adaptation that anyone can replicate.

## Key Topics
- Why a wood stove was converted to an alcohol burner (cost, regulations, portability)
- The simple bean tin burner design and how it sits inside the stove body
- Lighting and extinguishing the flame safely
- European Ecodesign 2022 (Ecodesign) regulations on solid fuel appliances
- Ventilation and safety requirements (CO/CO₂ monitoring)
- The popularity of ethanol stoves (17 million sold in the US)
- The concept of using the stove's steel mass as a thermal store (mass heater)
- Potential future improvement: a fuel feed system

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — used as the primary demonstration fuel in the bean tin burner
- **Ethanol** — listed as a suitable alternative fuel; also the basis of commercial ethanol stoves
- **Isopropanol (IPA)** — listed as a third suitable alcohol fuel
- **Steel** — construction material of both the outer stove body and the inner fluid-tight container
- **Bean tin (large steel can)** — used as the fuel vessel/burner

## Techniques and Methods
- **Bean tin burner** — filling a large steel can with alcohol and placing it inside the stove firebox as a simple open-pool burner
- **Metal snuffer plate** — a sliding metal plate used to extinguish the flame by oxygen starvation
- **Mass heating** — using the large steel body of the stove to absorb and radiate heat
- **Fuel swapping** — replacing spent tins with pre-filled ones rather than refilling a hot container
