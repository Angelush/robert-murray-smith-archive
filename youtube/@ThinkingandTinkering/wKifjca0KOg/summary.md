## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith upgrades his no-weld plumbing-fitting rocket stove (from videos 1743/1744) by replacing the standard steel chimney section with a 1.5-meter fireproof glass tube sourced from patio heater replacement parts. The experiment is a success — the glass survives the heat, and for the first time Robert gets a clear view of the rocket stove's internal flame behaviour, revealing a striking vortex/spiral effect inside the chimney tube.

## Key Topics
- Retrofitting an existing no-weld rocket stove kit with a transparent glass chimney
- Fabricating a steel support ring to hold the glass tube in position
- Observing and discussing the unexpected vortex flame behaviour visible through the glass
- Practical use as a decorative patio heater
- Potential future modifications: pellet hopper feed, air injection vents

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Fireproof/tempered glass tube** — ~1.5 m tall, 100 mm diameter; sourced from Amazon as a "glass tube for patio heater" replacement part; rated to 800–900 °C
- **4-inch plumbing fittings** — the existing no-weld rocket stove chassis
- **Mild steel bar** — rolled into a support ring to seat the glass tube
- **Silicate sealant** — used to seal the glass tube into the steel collar at the base
- **Wood fuel** — used to fire the stove during the test

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ring fabrication** — a length of steel bar (~33 cm) cut with an angle grinder and rolled into a circular collar to support the glass tube
- **Welding/bolting** — the ring is fixed inside the existing plumbing fitting to create a ledge for the glass to rest on
- **Silicate sealing** — sealing the base of the glass tube with high-temperature silicate sealant
- **Live fire test** — stove lit outdoors with safety glasses worn in case of glass failure

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Vortex / swirl**: Robert highlights the vortex as a genuine surprise — he had never had such a clear view of the flame before, normally only peering down the chimney ("that's why I have no fringe").
- **Air holes / vents**: He was already thinking about adding air injection holes to the steel collar (not the glass — drilling tempered glass would shatter it).
- **Pellet hopper**: He mentions wanting to add a pellet hopper for continuous, decorative spiral flames.
- **Rocket stove purpose**: He pushes back on a commenter suggesting the straight-up design is wrong — noting that rocket stoves were originally developed for cooking (heat straight up to a pan), and the heating use-case is a different application.
- **Glass temperature limits**: He cautions that with the glass present you don't want temperatures too high, and suggests clean-burning fuel; he also notes carbon deposits burn off around 400 °C.
- **Sourcing**: The glass tube was found on Amazon using the search term "glass tube for patio heater."
- **Thermal water heating version**: He explicitly says he wouldn't modify *this* unit for water heating — for that he'd use steel/copper cladding and insulation on a separate build.
- **Safety**: Wore glasses but admits he was nervous; draws the line at a full face shield ("I don't want to go blind").
- **Sound**: Notes the roar didn't record well — describes it as low, "a bit like a Sonderhuss tube."
