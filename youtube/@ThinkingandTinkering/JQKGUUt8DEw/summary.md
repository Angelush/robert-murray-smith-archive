## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to build a simple water drop microscope using everyday materials — a technique inspired by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, the 17th-century "father of microbiology" who used water droplets as lenses. The build requires only a torch, a glass jar, strips of plastic, and a hole punch, and results in a functioning microscope capable of viewing microscopic detail. Robert tests it on water from a muddy puddle and successfully captures magnified images, though finds mostly dirt rather than living organisms.

## Key Topics
- Antonie van Leeuwenhoek and the history of the water drop microscope
- Building a DIY water drop microscope from household items
- Using a water droplet suspended in a punched hole as a convex lens
- Testing the microscope on puddle water
- The origins of microbiology in the late 17th century

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Water (clean, for the lens droplet; dirty puddle water as the specimen)
- Plastic strips/squares (used as slides and lens holder)
- Aluminium strip (bent at 45 degrees as a light reflector)
- Glass jar / beaker (to hold specimen water)
- Torch / flashlight (light source)
- Two pens (used as spacers to set focal distance)

## Techniques and Methods
- Hole-punching a strip of plastic to create a lens aperture
- Suspending a water droplet over the punched hole to form a convex water lens
- Reflecting torchlight upward through a specimen using a 45-degree aluminium mirror
- Adjusting focus by raising and lowering one's head relative to the lens
- Capturing microscope images using an iPhone camera held over the lens
