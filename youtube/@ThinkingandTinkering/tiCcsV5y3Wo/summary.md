## Overview
Robert demonstrates the Dynapod Mark 2, a modular pedal-powered platform, being used to charge a mobile phone. He attaches a commercially available hand-crank generator (Gosub Technology F20) to the Dynapod's output shaft via a pulley-and-belt system, and shows that 10 minutes of pedalling can deliver around 20% charge to a fully depleted phone.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to Dynapod Mark 2 and its off-the-shelf, modular design philosophy
- Selection and integration of the Gosub Technology F20 hand-crank generator
- Pulley sizing and belt drive between the generator and the Dynapod output shaft
- Live voltage and current measurements from the generator (regulated and unregulated outputs)
- Real-world phone charging test from 0% battery

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Gosub Technology F20 hand-crank generator (20 W rated, ~£20)
- M6 bolts (used to mount the generator to the Dynapod frame)
- Pulley belt (hand-made by Robert)
- USB cables/ports (two USB outputs on the generator unit)

## Techniques and Methods
- Modular bolt-on attachment of the generator to the Dynapod frame using M6 bolts
- Pulley ratio selection: large pulley on the generator, small pulley on the output shaft, to reduce pedalling resistance
- Hand-made belt fabrication to connect the pulleys
- Multimeter measurement of output amperage and voltage (regulated 5 V output vs. unregulated ~25–30 V output)
- Voltage selection via onboard switch (3, 5, 6, 9, 12 V regulated options)

## Robert's Own Replies
- The Dynapod is time-consuming to build; he notes he has made quite a few and it "is a pain."
- He attempted to turn off the generator's backlight but could not figure out how.
- He acknowledges the phone's charge indicator doesn't give an exact figure but confirms it works; notes the device is advertised as a 20 W generator.
- He prefers to give finished Dynapods away to people who could actually use them rather than sell them.
- He remarks humorously that all the sweat generated while pedalling "basically goes to waste right now" (implying no heat recovery).
- He notes the generator's "Tardis blue" colour with amusement.
- On the resemblance to a standard exercise bike: he suspects that under the cover it looks the same as any other exercise bike.
