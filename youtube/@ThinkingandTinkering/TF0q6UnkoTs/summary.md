## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith provides a practical guide to designing a wind turbine from first principles, arguing that most people approach the problem backwards by starting with a motor. His core advice is to begin with your local wind speed data, use that to determine what size turbine you can realistically build, and only then select a motor that matches the turbine's output torque and RPM.

## Key Topics
- Why starting with a motor is the wrong approach to wind turbine design
- Using local wind speed data as the starting point for design decisions
- Horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWT) vs vertical axis wind turbines (VAWT) — trade-offs for built-up areas
- Using the Omni Calculator tool to determine turbine dimensions and expected power output
- Understanding shaft RPM and torque from the calculator to select an appropriate motor/generator
- Gear ratios needed to match turbine RPM to motor rated RPM
- Sizing support electronics (inverters, charge controllers) based on expected power output

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- DC motor/generator (example: ~3,500 RPM, 0.5 Nm torque, 12V, ~130W, ~£40)

## Techniques and Methods
- Looking up average and maximum wind speed data for your location online
- Using Omni Calculator to compute power output, RPM, and torque for HAWT or VAWT designs given wind speed and turbine dimensions
- Calculating gear ratio requirements (turbine shaft RPM → motor rated RPM)
- Working backwards from available torque through gear ratio to determine required motor rated torque
- Matching motor specs (rated RPM, rated torque) to turbine output to ensure operability
