## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to solder stainless steel in preparation for building a Ghillie (Kelly) Kettle. Using a toilet brush holder as the stainless steel vessel — previously featured in video 1726 as a rocket stove — he shows how to seal its leaking rolled seams to create a watertight container suitable for boiling water. The key takeaway is that stainless steel is surprisingly easy to solder using the right flux and a tin-rich solder, much like soldering copper pipe.

## Key Topics
- Why rolled seams in cheap stainless steel vessels leak and why conventional sealants won't work for water-boiling applications
- Soldering stainless steel as a practical solution
- Making a DIY flux from phosphoric acid and copper carbonate
- Surface preparation and cleaning before soldering
- The broader project goal: combining the soldered vessel with a rocket stove (from video 1726) to form a "Rocket Kettle"

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel toilet brush holder** — the vessel being repurposed as a Ghillie Kettle body
- **IPA (isopropyl alcohol) / acetone** — for degreasing the joint surfaces before soldering
- **Phosphoric acid (75% concentrated, 50g)** — primary acid component of the DIY flux; also noted as present in rust removers and Coca-Cola
- **Copper carbonate (5g)** — reacts with phosphoric acid to form a solderable coating on stainless steel
- **Solder (>50% tin content)** — specified as the minimum requirement; melts at 200–300°C depending on type
- **Steel wool** — for mechanical cleaning of rust or surface spots
- **Hydrochloric acid / zinc chloride** — mentioned as an alternative flux option (not used)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Surface cleaning** — degreasing with IPA or acetone, followed by mechanical abrasion with steel wool for any rust, then a final acetone wipe
- **DIY flux preparation** — dissolving copper carbonate into concentrated phosphoric acid to produce an acid flux that promotes solderability on stainless steel
- **Flux application** — applying the acid flux directly to the joint area prior to soldering
- **Soldering stainless steel** — using a tin-rich solder (≥50% tin) analogous to the technique used for copper plumbing fittings
- **Post-solder cleaning** — rinsing the joint after soldering to remove acid flux residue
