## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to create a bioplastic composite by combining casein (a protein-based plastic derived from milk) with graphene powder made in a previous video. The resulting material is pressed into a mold at 135°C under 20 tons of pressure, producing a dense plate. Testing shows it to be approximately twice as strong as steel by volume and ten times stronger by weight, and capable of stopping a 9mm bullet at 3mm thickness.

## Key Topics
- Recap of protein-coated graphene in water from a previous video (video 1933)
- Casein bioplastic: the earliest known bio-based plastic, made from milk
- Combining graphene powder (0.5% by weight) with casein powder to form a composite
- Hot-press molding of the composite material
- Stab resistance and ballistic resistance testing

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene powder** — derived from a protein-coated graphene-in-water dispersion, dried by centrifuge at 1500 RPM for 45 minutes
- **Casein** — bio-based plastic made from milk; described as the earliest known bio-based plastic
- **Graphene–casein composite powder** — 0.5% graphene by weight added to casein powder, producing a silvery powder

## Techniques and Methods
- **Centrifugation** — 1500 RPM for 45 minutes to dry graphene suspension into powder
- **Casein extraction from milk** — simple home process yielding white powder
- **Powder blending** — mixing 0.5% graphene powder into casein powder
- **Hot-press molding** — powder poured into a steel mold, heated to 135°C, compressed under ~20 tons of pressure
- **Stab resistance testing** — manual stabbing with a sharpened screwdriver (~30–40 attempts)
- **Ballistic testing** — fired a 9mm round at a 3mm-thick plate at a firing range
