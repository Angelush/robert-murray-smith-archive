## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith revisits the concept of "forever batteries," examining two historical examples — the Oxford Bell's Zamboni pile (running since 1840) and the Carpon pile (running since 1950) — and then replicates a modern version of the Carpon cell using graphite and simple electrolytes. He demonstrates that heating the electrolyte increases the cell's output voltage, supporting the hypothesis that a thermal (thermoelectric) mechanism drives these cells, and argues this represents a promising but underexplored avenue for energy generation.

## Key Topics
- The Oxford Bell / Zamboni pile and the debate over its operating mechanism (electrostatic vs. slow corrosion)
- The Carpon pile: construction, history, and operating principle
- Replication of a Carpon-type cell using graphite foil and sodium carbonate
- A second replication using copper chloride solution, graphite (Grafo), and copper electrodes, based on a Chinese research paper
- Thermal effect hypothesis: ion movement and differential carbon conductivity as the source of EMF
- Experimental demonstration that heating the electrolyte raises cell voltage
- Broader research context: graphene water-droplet generators, all-carbon batteries, gas-graphene generators
- The philosophical question of whether these devices are batteries or thermal generators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite foil** — used as electrodes in the Carpon cell replication
- **Sodium carbonate (washing soda)** — electrolyte in the first replication
- **Copper chloride solution** — electrolyte in the second replication (fish-tank setup)
- **Grafo (graphite sheets)** — used as electrodes in the second replication
- **Copper** — used as electrodes in the second replication
- **Aluminium chloride** — electrolyte used in the heated-cell demonstration
- **Platinum and gold** — original electrodes in the historical Carpon pile
- **Sulfuric acid** — original electrolyte in the historical Carpon pile
- **Graphene** — mentioned in the context of related research (water droplet charge generation, gas generators)

## Techniques and Methods
- Building a Type 3 Carpon cell from the French patent (FR 577087) using all-carbon materials
- Connecting the cell across a 10 Ohm resistor and measuring voltage/current output
- Fish-tank replication: 10 sheets of Grafo with copper electrodes in copper chloride solution
- Heating the electrolyte on a hotplate to test the thermal-effect hypothesis
- Measuring voltage change with temperature to confirm thermoelectric behaviour (1.5 V cold → 2.3 V at 35°C)
