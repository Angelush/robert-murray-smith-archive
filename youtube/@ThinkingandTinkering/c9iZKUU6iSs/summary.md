## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple Tesla turbine in roughly ten minutes using CDs, small magnets, and hot glue. He constructs a disc stack by alternating CDs and magnets (aligned north-south) and fits them into a basic plastic case with a tangential water inlet pipe. The experiment culminates in a bonus test: holding a coil near the spinning magnet assembly generates approximately half a volt, proving even this rough build can act as a small generator.

## Key Topics
- Tesla turbine operating principles
- Quick, low-cost disc stack construction using CDs and magnets
- Building a tangential inlet housing from plastic pipe and a CD case
- Testing the turbine under tap water flow
- Generating a measurable voltage (~0.5 V) from the spinning magnet array using a hand-held coil
- Scaling potential: performance improvements with compressed air and better construction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- CDs (used as turbine discs)
- Small neodymium-style magnets (arranged north-south at 60° intervals on each CD)
- Super glue (bonding magnets to CDs)
- Hot glue (attaching the inlet pipe to the case)
- 15 mm plastic pipe (tangential water inlet)
- CD case (turbine housing/spindle)

## Techniques and Methods
- Marking CDs at 60° intervals for magnet placement
- Alternating north-south magnet orientation across stacked CD layers
- Using magnets as self-aligning clamps during glue curing
- Drilling 5 mm holes in the case spindle for flow-through
- Cutting plastic pipe at an angle to create a tangential inlet
- Melting an inlet hole through the case with a hot soldering iron
- Holding a coil against the rotating magnet stack to measure generated voltage
