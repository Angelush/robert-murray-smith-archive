## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests the electric version of his paper bicycle — a bike frame built from rolled-up newspapers — by riding it around a car park. The test is a success: the paper frame holds up under his weight, the gear system works silently and effectively, and the 120W motor gets him moving. He considers the build a genuine win and is already planning a Mark 2 version with a 500W motor.

## Techniques and Methods
- Rolling newspapers into structural tubes sized similarly to steel tubing
- Assembly of a functional bicycle frame from paper tubes
- Integration of a 120W electric motor onto the paper frame

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Frame is the point, not speed:** Robert repeatedly emphasises that his focus was entirely on whether the paper frame could hold his weight — the motor and speed were secondary. He considers any ride at all to be remarkable given the material.
- **Material density comparison:** He notes he made the paper tubes approximately the same cross-sectional size as steel tubes, defending the structural approach to sceptics.
- **Mark 2 confirmed:** He states he is likely to get on with a Mark 2 build.
- **Braking as the weak point:** He identifies the steering column head as the most fragile part, noting that an 80kg cyclist can reach 30–35 kph and braking forces would be the likely failure mode.
- **Videos are about materials and techniques:** He clarifies that the broader video series is really about what can be achieved with unconventional materials — "what can be built from this is just limited by imagination."
- **Defensive of the achievement:** When commenters seemed unimpressed, he pushed back: "do you think you might be focussing on the wrong thing?" and called it a world record since no one else has built one.
- **Open to a build process video:** He acknowledged the build process might be worth documenting in a dedicated video, though noted it would be "a bit long and tedious."
