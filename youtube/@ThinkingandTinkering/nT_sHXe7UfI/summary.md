## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a cheap children's skipping toy purchased at Asda — whose curved hemisphere shape reminds him of an Icewind turbine — into a simple, low-friction air motor rotor. By mounting the toy halves on a bottle-based float and setting it in a water-bearing (a technique covered in previous videos), he demonstrates a nearly frictionless spinning rotor that runs on a brief puff of air, and notes it could easily become a generator or motor with the addition of magnets and a coil.

## Key Topics
- Upcycling/repurposing a disposable children's toy for a functional prototype
- Water float bearings as a low-friction bearing method
- Building a simple rotor from toy hemispheres
- Converting the build into a generator or air motor
- A community motor-building competition Robert is running

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Children's spinning hoop toy (hemispheres, curved shape resembling Icewind turbine blades)
- 250 ml plastic bottle (provides buoyancy/displacement in the water bearing)
- Tin can (water vessel for the float bearing)
- 4 wooden dowels, 38 cm each (frame)
- 2 timber squares, 21 cm each (frame top and bottom)
- 3 mm steel bar (rotor restraint/guide, not a true bearing)
- 6 mm and 4 mm drilled holes
- Water (bearing medium)
- Glue (assembly)
- Magnets and coil (mentioned as optional additions to convert to generator)

## Techniques and Methods
- Sawing and disassembling a toy into usable components
- Drilling to size (6 mm hole in bottle base, 4 mm hole in frame top)
- Water float bearing construction (rotor floats on water inside a tin can for near-zero friction)
- Wooden dowel-and-timber frame assembly
- Using displacement/buoyancy (Archimedes principle) to float and centre the rotor
