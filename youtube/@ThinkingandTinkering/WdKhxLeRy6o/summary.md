## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith scans two medieval wooden grotesques (often called gargoyles) mounted on historic buildings in Canterbury, UK, which he explains were originally used as signs for brothels serving pilgrims. He processes the 3D scans using free and accessible software tools to clean, align, and extract the faces, then 3D prints them. The key takeaway is that historic street art can be digitally preserved and reproduced using consumer-grade scanning and printing tools.

## Key Topics
- History of Canterbury as a pilgrimage city and the medieval context of the grotesques
- Distinction between gargoyles (with drainage spouts) and grotesques (purely decorative)
- 3D scanning the wooden grotesque carvings in situ
- Mesh repair and hole-filling in Revo Scan software
- Model alignment, cropping, and splitting in Windows 3D Builder
- Slicing and 3D printing the models with a Prusa printer
- Uploading the original-scale files to Thingiverse for public download

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar FDM filament (~220g used for the print)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D scanning using a Revo Scan Miraco scanner (referenced in comments as reviewed in video 2091)
- Hole detection and filling in Revo Scan (PC version)
- Mesh alignment and squaring using a reference cube in Windows 3D Builder
- Model splitting/cropping in 3D Builder to isolate the grotesque face from wall geometry
- Slicing in Chitubox/Cura (Prusa slicer referenced) with supports, 15% infill, and a brim
- Exporting and saving in STL format
- Uploading finished scan files to Thingiverse

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Software workflow clarification:** Robert used four programs in total — Revo Scan Studio (on the scanner), Revo Scan (PC), 3D Builder, and Cura. He notes he could have gone straight from scanner to slicer using Prusa's built-in split tool, skipping 3D Builder entirely. He chose to highlight 3D Builder because many users don't know it exists, comparing it to Paint — basic but useful for quick tasks.
- **On Blender:** He acknowledges knowing Blender but deliberately avoided it here, noting it "has no place" in this particular workflow and that different approaches are often "much of a muchness."
- **Scanner model:** Confirmed he used the **Revo Scan Miraco**, reviewed in his video **#2091**.
- **Retired status:** Casually mentions he is "basically retired," offered as context for why he has time for projects like this.
