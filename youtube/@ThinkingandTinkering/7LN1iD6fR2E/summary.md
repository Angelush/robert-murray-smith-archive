## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to synthesize a carbon-doped graphitic carbon nitride (g-C₃N₄) material capable of storing hydrogen at ambient temperature and atmospheric pressure, based on published research papers. The material achieves an impressive 10% hydrogen storage by weight — exceeding the US government's 8% guideline — and can be made at home from cheap, readily available precursors (urea and table sugar) for a fraction of the commercial price.

## Key Topics
- Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C₃N₄) as a versatile material and its many applications
- Carbon-doped g-C₃N₄ specifically optimised for hydrogen storage
- DIY synthesis route from urea and sugar
- Cost comparison: commercial g-C₃N₄ (~$20–$100/gram) vs. DIY (estimated ~$1–2/kg)
- Hydrogen storage and release mechanism (absorbed at ambient conditions, released at ~300°C)
- Potential use as a supercapacitor or battery electrode material (briefly noted)
- Integration into a DIY hydrogen fuel system (storage tank with heater using exhaust gas)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Urea** — primary precursor (~$2/kg fertilizer grade); 150 g used in the recipe
- **Table sugar (sucrose)** — carbon dopant precursor; 15 g used in the recipe
- **Water** — solvent for mixing precursors into a syrup
- **Carbon-doped graphitic carbon nitride (g-C₃N₄)** — the product; stores 10% H₂ by weight
- **Graphitic carbon nitride (undoped)** — reference material made in a prior video
- **Hydrogen** — the gas being stored/released
- **Ammonia** — byproduct of the synthesis process (safety warning given)
- **Lithium** — mentioned as a modifier that reportedly doubles energy density of lithium-ion batteries when combined with g-C₃N₄ (not used here)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dissolving precursors in water** — urea and sugar mixed with sufficient water on a heated stirrer at ~80°C for ~30 minutes to form a homogeneous supersaturated syrup
- **Drying step** — liquid poured into porcelain crucibles ("sugar balls") and heated to ~200°C (transcript says "2 degrees", clearly a transcription error) for one hour
- **Pyrolysis/calcination** — temperature raised gradually to 550°C and held for one hour in a homemade kiln; this drives the polymerisation of urea into the g-C₃N₄ framework
- **Controlled cooling** — kiln switched off and left to cool for several hours after firing
- **Comparison of products** — visual comparison between undoped and carbon-doped g-C₃N₄ to confirm successful synthesis

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief acknowledgements, but several contain useful technical and contextual points:

- **Not his original research**: He explicitly states he is replicating and distilling published papers, and directs people to Google Scholar for the primary literature (e.g., searching "synthesis of graphitic carbon nitride urea").
- **Microwave synthesis feasibility**: He notes the polymerisation reaction window is tight — 520–550°C — with thermal decomposition above 650°C. He suggests wiring a stop-start relay to an Arduino with an IR temperature sensor to make microwave synthesis feasible.
- **Hydrogen absorption is passive**: Confirms the material absorbs hydrogen at atmospheric pressure and ambient temperature — no pressurisation required.
- **HER (Hydrogen Evolution Reaction) potential**: He was thinking about carbon doping raising the electrochemical potential for hydrogen evolution and thus raising cell voltage.
- **Home storage as a starting point**: Emphasises that waiting for ideal conditions means nothing ever gets built — using what works now and iterating is the right approach.
- **Building a complete DIY system**: Mentions he is working toward showing the full hydrogen workflow (kiln → material → welder → tanks) so someone with very few tools can do it all.
- **Priorities comment**: Wryly notes a video on serious hydrogen storage gets a few thousand views while melting gummy bears gets millions — "we may have an issue with priorities."
- **Books available**: Mentions he has written books available from his webshop at `https://secure.workingink.co.uk/working-ink-shop/`
