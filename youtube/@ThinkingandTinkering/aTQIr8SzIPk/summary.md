## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a new carbon-only "superconductive" ink he has developed, which achieves significantly lower resistivity than his previous conductive ink — at the cost of losing that ink's "swag" (shear-thickening) properties. He shows that simple mechanical post-processing (burnishing with cloth, then annealing with a spoon) can dramatically reduce sheet resistance, and outlines his goal of eventually putting this ink into a pen for drawing circuits.

## Key Topics
- Introduction of a new high-conductivity carbon ink, distinct from his earlier conductive ink with "swag" properties
- Sheet resistance measurement using a quality ohm meter and a square probe with clamp
- Three-stage resistance comparison: as-dried, burnished, and mechanically annealed
- Conversion of measured resistance values to ohms per square per mil (standard unit)
- Ink application methods: doctor blade and brush
- Plans to formulate the ink for use in a drawing pen for electronic circuits

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned

- **Cotton fabric (soft cloth)** — used for burnishing the dried ink film
- **Metal spoon / plastic spoon** — used for mechanical annealing (plastic spoon used to rule out metal contamination)
- **Graphite** — cited in comments as the best cost-performance conductive filler
- **Silver** — cited in comments as the best-performing conductive filler but too expensive
- **Ammonia** — suggested in comments as a potential complexing agent to improve the ink
- **Triethanolamine** — suggested in comments as an alternative complexing agent
- **Acetone / IPA (isopropyl alcohol)** — mentioned in comments for cleaning surfaces before coating, to remove grease

## Techniques and Methods
- **Doctor blade coating** — initial film deposition to a controlled thickness (~10 microns)
- **Brush application** — alternative deposition method; makes the ink suitable for pens
- **Burnishing** — rubbing the dried film with a soft cotton cloth to reduce porosity and improve conductivity
- **Mechanical annealing** — pressing the film with the back of a spoon to further consolidate the carbon layer (reduces thickness to ~9 microns)
- **Sheet resistance measurement** — using a square probe and clamp for consistent contact; ohm meter set to low-ohm range
- **Normalization to ohms per square per mil** — multiplying by 10/25 to convert from 10-micron film measurements to a standard 1-mil baseline

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Graphene is the carbon source**: Robert confirms the ink is a graphene ink, not plain graphite.
- **Cost vs. performance trade-off**: For cost-benefit, graphite is the best option; silver performs best but is expensive. This ink sits in between.
- **Independent process testing**: A friend tested the ink under controlled process conditions and achieved **120 milliohms/sq/mil** — better than some silver inks. Robert is awaiting the full test report.
- **Ambition for sub-ohm conductivity**: He is aiming to get the ink below **1 ohm per square per mil**.
- **Formula not shared**: He does not have an exact, shareable formula for the ink.
- **Wetting properties matter**: Adhesion and performance differences on various substrates are attributed to wetting properties.
- **Complexing agents as potential improvement**: Suggests trying ammonia or triethanolamine to modify the ink's chemistry.
- **Surface preparation**: For the waterproof ink variant, wash the substrate with acetone or IPA before coating to remove grease; the water-based variant works on absorbent materials (e.g., leather) but not plastics.
