## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores propane and bio-propane as practical alternatives to petrol, beginning with a demonstration of how trivially simple it is to convert a petrol generator to run on LPG. He then traces the evolution of bio-propane production — from pyrolysis and fermentation of biomass to cutting-edge biological methods using algae and bacteria — concluding that these technologies make propane a genuinely renewable "drop-in" fuel that requires no changes to existing machinery.

## Key Topics
- The storage problem with modern petrol (ethanol blending causes separation over 3–6 months)
- Propane/butane/LPG as a long-shelf-life, cleaner-burning alternative
- How simple a petrol-to-LPG generator conversion actually is
- Conventional bio-propane production: pyrolysis, gasification, fermentation, hydrogenation
- Algae as a new biological source for bio-propane
- Bacteria (*E. coli*) engineered to produce propane at commercial levels
- Propane as a "drop-in fuel" requiring no infrastructure changes
- Robert's broader optimism about emerging energy technologies

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Propane** — the primary fuel discussed; storable indefinitely, clean burn
- **Butane** — mentioned alongside propane as an LPG component
- **LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas)** — mixture used in barbecues and vehicle conversions
- **Ethanol** — blended into modern petrol, causes separation on long storage
- **Petrol (gasoline)** — contrasted with LPG; "dirty burn," short storage life
- **Agricultural waste, plant oils, organic matter** — raw feedstocks for conventional bio-propane
- **Algae** — photosynthetic organisms highlighted as a new bio-propane feedstock
- **Escherichia coli** — bacteria used by Finnish scientists to produce propane at commercial levels

## Techniques and Methods
- **DIY LPG conversion** — dangling a gas hose over a carburettor air intake and securing with tape; engine vacuum draws gas in; air-to-fuel ratio adjusted by throttling the valve
- **Commercial LPG conversion kits** — metal plates with fittings for hose attachment
- **Pyrolysis** — thermal decomposition of biomass to produce propane
- **Gasification** — converting biomass into gas-phase fuels
- **Fermentation** — biological conversion of organic matter into propane
- **Hydrogenation of organic oils and fats** — catalytic conversion to propane
- **Waste conversion** — processing organic waste materials into propane
- **Biological/microbial production** — engineered bacteria (*E. coli*) and algae used as living factories for propane synthesis
