## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues that the 2022 energy/heating crisis can be addressed by reviving historical heating strategies used by our ancestors: rather than heating entire buildings with convection, we should heat *microclimates* around people using radiant and conductive sources. He surveys historical solutions—from hooded chairs to Japanese kotatsus—and suggests modern equivalents like heated seat pads and carbon heaters can achieve the same effect cheaply and efficiently.

## Key Topics
- The false assumption that modern approaches are inherently superior to historical ones
- Convection (forced air) heating vs. radiant and conductive heating
- The concept of "microclimate" heating — warming the person, not the space
- Two physical limitations of radiant heat: inverse square law and radiant asymmetry
- Historical furniture and devices designed to create personal microclimates
- Modern equivalents and the practicality of stationary daily routines (TV watching, desk work)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon** — used in carbon heaters; noted as easy to make and effective at low voltage
- **Ceramic, rubber** — materials used for hot water bottles
- **Ceramic tile, stone, wood** — materials used for foot stoves
- **Metal** — used for bed warmers (pans filled with embers)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Radiant heating** — heat emitted from a source (e.g. open fire) that warms by radiation; diminishes with the square of distance
- **Conductive heating** — direct contact heat transfer (e.g. hot water bottle, heated brick on lap)
- **Microclimate creation via hooded chairs** — traps radiant heat around the sitter; estimated equivalent to wearing a thick jumper and coat
- **Folding screens as thermal barriers** — placed behind seating to reflect radiant heat forward and block cold drafts
- **Inglenook fireplace seating** — seats placed inside the fireplace alcove to maximise radiant exposure
- **Foot stoves (Dutch)** — wooden box with holes containing hot bricks, covered with a blanket for leg/foot warming
- **Kotatsu (Japanese)** — low table with a brazier underneath, heavy blanket draped over the table
- **Bed warming** — metal pans with embers or hot bricks placed inside draped beds to pre-warm the sleeping space
- **Desk radiant heater** — placing a radiant heater under a desk to create a focused microclimate for seated work

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Foot stove revival:** Robert says he is "thinking about a modern version of the foot stove," suggesting a follow-up project.
- **Running cost of seat pad:** He states a seat warming pad costs **1p per hour** to run — a key practical data point not mentioned in the video itself.
- **Mould concerns (repeated):** Several commenters worried that not heating the whole house causes damp and mould. Robert pushes back: *"I don't think so — I think it is just something we say."* He notes he grew up in England (a damp country) without whole-house heating and didn't have mould problems. His advice: dry windows each morning and keep pots of salt around. He specifically mentions calcium carbonate as a damp remedy.
- **Personal lifestyle context:** Robert is 60, grew up in England without central heating, and recalls that as a child his job was to light the fire each morning ("that was cold lol"). He describes himself as a "fat old boy" who doesn't feel cold much, but says his wife, being thin, really feels it — which he uses to illustrate individual variation in thermal comfort.
- **Cultural attitude:** He agrees with commenters that society has "got too lazy and comfortable" and thinks people will be *forced* to adapt — and that this "is no bad thing."
- **Chinese diesel heater:** He mentions having done a teardown video of a Chinese diesel heater and endorses advice given about them in the comments.
- **Dividing rooms in winter:** He suggests using folding screens to partition off smaller areas of a room for winter use.
- **Cold bedroom preference:** He personally likes sleeping in a cold bedroom, saying he sleeps better for it.
