## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues that the key determinant of wind turbine output is not machine efficiency but the energy available in the wind itself — governed by blade swept area and wind speed. Using Betz's Law and a real commercial turbine as a worked example, he shows that a £380 "1 kW" turbine can realistically produce only ~50 watts in average UK wind conditions, and concludes that cost-per-watt is the metric that actually matters, making simple DIY builds far more sensible than expensive high-efficiency commercial units.

## Key Topics
- Energy in wind is set by swept blade area and wind speed — not by machine design
- Betz's Law: maximum theoretical energy extraction is 60% of available wind energy
- Best commercial turbines achieve ~80% of the Betz limit (~48% overall efficiency)
- Worked example: Tesup/Master 940 turbine (0.8 m blade, £380) yields only ~50 W in average UK conditions (4.2 m/s)
- Cost-per-watt as the correct metric for evaluating wind generation
- Diminishing returns: the final 20% of efficiency gains account for ~80% of engineering cost
- DIY builds (repurposed fans, soil pipe, bicycle wheels) as cost-effective alternatives
- The HAWT (horizontal axis) vs VAWT (vertical axis) debate dismissed as largely irrelevant
- UK planning permission rules: vertical turbines require permission; horizontal ones generally do not

- Using an online wind power calculator (Omni Calculator) with blade length and wind speed inputs

- Cost-efficiency comparison: total build cost vs. commercial unit cost per watt generated
