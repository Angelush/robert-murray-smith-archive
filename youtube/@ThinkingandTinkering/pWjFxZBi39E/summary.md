## Overview
Robert demonstrates a simplified approach to building a Joule Thief circuit by replacing the traditional ferrite toroid core with a hand-rolled paper tube wound with copper wire. He then extends the design by adding a second bifilar coil over the first, enabling wireless near-field power transmission to a second LED with no direct electrical connection.

## Key Topics
- Why traditional Joule Thief circuits are tedious to build (toroid winding)
- Using a paper tube as a substitute for a ferrite core
- Winding a bifilar coil on a paper tube
- Identifying and connecting the two coils correctly
- Basic Joule Thief circuit construction (components and wiring)
- Demonstrating the circuit lighting an LED from a 1.2V battery
- Adding a second bifilar coil for near-field wireless power transfer to a second LED
- Credit to Patrick Kelly's book as the source of this technique

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 12 cm wide strip of paper (rolled into a tube ~10 cm inner width)
- 28 SWG enamelled copper wire (bifilar wound)
- 1K resistor
- LED (two used in the extended version)
- 2N2222 NPN transistor
- 1.2V battery (not fully charged during demo)

## Techniques and Methods
- Rolling paper into a tube to act as a coreless coil former
- Bifilar winding: running two wires side-by-side simultaneously (or folding a single length in half)
- Identifying coil ends using a resistance meter or a battery-and-LED continuity test
- Joining the correct ends of the two coils by soldering (cross-connecting)
- Basic Joule Thief circuit assembly (no PCB — freeform/point-to-point soldering)
- Stacking a second bifilar coil over the first for near-field inductive coupling
