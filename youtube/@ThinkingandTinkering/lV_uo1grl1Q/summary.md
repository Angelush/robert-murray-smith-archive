## Overview
Robert demonstrates a simple, low-cost method for making hyperbolic (hyperparabolic) funnel cones by exploiting the natural curve that forms when a stretched fabric sheet is weighted at a central point. He connects this geometry to real-world phenomena including coin wishing wells, Kepler's laws of planetary motion, and Einstein's spacetime curvature. The resulting cones can be used for water vortex experiments or other applications.

## Key Topics
- Spiral vortex coin wishing wells as a familiar example of hyperbolic geometry
- The mathematics of hyperparabolic curves and their occurrence in nature
- Practical fabrication of a hyperbolic funnel using a bed sheet and round frame
- Stabilising the natural curve with polyurethane resin
- Using the finished cone for water vortex demonstrations (inspired by Viktor Schauberger)
- Scalability and variations of the technique

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bed sheet (cotton)** — used as the flexible membrane stretched over the frame; chosen for its elasticity and absorbency
- **Cut-off section of a gas canister** — used as the circular frame (a waistband noted as an alternative)
- **Rubber bands** — to fasten the sheet to the frame
- **6 mm rod** — used to push down the centre of the sheet and form the curve
- **Carb body resin (polyurethane resin)** — painted onto the sheet to harden it and hold the hyperbolic shape; sold for car bodywork repairs
- **Fibreglass** — mentioned as an optional reinforcement for larger cones

## Techniques and Methods
- Stretching a fabric sheet tightly over a circular frame to create a drum-like surface
- Applying a central point load (rod) to naturally generate a hyperparabolic curve
- Pulling out wrinkles to ensure a clean, uniform curve before curing
- Painting resin onto the fabric surface and allowing it to cure to lock in the shape
- Sizing the exit hole to fit a water outlet for vortex flow experiments
