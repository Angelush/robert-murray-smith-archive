## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the construction of a Dirod electrostatic generator/motor — a simpler alternative to the Wimshurst machine — using acrylic plates and aluminium foil tape. After assembling and testing the device, he discovers it generates measurable voltage (around 1.5V) and microamp-level current with no magnets or coils, sparking the idea of a fully printable, stackable, low-cost generator.

## Key Topics
- History of electrostatic generators and their revival (Jeff Ramenko, A.D. Moore)
- The Dirod machine: disc/radial form, operating as both generator and motor
- Constructing the Dirod from acrylic and aluminium foil tape
- Using a paper plan/template system for accurate part registration
- Testing and measuring output voltage and current
- Concept of stacking multiple plates to increase power output
- Potential for a fully printed electrostatic generator
- Sealing the device from the atmosphere to improve performance

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic sheet** — used for the two stationary plates and the rotating disc
- **Aluminium foil tape** — builders' insulation/ventilation tape; used for the conductive sectors on the disc and plates
- **Soft solder (tin wire, 2mm)** — used to make rivets connecting aluminium sectors on both sides of the disc
- **Carbon fibre tow** — used to make the collector brushes
- **Conductive ink** — mentioned as an alternative to aluminium tape for a fully printed version
- **Desiccant** — proposed to be added inside a sealed enclosure to protect from humidity
- **Polyurethane varnish** — mentioned in comments as a possible sealant

## Techniques and Methods
- **Template/plan-based layout** — drawing a precise paper plan to register all parts and cut aluminium sector templates
- **Soft solder riveting** — drilling 2mm holes through the disc, inserting tin wire, and hammer-mushrooming it flat to electrically connect aluminium sectors on both sides
- **Brush fabrication** — carbon fibre tow bent, tied, and inserted into drilled holes to form collector brushes
- **Bolted plate assembly** — four corner bolts hold the two acrylic plates apart with the rotating disc between them
- **Through-bolt electrical connection** — a single bolt connects the collector on the front plate to the collector on the rear plate, forming the high-voltage output point
- **Multimeter testing** — measuring output voltage (volts setting) and current (amps setting) while manually rotating the disc

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Uncertainty about the effect's source:** Robert candidly notes he isn't certain of the mechanism — it may be triboelectric interaction between the acrylic disc and the carbon brush, not pure electrostatic induction. He cautions against drawing firm conclusions before further investigation.
- **Tesla turbine link:** He expresses genuine enthusiasm for the idea of coupling the Dirod with a Tesla turbine.
- **Bearing and torque:** Confirms he did test with a bearing and found the torque to be exceptionally low.
- **Plans video:** Confirms a separate plans/drawing video was completed.
- **Copper vs. aluminium:** Notes copper would work fine but aluminium is cheaper — he's always mindful of cost.
- **Bonetti machine comparison:** Agrees with a commenter that a Bonetti-style machine (no contact) would be relevant, noting it would need a starting charge but that wouldn't be a major issue; confirms surface area matters for output.
- **Conductive ink adhesion:** Reassures a commenter that the ink adheres mechanically well to acrylic based on prior testing.
