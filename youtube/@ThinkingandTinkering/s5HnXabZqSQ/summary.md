## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith answers a frequently asked question: can his conductive ink be airbrushed? He demonstrates the process live, showing how to prepare a thinned, filtered version of the ink suitable for an airbrush with a 2.3 micron nozzle. The video concludes with a sheet resistance measurement confirming the airbrushed film is genuinely conductive.

## Key Topics
- Whether conductive ink can be used in an airbrush
- Formulating a thinner for conductive ink
- Using washing-up liquid as a surfactant/flow aid
- Filtering the ink to remove agglomerated particles
- Airbrush technique (short sharp bursts vs. long bursts)
- Burnishing dried conductive films
- Measuring sheet resistance of the resulting film

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — the subject material being airbrushed
- **Deionized water** — component of the thinner (70% ratio)
- **Isopropanol alcohol (IPA)** — component of the thinner (30% ratio)
- **Fairy washing-up liquid** — added as a surfactant to aid flow through the airbrush
- **Screen printing mesh (230 mesh US / ~63 microns)** — used to filter agglomerated particles from the ink
- **Paper** — substrate used for the demonstration

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ink thinning** — mixing 25 mL ink with 5 mL thinner (70:30 deionized water:IPA) and 25 mL washing-up liquid
- **Gravity/mesh filtration** — pouring the mixed ink through a 230-mesh (~63 µm) screen printing mesh to remove agglomerates
- **Airbrushing** — applying the ink in short, sharp bursts to prevent the needle drying and maintain air pressure
- **Multiple thin coats** — recommended technique (coat, dry, repeat) to build conductivity without runs
- **Burnishing** — post-drying treatment with a plastic spoon to improve film conductivity
- **Sheet resistance (square resistance) measurement** — using a square copper probe clamp at the kΩ setting; result was 2.64 kΩ for a single coat on paper

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief social replies, but two contain substantive content:

- **On graphene (monolayers):** He clarifies that single-layer graphene is not very practical for home experimenters — it requires expensive equipment to use well. His channel is aimed at inspiring home inventors, so he hasn't focused on monolayer graphene, though he hints at something "up and coming" on the topic.
- **On candle-flame graphene:** He notes that graphene is produced in a candle flame but in very small quantities that are difficult to extract.
- **General philosophy:** His reply *"all i do mate is make it up and see what happens — if it doesn't work I change it one variable at a time until it does"* is a concise statement of his experimental method and is worth noting as characteristic of his approach.
