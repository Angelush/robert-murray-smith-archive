## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to create "found art" — a deer's head sculpture — using salvaged parts from a child's bicycle (the seat and handlebars). The piece is inspired by Picasso's famous *Bull's Head* sculpture, and the video doubles as a broader meditation on creativity: that it is a transferable, trainable skill best exercised by staying observant of what's around you rather than seeking specific materials.

## Key Topics
- The concept of found art and its artistic legitimacy
- Picasso's *Bull's Head* as the direct inspiration for the piece
- Kettle's Yard (Cambridge, not Oxford) as a reference point for found art
- Robert's philosophy of salvaging discarded objects and reimagining their use
- Creativity as a "muscle" that strengthens with practice and transfers across disciplines
- The mindset shift from "looking for something specific" to "seeing what you find"

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Child's bicycle (salvaged/found)
  - Handlebars (with brakes attached)
  - Bicycle seat
  - Handlebar stem
  - Seat retaining bolt
  - Seat bracket

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of a salvaged child's bicycle to extract key components
- Inserting the handlebar stem into the seat bracket as a mounting/extension point
- Threading the handlebars through the stem to raise and position them as "antlers"
- Wall-mounting the assembled sculpture as a display piece

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On subjectivity in art:** Robert emphasises that found art works because viewers bring their own interpretation — "you see what you see mate — that's art."
- **On creativity as a life philosophy:** He describes creativity as opening doors: "one door always opens on at least 6 others." He also stresses it is not competitive and should never be used as such.
- **On attitude and perspective:** In a notably thoughtful reply, he writes: *"it's never what it is but always how you look at it"* — framing creative and personal outlook as the root of problem-solving.
- **On his workshop's organised chaos:** He jokes about periodic clean-ups revealing forgotten finds at the bottom of piles, consistent with his salvage-oriented approach.
