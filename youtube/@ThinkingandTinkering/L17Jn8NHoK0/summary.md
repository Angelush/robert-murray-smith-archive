## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to salvage the motor and rollers from a broken belt sander for use in a homemade Van De Graaff generator. He disassembles the sander step-by-step, explaining what went wrong with it (an overheated, melted fan belt) and identifying the key components — rollers, motor housing, pulleys, and roller bearings — that can be repurposed. He notes that a new belt sander can be purchased for around £10 if you don't have an old one to strip.

## Key Topics
- Disassembling a broken belt sander to harvest its motor and rollers
- Identifying and extracting reusable components (rollers, pulley, roller bearings, fan, motor)
- Plans to coat the rollers with different materials to position them correctly on the triboelectric series
- Cost-effectiveness of using a cheap belt sander as a Van De Graaff drive mechanism

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Plastic rollers (from the belt sander, to be coated with triboelectrically appropriate material)
- Melted rubber fan belt (identified as the cause of the sander's failure)
- Roller bearing (salvaged from the sander internals)

## Techniques and Methods
- Mechanical disassembly using screwdrivers and pump pliers
- Using a circlip tool/improvised lever to remove retained components
- Jamming a tool under a roller to counter-torque a seized nut
- Identifying failed components by visual inspection during teardown

## Robert's Own Replies
- Clarifies that the specific sander model was a cheap Chinese import and the exact model is unknown, but it doesn't matter much — as long as the top roller fits inside the top electrode, belt width is not critical. A narrower belt will still allow the generator to reach 350,000–500,000 V; it will just charge more slowly. Maximum voltage is determined by the top electrode diameter, not belt width.
