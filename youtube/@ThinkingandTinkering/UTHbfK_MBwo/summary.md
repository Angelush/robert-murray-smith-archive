## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the O-Wind turbine, a spherical vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) that won the James Dyson Award in 2018, designed by two Lancaster University students. He reverse-engineers the design from its US patent, 3D prints a working model using TinkerCAD, and demonstrates its ability to spin in a single rotational direction regardless of which side the wind hits. He notes significant design changes between the patent and the commercial product being launched via crowdfunding.

## Key Topics
- Background on the O-Wind turbine and its James Dyson Award win (2018)
- Creators: Nicholas Aran and Yasin Nurani, Lancaster University
- Patent analysis: US Patent 20210349 50 A1
- Tetrahedral structure: how four triangular faces interlock
- The Venturi effect and directional fin channels as the two core mechanisms
- 3D printed prototype built from the patent in TinkerCAD
- Physical demonstration using a hair dryer to verify unidirectional rotation
- Differences between the patent design and the forthcoming commercial product
- Skepticism about a "700× better than solar" claim circulating in media

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel bar (8 mm diameter) — used as the axis of rotation in the prototype
- Skate bearings (608 type, 22 mm × 8 mm × 7 mm) — mounted in the foot of the prototype for low-friction rotation

## Techniques and Methods
- Patent analysis and reverse engineering from US patent drawings
- 3D modelling in TinkerCAD to replicate the patented geometry
- FDM 3D printing of the prototype
- Wind testing with a handheld hair dryer to confirm unidirectional rotation behavior

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert notes that the company is partnered with Dyson, and after he published his TinkerCAD model, Dyson's lawyers contacted him to take it down. He found this frustrating since the design is straightforward to replicate and a fair-use argument could apply, but he chose not to pursue the dispute. He implies the legal pressure is applied broadly to anyone replicating the design.
