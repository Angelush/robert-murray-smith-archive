## Overview
This video is a foundational introduction to gear ratios, building the concept from first principles using circles. Robert demonstrates that a gear ratio is simply the ratio of two circumferences — which is identical to the ratio of their radii — and extends this to show how friction wheels, pulleys, belts, chains, peg gears, flap gears, and toothed gears are all expressions of the same underlying relationship. The video concludes with a practical guide to calculating how many teeth fit on a gear of a given size.

## Key Topics
- Circles as the conceptual basis for cogs: center, edge, radius, circumference
- How circumference directly maps to distance traveled in a straight line
- Larger circles travel further per revolution, requiring more energy ("no free lunch")
- Gear ratio defined as the ratio of circumferences, which equals the ratio of radii
- Mathematical proof: circumference = 2πr, so constants cancel and only radii matter
- Evolution of gear mechanisms: friction wheels → belt/pulley → chain/sprocket → peg gears → flap gears → toothed gears
- How tooth pitch (tooth width + hollow width) determines the number of teeth on a gear
- Meshing requirement: teeth on both gears must be the same size
- Counter-rotation: meshing gears turn in opposite directions
- Teaser for the next video: making peg gears and caged gears

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Small plastic flap-gear demonstration models (used to illustrate tooth engagement)

## Techniques and Methods
- Drawing circles with a compass set to a specific radius to establish gear size
- Rolling a circle on paper to unroll circumference into a measurable straight line
- Calculating tooth count: divide circumference by tooth pitch (tooth width + hollow width)
- Practical example given: 15 mm tooth + 16 mm hollow = 31 mm pitch; a 310 mm circumference yields 10 teeth; a 620 mm circumference yields 20 teeth for a 2:1 ratio
