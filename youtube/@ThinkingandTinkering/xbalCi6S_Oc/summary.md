## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates Part 1 of a home synthesis of graphene oxide using a modified Hummer method, working through the low- and mid-temperature stages of oxidizing graphite into graphite oxide and then into a graphene oxide suspension. The video ends with a promising golden-brown graphene oxide solution, with the next steps (sonication, concentration, and reduction to graphene) planned for follow-up videos. His ultimate goal is to produce a low-cost conductive ink for printing electronics.

## Key Topics
- "Jungar (garage) science" philosophy — doing serious science at home
- Graphene properties, particularly electrical conductivity
- The three-stage synthesis pathway: graphite → graphite oxide → graphene oxide → graphene
- Why graphite oxide is easier to exfoliate than graphite (hydrophilic, dissolves in water)
- Safety hazards of working with concentrated acids and strong oxidizers
- Centrifugation and washing as alternatives to filtration for separating graphene oxide
- Sonication as a necessary next step to properly disperse graphene oxide flakes
- Reduction methods for converting graphene oxide to graphene (LightScribe DVD burner, photo flash)
- Application goal: conductive ink for printed electronics

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Deionized/distilled water** — solvent and wash medium throughout
- **Concentrated sulfuric acid (98%)** — sold as drain cleaner; primary reaction medium (~75 mL used)
- **Hydrochloric acid** ("Spirit of Salts") — used as a dilute wash solution after centrifugation
- **Hydrogen peroxide** — listed as 9% at purchase; 90% used to quench the reaction (150 mL in 600 mL water)
- **Sodium nitrate** ("nitrate of soda") — fertilizer-grade oxidizer; ~2.5 g added to sulfuric acid
- **Potassium permanganate** ("potassium manganate") — strong oxidizer, purple crystals; 15 g added gradually over 1 hour
- **Graphite powder** — purchased online; starting material for synthesis
- **Graphite oxide** — oxidized intermediate product; silvery-brown precipitate
- **Graphene oxide** — target product of this video; golden-brown colloidal suspension

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ice bath temperature control** — maintaining reaction below 5°C during the low-temperature stage
- **Controlled reagent addition** — potassium permanganate added at ~1 g per 3 minutes over 1 hour to prevent exothermic runaway
- **Magnetic stirring** — using a home-built stir plate throughout the reaction
- **Water bath temperature ramping** — manually adjusting hot/cold water mix to hold 45°C during mid-temperature stage
- **Reaction quenching** — adding dilute hydrogen peroxide/water to terminate oxidation
- **Centrifugation** — used instead of filtration to separate graphite oxide from solution
- **Successive washing** — dilute HCl wash followed by deionized water washes until precipitate forms
- **Settling and decanting** — allowing larger unreacted graphite particles to sink; pouring off the upper golden-brown graphene oxide fraction
- **Sonication** — described as the required next step to break up large flakes and achieve proper dispersion (not yet completed in this video)
- **Reduction methods (planned)** — LightScribe DVD burner or camera photo flash to convert graphene oxide film to graphene

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Flake size** depends on the size of the starting graphite and on post-processing — over-sonication is notorious for splintering flakes to small sizes.
- **Solubility** of graphene oxide is approximately 2–4 mg/mL depending on the production method and number of attached hydrophilic groups (roughly 1–2 g per 500 mL).
- **Clarification on progress**: at the end of Part 1 he has graphite oxide, not yet graphene oxide — sonication is still required to properly exfoliate and disperse it.
- **Sonication** is essential; GO and graphene tend to re-agglomerate without it or without a dispersant like PVP. A simple bath sonicator (~80 W, 40 kHz) is sufficient.
- **Improved audio version** exists — he kept this video for the rich comment thread but directed viewers to his "improved faster method for making graphene oxide" video (approx. 20 minutes, better sound).
- **Cost**: commercial graphene runs ~£100/gram; his method produces it for ~£1.50/gram.
- **Reduction methods**: he attempted microwave reduction but burnt the sample and sought advice. He also discussed the LightScribe method and photo flash, and was exploring an electrochemical method.
- **CVD (Chemical Vapour Deposition)** detailed recipe shared in comments (McEuen group method using copper foil, hydrogen/methane/argon flows, 1000°C furnace); noted sugar can be used as a carbon source and copper deposition is autocatalytic.
- **Petrik graphene** noted as controversial — likely using a graphite intercalated compound (GIC) with a catalyst rather than plain graphite as claimed in his patent.
- **Goal**: produce a conductive ink to print electronics — he had already made a paper-based radio, speaker, and transformer.
- **Ammonia**: can aid micelle formation and dispersion but is not expected to act as a chemical reductant for GO.
