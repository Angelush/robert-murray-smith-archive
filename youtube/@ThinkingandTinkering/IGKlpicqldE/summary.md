## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to produce methanol from deciduous wood using dry (destructive) distillation. By loading oak twigs into a simple paint-can retort, heating it, and passing the vapour through a copper coil condenser, he collects a water/methanol mixture. The key insight is that plants naturally contain methanol, which can be boiled off at 300–400°C and condensed into a usable liquid fuel.

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Methanol** — the target product, collected as a condensed liquid
- **Oak twigs** — feedstock used for methanol production; other deciduous woods (beech, ash, birch) also mentioned
- **Pine** — used in the previous video; produces syngas rather than significant methanol
- **Syngas components** — methane (~17%), carbon dioxide (~38%), carbon monoxide (~20%), hydrogen (~2%), oxygen (~2%), nitrogen (~17%) — from pine feedstock
- **Microbore copper pipe** (8 mm) — used to form the condensing coil
- **Water** — first fraction collected, discarded before methanol fraction begins

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dry / destructive distillation** — heating wood in a sealed retort to drive off volatile compounds
- **Condensing coil fabrication** — bending 8 mm microbore copper pipe around a 75 mm plastic pipe former to create a coil condenser
- **Fraction separation** — discarding the initial water fraction, then collecting the methanol-rich fraction that begins around 300–400°C
- **Flame test** — dipping a cotton bud in the collected liquid and igniting it; water will not light, methanol burns with a characteristic blue flame
