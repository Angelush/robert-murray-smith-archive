## Overview
Robert demonstrates low-cost hydrogen production via water electrolysis, exploring two enhancements: replacing standard stainless steel electrodes with carbon felt (activated carbon) to increase surface area, and applying an external magnetic field to boost hydrogen output. The key takeaway is that both techniques significantly increase the rate of hydrogen gas production, with the magnetic field effect — reportedly doubling output based on a 2020 paper — being the more surprising finding.

## Key Topics
- The hydrogen economy and why hydrogen is a desirable clean fuel
- Common hydrogen sources: water, ammonia, and urea electrolysis
- Energy costs of splitting different molecules (water > ammonia > urea)
- Role of electrode surface area in electrolysis reaction rate
- Carbon felt as a high-surface-area electrode material
- External magnetic fields as a catalyst-free method to boost hydrogen evolution

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — primary electrolyte medium and hydrogen source
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — alkaline additive to increase solution conductivity
- **Stainless steel** — standard electrode material; corrosion-resistant in alkaline solutions
- **Carbon felt (activated carbon felt)** — 100% carbon material, originally sourced for battery research; used here for its large surface area
- **Stainless steel staples** — used to attach carbon felt to the stainless steel backing
- **Ammonia** — mentioned as an alternative hydrogen source (splits into N₂ + H₂)
- **Urea** — mentioned as another alternative hydrogen source (lower energy to split than water)
- **Neodymium/strong magnets** — externally applied north–south pair to enhance hydrogen evolution

## Techniques and Methods
- **Alkaline water electrolysis** — passing current through stainless steel electrodes in a sodium hydroxide solution
- **Surface area enhancement** — stapling activated carbon felt to stainless steel electrodes to dramatically increase reactive surface area
- **External magnetic field application** — placing strong magnets (north and south poles) on either side of the electrolysis cell to increase hydrogen production rate
- **Electrode spacing reduction** — bringing electrodes closer together to lower resistance and reduce energy draw
