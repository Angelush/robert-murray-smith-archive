## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith gives a concise, skeptical overview of the LK-99 superconductor hype that swept the internet in mid-2023. He explains the long-standing promise and limitations of superconductors, contextualizes the premature viral spread of the LK-99 paper, and argues that the excitement says more about society's hunger for a "quick fix" than it does about the science. His conclusion is that the episode will likely do more reputational damage than good to the researchers involved.

## Key Topics
- What superconductors are and why they matter (zero electrical resistance, Meissner effect)
- The major limitation: current superconductors only work at extremely low temperatures or enormous pressures
- The search for a room-temperature, ambient-pressure superconductor
- The history and naming of LK-99 (originated in 1999, named after its two authors)
- How LK-99 went viral — first in China, then globally — without one author's consent and with admitted errors in the paper
- Replication attempts by ten universities and their mixed/negative results
- The prior scandal of Ranga Diaz (University of Rochester), who retracted a 2020 room-temperature superconductivity claim due to fabricated results
- The societal psychology behind hype cycles: desperation for easy answers
- Financial opportunism — superconductor company stocks spiking on the news
