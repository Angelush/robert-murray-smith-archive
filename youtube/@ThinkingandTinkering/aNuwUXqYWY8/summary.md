## Overview
Robert demonstrates a 3D-printed plate based on the Tusi couple mechanism, intended to replace drive belts in a custom gantry system. He salvages parts from a cheap budget 3D printer and shows a working geared proof-of-concept, with a planned transition to a friction drive. The core insight is that the Tusi couple, Goodman mechanism, and hypocycloid drive are fundamentally the same mechanism in different arrangements.

## Key Topics
- Converting the Goodman mechanism into a 3D printer gantry system
- Disassembling a budget Easy 3D mini printer for donor parts
- Design and printing of a 250×250 mm Tusi couple print plate
- Geared vs. friction drive approaches
- Relationship between the Tusi couple, Goodman mechanism, and hypocycloid drive
- Workaround for restricted print area on the Bambu/Alibabus Centari Carbon printer

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel guide rods (salvaged from budget printer)
- Brass bushings (sliding on silver steel rods)
- 5 mm acrylic (used as print bed in donor machine)
- Plastic clamp components
- Springs and screws (print bed levelling)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of a budget FDM printer for parts harvesting
- Print-in-place 3D printing on a Bambu/Centari Carbon bed (250×250 mm)
- Removing the filament cutter block and resetting print area to zero in slicer settings to use the full bed
- Press-fitting brass bushings into printed parts
- Geared 2:1 drive implementation (40-tooth to 20-tooth gear ratio) as a proof-of-concept before switching to friction drive
- Friction drive design (planned next step, chosen over magnets)
