## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith retrofits one of his previously-built rocket stoves with a homemade biodiesel/vegetable-oil burner insert, using a copper preheater coil and a drilled end-cap jet nozzle. He successfully achieves a roaring flame on the first run using vegetable oil, demonstrating proof-of-concept for an oil-burning rocket stove heater. The video serves as a prototype demonstration, with Robert noting several improvements to pursue in future iterations.

## Key Topics
- Revisiting a previously-built rocket stove and its history (sand battery, Stirling engine experiments)
- The preheating principle common to primus stoves, alcohol lamps, and oil burners
- Designing and fabricating a copper preheater coil and burner head insert
- First test run using vegetable oil as fuel
- Post-run analysis and planned improvements

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Biodiesel** — the intended primary fuel; noted as easier to vaporise than vegetable oil due to lighter molecular weight
- **Vegetable oil** — used as the test fuel for the first run; described as a harder fuel to vaporise
- **Motor oil** — mentioned as another compatible fuel
- **8mm micro-ball copper pipe** — used for the preheater coil; chosen for its bend-friendly properties
- **Kiln-dried sand** — used to fill the copper pipe before bending to prevent kinking
- **Methanol** — briefly clarified in comments as being mostly in the glycerine byproduct and removed from his biodiesel

## Techniques and Methods
- **Preheater coil fabrication** — bending 8mm copper pipe around a tight radius, filled with dry sand to prevent collapse
- **Burner head assembly** — two 90-degree bends with a stop-end cap, hole drilled in the cap to create a jet nozzle
- **Gravity-fed open reservoir** — simple unpressurised oil feed with a control valve; deliberately kept unpowered
- **Wood-fire priming** — using wood in the rocket stove firebox to initially heat the coil until the oil vaporises and sustains combustion

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Safety / flashback concern**: Robert explains why a flame arrestor is unnecessary — the cold, thick oil acts as its own arrestor, and the small drilled hole doesn't allow enough pressure to build for blow-back.
- **Design philosophy**: He explicitly wants the stove to remain unpowered — no blower, no compressed air — keeping it simple and accessible.
- **Jet hole size**: He drilled three holes but acknowledges a single smaller hole might be better; a needle in the hole is another option.
- **Fuel clarification**: His biodiesel had the methanol removed; methanol is mostly retained in the glycerine byproduct.
- **Copper pipe bending**: Confirms kiln-dried sand (not ordinary sand) should be used when bending the pipe.
- **Build time**: The burner insert took about an hour to make, not counting the pre-existing stove.
- **Originality note**: Acknowledges that oil burners and rocket stoves have existed for decades, but argues that a simple slide-in insert to adapt an existing rocket stove to burn oil is a genuinely useful contribution.
