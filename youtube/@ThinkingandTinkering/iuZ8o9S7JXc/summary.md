## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith responds to a critical comment from a subscriber by investigating the real-world performance of commercial small wind turbines, revealing a significant gap between rated power and actual output at typical UK wind speeds. He argues that his low-cost DIY drum-based generator (built from scrap for ~£10) produced comparable output to a £1,000 commercial 1 kW turbine, suggesting that the entire design paradigm borrowed from grid-scale turbines is wrong for small, low-altitude, turbulent-wind environments.

## Key Topics
- Reality check on commercial small wind turbine performance vs. rated specifications
- Power curves: cut-in speed, rated speed, emergency shut-off speed
- Mismatch between grid-scale turbine design assumptions and domestic/rooftop wind conditions
- Turbulent, low-altitude wind vs. consistent high-altitude wind
- DIY drum generator outperforming commercial units at low wind speeds
- Flywheel effect for smoothing turbulent wind output
- Low-torque, high-speed design as the preferred approach for small turbines
- Vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs) as better suited to domestic use
- Philosophy of open investigation over recipe-following; all designs placed in public domain
- Turning negative criticism into productive research

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Scrap wood (used to build the prototype DIY generator, cost ~£10)

## Techniques and Methods
- Reading and interpreting manufacturer power curve data sheets
- Comparing DIY prototype output against commercial turbine power curves at equivalent wind speeds
- Using flywheel/high-inertia drum design to iron out turbulent wind gusts
- Testing generator on a car park floor and car roof at 8–10 mph wind speeds
- Multi-coil configuration (one coil vs. two coils) to increase power output
