## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces tensegrity — a structural principle combining compression and tension — through the lens of the popular "floating table" demonstration. He traces tensegrity's history from 1920s Russian art through Buckminster Fuller and Kenneth Snelson, builds a physical model using 3D-printed parts and magnets, and then broadens the discussion to show how tensegrity structures are being actively investigated for real-world energy generation applications.

## Key Topics
- History and definition of tensegrity (compression vs. tension vs. shear forces)
- Historical milestones: Russian constructivism, Kenneth Snelson's Needle Tower (1948/1960), Buckminster Fuller coining the term
- Real-world tensegrity applications: architecture (1988 Seoul Olympics roof), Kurilpa Bridge (Australia, 2009), NASA's Super Ball Bot (2014)
- Biotensegrity: muscles, skeletal structures, and cell membranes
- How a floating table works mechanically (central cable plus minimum three stabilising cables)
- Building a floating table with 3D-printed parts, string, and neodymium magnets with copper wire contacts
- Tensegrity applied to energy generation: wind turbines, wave energy machines, adaptive solar cells on buildings
- Energy scavenging from tensegrity elements via triboelectric harvesting and linear generators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper wire (fine)** — attached to slots in the 3D-printed structure to serve as electrical contacts
- **Neodymium magnets (4 mm × 15 mm, axially magnetised)** — used in place of string to achieve the tensegrity suspension
- **Metal washers and plastic spacer** — used to set the gap distance between the two magnet-equipped structures
- **3D-printed PLA/plastic** — the two structural discs/cups forming the floating table frame (material implied by printing context)

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — printing two mirror-image structural components to form the top and bottom of the floating table
- **String-based tensegrity assembly** — threading and tensioning three or more cables to stabilise a suspended tabletop
- **Magnet-based tensegrity assembly** — replacing string with attractive neodymium magnets plus spacers to achieve the same floating effect
- **Triboelectric energy harvesting** — using stretching/movement of tensegrity rods or ropes to generate electricity
- **Linear generator integration** — embedding linear generators within tensegrity structural elements for energy scavenging
- **Adaptive solar cell mounting** — attaching photovoltaic elements to the faces of tensegrity structures on building sides
