## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores how simple geometric properties of circles, discs, and cones underpin a surprising range of mechanical engineering devices. Starting from the basic observation that points at different radii on a spinning disc travel at different speeds, he traces this principle through continuously variable transmissions (CVTs), tapered roller bearings, the Evans differential, and competing drive systems. His central takeaway is that rods, cones, and spheres each have distinct strengths and limitations — none is universally best — and that elegant simplicity in geometry gives rise to remarkably complex and useful machines.

## Key Topics
- The geometry of circles and discs: speed difference at different radii
- Disc-based CVTs (as used in lawnmowers)
- Cones as a natural extension of stacked discs; cone rolling in circles vs. cylinders rolling straight
- Car differentials and why outer wheels must turn faster in corners
- Henry Timken and the invention of the tapered roller bearing (~1898)
- Construction and geometry of tapered roller bearings (inner race, roller, outer race)
- Advantages and limitations of tapered roller bearings (friction, misalignment sensitivity)
- The Evans differential as a cone-based CVT
- Cross roller bearings (referenced from video 2326)
- Competing drive systems: NF Drive (spheres), Archimedes Drive (rods), cross roller drive
- Files for the Evans differential available on Thingiverse
