## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the origins of the Rodin coil, rooted in Marco Rodin's "Vortex Based Mathematics" using base-9 number patterns mapped onto a toroid. He takes a pragmatic, skeptical view of the mystical claims around the coil while demonstrating how to wind a flat 12-point star version as a potentially easier alternative to the Fowler Harbur coil. A quick test with rotating magnets confirms it generates a small voltage, though nothing remarkable.

## Key Topics
- Marco Rodin's Vortex Based Mathematics and the significance of the number 9
- Critique of base-9 as "special" — all integer bases produce patterns
- Modular arithmetic and how it generates the repeating number sequences used in Rodin's mapping
- Mapping the Rodin number table onto a toroid to derive coil winding patterns
- The flat "star coil" projection of the toroid Rodin coil (12-point, 30-degree geometry)
- Step-by-step winding method: counting 6 pins forward (including current pin) repeatedly
- Practical construction: wooden disc, drilled holes, plastic shelf inserts as pin protectors, cotton string + superglue, hot glue center
- Quick electromagnetic test with a rotating magnet turntable

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wooden disc (substrate for the flat coil)
- Thin wire (for winding)
- Plastic shelf inserts (as pin guards/bobbins)
- Washers and nuts (to secure pins)
- Cotton string (to tie off nodes)
- Super glue (to harden cotton string knots)
- Hot glue (to fix a central wooden disc)
- Magnets (north-south alternating, on a turntable for testing)
- Resin (mentioned as the intended final step to stiffen the coil)

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling 12 equidistant holes at 30-degree intervals in a circular wooden disc
- Rodin winding sequence: start at pin 1, count forward 6 (inclusive) repeatedly until returning to pin 1
- Securing wire with tape at the back before winding
- Tying nodes with cotton string and applying super glue to lock them
- Hot-gluing a central wooden disc to stabilize the coil before removal from the jig
- Electromagnetic induction test: holding the coil above a spinning magnet turntable and measuring millivolt output
