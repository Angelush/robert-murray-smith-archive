## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a 2024 paper from UC Santa Barbara researchers who designed a passive, power-free roofing tile that automatically switches between heat-absorbing and heat-reflecting modes using a wax motor actuator. The system combines a super-black coating, a radiative-cooling white paint, and a wax-powered mechanical linkage to cut household heating and cooling energy use by up to 2.5× compared to static alternatives. Robert highlights the elegance and robustness of the design and notes it is accessible enough for DIY experimentation.

## Key Topics
- The scale of domestic energy use for heating and cooling (~55%)
- Wax motors (linear actuators): how they work and where they are found
- Vantablack and competing super-black coatings as solar absorbers
- Purdue University's radiative cooling "whitest white" paint
- The UC Santa Barbara switchable roofing tile — design, operation, and results
- Potential longevity of wax-motor-based roof tiles (decades)
- Accessibility of the concept for DIY experimentation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Wax motor** — copper cup filled with wax; expands 5–20% on melting to drive a linear actuator
- **Vantablack** — super-black coating absorbing 99.96% of UV, visible, and infrared light; used as the heat-absorbing side of the tile
- **Radiative cooling white paint** — developed by Purdue University; reflects heat back to space; used as the heat-rejecting side of the tile
- **Copper** — material of the wax motor cup

## Techniques and Methods
- Wax-motor-driven pivot-and-linkage mechanism to flip a louvre between black and white faces
- Dual-sided surface coating: one face painted super-black (solar absorber), opposite face painted radiative white (solar reflector)
- Passive thermal actuation — no electronics or external power required; ambient temperature alone drives the switching
- Fabrication of a 100 mm × 100 mm (4 in × 4 in) prototype roofing tile
