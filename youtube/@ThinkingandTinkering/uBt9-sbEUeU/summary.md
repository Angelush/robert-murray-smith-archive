## Overview
Robert receives a donated air compressor unit from the team at Canterbury Automotive, disassembles and cleans it, then connects it to his human-powered "Dynapod Mark III" pedal cycle. The experiment successfully builds measurable air pressure in the 150-litre tank, demonstrating that pedal power can charge a compressed air vessel as a form of mechanical energy storage.

## Key Topics
- Receiving and inspecting a donated compressor unit from Canterbury Automotive
- Disassembly and diagnosis of the compressor (motor burn-out suspected, compressor head confirmed functional)
- Cleaning and reassembling the compressor head
- Connecting the compressor to the Dynapod Mark III pedal-powered machine
- Building compressed air pressure via human pedal power
- The concept of a compressed air tank as a "mechanical battery" — storing energy in pneumatic form
- Identified limitations and planned improvements (gear ratio, belt quality)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Plastic piston rings (inside the compressor head)
- Inner tube (used as an improvised fan/drive belt)

## Techniques and Methods
- Manual compression test (spinning the flywheel by hand to verify piston and valve function)
- Belt-drive power transmission from pedal cycle to compressor
- Pressure gauge monitoring to track energy stored in the tank
