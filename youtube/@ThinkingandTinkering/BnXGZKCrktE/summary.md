## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to extract and purify casein — the protein found in milk — and use it to create imitation ivory, lacquers, paints, and glues. Prompted by news of British farmers being paid below cost for milk, the video argues that casein-based materials represent a viable, historically proven alternative to petrochemical plastics. The key insight is that purification steps (typically skipped in other online tutorials) are what determine whether the resulting plastic is high quality or crumbly and poor.

## Key Topics
- Historical context of casein plastics before and after World War II
- Why most milk-plastic YouTube tutorials produce poor results (insufficient purification)
- Casein extraction from skimmed milk powder using acid precipitation
- Purification via alkaline dissolution (ammonia) and solvent washing (methanol/ethanol)
- Making imitation ivory by molding purified casein
- Making casein lacquer/paint by dissolving casein in an alkali
- Hardening casein with formaldehyde (formalin) to form cross-links
- Historical material Galalith (commercial casein plastic)
- A 1907 Cornell University book on casein recipes as a reference

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Skimmed milk powder** — preferred source due to low fat content; mixed at 60g per 600mL water
- **Citric acid** — used as the precipitating acid (1 tsp in 100mL water); vinegar and lemon juice cited as alternatives
- **Hydrochloric acid** — mentioned as the industrial standard precipitating acid (easily neutralized with sodium bicarbonate)
- **Ammonia (35% household cleaner)** — used to dissolve casein for purification; 10mL per batch
- **Sodium bicarbonate, sodium carbonate, sodium hydroxide** — cited as alternative alkalis for dissolving casein
- **Methanol** — used for solvent-wash purification of raw casein to remove salts and fat
- **Ethanol** — cited as an alternative to methanol for the solvent wash
- **Formalin (40% formaldehyde solution)** — used to cross-link and harden the final casein plastic; discussed with safety caveats
- **Glycerol** — mentioned in comments as a humectant additive to control plasticity
- **Honey** — mentioned in comments as an alternative humectant
- **Graphite** — used to stain the imitation ivory piece in the demonstration
- **Ammonium ferric chloride** — speculatively mentioned in comments as a possible alternative cross-linker

## Techniques and Methods
- **Acid precipitation** — adding a weak acid to hot milk to denature and coagulate casein micelles into clumps
- **Filtration/picking** — physically separating precipitated casein from the whey liquid
- **Alkaline dissolution** — dissolving raw casein in ammonia or other alkalis to purify it, then re-precipitating with acid
- **Solvent washing** — stirring casein in methanol or ethanol for ~5 minutes to displace water and dissolve out residual fats and salts
- **Hot-water kneading** — working casein in near-boiling water (repeatedly microwaved) to intertwine protein fibers into a cohesive, rubbery plastic mass, analogous to making bread dough
- **Mold pressing** — shaping the plastic casein mass into a mold and leaving it to air-dry
- **Formalin cross-linking** — submerging the shaped piece in formalin; soak time is proportional to piece thickness
- **Film casting** — spreading dissolved casein (in alkali) on glass and drying to produce a transparent plastic film for lacquers and paints

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On acid choice:** Any acid works; "best" depends on context — acetic acid (vinegar) is best for home use, sulphuric acid for commercial scale due to cost and waste-stream considerations. He emphasises that only the experimenter can determine what is most appropriate through their own testing.
- **On air drying vs. formalin:** Air drying does make the material hard but does not waterproof it; formalin cross-linking is needed for waterproofing.
- **On humectants:** He uses glycerol as a humectant (roughly 5% by volume as a starting point) and has found glycerol and honey both work well; he adjusts ratios by feel based on desired plasticity.
- **On Galalith:** Casein plastic was popularised commercially in the 1920s for faux jewellery under the trade name Galalith; it uses formaldehyde and high pressure, and companies still sell it in rods and sheets. It is particularly suitable for knife handles and lathe-turning.
- **On purification being essential:** Without purification, the finished piece will rot. He recommends leaving it overnight at minimum, but encourages experimentation to find the right time.
- **On proteins other than casein:** He speculates that gelatine could be substituted (both are polypeptides) and sees no fundamental reason it would not work.
- **On the 1907 book:** He mentions that a companion upload of the book was being completed (~100 pages on casein paints) and credits a collaborator ("Steve") for the work on it.
- **General tone:** Consistently encourages viewers to experiment themselves, framing failures as learning opportunities and citing the etymology of "re-search" ("look again").
