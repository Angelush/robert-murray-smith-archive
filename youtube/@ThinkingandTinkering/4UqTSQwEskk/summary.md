## Overview
Robert announces and demonstrates Version 2 of his conductive ink, sold through his shop. The new formulation delivers a major improvement in conductivity — dropping from ~36 ohms to ~8 ohms in a square-probe thin-film test — while also being tougher, more water-resistant, and better at adhering to plastic surfaces like UPVC.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of Version 1 conductive ink and motivation for improvement
- Conductivity comparison between Version 1 and Version 2 using a square probe measurement
- Improved physical properties of Version 2 (durability, water resistance, plastic adhesion)
- Availability of Version 2 in the shop

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink (Version 1 & Version 2)** — the primary subject; a carbon/graphite-based conductive paint sold commercially
- **UPVC** — used as a substrate to demonstrate improved plastic adhesion
- **Wax paper** — used as a substrate for conductivity testing
- **Manganese dioxide** — mentioned in comments as an additive to create a mixed thermal-coefficient ink with built-in temperature self-limiting behaviour
- **Water** — confirmed in comments as the thinner for the ink

## Techniques and Methods
- **Square probe (four-point-style) measurement** — used to characterise sheet resistance of the thin-film ink coating; probe spacing equals contact length for a normalised "ohms per square" reading
- **Meyer rod / tape-and-roller technique** — mentioned in comments as a way to lay down a controlled-thickness film for reproducible testing
- **Painting onto substrates** — air-dry application onto wax paper, UPVC, and other surfaces; lacquering over the dried ink noted as a method to improve humidity resistance
- **Silk screening** — confirmed in comments as a viable deposition method; Robert notes that film thickness directly controls resistance

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Store status:** Robert later clarified he no longer runs the store.
- **Version 1 discontinued:** Only Version 2 is now produced.
- **Manganese dioxide additive:** A significant technical insight — mixing MnO₂ into the ink introduces both positive and negative thermal coefficient materials, creating a self-limiting temperature behaviour. The maximum temperature can be tuned by adjusting the MnO₂ ratio, making it much safer for heating applications.
- **Temperature range:** The ink can handle up to ~400 °C under strain; 100 °C is no problem.
- **Thinning:** Water is the thinner.
- **Application notes:** Paint on and air dry; lacquering over the dried ink reduces humidity sensitivity; silk screening works; thickness governs resistance (resistance is geometric, resistivity is a material constant).
- **Shipping:** During COVID, shipping to India was suspended due to high rates of lost packages.
- **Future content:** Robert indicated plans for a follow-up video on ink formulation details (without fully disclosing the recipe) and demos using the new ink.
