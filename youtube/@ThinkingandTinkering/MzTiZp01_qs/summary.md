## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a method for synthesizing copper nanoparticles with a narrow size distribution, suitable for use as inkjet-printable conductive ink. The process uses ethanolamine ligands to complex copper ions in solution, which are then reduced by ascorbic acid to form a stable colloidal suspension. The resulting ink can be printed and then photonically sintered (flash sintered) to produce conductive copper traces on paper or other substrates.

## Key Topics
- Synthesis of copper nanoparticles with narrow size distribution
- Role of ethanolamine ligands in complexing copper ions
- Use of ascorbic acid as a reducing agent
- Formulating the nanoparticle suspension as an inkjet-compatible ink
- Photonic (flash) sintering to convert printed ink into conductive copper traces
- Troubleshooting: recognising and correcting over-addition of copper salt

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ethylene diamine** — one of two amine ligands used to complex copper ions
- **Monoethanolamine (MEA)** — second amine ligand; 75 mL used in the demonstrated batch
- **Ascorbic acid** — reducing agent; 10 g dissolved in 150 mL water
- **Copper metal-organic precursor** — copper salt used by Robert; copper chloride or copper sulfate noted as alternatives
- **Water** — solvent base (150 mL)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Ligand complexation** — ethanolamine amines complex copper ions to keep them in a clear, stable solution before reduction
- **Chemical reduction** — ascorbic acid reduces copper ions to copper(0) nanoparticles at ~50 °C under stirring
- **Colloidal stabilisation** — controlled addition of copper salt prevents aggregation and partial oxidation; turbid yellow colour signals over-addition
- **Inkjet printing** — the stable colloidal suspension is loaded directly into an inkjet printer; multiple overprints recommended to build up sufficient copper thickness
- **Photonic sintering (flash sintering)** — xenon flash unit used to sinter printed copper nanoparticles into conductive traces; resistance reduced from effectively infinite down to ~130 kΩ in five flashes in a prior experiment

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Temperature and concentration matter:** different temperatures and concentrations will affect the outcome; he suggests reviewing a related video using glycol as an alternative approach.
- **Reduction is essential:** the copper salt must be chemically reduced to copper(0); it does not deposit as copper on its own.
- **Simple recipe shortcut:** ascorbic acid and copper sulphate are the core ingredients when asked for a minimal version.
- **Why the amines?** The two amines (ethylene diamine and monoethanolamine) complex the copper ions to improve copper ion concentration in solution; ammonia works the same way but is too malodorous to use practically.
