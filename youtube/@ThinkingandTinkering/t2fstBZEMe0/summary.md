## Overview
Robert demonstrates a multi-cylinder version of a Frenette friction heater built from cheap, off-the-shelf materials — specifically a stainless steel vacuum cup bought for £1 and a 250 ml paint tin. By nesting three concentric cylinders (inner, middle, outer) and spinning the middle one in oil, he doubles the active surface area compared to his earlier single-cylinder design, achieving 43°C in just 3 minutes versus 5–6 minutes previously.

## Key Topics
- Frenette friction (viscous drag) heater concept revisited
- Multi-cylinder design to utilise both inner and outer surfaces simultaneously
- Sourcing accessible, low-cost components (pound-shop items)
- Assembly, sealing, and centering techniques
- Performance comparison with a previous single-cylinder prototype

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel vacuum cup** — sourced from a pound shop; provides the inner/outer cylinder pair with fine tolerances
- **250 ml paint tin** — used as the third (middle) cylinder
- **Oil** — working fluid filling the narrow gap between cylinders to generate viscous friction heat
- **Silicone sealant** — used to seal the assembly and prevent oil leaks
- **6 mm bolt** — used to bolt the stator and spread load
- **Repair washers** — used to clamp thin metal and distribute clamping load

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting tops off metal containers with a Dremel (hacksaw or scissors as alternatives)
- Using tape wrapped around a component to achieve a snug, centered fit inside a larger cylinder before drilling
- Drilling a 6 mm center hole through all layers simultaneously to ensure alignment
- Silicon sealant application to seal gaps and prevent oil leakage
- Thermocouple monitoring to measure temperature rise over time
- Chucking the rotor assembly into a drill/lathe to spin it
