## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to recycle ABS plastic from old computer cases and monitors into usable flat sheets. He walks through smashing, chipping, and pressing the plastic using basic tools and a heated steel plate. The result is a roughly 1mm thick sheet of reclaimed ABS suitable for making models or enclosures.

## Key Topics
- Identifying ABS plastic in discarded electronics (monitors, computer cases, equipment enclosures)
- Smashing and pelletizing plastic using a food processor
- Melting and pressing chips into flat sheet material
- Practical notes on fumes, safety, and water-softening as an alternative method
- Potential uses: model-making, molds, injection molding, 3D printing filament context

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — the primary plastic recovered from computer/monitor casings; softens at ~80°C
- **Styrene plastic** — mentioned as an alternative type found in similar electronics enclosures
- **Aluminium foil/sheet** — used as a clean surface layer on the steel pressing plate

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mechanical smashing** — breaking the plastic case with a hammer
- **Pelletizing/chipping** — processing smashed pieces in a semi-industrial food processor (glass or metal bowl recommended, not acrylic)
- **Heated plate pressing** — spreading chips on a hot steel plate until sticky/squidgy, then pressing in a manual press to form sheet
- **Sawing** — trimming the cooled sheet to square it up
- **Water softening** — discussed as an alternative (softens plastic, reduces fumes) but rejected for sheet-making due to poor bonding after pressing
