## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to create conductive plastic coatings by dissolving polycaprolactone (PCL) in dichloromethane (DCM) and mixing in carbon/graphite powder, as an alternative to carbon-black-filled HDPE which had become hard to source. The coating is applied to metal electrodes (such as stainless steel mesh) via dip-coating, building up layers until the desired thickness is achieved. The key principle is the percolation threshold — finding the minimum amount of conductive additive needed to form a continuous conductive network within the dried plastic matrix.

## Key Topics
- Supply chain issues with carbon-black-filled HDPE during COVID-19 and the motivation for finding alternatives
- Dissolving plastics in solvents to create a dip-coating medium
- Percolation threshold: the concept of minimum conductive filler needed for conductivity
- Composite material behavior: balancing plastic and graphite properties by varying filler percentage
- Binary search method for finding the optimal filler concentration
- Dip-coating technique for applying conductive coatings to metal electrodes
- Safety considerations when working with toxic solvents like DCM

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polycaprolactone (PCL)** — the plastic binder, 100 g used; chosen because it dissolves easily in DCM
- **Dichloromethane (DCM / dichloromethane)** — solvent for PCL, 500 mL used; noted as fairly toxic, requires fume cupboard
- **Carbon / graphite powder** — conductive additive, 50 g used (30% by weight of solids)
- **Carbon-black-filled HDPE** — the preferred material Robert normally uses for electrodes, unavailable due to supply issues
- **ABS (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene)** — mentioned as an alternative plastic that dissolves in acetone
- **Acetone** — mentioned as solvent for ABS
- **Cyanoacrylate (crazy glue)** — mentioned as a previously demonstrated binder for graphite; noted as smelly and fast-setting
- **Stainless steel mesh** — substrate being coated in the demonstration
- **Steel rod** — used as a stirring/dipping tool

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dissolution of plastic in solvent** — PCL dissolved in DCM over ~8–9 hours with periodic shaking to produce a thick syrup
- **Pre-wetting carbon powder with solvent** — adding a small amount of DCM to the carbon before combining with the plastic syrup, to prevent lumping and aid dispersion
- **Dip-coating** — immersing stainless steel mesh in the mixture, withdrawing, and allowing the solvent to evaporate (~20 minutes per cycle); repeated to build up coating thickness
- **Binary (bubble) search for percolation threshold** — starting at 40% filler, halving or splitting the range iteratively until the minimum conductive concentration is found
- **Viscosity control** — adjusting solvent quantity to achieve a consistency that coats evenly without running off or being too thick to apply

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Solvent identification**: Confirmed the two key materials are dichloromethane and polycaprolactone.
- **PCL solubility**: Noted that PCL dissolves very easily in DCM, and offered to help a commenter who received the wrong material.
- **Graphite mixing limit**: Highlighted a practical constraint — it is hard to mix more than ~3% graphite into some systems because above that concentration graphite acts as a lubricant and prevents proper mixing.
- **Viscosity concerns**: Acknowledged a commenter's suggestion but flagged viscosity as a potential challenge.
- **No pre-made product**: Explicitly stated he won't sell the finished coating — the point is for makers to mix their own, since the resulting coating will differ based on their process.
- **Application context**: Added that the coatings are useful for protecting electrodes in caustic environments.
- **General philosophy**: Emphasized experimentation throughout — "the answers here are really just experimentation" — and noted "there are a million ways of doing anything; each method is just another tool in your armory."
