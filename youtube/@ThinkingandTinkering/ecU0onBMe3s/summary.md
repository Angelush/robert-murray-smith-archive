## Overview
This video explores how the widespread adoption of coal during the Industrial Revolution transformed domestic life in Britain far beyond its industrial applications. Robert argues that coal fundamentally changed how people cooked, what food tasted like, and how household labour was organised — with particular impact on women. The video concludes by connecting these historical shifts to the modern challenge of transitioning away from fossil fuels.

## Key Topics
- The shift from wood to coal as the dominant domestic fuel during the urbanisation of the 19th century
- How coal's combustion properties (sooty smuts vs. wood ash) forced a complete rethinking of cooking
- The transition from open-fire cauldrons to enclosed cooking ranges, and what was lost in flavour and culture
- Coal's role in dramatically increasing the burden of domestic cleaning and laundry
- The social impact of coal on women's roles in the home
- The parallels between the coal transition and today's energy transition away from fossil fuels

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Coal** — the primary fuel discussed; burns hotter and longer than wood but produces greasy, snowflake-like soot "smuts"
- **Wood** — the prior domestic fuel; burns to ash with a pleasant flavour when in contact with food
- **Carbolic soap** — required to cut through coal smuts; only effective when activated with hot water, which itself required burning more coal

## Techniques and Methods
- **Open-fire roasting** — meat cooked directly over flame, absorbing smoke flavour and sealing in juices; contrasted unfavourably with oven baking
- **Cooking range / enclosed oven cooking** — introduced to isolate food from coal smoke; enabled oven and hob cooking but changed flavour profiles
- **Wash-day laundering** — described as a social institution; coal increased its frequency from monthly to weekly and required hot water and carbolic soap for effective cleaning
