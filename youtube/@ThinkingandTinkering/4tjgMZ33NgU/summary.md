## Overview
This video continues Robert Murray-Smith's ongoing "Darwin wind turbine" project, reviewing two community-submitted wind pipe designs. He 3D-printed and tested both designs — one by Tony Jackson and one by Emile Davis — evaluating their hyperbolic funnel geometry and practical buildability, concluding that both work well but each has distinct trade-offs.

## Key Topics
- Community design submissions for the Darwin wind turbine project
- Hyperbolic funnel wind capture geometry and its aerodynamic rationale
- Comparison of two 3D-printed wind pipe designs (Tony Jackson's stacked funnel vs. Emile Davis's sectional ring design)
- Practical considerations: 3D printer dependency, scalability, throat opening size
- Venturi effects and potential future additions (Wells turbines)
- Rotating cowl suggestion from the community (credited to Paul Rogers, who also did a wind simulation)
- LED lighting test to demonstrate turbine output

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D printer filament (PLA implied, not specified)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing at quarter-scale for rapid prototyping and cost reduction
- Hyperbolic funnel geometry for wind capture (directing airflow downward)
- Stacked vs. sectional modular assembly approaches
- Domed top design to reduce air disturbance and improve flow
- Venturi throat constriction to increase wind speed (discussed as a future step)
- Wind-powered LED test to verify turbine performance
- Wind simulation (Paul Rogers' community contribution)
- Hand-building hyperbolic funnels using rubber balloons and string (referenced from prior video)
