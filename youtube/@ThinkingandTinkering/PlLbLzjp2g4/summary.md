## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed portable wind turbine built around a low-RPM brushless DC generator (previously featured in video #1964). The turbine mounts on a standard camera tripod for easy deployment and collapses for transport. Tested on a hillside in winds of 3–4 m/s, it successfully generated enough power to light an LED panel and, with a voltage regulator added, can output a steady 5.2V for charging phones and other electronics.

## Key Topics
- Design and construction of a portable, collapsible wind turbine
- Use of a brushless DC generator optimised for low RPM
- Modular design philosophy (generator compatible with planetary gearbox from video #2009)
- Mounting on a camera tripod for portability
- 3D-printed parts (designed in Tinkercad, files on Thingiverse)
- Field testing and power output measurement
- Voltage regulation for practical USB-level output

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Super glue** — used to bond blades into the hub
- **M4 bolt (50mm)** — fastens the hub assembly
- **Thrust washer (47×25mm, 11mm thick)** — used in the carrier/swivel section
- **8mm steel pin** — forms the rotation axis in the carrier section
- **Bearings** — fitted onto the steel pin in the carrier section

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing parts designed in Tinkercad, shared via Thingiverse
- Blade assembly: press-fitting blades level with a surface before gluing
- Wrong-hand (anti-clockwise) threaded nose cone to secure blades and allow folding
- Current rectification of generator output
- Voltage regulation to 5.2V DC using a dedicated voltage regulator module
- Wind speed measurement with an anemometer during field testing

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Files location**: Thingiverse files are in the video description; Robert was on page 3 of "most popular this month" on Thingiverse at time of posting.
- **Electronics housing**: There is a dedicated space for electronics beneath the rear cap of the turbine body.
- **Voltage output**: The regulator is set to 5V as that suits most charging needs, but it can be adjusted to whatever voltage is required.
- **Power output estimate**: For this blade size, expect roughly 2–5W at wind speeds of 4–6 m/s; links to an online wind turbine calculator (omnicalculator.com).
- **Design status**: The design is essentially complete as a standalone unit; minor future tweaks might include a supercapacitor in-line, refined voltage regulation, and blade length adjustments.
- **Blade noise**: The impressive noise during testing was from the blades moving quickly in light wind, not from the wind itself.
- **Modularity intent**: Robert confirms the modular approach is deliberate so that builds stack on one another over time.
