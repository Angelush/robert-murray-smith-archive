## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a hank drum using two aluminium woks as an accessible alternative to the traditional propane tank method. He cuts vibrating tongues into one wok using angled hole saw cuts, joins both woks together using an aluminium welding rod, and demonstrates the resulting bell-like tone. The video concludes that a hank drum is achievable without welding equipment or a propane tank.

## Key Topics
- What a hank drum is and how it is traditionally made
- Why woks are a practical substitute for propane tanks
- Cutting vibrating tongues using a hole saw at an angle
- Tuning the tongues by adjusting their length or adding mass
- Joining the two wok halves using aluminium welding rod and a hot plate
- Final sound demonstration

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium wok (×2) — used as the drum body
- Aluminium welding rod (zinc-based solder) — used to fill guide-drill holes and to join the two wok halves
- Steel plate — used to press the woks together on a hot plate during joining
- JB Weld — mentioned as a possible adhesive alternative for joining the halves

## Techniques and Methods
- Angled hole saw cutting on a pillar drill to produce vibrating tongues
- Filling unwanted guide-drill holes with aluminium welding rod and heat
- Hot-plate preheating of aluminium before applying the welding rod (to overcome the large thermal mass)
- Tuning by lengthening tongues with a hacksaw or shortening/adding mass with aluminium weld
- Screwing off handles to allow the two woks to sit flush together

## Robert's Own Replies
- On the name "hank drum": Robert notes he believes the inventor derived the name from "tank drum" and was amused to learn the etymology alongside his viewers.
- On the aluminium welding rod: He is genuinely impressed by how well it performs for this application, though he would not rely on it for structural work.
- On the pillar drill limitation: He confirms he avoided a larger central tongue because the wok wouldn't fit in his pillar drill and he was concerned it would move unpredictably.
- General tone: Robert is relaxed and collegial, thanking commenters for tips on tuning and welding and acknowledging he left the drum lying around because he wasn't sure what musical use to make of it — he was primarily exploring frequency and resonance rather than making music.
