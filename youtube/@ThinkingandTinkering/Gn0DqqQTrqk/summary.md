## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make traditional rivets from metal bar stock and use them to join two flat pieces of metal. The video was prompted by a collaborator ("Luke") who needed an attractive fastening method with no protrusion on either side. Robert covers both cold and hot riveting techniques, explaining when each is appropriate.

## Key Topics
- What rivets are and why they're useful compared to screws/bolts
- Making a rivet-making die from steel bar
- Forming countersunk rivets by hammering bar stock into a die
- Applying rivets to join two pieces of metal (cold vs. hot technique)
- Annealing to relieve brittleness after cold working
- Using washers to spread load on soft metals

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Brass bar (4 mm)** — chosen for aesthetic appearance; used to make the rivets
- **Aluminium sheet** — the two pieces being joined in the demonstration
- **Steel bar (5 mm)** — used to make the rivet-making die
- **Steel washers** — used to spread clamping load when riveting soft metals like aluminium

## Techniques and Methods
- **Die fabrication** — drilling and countersinking a hole in a steel bar to create a forming die
- **Cold riveting** — hammering bar stock through the die at room temperature to form the rivet head
- **Hot riveting** — heating the metal with a blowtorch while hammering, reducing brittleness and improving flow
- **Annealing** — heating the finished rivet and allowing it to air-cool (not quench) to restore ductility
- **Dome forming** — striking around the edges (not straight on) to mushroom the rivet tail into a dome
- **Countersinking** — pre-drilling a countersunk hole in one workpiece so the rivet head sits flush
