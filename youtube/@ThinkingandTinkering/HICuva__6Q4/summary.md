## Overview
This video demonstrates Robert's method for washing and purifying graphene oxide (GO) after synthesis, aimed at hobbyists who lack centrifuge equipment. Rather than the standard filtration approach (which clogs filter paper), he developed a diffusion-based dilution method followed by slow air-drying, grinding, and a final HCl/deionized water wash. The process takes about a week but requires minimal active time and produces a usable brown GO powder from roughly 5g of graphite starting material.

## Key Topics
- Why standard filtration fails for homemade GO (gel-like consistency clogs filters)
- Diffusion dilution method to remove sulfuric acid from raw GO
- Why tap water (rather than deionized) is used in early washing stages to prevent GO dispersion
- Slow drying to avoid thermal reduction of GO back to graphene
- Grinding dried GO into powder using a coffee grinder
- Final purification wash with HCl and deionized water using a DIY vacuum filtration setup
- Separating residual graphite from GO via sonication and decanting

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide (GO)** — the product being purified, described as orange goop post-synthesis
- **Sulfuric acid (96–98% concentrated)** — present in raw GO from the synthesis step; removed by dilution
- **Potassium and magnesium salts** — residual contaminants from the synthesis reaction
- **Hydrochloric acid (10% solution)** — used in the final wash to dissolve residual metal salts; prepared from 35% HCl diluted 2:3 with deionized water
- **Deionized water** — used in the final HCl wash and for dispersing GO; tap water used in early dilution stages intentionally
- **Tap water** — used early on specifically because its ion content prevents GO from dispersing, keeping it as a settleable sludge
- **Unreacted graphite** — co-product remaining in the powder; separated later by sonication

## Techniques and Methods
- **Diffusion dilution washing** — diluting raw GO suspension with 2L tap water repeatedly to reduce sulfuric acid concentration by ~40x per wash cycle
- **Decanting / siphoning** — pouring or drawing off the water layer after GO settles, without filtration
- **Slow air-drying** — spreading GO in a shallow Pyrex dish on top of a radiator at temperatures well below 70°C over several days
- **Coffee grinder milling** — grinding dried GO sheets into powder (~5 minutes)
- **Vacuum filtration with DIY Büchner funnel** — homemade setup using a drilled acrylic disc, a funnel, a wine bottle, and a ~£10 eBay vacuum pump for the final HCl/water wash
- **pH monitoring** — washing with deionized water until effluent reaches pH ~7 (6.5–7.5)
- **Sonication-based graphite separation** — dispersing powder in DI water (~4 mg/mL) with a bath sonicator; GO disperses, graphite sinks, and the GO-rich supernatant is decanted

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Drying time and acid residue**: If drying takes too long, residual acid may still be present; GO is somewhat hygroscopic and takes a surprisingly long time to dry.
- **Temperature and UV**: Always dry below 70°C and never in direct sunlight — UV reduces GO back to graphene.
- **HCl wash is not always needed**: For the exfoliation method, the HCl wash is only to remove insoluble salts; it is not strictly necessary if purity demands are low.
- **Tap water vs. deionized**: He normally uses room-temperature DI water but acknowledges other approaches can work.
- **Product loss is expected**: Some GO will be lost during washing — this is an unavoidable trade-off for purity.
- **Large-flake GO behaves as a liquid crystal**: Over-washing large-flake GO can be counterproductive due to its liquid crystal properties.
- **Higher purity requires dialysis**: The home method does a good job but true purification requires dialysis.
- **Graphene restacking**: Restacking depends on the number of broken or dangling carbon bonds; passivation with hydrogen could prevent it.
- **Freeze-drying trick**: Acknowledged a commenter's suggestion about freeze-drying as valid, but cautioned against adding magnetic components (e.g., iron) as contaminants would ruin GO for most downstream uses.
- **Safety warning**: Strongly advises inexperienced users to seek assistance from a professor before attempting Tour-method synthesis, due to concentrated acids and potential explosive mixtures.
- **Not oxidised enough**: If the product doesn't behave as expected, the reaction phase should be extended (before adding water) to achieve higher oxidation.
- **Colloid formation**: Once salts are removed, GO forms a colloid — acceptable for most uses; higher purity requires a different strategy like dialysis.
