## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a universal motor salvaged from a vacuum cleaner to explain how it works, how to diagnose and repair common faults, and how to control it. The key takeaway is that universal motors — found in power tools, vacuum cleaners, and washing machines across Europe — are surprisingly easy to repair and can have their torque and speed controlled independently by separating the field coil and rotor coil power supplies.

## Key Topics
- What a universal motor is and why it's called "universal" (runs on AC or DC)
- Common applications: vacuum cleaners, power tools, washing machines (UK/Europe)
- Internal components: stator coils, rotor, commutator, brushes, end caps, fan
- Diagnosing faults using a multimeter (resistance/continuity testing)
- Why coils fail: overheating, burnt insulation, broken crimps
- Repairing a broken stator coil by soldering in replacement wire
- Independent control of field coils vs. rotor coils for torque and speed
- Comparison with induction motors used in North American washing machines

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wire wool — used to clean the commutator
- Replacement wire (matched gauge) — for repairing broken coil windings
- Solder — for joining the repair wire at the broken crimp joint

## Techniques and Methods
- Multimeter resistance/continuity testing to locate a broken coil winding
- Micrometer measurement of wire diameter to determine correct replacement wire gauge
- Cleaning the commutator with wire wool
- Repairing a broken crimp joint by soldering in a short length of matched-gauge wire
- Separating field coil and rotor coil power supplies for independent torque/speed control
- Voltage control of rotor supply to adjust motor speed
- Voltage control of field coil supply to adjust motor torque
