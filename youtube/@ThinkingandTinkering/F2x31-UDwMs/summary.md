## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith walks through the fundamental mathematics behind wind turbine power generation, starting from first principles (force, mass, kinetic energy) and building up to the real-world efficiency losses that reduce a turbine's practical output. The video demystifies the Betz limit and tip speed ratio, showing that the physics behind wind energy is straightforward once you break it down step by step.

## Key Topics
- Energy as force times distance, and wind as discrete particles imparting kinetic energy
- Calculating the mass of air passing through a turbine swept area using air density (~1.22 kg/m³)
- Difference between HAWT (Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine) and VAWT (Vertical Axis Wind Turbine) swept area geometries
- Deriving available power from velocity and swept area (kinetic energy formula: ½mv²)
- The Betz limit (59.3% theoretical maximum efficiency)
- Real-world efficiency losses: transmission (3–10%), mechanical (~0.3%), electrical (1–1.5%), siting (~5%), maintenance downtime (~2–3%)
- Tip speed ratio — what it actually means physically and why ~6 is the common design target

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Air (density ~1.22 kg/m³)

## Techniques and Methods
- Dimensional analysis to derive power from first principles (F=ma, E=Fd, KE=½mv²)
- Using an arbitrary time interval to convert wind speed into a volume of air, which then cancels out to give power
- Geometric area calculation for HAWT (πr²) vs VAWT (height × depth)
