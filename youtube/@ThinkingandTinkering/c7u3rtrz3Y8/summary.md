## Overview
This video explores prime numbers and their curious properties, focusing on the claim that every natural number greater than one is either prime or expressible as a product of primes (the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic). The presenter walks through a proof by mathematical induction to demonstrate this holds for all natural numbers, not just specific examples. The video was likely made as a companion piece to a Robert Murray-Smith video on Pythagoras, with Robert planning a follow-up on inductive reasoning.

## Key Topics
- The paradoxical nature of primes: infinite in count yet unpredictable in sequence
- Definition of a prime number (exactly two distinct factors: 1 and itself)
- Why 1 is not a prime
- The claim that every natural number > 1 is either prime or a product of primes
- Proof by mathematical induction of that claim
- The concept of indexed natural numbers (Q₁, Q₂, … Qₙ) for formal reasoning

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mathematical induction** — establishing truth for a base set of numbers, then proving the claim extends to the next number (Qₙ₊₁), thereby covering all natural numbers
- **Proof by cases** — splitting the inductive step into two cases: Qₙ₊₁ is prime, or Qₙ₊₁ is composite (and therefore its factors, being smaller, fall within the already-proven set)
- **Factor expansion** — showing composite factors can themselves be decomposed into prime products, allowing the whole number to be written as a single product of primes
