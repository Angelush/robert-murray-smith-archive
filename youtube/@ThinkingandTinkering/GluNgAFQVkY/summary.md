## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a resin 3D printer (Elegoo Saturn 16K Ultra) as a UV exposure unit to create cyanotype prints — a 19th-century photographic process invented by John Herschel in 1842. He walks through the full workflow from coating paper with the cyanotype chemistry to developing and then toning the prints in tea to produce permanent, fade-resistant black iron-tannate images.

## Key Topics
- History of early photography: Daguerreotype, Fox Talbot's silver nitrate process, and Herschel's cyanotype
- How resin 3D printers work as UV light sources via their LCD masking screen
- Preparing cyanotype solution from raw chemicals
- Converting a digital image to an STL file for use as a print mask
- Exposing, developing, and drying cyanotype prints
- Toning cyanotypes with tea to improve permanence by forming insoluble iron-tannate

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium ferrocyanide** — one of two cyanotype sensitizer chemicals (Solution A)
- **Ferric ammonium citrate** — second cyanotype sensitizer chemical (Solution B)
- **Deionized/ironized water** — used to dissolve the chemicals (available in supermarkets for irons)
- **Watercolor paper (~240–300 GSM)** — substrate for the cyanotype coating
- **Iron sulfate** (garden fertilizer) — demonstrated reacting with tea to show the tannin-iron chemistry
- **Tea** — used as a toning bath; tannins react with residual iron salts to form permanent black iron-tannate compounds
- **Mercury vapor and iodine** — mentioned as reagents in the Daguerreotype process (historical context)
- **Silver nitrate** — mentioned as the basis for Fox Talbot's process and modern photography

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cyanotype sensitization**: mixing equal parts Solution A (ferric ammonium citrate in deionized water) and Solution B (potassium ferrocyanide in deionized water), brushing onto watercolor paper, and drying with a hairdryer
- **Image preparation**: downloading a royalty-free image from Pixabay, inverting colors in Paint, converting JPEG to STL via an online converter, importing into Chitubox slicer with a 1 cm height and 900-second (15-minute) bottom exposure time
- **UV exposure using resin printer**: removing the resin tank, placing coated paper on the LCD screen, using the FEP film layer as a pressure spring, and running the print in "mandatory printing" mode to bypass the resin-absent error
- **Development**: washing the exposed print under running tap water
- **Tea toning**: immersing the developed cyanotype in a tea bath for ~30 minutes to convert iron salts into permanent, light-fast black iron-tannate
- **Drying flat**: pressing the wet print under weights to prevent warping
