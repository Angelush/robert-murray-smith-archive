## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to convert seaweed into a graphitized carbon electrode for use in a supercapacitor, framing it within the context of the 2023 Earthshot Prize and seaweed's broader environmental potential. He collects bladderwrack from a beach, carbonizes it in a kiln using catalytic graphitization, and builds a working supercapacitor that powers a small motor. The key takeaway is that seaweed — already valued for bioplastics, animal feed, carbon sequestration, and fertilizer — is also a promising energy storage material.

## Key Topics
- The 2023 Earthshot Prize and its seaweed-related entrants (Notpla bioplastics, Sea Forest animal feed)
- Seaweed's multiple applications: bioplastics, animal feed, methane reduction, carbon sequestration, fertilizer, energy storage
- Collecting bladderwrack seaweed for carbonization
- Catalytic graphitization process to produce conductive carbon from biomass
- Assembling and testing a seaweed-derived supercapacitor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bladderwrack seaweed** — primary carbon precursor, chosen for high iodine content
- **Activated carbon** — used as packing material to exclude oxygen and absorb noxious off-gassing during pyrolysis
- **Stainless steel pot** — kiln vessel; donates trace iron as a graphitization catalyst
- **Stainless steel wire** — used to sandwich the precursor material inside the pot
- **Iron (Fe)** — graphitization catalyst (donated by the stainless steel)
- **Nickel and Cobalt** — noted as alternative catalytic metals for graphitization
- **Hydrochloric acid** — used to wash resultant carbon; ferric chloride recovered indicates iron migration
- **Ferric chloride** — byproduct detected after acid washing, confirming iron catalysis
- **Super P (carbon black)** — suggested conductive additive if the carbonized product is insufficiently conductive
- **Graphite** — alternative conductive additive; also used as the current collector (grafoil)
- **Grafoil** — preferred current collector for the supercapacitor electrodes
- **Sulfate-based electrolyte** — used in the assembled supercapacitor device
- **Seaweed (general)** — noted as raw material for Notpla bioplastic and Sea Forest animal feed

## Techniques and Methods
- **Beach collection** of fresh bladderwrack seaweed followed by air-drying
- **Blending/food processing** of dried seaweed into fine powder before and after carbonization
- **Catalytic graphitization** — pyrolysis in a kiln at 650–900 °C (soak ~4 hours), with iron from the stainless steel pot acting as catalyst to partially graphitize the carbon
- **Oxygen exclusion** during pyrolysis by packing the pot in activated carbon
- **Conductivity testing** of the resultant carbon to verify graphitization success
- **Electrode fabrication** — mixing carbon powder with a binder, coating onto current collector (grafoil), and drying
- **Supercapacitor assembly** — stacking two electrodes with electrolyte between them
- **Electrochemical testing** — charging to a set voltage and discharging through a precision load; multimeter monitoring
