## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a miniaturised version of the Drawdio circuit — a musical electronic device — fabricated using conductive ink drawn with a dip pen onto paper. The circuit measures just 1cm × 2cm with SMD components, proving that functional, real circuits can be created through painting, printing, or stencilling with conductive ink. He highlights the commercial potential of this approach for mass-produced items like playable birthday cards.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Drawdio project (a pencil-based musical circuit)
- Redesigning the Drawdio circuit at miniature scale using conductive ink
- Using a dip pen for precise, fine-line circuit drawing
- Placing SMD (surface-mount) components on a painted circuit
- Commercial and mass-production potential of printed conductive ink circuits
- Indiegogo campaign promotion for his conductive ink project

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — used to draw the circuit traces onto paper, applied with a dip pen and syringe
- **Paper** — substrate for the painted circuit

## Techniques and Methods
- Drawing circuit traces with a **dip pen** for fine-line control
- Applying conductive ink via a **syringe** to connect components
- **Spot-gluing** SMD components onto the painted circuit
- Using a **magnifying glass and safety pin** for placing tiny SMD components by hand
- **Silk-screen printing** discussed as a method for mass-producing circuits
