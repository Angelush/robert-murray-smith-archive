## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the commercial Aeromine wind device and then builds a DIY alternative inspired by the largely forgotten "Erasmus Darwin horizontal windmill" — a lost technology invented by the polymath Erasmus Darwin (grandfather of Charles Darwin). Using only plywood, dowels, and basic tools, he constructs an octagonal louvred tower that channels wind upward through a central turbine, arguing this approach makes better use of the structural tower than conventional mast-mounted turbines.

## Key Topics
- The Aeromine commercial wind device: how it uses F1-style wing shapes to create low pressure and drive a turbine
- Challenges of DIY wing fabrication vs. the simplicity of a louvred tower approach
- Erasmus Darwin's horizontal windmill: its history, near-total obscurity, and genius design principles
- Construction walkthrough of a plywood louvred octagonal tower (1.2m prototype)
- Efficiency argument: tower footprint doubles as wind-capture area, beating conventional turbines and arguably solar on a per-footprint basis
- Bird safety, visual discretion, and planning/permitting advantages of a horizontal turbine orientation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **3mm plywood** — cut into strips to form the louvred flap panels
- **12mm exterior plywood** — used to make the octagonal top and bottom plates
- **Wooden dowel** — flap pivots; flaps are glued to the dowel for rotation
- **Foam board / PVC fascia or soffit board** — mentioned in comments as potentially superior substitutes for the plywood flaps

## Techniques and Methods
- **Table saw ripping** — cutting plywood sheets into uniform strips for flaps
- **Dowel-and-glue flap assembly** — gluing each strip to a dowel to create pivoting louvre vanes
- **Octagonal frame construction** — cutting corners of plywood squares to produce octagons; one with a central hole (top) and one without (bottom)
- **Slotted panel assembly** — drilling aligned holes in top and bottom bars and slotting dowel-mounted flaps into them to form self-actuating louvre panels
- **Passive one-way flap action** — flaps open inward on the windward side and close on the leeward side, directing all airflow upward through the central aperture

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Octagonal shape rationale:** "helps with catching the wind from any direction but balances against the need for increasing the number of sides to do that" — explaining why eight sides was the practical choice.
- **Material alternatives:** Agreed that foam board, PVC fascia, or soffit board would be "miles better" than plywood for the flaps, but chose plywood as the most universally available material.
- **Sealing the flaps:** Suggested rubber strips or foam rubber strips as an easy fix for air leakage around the flap edges.
- **Faraday cage / antenna effect:** Noted that a Faraday cage is essentially an antenna and suggested fitting a rectifier to it — a tangential but interesting electrical aside.
- **Self-deprecation on originality:** "it's not my idea I am just copying someone else — it is just plain awesome when it works" — crediting Erasmus Darwin.
- **Forum idea:** Mentioned plans to start a community forum, apparently discussed with commenters elsewhere.
