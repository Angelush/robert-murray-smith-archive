## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a surprisingly simple method for joining copper and aluminium — two dissimilar non-ferrous metals that are traditionally difficult to bond — using a commercially available aluminium brazing rod (Durafix). Inspired by a viewer question from James Conley, he shows that with basic surface preparation and a standard butane/propane torch, a strong joint can be achieved in under a minute, which he describes as opening up a huge range of possibilities for makers and hobbyists.

## Key Topics
- The longstanding challenge of joining dissimilar non-ferrous metals (copper and aluminium)
- Introduction and practical demonstration of the Durafix aluminium brazing/welding rod
- Surface preparation for both copper and aluminium
- The brazing process using a standard DIY torch
- Practical applications: electrical contacts, HVAC, radiators, heat exchangers
- Comparison to previous methods (epoxy resin, tight-fit joints)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Durafix aluminium brazing rod** — the primary joining material; believed to be flux-cored, likely zinc-based (possibly zinc and bismuth, maybe magnesium)
- **Copper** — one of the metals being joined; cleaned with wire wool
- **Aluminium** — the other metal; cleaned with stainless steel brush or aluminium oxide (glass) paper
- **Epoxy/loader resin** — Robert's previous (less satisfactory) method for joining metals, shown failing under heat during the demo
- **Wire wool** — used to clean the copper surface
- **Aluminium oxide abrasive paper** — used as an alternative to stainless steel for cleaning the aluminium surface

## Techniques and Methods
- Surface cleaning of copper with wire wool prior to brazing
- Surface cleaning of aluminium with a stainless steel brush or aluminium oxide abrasive paper (both found effective)
- Stacking the two metal pieces with a weight applied during heating
- Applying heat with a standard butane/propane torch for approximately one minute until brazing temperature is reached
- Flux-cored rod technique (no separate flux applied)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Rod brand confirmed as Durafix** — he bought a name-brand rod specifically to give the technique the best chance of working; suggests trying cheaper versions only after gaining familiarity.
- **Flux-cored rod** — he believes the rod is flux-cored and recommends not adding arbitrary external flux, as flux is typically matched to the solder and the metals it's intended to join.
- **Likely zinc-based composition** — speculates the rod feels like zinc, possibly with bismuth or magnesium, though he is uncertain of the exact alloy.
- **Not suitable for ferrous metals** — notes that ferrous metals apparently contaminate the solder, so this technique is limited to non-ferrous applications.
- **Galvanic corrosion caveat** — acknowledges there could be a corrosion concern in wet conditions, but notes that in many use cases (e.g., IP-rated enclosures for electrical contacts) this is unlikely to be an issue.
- **HVAC industry validation** — confirms after checking that this joining method is widely used in HVAC for radiator and heat exchanger repair, lending credibility to the joint's longevity and real-world durability.
- **Practical applications he highlights** — electrical joints, radiators, HVAC projects, and quick mechanical joints where drilling and tapping would be impractical.
- **Standard torch confirmed** — used a standard torch from a DIY big-box store; nothing specialist required.
- **Took ~30 minutes to get used to** — notes there is a small learning curve but the technique is accessible.
