## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the Zamboni Pile (dry pile battery), using the famous Oxford Electric Bell — a device that has rung continuously since 1840 — as a jumping-off point. He explains the history, construction, and debated operating mechanism of these ultra-long-lasting batteries, then demonstrates how to build a simple version at home using aluminium foil and conductive ink. The central question is whether the device is electrostatic (and thus theoretically perpetual) or electrochemical (and thus limited by reactant depletion).

## Key Topics
- The Oxford Electric Bell: history, construction, and operating characteristics (2 kV, ~1 nanoamp, ~2 Hz)
- History of dry piles: Zamboni, de Luc, Baran, and other early 19th-century contributors
- Unusual pile materials explored historically: wood, beetroot, radish
- Role of the Oxford dry pile in WWII as a portable high-voltage low-current battery (infrared night-vision scopes, electrostatic voltmeters)
- The fundamental debate: electrostatic contact potential vs. electrochemical reaction as the operating mechanism
- Practical demonstration: building a simple dry pile from aluminium foil and conductive ink

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Tin foil** — used in Zamboni's original pile discs
- **Zinc** — traditional anode material in dry piles; zinc sulfur-impregnated paper in Zamboni's design
- **Manganese dioxide (MnO₂)** — traditional cathode material; also notable for releasing oxygen on heating
- **Sulfur** — outer sealing layer of the Oxford pile
- **Lead sulfide** — original cathode material in the WWII infrared image converter tube
- **Lead iodide** — replacement cathode material in the same tube
- **Aluminium foil** (roofing foil) — used as the anode in Robert's homemade demonstration pile
- **Conductive ink** (non-waterproof, carbon/graphite-based) — used as the cathode layer in the demo
- **Electro-smog graphite paint** (mentioned as containing graphene) — suggested as an alternative carbon source

## Techniques and Methods
- Stacking dissimilar material layers (foil / paper separator / conductive coating) to form a voltaic pile
- Painting conductive ink onto paper and allowing it to dry before cutting
- Cutting dried sheets into ~25 mm squares to form individual cells
- Stacking hundreds to thousands of cells into a pile, adding aluminium contact plates, and wrapping with tape
- Charging a 100 nF capacitor from the pile to demonstrate output and illuminate an LED
