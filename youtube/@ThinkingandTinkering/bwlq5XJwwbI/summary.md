## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple wind turbine generator using a repurposed PC fan motor and a recycled CD as the blade assembly. He cuts the CD into 16 segments, heats the polycarbonate with a heat gun to twist the blades to approximately 35 degrees, and glues the assembly onto a previously converted PC fan motor. The completed turbine successfully lights an LED and produces around 2 volts from an artificial wind source, proving the concept as a viable scrap-materials project.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing PC fans as wind turbine generators
- Using a CD as a turbine blade rotor
- Blade geometry: 16 blades at 22.5 degrees each
- Thermoplastic shaping of polycarbonate blades
- Generator output measurement (voltage, LED test)
- Practical next steps for storing or using the generated power

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PC fan (motor stripped of original blades and electronics, converted to a generator)
- Old CD (used as rotor/blade material — identified as polycarbonate)
- Card (mentioned from a prior video to improve output)
- LED (used as output indicator)
- Adhesive/glue (for attaching CD to motor hub)

## Techniques and Methods
- Stripping a PC fan down to its motor and converting it to a generator (covered in prior videos)
- Marking and dividing a CD into 16 equal segments using a paper jig
- Cutting blade shapes with a Dremel/multi-tool rotary cutter
- Heating polycarbonate with a heat gun to soften and twist blade pitch (~35°)
- Testing output with a voltmeter and LED under a blower fan

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Blade pitch variation:** Acknowledges that on larger turbines blade pitch should vary along the length (faster at the tip), but notes this is irrelevant here since the blades are only a few centimetres long — the speed difference is negligible.
- **Charging a battery:** Confirms charging a battery is the logical next step, but expresses frustration that forums on the topic are "confusing and contradictory and very very often just plain wrong."
- **Complete power system route:** Explains the standard path is controller → battery → inverter → home, describing these as "plug-in components off the shelf." He seems mildly exasperated that viewers ask repeatedly rather than working it out themselves.
- **Speed and power limits:** Notes that to get significant power the turbine needs to spin much faster, and in theory you could spin it fast enough to burn out the wires — that being the upper limit.
- **Rectification and capacitor:** Briefly suggests rectifying the AC output and adding a capacitor as an improvement.
