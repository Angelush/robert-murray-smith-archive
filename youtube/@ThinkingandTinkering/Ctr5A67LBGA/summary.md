## Overview
Robert builds a small wind turbine he calls "the Daisy" using 40 polycarbonate teaspoons glued around acrylic discs, with the blades heat-bent to roughly 30° pitch. Neodymium magnets glued to the back of the rotor turn it into a generator when paired with a salvaged microwave oven turntable coil. The turbine successfully lights a 12V LED when spun by a hair dryer, with Robert noting it outputs about the same as any comparable turbine.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing waste plastic teaspoons as wind turbine blades
- Blade pitch adjustment via heat-bending
- Simple axial-flux generator construction using permanent magnets and a salvaged coil
- Low-speed, high-torque characteristics of this rotor style
- Philosophy of presenting ideas for others to develop rather than finished solutions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polycarbonate teaspoons** — used as rotor blades (40 total)
- **Blue acrylic sheet** — cut into two disc sizes (100 mm and 75 mm) to form the hub
- **Superglue / solvent acrylic glue** — bonding spoons to the acrylic discs
- **Neodymium magnets** — 8 glued to the back of the rotor to induce current in the coil

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hole saw cutting** — used to cut acrylic discs from sheet stock
- **Solvent/superglue bonding** — attaching polycarbonate spoons to acrylic hub
- **Heat-gun bending** — softening spoon handles to twist blades to ~30° pitch, then holding until set
- **Axial-flux generator assembly** — magnets glued directly to rotor back, coil fixed to wooden stand in proximity
- **Hair dryer wind simulation** — used to test rotation and power generation indoors

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Blade twisting tip**: Suggests twisting spoons *before* gluing if heat risks softening the adhesive.
- **Magnet placement**: Acknowledges that gluing magnets directly gives a 1:1 ratio, but notes this rotor style is known for low speed and high torque — gearing may not be optimal.
- **Power output**: Confirms he tested the generator and "it outputs about the same as any other turbine."
- **Betz law clarification**: Explains that Betz's law applies to Newtonian fluids where viscous stresses are linearly proportional to local strain rate, implying standard fluid assumptions may not fully apply here.
- **On criticism of low power**: Pushes back firmly — notes that in many countries 100 W/day is a meaningful level of consumption, and that his channel is about presenting ideas for others to develop, not handing out ready-made solutions.
- **Sunflower name**: Liked a commenter's suggestion of calling it "the Sunflower."
- **Follow-up video**: Confirmed a measurement/test video was already filmed and scheduled for the next day.
