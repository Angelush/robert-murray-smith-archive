## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to build a pseudo-magnetic levitation bearing for a flywheel using salvaged speaker magnets. He explains the different types of magnetic levitation — static, dynamic, and diamagnetic — then focuses on pseudo-levitation as the most practical DIY approach, showing how it can support surprisingly heavy loads (e.g., a 2.5 kg weight) with very low friction, making it ideal for long-spinning energy storage flywheels.

## Key Topics
- Types of magnetic levitation: static (permanent magnet, theoretically impossible), dynamic (spinning magnets, e.g. Levitron), and diamagnetic (pyrolytic graphite)
- Pseudo-levitation: stabilising one axis mechanically while the other is magnetically supported
- How magnetic force scales with the square of distance
- Repurposing speaker magnets as a cheap, well-engineered magnet source
- Building a vertical-axis pseudo-levitation bearing from speaker parts
- Demonstration with a 2.5 kg free weight as a flywheel load
- Potential to extend the design into a flywheel generator (with added coils and magnets)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Speaker magnets** — salvaged from loudspeakers; used as both the base pot magnet and the floating ring magnet
- **Ring magnet** (from speaker) — the levitating upper magnet
- **8 mm steel bar** — used as the central shaft/axle, centered through the speaker's base plate
- **Wooden plug** — inserted into the ring magnet's center hole to carry the bearing
- **Small ball bearing** — placed in the wooden plug to prevent the upper magnet from wobbling laterally
- **Pyrolytic graphite** — mentioned as a diamagnetic material capable of passive levitation (not used in build)
- **Free weight (2.5 kg)** — used as a demonstration flywheel mass

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling speaker drivers: removing the cone, voice coil former, and top plate to expose the pot magnet and base plate
- Drilling through the speaker magnet's centre dimple to create a precisely centred axle hole
- Pressing/inserting an 8 mm bar through the base plate for a stable, square shaft
- Inserting a wooden plug into a ring magnet's centre and embedding a ball bearing for lateral constraint
- Stacking a second (ring) magnet above the pot magnet in repulsive orientation to increase lift force
- Spinning the flywheel load to demonstrate extended spin-down time under pseudo-levitation
