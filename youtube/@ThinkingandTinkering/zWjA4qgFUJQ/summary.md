## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the application of biomimicry — specifically whale tubercles (bumps on humpback whale flippers) — to vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) blade design. He 3D prints and assembles two identical small VAWTs, one with smooth blades and one with tubercle-patterned blades, then tests both under a fan to compare RPM. The tubercle version spins noticeably faster (149 RPM vs ~100 RPM), supporting the hypothesis that tubercles meaningfully improve VAWT efficiency.

## Key Topics
- Problems with horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWTs): lightning strikes, wildlife hazard, structural failure, fire risk
- Introduction to vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs): types (Darrieus, Savonius, helical), advantages, and disadvantages
- Biomimicry and the study of humpback whale tubercles as hydrodynamic optimisations
- 3D printing and assembly of a simple VAWT test rig
- Comparative RPM test: smooth-blade vs tubercle-blade VAWT
- Four proposed advantages of tubercles on VAWTs: efficiency at low wind speed, noise/vibration reduction, durability, and energy capture in turbulent winds
- Urban applications of VAWTs where turbulent wind is common

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Cyanoacrylic (super) glue** — used to bond rods into blade hubs
- **Reflective aluminium strip** — applied to rotor for tachometer measurement
- **3D-printed PLA/filament** (implied) — blades, hub discs, cradle components
- **Skateboard bearings (22 mm × 8 mm × 7 mm)** — used in the rotor assembly
- **8 mm washers** — used as spacers in the rotor/cradle assembly
- **Steel/carbon rods** — 100 mm and 75 mm lengths used as axle and structural members

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — blades (with and without tubercles), hub tops/bottoms, cradle base and uprights sourced from Thingiverse
- **VAWT assembly** — three-blade rotor construction with rod-and-coupler axle system and bearing-mounted cradle
- **Digital tachometer measurement** — reflective strip + optical tachometer used to measure RPM under controlled fan airflow
- **Comparative A/B testing** — same fan speed applied to both smooth and tubercle blade rotors under identical conditions

## Robert's Own Replies
- **This is a rough-and-ready proof-of-principle test** — he explicitly cautions against over-interpreting the results, but says it is sufficient to justify deeper investigation. He confirms he kept width, length, and breadth consistent between the two blade versions.
- **Tubercle geometry is an open research area** — he acknowledges his choices of tubercle position, spacing, and size were fairly arbitrary, and agrees there is much to explore there.
- **Tubercles in aviation** — he notes the airline industry is conservative (for good reason) but that aircraft with tubercles are actively being investigated.
- **Tubercles are not limited to wind turbines** — he jokes "if it moves, stick a tubercle on it," and points to the Wells turbine as a relevant related design.
- **Water applications** — when asked about hydro/tidal use, he notes lift depends on both fluid density and velocity, so denser water moving more slowly complicates direct comparison; he considers it a valid avenue.
- **Further reading** — he directs people to Google Scholar for research papers and links a related YouTube video (`https://youtu.be/TX7zmfKiUEU`).
- **All his designs are freely available on Thingiverse** — he welcomes anyone to use, build on, or modify them.
