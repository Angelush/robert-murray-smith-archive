## Overview
Robert uses the Anycubic Kobra S1 multi-material 3D printer to print a "V Gate" — a magnet-based spinning mechanism that gained notoriety as a supposed "free energy" device. Rather than running a standard benchmark print, he designs the V Gate wheel in Tinkercad, slices and prints it, and uses the video as a first real test of the machine's print quality and workflow. He emphasises he has no interest in free energy claims and has a different application in mind for the structure.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Anycubic Kobra S1 as a multi-material/multifunction printer
- The V Gate concept — a magnetic mechanism using repulsion or attraction to produce rotation, historically promoted as a "free energy" device
- Magnets as springs (repulsive and attractive sides)
- Designing the V Gate wheel in Tinkercad and exporting as STL
- Slicing workflow in the Anycubic slicer (nozzle settings, filament selection, slicing)
- Sending prints to the machine via USB or remote WiFi (Workbench feature)
- WiFi connectivity limitation: only 2.4 GHz supported
- Time-lapse capture using a USB drive
- First-print quality assessment of the Kobra S1

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA filament** — white, used for the print
- **Magnets** — referenced as the functional components of the V Gate mechanism (not printed, but central to the design concept)

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D modelling in Tinkercad** — designing the V Gate wheel and exporting as STL
- **FDM (Fused Deposition Modelling) printing** — using the Anycubic Kobra S1 with a 0.4 mm nozzle
- **Slicing in Anycubic Slicer** — importing STL, selecting filament and nozzle settings, slicing to G-code
- **G-code transfer via USB stick** — saving sliced file and physically carrying to the printer
- **Remote printing via Workbench** — connecting to the printer over 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi for wireless file transfer and live camera monitoring
- **Time-lapse recording** — using a USB drive inserted into the printer's side port, activated before starting the print
