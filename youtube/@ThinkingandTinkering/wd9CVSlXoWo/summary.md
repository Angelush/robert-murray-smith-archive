## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith 3D prints and assembles a demonstration model of the Di Pietro air engine, invented by Angelo Di Pietro in 1999, to explore how it overcomes the key limitations of conventional vane motors. He dissects both engine types side by side, explaining the Di Pietro's clever central rolling-ring mechanism and valve system. While praising the design's elegance, he notes his printed model is a learning tool rather than a precision working engine, and flags the valve arrangement and vane sealing as areas he wants to investigate further.

## Key Topics
- Overview of the vane (vee) motor — how it works, where it's used, and its two main problems: chamber leakage and friction from vanes pressing against the outer wall
- Introduction to the Di Pietro air engine as a direct response to those limitations
- Step-by-step assembly of the 3D-printed Di Pietro model
- The central rolling-ring mechanism — a key innovation where the central disc orbits without rotating, dramatically reducing friction
- The valve chest and valve system — how the rotating valve alternately opens the inlet and connects residual exhaust to an outlet port
- Di Pietro's claim of 100% efficiency gain over competing air engines, and Robert's scepticism
- Limitations of FDM-printed models for pneumatic engines (tolerances and leakage)
- Di Pietro's background working on the Wankel engine as context for the design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA or similar FDM filament — printed at 0.2 mm layer resolution for the demo model
- Skater bearings — 22 mm × 7 mm × 8 mm, used in the bottom plate and central carriage
- Elastic bands / rubber bands — used as vane-return springs (cut in half, knotted, and threaded through)
- Super glue — for securing rubber bands and aligning plates
- TPU — mentioned by Robert in comments as a future material to try for a printed version

## Techniques and Methods
- FDM 3D printing of all structural components (body, vanes, valve, carriage)
- Eccentric/orbital assembly of the central rolling-ring carriage using axles and spacers
- Threading rubber bands with a needle through internal holes as a spring substitute
- Alignment-notch assembly method for stacking motor plates
- Use of a 2 mm clearance fit between valve and valve chest for air metering
- Prony brake suggested (in comments) as the method for measuring static and dynamic torque

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Di Pietro–Wankel connection:** Robert confirms Di Pietro worked on the Wankel engine and explicitly credits it as an inspiration — "no surprises there."
- **Efficiency context:** Notes that Danfoss vane motors already achieve ~86% overall efficiency, suggesting the competitive landscape is tighter than Di Pietro's claims imply.
- **Sealing as the fundamental problem:** "Seals are always the bane of rotary engines — it's probably why the piston has been so predominant for so long."
- **His own concerns:** Admits the valves worry him and that he dislikes the potential for vanes to leak air — flagged as open design questions.
- **Resin printer:** Considering upgrading to a resin printer for tighter tolerances, which are critical for any pneumatic engine.
- **TPU experiment:** Was thinking of printing a similar unit in TPU to explore flex-based sealing.
- **No performance test yet:** Clarifies there was no efficiency test — this is purely a demonstration model; upgrades are needed before a proper test run.
- **Torque measurement method:** Suggests a Prony brake to measure static and dynamic torque, graphing the curves and extrapolating.
- **Personal note:** Briefly acknowledges that things have changed since his wife's passing, responding to a commenter's Christmas wishes.
