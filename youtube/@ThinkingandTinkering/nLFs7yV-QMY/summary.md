## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents a novel carbon-based wave energy generator — an evolution of his earlier "perpetual battery" device — that uses two carbon rods in saltwater to generate electricity through wave motion rather than galvanic action. By substituting carbon for dissimilar metals, he eliminates corrosion and galvanic concerns, making the device durable and suitable for marine environments. He tests a 10-unit prototype both in the sea and in a lab simulation, achieving up to ~11 microamps, and releases the design to the public domain.

## Key Topics
- Evolution from the "perpetual battery" (patent #4153757 by William Clark) to a carbon-only design
- Why carbon rods were chosen — no galvanic action, no corrosion in seawater
- Construction of a single-unit and a scaled 10-rod prototype using KB (Kelvin) blocks
- Live sea test and lab simulation of wave motion
- Surface area as a key parameter for power output
- Public domain release of the design for open development
- Reference to a companion video explaining the physical mechanism

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon rods** — salvaged from zinc-carbon (lantern) batteries; primary electrodes
- **Salt water** — tap water with table salt, used to simulate seawater in the lab
- **Wax** — present in battery-grade carbon rods as a binder; can be burned out to improve conductivity
- **Clay and graphite** — combined and baked to make high-surface-area clay tablets as an alternative electrode material
- **KB (Kelvin) blocks** — plastic terminal blocks used as the structural housing for the rods
- **Copper tube / aluminium tube** — used in the earlier metal-based version (not used here)
- **Nylon block** — used in the earlier "perpetual battery" construction
- **5.1 kΩ resistor** — used as a load for current measurement

## Techniques and Methods
- Extracting carbon rods from zinc-carbon lantern batteries
- Drilling out KB blocks (~8 mm) to accept carbon rods
- Inserting stripped wire into the block hole before press-fitting the carbon rod
- Wiring 10 units in parallel and soldering all leads together
- Burning wax out of salvaged carbon rods to improve surface contact
- Making clay–graphite tablets via mixing, pressing, and kiln-firing
- Dipping/sloshing the device in saltwater to simulate wave action
- Measuring output current with a multimeter on the microamp range

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Not galvanic**: Repeatedly and firmly states the effect is not galvanic action, directing questioners to a separate video explaining the mechanism.
- **Voltage calculation**: Tells viewers to calculate voltage themselves using Ohm's law (`V=IR`), since current (I) and resistance (R) are already known — he declines to give the number directly.
- **Sea confirmation**: Confirms he personally tested the device in the sea.
- **Symmetry insight**: Notes the device is geometrically symmetric, so it is the *movement* (wave action) alone that drives the output — an important clarification of the working principle.
- **Barocaloric effects**: Mentions he would look at barocaloric effects as a related area worth exploring.
- **Ruthenium electrodes**: Notes that chlorine-generator electrodes (suggested by a commenter) are ruthenium-coated and expensive.
- **Long-term testing**: Expresses a wish to have access to a creek so he could leave a device deployed for months to observe long-term behaviour.
- **Community engagement**: Acknowledges receiving ~600 comments per day, apologises for not being able to reply to all, and consistently encourages people to *build and test* rather than just asking questions.
