## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith outlines a low-cost, passive alternative to mechanical solar tracking: placing fluorescent prism-shaped structures on top of solar cells to redirect oblique morning and evening sunlight downward onto the panel. The key insight is that fluorescence in the material can offset the light loss caused by adding a layer on top of the cell, making the concept potentially viable. He proposes using cheap household materials — jello and highlighter pens — as a proof-of-concept prototyping medium before investing in specialist materials.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of mechanical solar tracking (cost increase roughly cancels energy gain)
- The cosine effect: solar panels lose efficiency when sunlight hits at a low angle
- Using prisms to redirect off-angle sunlight onto solar cells
- Why plain glass/prisms are problematic (light absorption reduces output)
- Fluorescent materials as a way to compensate for that absorption loss
- Optimal prism geometry (90° triangle, sawtooth, rounded prism) — an open question
- Using jello + highlighter pen as an inexpensive experimental proxy
- Call for community collaboration to test the idea

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Jello (gelatin)** — used as a transparent fluorescent medium for prototyping
- **Highlighter pen ink (fluorescent dye, water-based)** — mixed into jello to induce fluorescence
- **Glass** — discussed as a prism material; noted to absorb light and reduce cell output
- **Acrylic binder** — mentioned in comments as a potential alternative binder for the fluorescent dye

## Techniques and Methods
- Placing fluorescent prism-shaped structures over solar cells to passively redirect sunlight
- Using household jello + highlighter as a low-cost fluorescent test medium
- Comparing prism geometries (right-angle triangle, sawtooth, rounded prism) for optimal light redirection
- Using a Fresnel lens as a potential mold for fabricating test prisms (suggested in comments)
- Creating negative/positive molds from jello to replicate prism features

## Robert's Own Replies
- He has already read research papers on fluorescence and solar light guides and considers the idea practical based on that literature, though he acknowledges some papers disagree — his response is simply to test it.
- He clarifies the dual goal of the approach: redirecting usable light *and* wavelength-shifting otherwise unusable light into the usable range.
- He distinguishes **fluorescence** (requires incident light of a specific wavelength to trigger emission) from **luminescence** (does not require that), suggesting a commenter may have meant the latter.
- He expresses interest in using an existing Fresnel lens as a mold for test prisms.
- He favours the sawtooth geometry as worth testing, though acknowledges he doesn't yet know the optimal light direction.
- He notes the experiments are ongoing but stalled due to lack of funds.
- On solar tracking cost-benefit: he argues tracking only makes economic sense for off-grid/battery systems, not grid-tied installs, and believes a cost-benefit case can still be made.
- He thought the "cosine effect" term applied mainly to radar/lidar, not solar — open to being corrected.
- He recommends searching Google Scholar under "light guides, fluorescence and solar" for relevant research papers.
