## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith and his collaborator Luke convert two donated steel oil drums from Canterbury Automotive into vertical-axis wind turbines (VAWTs). Luke builds a Savonius drag-type turbine by splitting a drum in half and offsetting the halves, while Robert builds a lift-type VAWT similar to one he previously made from a washing machine. Both turbines spin successfully at low wind speeds (~2 m/s), demonstrating what can be achieved by repurposing discarded materials.

## Key Topics
- Upcycling and repurposing discarded industrial materials
- Savonius wind turbine design (drag-type, built by Luke)
- Vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) lift-type design (built by Robert)
- Comparing drag-type vs lift-type VAWT operation
- Low-wind-speed performance of both designs

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel oil drums** — donated by Canterbury Automotive; the primary structural material for both turbine rotors
- **Trolley legs** — salvaged metal legs used to construct the rotor cradle
- **Bike hub** — halved and welded into the frame; used as a thrust bearing and guide bearing
- **X-frame** — salvaged from a previous wind generator project by Robert; used as the generator base

## Techniques and Methods
- **Marking and cutting oil drums** — using a T-shirt-style jig with a block and pen to scribe an even horizontal line around the drum before cutting
- **Rotor cradle fabrication** — cutting salvaged steel to size and bolting or welding it to form a horizontal mounting cradle
- **Bike hub bearing modification** — chopping a bike hub in half, welding one half into the frame center as a thrust bearing, and using the other half on the top arm as a guide bearing
- **Welding** — used throughout to assemble the frame, cradle, and bearing components
