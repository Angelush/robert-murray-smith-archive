## Overview
This video demonstrates the formulation of a DIY conductive ink thermal paste using three ingredients, addressing community concerns about conductive ink migrating onto circuit boards. The paste is tested on a laptop CPU under a 10-minute torture test, achieving a peak temperature of 87°C compared to 91°C with the original thermal paste — a 4°C improvement suggesting meaningful thermal potential.

## Key Topics
- Community concern about conductive ink migrating off chips onto PCBs
- Three-ingredient formulation for a graphene conductive ink thermal paste
- Role of each ingredient (conductor, gelling agent, anti-drying agent)
- Mixing technique and consistency adjustment by feel
- 24-hour settling period and its effect on final consistency
- Baseline CPU temperature testing with stock thermal paste
- Comparative benchmark after applying the new paste
- SpeedFan software for temperature monitoring

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink with graphene** — the primary thermally/electrically conductive component
- **Fumed silica (fume silica)** — gelling agent used to thicken the ink to paste consistency; chosen for not interacting with ink properties
- **Glycerin / glycerol** — added in a very small quantity as an anti-drying agent

## Techniques and Methods
- Incremental mixing by eye/feel (no precise weights used) — adding fumed silica gradually until desired consistency is reached
- Thinning adjustment by adding more ink if paste becomes too thick
- 24-hour rest period to allow the mixture to settle and reach final consistency
- Laptop disassembly and heatsink removal for thermal paste application
- CPU torture test (100% load for 10 minutes) using SpeedFan software for temperature logging
- Before/after comparison benchmark under identical load conditions
