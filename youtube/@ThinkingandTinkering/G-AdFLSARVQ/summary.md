## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a magnetic gear system built from plastic discs, small neodymium magnets, and a repurposed toaster electromagnet. The gears transmit rotational motion without physical contact, eliminating friction and wear. The video shows the system generating a measurable voltage and current, and explores its potential applications in wind generators.

## Key Topics
- Magnetic gears as a contactless, frictionless alternative to mechanical gears
- Building on a previous video's plastic disc gear system by replacing toothed belts with magnets
- Electricity generation using a toaster coil (electromagnet used as a pickup coil)
- Torque limitations of magnetic gears and the "slip" phenomenon
- Matching magnet sizes to gear sizes for effective coupling
- Wind generator applications and automatic overload protection via slip
- Low-cost prototyping philosophy: model small before scaling up

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Small neodymium disc magnets: 5 mm diameter, 3 mm thick (small gear) and 10 mm diameter, 3 mm thick (large gear)
- Plastic discs (builder's board) used as gear blanks
- Blue plastic tape (to hold magnets in place and cover the gear faces)
- Electromagnet coil salvaged from a toaster (used as a generator pickup)
- 100 ohm resistor (wired in series for current measurement)
- PC fan (referenced from previous video)
- Toothed belt (used in the previous version, replaced here by magnets)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting circular discs from builder's board using a circular saw
- Drilling evenly spaced holes around the disc perimeter to seat magnets
- Arranging magnets in alternating north–south polarity around each disc
- Gluing and taping magnets in place for a simple, low-cost build
- Measuring AC output voltage and current with a multimeter (no rectification circuit)
- Iterative bench prototyping to discover design rules before scaling

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Segmented metal plate design:** Robert mentions reading about using a segmented metal plate between two magnet sets, noting this is an interesting variation worth exploring.
- **Fixed magnet / rotating metal rotor variant:** He notes that fixing one magnet set and using metal pieces as the rotor changes the torque profile — he was considering a demo model of this.
- **High-torque options exist:** He acknowledges that high-torque magnetic gear configurations have advanced considerably and are not limited to low-torque use cases.
- **Channel philosophy:** When someone suggests a more polished approach, he clarifies: *"not the point on this channel, mate"* — reinforcing his low-cost, accessible prototyping ethos.
- **Scaling is the obvious next step:** Consistent with the video, he agrees a bigger coil is the clear follow-on experiment.
- **General wisdom:** *"like all systems mate it is a good solution for some problems and a bad solution for all problems"* — his pragmatic engineering outlook.
- **Rectangular magnets:** He mentions having a bunch of rectangular magnets and is interested in trying them in a magnetic gear configuration.
