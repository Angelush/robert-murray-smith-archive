## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith designs and 3D prints a DIY water motor based on the turgo turbine (a development of the Pelton wheel), assembles it with standard bearings and hardware, and tests it with a garden hose. The experiment is a work in progress: the housing worked well, but the three 2mm nozzle holes produced insufficient jet force, pointing toward a redesign using a slot nozzle instead.

## Key Topics
- History and modern industrial use of water motors (Pelton wheel era through present)
- Applications of water motors: sewing machines, fans, food mixers, washing machines, potato peelers, home generation, Meccano sets
- The turgo turbine as an efficient development of the Pelton wheel
- 3D printing a turgo turbine housing in TinkerCAD
- Assembly with 608 bearings and 8mm rod
- Using D-shaped draft excluder foam as a water seal
- Testing with a garden hose and evaluating nozzle design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA/3D-printed parts (turbine, housing, housing top, foot/drain)
- 608 bearings (7mm deep, 8mm center bore, 22mm wide)
- 8mm steel rod
- 8mm washer
- D-shaped draft excluder (foam weather stripping, white or brown)
- Small C-clamps (4x) to secure housing lid

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in TinkerCAD
- FDM 3D printing of all major components
- Press-fit bearing installation with washer spacer to prevent binding
- Gluing foot to housing
- Draft excluder applied as a low-cost water seal
- Garden hose pressure testing (open flow vs. thumb-restricted high-pressure jet)
- Nozzle angle set at 30° to match turgo turbine optimal jet angle (25–30°)
