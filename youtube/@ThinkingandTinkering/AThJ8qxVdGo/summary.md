## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a 3D-printed electrostatic (corona/atmospheric) motor of his own design, built from just five part types and requiring no soldering. He explains that atmospheric electricity — the natural voltage gradient of ~100 V/m between Earth's surface and the upper atmosphere — is a real, harness-able energy source, and that electrostatic motors, long dismissed as mere curiosities, are becoming increasingly significant.

## Key Topics
- Atmospheric electricity and the Earth's natural electrostatic gradient (~100 V/m per meter altitude, up to hundreds of kilovolts at ~52 km)
- Electrostatics as "the magnetism of electricity" — like charges repel, unlike attract
- Corona/atmospheric/electrostatic motors — all variants of the same principle
- Robert's custom 3D-printed electrostatic motor design (five part types)
- Assembly walkthrough: copper tape combs, nail electrodes, aluminium foil drum
- Live demonstration with a high-voltage supply
- Industrial and independent development of electrostatic motors (Laser Hacker's "Atmos", the "C motor", work of Oleg Jefimenko)
- Public domain release of all his designs
- Plans to run the motor from atmospheric electricity via an antenna wire

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper tape** (1 cm wide) — used as the conductive strip on each electrode comb bar
- **Steel nails / pins** (30 cm, 1.5 mm diameter) — electrode pins pushed through copper tape to form combs
- **Aluminium foil** (HVAC duct tape/foil, 100 mm / 4-inch wide) — wrapped around the rotating drum as the rotor surface
- **PLA or similar 3D-print filament** (implied plastic) — bulk of the machine structure
- **M4 bolts** (4 mm, 25 mm length) — electrical connection points on comb bars
- **M8 threaded bar** — axle/spacer rods for the frame
- **Skate bearings** — fitted into end plates for the drum axle
- **4 mm crimp connectors** — joining electrode cables

## Techniques and Methods
- **3D printing** — all structural parts (rods, end plates, drum) printed in plastic
- **Copper tape electrode assembly** — pressing nails through copper tape to achieve low-resistance electrical contact (~0.2 Ω) without soldering
- **Alternating comb polarity** — red and black comb sets face opposite directions and alternate around the drum to create directed electrostatic force
- **Inductive electrostatic drive** — positive charge on one comb set induces negative charge migration, producing rotational torque
- **High-voltage supply testing** — initial validation at ~2.5 kV in the microamp range before transitioning to atmospheric harvesting
- **Thumbscrew/lever adjustment** — fine positioning of nail height and angle for optimisation
- **Antenna wire** — proposed method for harvesting atmospheric electricity to drive the motor without mains power

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On the power source:** Clarifies that the mains-powered high-voltage supply is just one convenient source; a wire antenna in the air works equally well, as shown in other experiments — the motor is not dependent on mains power.
- **On the technology's age and relevance:** Acknowledges freely that electrostatic motors are old technology (centuries old) and not novel, but pushes back firmly on dismissiveness — this class of motor is becoming more than a curiosity and deserves serious attention.
- **On the philosophy of atmospheric harvesting:** Confirms that using an antenna wire to harvest atmospheric electricity is exactly the intended direction, linking it to the concept of "wind walls and wind catchers."
- **On design files:** States the files are on Thingiverse (free, public domain, consistent with his stated policy).
- **On audience tone:** Gently but humorously rebukes a commenter who demanded files rudely, and another who was closed-minded, while remaining good-natured throughout.
