## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the mathematical principle behind "impossible gears" — a series of gear arrangements he explored across five videos that week. The key insight is that when one gear circle rolls around another, the path traced by any point on it becomes an epicycloid rather than a circle, and the length of that epicycloid curve equals the original circumference. This geometric property satisfies both the distance and arc-length constraints simultaneously, making the seemingly impossible gear ratios work.

## Key Topics
- The concept of impossible gears and how they function mechanically
- Circles, radii, and gear ratios — starting from the simple 1:1 case
- Calculus and the idea of infinitesimally small points tracing paths
- Leibniz's philosophical concept of the monad as an analogy
- Epicycloids — what they are and why they arise when a circle rolls around another circle
- The general formula for the number of epicycloid lobes: if circle B has radius N and the rolling circle has radius 1/N, it draws N+1 lobes
- Relationship to planetary gear systems
- How the drive and output are taken from smaller vs. larger circles in the actual gear arrangements

## Techniques and Methods
- Geometric reasoning with fixed vs. moving circle centers
- Point-tracing analysis (reducing gear motion to the path of a single point, then generalising)
- Epicycloid construction as the basis for the gear tooth profile
- Ratio derivation: rolling circle radius = 1/N, fixed circle radius = N, lobes = N+1
