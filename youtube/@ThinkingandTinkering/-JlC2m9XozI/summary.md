## Overview
This video is the fourth and final installment in Robert Murray-Smith's series on building a cuboctahedron-shaped wind generator. He demonstrates the completed device spinning using only a small household fan, measures voltage and amp output from its stepper motor generator, and walks through the construction details. The key takeaway is that a functional, weather-tight wind turbine can be built cheaply from scrap aluminium, kitchen bowls, and old venetian blind slats.

## Key Topics
- Final demonstration of the cuboctahedron wind generator spinning under a small fan
- Voltage and current (amp) readings from the stepper motor used as a generator
- Behaviour under short-circuit load while spinning
- Detailed walkthrough of the physical construction and structural design
- Discussion of potential stiffening improvements (struts, top ring)
- Design philosophy: low cost, replicable, open-source
- Plans to test outdoors in real wind conditions
- Reflection on the full design journey (mathematical model → finished device)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Aluminium sheet** — cut into a hexagonal plate forming the main structural element; bent at 30 degrees to add stiffness
- **Kitchen bowls (×2)** — used as the spherical/domed enclosure; a hole was drilled in one to mount the assembly
- **Venetian blind slats** — used as the blade/cage elements; described as not load-bearing, only transferring wind energy to the aluminium plate
- **Stepper motor (NEMA stepper)** — used as the electrical generator; noted to have two thrust bearings in the end blocks
- **Small aluminium hub piece** — central structural hub taking all rotational forces

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling holes in kitchen bowl and aluminium plate for fastening
- Cutting a hexagonal shape from aluminium sheet to match the bowl diameter
- Bending aluminium tabs alternately +30° and −30° (three up, three down) to form a cage retainer
- Using a stepper motor in reverse (as a generator) driven by wind
- Short-circuit load test to observe generator behaviour under electrical load
- Screwed (not welded) assembly for disassembly and replaceability
