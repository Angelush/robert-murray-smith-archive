## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to make a conductive plastic filament suitable for 3D printing electronic components. Using a solvent casting method drawn from an academic paper, he dissolves a low-melting-point plastic in a solvent, mixes in graphite powder, evaporates the solvent, and rolls the resulting composite into filament. The video was prompted by a friend's project to 3D-print a conductive glove, and Robert confirms the concept works with a measured resistivity of ~9 ohms/cm.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: friend's project to 3D-print a functional conductive glove
- Requirements for 3D-printable conductive filament (low melting point, suitable conductivity)
- Solvent casting method as the preferred mixing technique
- Graphite-to-plastic ratio optimisation (15–40%)
- Rolling filament by hand as a low-tech alternative to a filament extruder
- Resistivity measurement of the finished filament

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Polycaprolactone (PCL) / "Polymorph"** — low-melting-point thermoplastic (~60°C), available from Radio Shack, Maplin, and eBay; ~£10 per 500 g
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — mentioned in comments as a second plastic tested and a planned next step
- **5-micron graphite powder** — flattened/flaked form, used as the conductive filler
- **Dichloromethane (DCM)** — primary solvent used to dissolve the plastic
- **Acetone** — alternative solvent mentioned
- **Diethyl ether** — alternative solvent mentioned
- **Glycerine** — mentioned in comments as a standard plasticiser to help with flexibility

## Techniques and Methods
- **Solvent casting** — dissolving plastic in solvent (~100 ml solvent : ~7.5 g plastic), stirring until fully dissolved (~1 hour)
- **Graphite dispersion** — adding graphite powder (15–40 wt%) to the dissolved plastic solution and stirring for a further hour
- **Solvent evaporation** — pouring the mixture onto a flat surface and leaving outdoors until solvent fully evaporates
- **Hand-rolling filament** — rolling the tacky composite by hand (or between two sheets of glass) to ~3 mm diameter before it fully solidifies
- **Resistivity testing** — measuring resistance of the finished filament (result: ~9 Ω/cm)
- **Filament extrusion** (referenced, not demonstrated) — DIY extruder kits noted as an alternative shaping method

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirms two plastics were tested: **polycaprolactone (PCL/Polymorph)** and **ABS**; ABS is considered more useful and he has some arriving.
- Notes that Polymorph already contains **plasticisers**, which may explain why his results came out well; suggests **glycerine** as a plasticiser for pure PCL.
- Acknowledges that turning the material into a consistent, usable filament requires making or buying a **filament extruder** (new cost ~£250).
- Was looking for **PLA chips** as a next material to try; was directed to a supplier (PrintAlot).
- Corrects the paper link to `http://www.fwgltd.co.uk`.
- Expresses intent to build an extruder and do more work on the project when time allows.
