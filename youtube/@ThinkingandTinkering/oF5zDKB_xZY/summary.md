## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history and practicality of gunpowder-powered engines, tracing the concept from early cannon-based attempts through the MythBusters 2006 test to Charlie Macklin's successful modern demonstration using a homemade slow-burning powder fed into an oscillating steam engine. The video concludes with Robert making his own black powder from scratch, explaining the chemistry and process, with the aim of eventually replicating Macklin's gunpowder engine himself.

## Key Topics
- The principle of engine operation via expansion of a working fluid
- Internal vs. external combustion engines, and compressed-air engines
- Historical alternative fuel engines (ethanol, acetylene/acetylene, coal dust)
- History of gunpowder engines: Christopher Huygens, George Cayley, Thomas Paine designs
- MythBusters Season 4, Episode 25 test of gunpowder engines (marked "busted")
- Charlie Macklin's successful oscillating steam engine powered by homemade gunpowder
- Why store-bought gunpowder burns too fast for use in an engine
- History of gunpowder (first recorded use 904 AD, China; formula standardized ~1758)
- Gunpowder chemistry: the roles of sulfur, carbon (charcoal), and potassium nitrate
- Mixing, milling, corning, and graphite-coating processes used in gunpowder manufacture
- How grain size and composition affect burn rate, and why this matters for different applications

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Potassium nitrate (saltpeter/KNO₃)** — oxidizer, 75% by weight; available as food-grade curing salt or stump remover
- **Carbon/charcoal** — fuel, 15% by weight; specifically softwood charcoal (pine) recommended over pure carbon
- **Sulfur** — fuel and ignition-temperature reducer, 10% by weight
- **Ethanol** — discussed as a historical expansion-engine fuel; exhaust was flammable vapor
- **Acetylene** — discussed as a dangerous alternative engine fuel; hazardous above 20 psi, forms explosive copper acetylide in copper/brass pipes
- **Coal dust** — historical powdered-fuel engine feedstock
- **Alcohol** — used wet-milling gunpowder ingredients
- **Graphite** — used to coat powder grains to prevent moisture absorption and static buildup
- **Limestone / lead** — non-sparking milling materials historically used in powder mills
- **Brown powder** — slow-burning variant made with under-charred (brown) charcoal or charred straw

## Techniques and Methods
- Dry-mixing the three gunpowder components in sealed paper cups by alternating/rotating
- Grinding/milling ingredients to fine powder (kitchen blender mentioned for initial sizing)
- Wet-milling with alcohol to achieve thorough mixing
- Pressing the wet mixture into "press cake" to remove moisture
- "Corning" — passing press cake through a sieve to produce granules of controlled size
- Tumbling corns in graphite to coat grains
- Drying potassium nitrate in an oven (~120 °C) to remove hygroscopic moisture
- Avoiding metal containers and static sources during mixing
- Charlie Macklin's engine method: firing homemade powder in a chamber with a particle filter, then ducting the expanding gas into a small brass oscillating steam engine via a spark ignition system
