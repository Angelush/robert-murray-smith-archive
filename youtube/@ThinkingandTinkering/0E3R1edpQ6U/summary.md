## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith documents a series of iterative lab experiments developing a low-cost, leaf-based (chlorophyll) solar cell using a deep eutectic solvent electrolyte and his own conductive ink. The video walks through progressive formulation improvements, from a baseline reading to a final result of approximately 0.5 watts per square meter — about 400 times better than a cuprous oxide cell, achieved under fluorescent light at low temperature. Though unfinished, the results are presented as a promising jumping-off point for home experimentation.

## Key Topics
- Development process and iterative experimentation methodology for DIY solar cells
- Chlorophyll-based dye-sensitized solar cell design
- Use of Japanese matcha green tea as a convenient chlorophyll source
- Performance benchmarking against cuprous oxide cells and commercial silicon solar cells
- Effect of temperature on cell output
- Cost comparison with commercial solar panels
- Future directions: stability testing, sunlight testing, alternative DES solvents, different conductive inks

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Chlorophyll** — the active light-absorbing material in the cell
- **Japanese matcha (green tea powder)** — used as a convenient source of chlorophyll to vary concentration
- **Choline chloride and ethylene glycol** — the deep eutectic solvent (DES) used as the electrolyte
- **Conductive ink (waterproof, nanoporous/microporous)** — homemade ink applied to the copper electrode; described as acting like a membrane
- **Copper plate/strip** — one electrode
- **Aluminium mesh** — counter electrode
- **Glass fibre tissue** — used as the separator between electrodes
- **Plastic sheet** — substrate for the cell assembly
- **Cuprous oxide** — referenced as a comparison material (~1–2 mW/m²)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Cell assembly** — sandwiching a glass fibre separator between a copper plate electrode and aluminium mesh electrode, held together with screws
- **Sonication** — sonicating the matcha/chlorophyll additive into solution for 30 minutes to ensure thorough mixing
- **Conductive ink coating** — painting the copper electrode with waterproof nanoporous ink before applying the electrolyte
- **Electrolyte drop-casting** — dropping the DES + chlorophyll solution onto the separator
- **Electrical characterisation** — measuring open-circuit voltage (V) and short-circuit current (mA) with a multimeter, then calculating power density (mW/m²)
- **Temperature testing** — warming the cell on a beaker of hot water and monitoring current vs. temperature response
- **Light response testing** — shining a supplementary light source on the cell and observing current change

## Robert's Own Replies
- **DES is required**: Confirms that a deep eutectic solvent is needed for the electrolyte ("you will need to use a DES I think mate").
- **Solvent identity clarification**: Clarifies that the solvent used throughout is the same DES (choline chloride/ethylene glycol) + chlorophyll; the variable changed was chlorophyll concentration via green tea addition.
- **Ink alignment**: Notes he aligned the ink on the copper, not the copper itself.
- **Acknowledging failures**: Humorously acknowledges that only the successful experiments make it on camera — failures "go in the bin."
- **Combining ideas**: Suggests he is thinking about how to combine the leaf-cell concept with another approach, pointing toward future hybrid experiments.
- **Grateful for community input**: Thanks a commenter (Phil Lancaster) specifically for very useful feedback that he planned to incorporate in the next set of experiments.
- **Rationale for showing unfinished work**: Explicitly states his intent was to show the iterative, unfinished side of research rather than just a polished "ta-da" result, as he thought viewers would find the process itself interesting.
