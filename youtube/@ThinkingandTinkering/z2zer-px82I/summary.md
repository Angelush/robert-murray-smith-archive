## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores three fundamental gear types — spur, helical, and double helical (herringbone) — comparing their properties, trade-offs, and fabrication methods. He demonstrates a 3D-printed display model combining all three gear types in a planetary-bearing arrangement, emphasizing that 3D printing democratizes access to complex gear geometries like herringbone gears that are prohibitively expensive to machine. The key takeaway is that herringbone gears in particular are underexplored and deserve much more experimentation now that 3D printing removes the cost barrier.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the three "basic" gear types: spur, helical, and double helical (herringbone/chevron)
- Pros and cons of each gear type (noise, strength, alignment, cost, thrust)
- Using helical gears at 45° as a functional substitute for bevel gears
- How herringbone gears cancel axial thrust inherent in helical gears
- Creating gears in Tinkercad and via dedicated gear generator tools (SDL/DXF export)
- Building a 3D-printed display model sourced/redesigned from Thingiverse
- Print-in-place planetary bearing using herringbone gears
- Historical note: Citroën's 1927 herringbone gear installation in a water turbine/generator that ran flawlessly until decommissioned in 2011
- How 3D printing removes traditional manufacturing constraints on complex gear design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned. (All components are 3D-printed; no specific filament materials are discussed.)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D modelling spur gears using Tinkercad's built-in "shapes metric gear" generator
- 3D modelling helical gears in Tinkercad (references a separate prior tutorial video)
- Constructing double helical (herringbone) gears by mirroring a helical gear and joining the halves
- Using an external gear generator tool to produce gear profiles from modulus and tooth-count parameters, exported as STL or DXF
- Splitting a ring gear in the slicer (Orca Slicer variant) and adding locating pegs for reassembly
- Print-in-place bearing fabrication using herringbone rollers at 30° chevron angle to prevent roller twist and drop-out
- Assembly with square-lug shafts, press-fit clips, and adhesive (glue) for final joining
