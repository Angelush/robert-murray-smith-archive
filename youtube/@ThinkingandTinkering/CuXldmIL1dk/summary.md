## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reacts with amusement and disbelief to two viral videos: an Indian product advertisement for "Colleen," a sanitary pad marketed with a "graphene chip," and a follow-up clip purporting to show that wetting the graphene strip from such a pad can illuminate a light bulb. He debunks both as pseudoscientific nonsense — the pad's graphene pattern is drawn on with a pencil, and the light bulb demonstration relies on a battery-powered "magician's light bulb" purchased online. The video is a lighthearted but pointed critique of graphene hype and consumer misinformation.

## Key Topics
- Graphene as a marketing buzzword being exploited by product manufacturers
- "Colleen" — an Indian sanitary pad brand claiming graphene health and absorption benefits
- A viral video falsely demonstrating a graphene-infused pad strip powering a light bulb
- The use of a trick "magician's light bulb" to fake the electricity generation demo
- Historical parallels to radium-in-toothpaste pseudoscience

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — claimed as a functional additive in the sanitary pad; Robert argues it is merely drawn on with a pencil and used as a buzzword
- **Radium** — cited as a historical analogy for dangerous/fraudulent ingredient marketing (radium in toothpaste)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pencil drawing on fabric** — the apparent method used to create the "graphene chip" pattern on the sanitary pad
- **Magician's light bulb (battery-powered)** — the prop identified by Robert as powering the light in the viral demo, purchased on Amazon for ~$14
