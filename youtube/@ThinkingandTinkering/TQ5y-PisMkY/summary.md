## Overview
A one-year follow-up video testing graphene-based conductive ink used as thermal paste in a PC build. The presenter (Luke) runs a series of CPU stress and performance benchmarks to compare temperatures against results from six months prior, finding notable improvements across most tests — with peak CPU temperatures dropping by up to 19°C depending on the workload. The video concludes that the conductive ink has continued to improve over time, and the decision is made to leave it in the system rather than remove it.

## Key Topics
- One-year performance review of graphene-based conductive ink as CPU thermal paste
- Comparison of current temperatures against six-month-old baseline data
- Impact of ambient temperature on test accuracy and result interpretation
- Decision to keep the thermal paste in place due to continued improvement

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene-based conductive ink** — used as thermal paste between CPU and cooler; the subject of the long-term test

## Techniques and Methods
- **Prime95 stress test** — CPU stress test run for 15–20 minutes to measure peak temperatures
- **3DMark (Night Raid)** — benchmark suite with built-in CPU temperature monitoring
- **In-game temperature monitoring** — using Speccy via Alt+Tab during Counter-Strike: Global Offensive and Rocket League sessions
- **MSI Afterburner** — attempted for in-game overlay monitoring (failed to work in this session)
- **Video render test** — 5-minute clip rendered at 1080p 60fps on "High" quality setting as a CPU load test
- **Ambient temperature correction** — cross-referencing historical weather data (14°C on 30 July) to account for seasonal differences in baseline readings
