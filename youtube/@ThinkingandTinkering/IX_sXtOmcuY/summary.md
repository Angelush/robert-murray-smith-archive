## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith reviews the Revopoint Inspire 3D scanner, sent to him free for review, giving his first-time user perspective. He unboxes it, explains how it works, and demonstrates it by scanning a fox skull and 3D printing the result. His conclusion is that it is surprisingly easy to use out of the box, produces good results for organic shapes, and the price point is reasonable.

## Key Topics
- Unboxing and hardware overview of the Revopoint Inspire scanner
- How structured-light infrared 3D scanning works (moiré fringes, point cloud generation)
- Software (Revo Scan 5.2) setup and workflow across multiple devices
- Practical use cases: garage mechanics scanning car parts, museum artefact scanning, replica fabrication
- Scanning a fox skull as a first-time demonstration and 3D printing the result
- Comparison with photogrammetry as an alternative

## Techniques and Methods
- Structured-light infrared scanning (moiré fringe projection with paired IR detectors)
- Point cloud capture and mesh generation
- Multi-scan merging (three scans stitched into a single mesh in software)
- Turntable-based static scanning (~360 frames per rotation / 1 frame per degree)
- STL export for 3D printing
- Handheld mobile scanning (mentioned as a planned follow-up)

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief acknowledgements, but a few add useful detail:
- **Scanning workflow clarification:** "you need to do more than one scan to get the bottom too — the software stitches it together — the scan was a few minutes, the editing a lot longer."
- **On scale/size accuracy:** "I didn't [scale it] — that was just how it printed — I think the scanner understands the actual size."
- **On Linux/Android compatibility:** Suggested running the software in WINE or using the Android version, noting Android is essentially a cut-down Linux.
- **Price:** Repeatedly expressed that the price point surprised and impressed him.
- **Future plans:** Confirmed he is looking forward to getting more use out of it, particularly mobile scanning at museums.
- **On wax coating (a commenter suggestion):** Said he preferred the wax idea as a method for improving scan quality on difficult surfaces.
