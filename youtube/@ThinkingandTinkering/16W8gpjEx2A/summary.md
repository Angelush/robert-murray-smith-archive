## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith guides Luke through a hands-on exploration of magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) electricity generation, starting from the basic principles of electromagnetic induction using solid conductors and magnets, then extending the concept to conductive fluids — salt water, and ultimately a metal-seeded flame. The key takeaway is that anything conductive moving through a magnetic field will generate electricity, which is the same principle underlying all motors, generators, and MHD systems.

## Key Topics
- The three fundamental requirements for electromagnetic generation: conductivity, movement through a magnetic field, and speed
- Demonstration that material magnetism is irrelevant — only conductivity matters
- The Thames Estuary as a real-world MHD generator (salty water in Earth's magnetic field, measured as early as 1856)
- Flame conductivity: how high temperature separates electrons from atoms, creating a plasma
- Using metal salts to increase flame conductivity
- The flame test as a method for identifying elements by emission colour
- How electrodes embedded in a flame replace crocodile clips to extract generated current
- MHD generation as a direct analogue to conventional solid-conductor generators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Brass** — used as a conductive solid in the initial induction demonstration
- **Steel** — tested as a conductor (non-magnetic behaviour in this context)
- **Aluminium** — tested for conductivity; confirmed conductive and non-magnetic
- **Copper / copper wire** — burns green in flame test; added to flame to increase conductivity
- **Potassium nitrate (KNO₃)** — used as a metal salt source; potassium burns pink/purple
- **Sodium** — present in string used to hold the sample; burns yellow
- **Salt water (brine)** — the conductive medium of the Thames Estuary example
- **Neodymium / permanent magnets** — used to create the magnetic field in demonstrations

## Techniques and Methods
- **Electromagnetic induction demonstration** — passing solid conductors (brass, steel, aluminium) through opposing magnetic poles and observing generation
- **Flame conductivity test** — using a butane/propane flame (max ~800 °C) to create a weakly conductive plasma
- **Metal salt seeding of flame** — adding copper wire and potassium nitrate to the flame to increase ionic conductivity, directly enabling MHD-style generation
- **Flame test / spectroscopic colour identification** — identifying elements by characteristic emission colours (copper=green, sodium=yellow, potassium=pink)
- **Electrode extraction** — embedding wire electrodes into the conductive flame to collect the generated EMF, analogous to crocodile clips on a solid bar
