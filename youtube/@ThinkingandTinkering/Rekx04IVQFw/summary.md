## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith presents the results of a second experiment in which a lump of raw coal was subjected to a low-cost, catalyst-free and metal-free process that transformed it from jet-black, poorly-conductive coal into a metallic silver, highly-conductive material consistent with graphite. Resistance dropped from roughly 3 megaohms down to 6 ohms, and the transformation penetrated all the way to the center of a large lump. He concludes the results are exciting but emphasizes that elemental analysis and crystallinity testing are still needed to fully characterize the product.

## Key Topics
- Converting coal to graphite via a proprietary low-cost process
- Electrical resistivity measurement before and after processing
- Visual color change as an indicator of structural transformation (black to metallic silver)
- Crystallinity change in carbon structure as the likely mechanism
- Need for elemental analysis and XRD to confirm composition and crystallinity
- Historical context: graphitization of coal is an idea dating back to the 1800s

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Coal (anthracite)** — raw starting material sourced from a local coal shop in Canterbury; ~3 megaohms initial resistance
- **Graphite** — the proposed product of the process; silver color, ~6 ohms resistance, crystalline carbon structure
- No metals or catalysts — explicitly stated as absent from the process

## Techniques and Methods
- **Resistivity/conductivity measurement** — probes applied to coal before and after processing to compare resistance (megaohms vs. ohms)
- **Unspecified conversion process** — described only as low-cost, metal-free, and catalyst-free; no further details disclosed
- **Physical sectioning** — large lump cut in half with a hacksaw to verify the transformation reached the interior
- **Elemental analysis** (planned) — to determine compositional changes between raw and processed material
- **XRD / crystallinity analysis** (planned) — to confirm the degree of graphitization

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Resistance drop confirmed**: He clarifies the resistance drops from around 4 MOhm to around 1 ohm on average (the video shows a single sample reading of 3 MOhm / 6 ohms).
- **No metals added**: He explains he pre-emptively stressed this because the conductivity invariably prompts accusations of metal contamination from commenters.
- **Supercapacitor test**: He has already tested the processed material as a supercapacitor and reports it works; plans follow-up videos.
- **Interior vs. surface**: Confirms the center of the sawed lump gave the same resistance reading as the surface.
- **Process emissions**: Describes it as a "pretty clean process" with no heavy emissions.
- **Other carbons**: Has only tried coal so far; open to testing other carbon sources.
- **Historical precedent**: Notes there are many published routes to coal graphitization on Google Scholar, and the concept dates to the 1800s.
- **Next steps**: Elemental analysis and XRD are needed before drawing firm conclusions; Part 3 is in progress.
- **Cutting the coal**: It cut easily with a standard hacksaw blade.
