## Overview
Filmed at the Dover Transport Museum's chain shop, Robert explores power transmission systems — from the 1986-era overhead pulley-and-belt machinery on display to modern equivalents hidden inside everyday devices. He uses the museum's large-scale mechanisms as a teaching tool to argue that no single transmission method is universally "best," and that understanding the full range of solutions — mechanical, electrical, and wireless — is essential for choosing the right one for a given context.

## Key Topics
- Overhead belt-and-pulley transmission systems at the Dover Transport Museum
- How legacy mechanical power transmission principles survive in miniaturised, hidden form in modern devices (lathes, drills, bicycles, motorcycles, cars)
- Comparison of mechanical vs. electrical power transmission, with efficiency trade-offs by distance
- The cost and material constraints of electrical grid infrastructure (copper scarcity)
- Wireless power transmission — historical context (Tesla, Wardenclyffe Tower) and modern applications (near-field phone charging)
- The importance of context when evaluating what "the best" solution means
- Centralised generation as a single point of failure

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper — cited as a scarce resource insufficient to provide global grid infrastructure at European/US quality standards

## Techniques and Methods
- Belt-and-pulley power transmission (flat belt from idler pulley to input pulley for tool engagement)
- Chain-drive transmission
- Gear-based transmission
- Rope and pulley systems
- Electrical power transmission via wires (generator → transmission → motor)
- Near-field / inductive wireless power transmission
