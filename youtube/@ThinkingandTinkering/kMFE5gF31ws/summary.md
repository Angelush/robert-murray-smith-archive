## Overview
Robert demonstrates an iterative adaptation of a rocket stove design, comparing a conventional plan-built rocket stove against his own experimental version that incorporates drilled air-intake holes to improve combustion. By adding 12mm holes to allow more oxygen into the burn chamber, he achieves complete combustion of the pyrolysis gases — producing only hot gas at the exhaust with no visible smoke or flame. He concludes this design approach is sound and announces a follow-up video building the final steel version from scratch.

## Key Topics
- Pyrolysis and why open wood fires are inefficient
- How rocket stoves capture and combust volatile gases driven off during pyrolysis
- The role of oxygen supply in achieving complete combustion
- Comparison between a preheated-air-chamber design and a drilled-hole air-intake design
- Vortex formation inside the combustion chamber
- Design considerations for a final production version (hopper feed, legs, chimney flue)
- Gasification principles applied to stove design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Wood** — primary fuel; undergoes pyrolysis inside the stove
- **Steel** — material ordered for the final production version of the stove
- **Volatile gases / pyrolysis gases** — water vapour and combustible gases driven off from wood during heating

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pyrolysis combustion** — burning wood partially to drive off volatile gases, then igniting those gases separately for complete combustion
- **Drilled air-intake holes** — 12mm holes drilled around the burn chamber to introduce additional oxygen at the combustion zone
- **Preheated air jacket / Fern chamber** — earlier design approach using a surrounding chamber to preheat incoming air before it enters the burn zone
- **Iterative prototype testing** — running successive design versions to observe combustion quality (smoke, flame, exhaust) and refine placement of air intakes
- **Gasification principles** — applying theory of wood gasification to inform stove geometry and airflow
