## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to make a simple alkaline gel polymer electrolyte for use in homemade batteries, particularly zinc or iron and nickel chemistries. The recipe is based on patent US5585208 and uses only two readily available components: PVA (builders' glue) and a strong alkali. The resulting solid film can serve as a battery separator, offering a safer and more handleable alternative to liquid electrolytes.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to alkaline gel/polymer electrolytes and their use in batteries
- Reference to patent US5585208 ("Alkaline Gel Electrolyte for Electrochemical Cells")
- Chemistry and composition of PVA (polyvinyl alcohol vs. polyvinyl acetate)
- Mixing ratios and concentration tolerances for the electrolyte
- Casting and drying the gel into a solid film
- Optional additives to improve strength and conductivity
- Practical safety considerations when handling concentrated alkali

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PVA (builders' PVA / white glue)** — mixture of ~60% polyvinyl acetate and ~30% polyvinyl alcohol, ~50–55% solids by weight; used as the polymer matrix
- **Potassium hydroxide (KOH)** — preferred alkali (also sodium hydroxide as alternative); used at 30% by weight in water; chosen for its relevance to nickel-based batteries
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)** — mentioned as an alternative alkali
- **Deionized water** — used to dilute both the PVA solution and to dissolve the KOH
- **Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)** — optional additive up to 20%, MW up to 10,000; improves film strength and conductivity
- **Titanium dioxide (TiO₂)** — optional additive at ~20%; improves conductivity of the dried film
- **Sodium stannate** — mentioned in comments as an electrolyte additive (0.1 mol in 7.5 M KOH solution)
- **"Dowex 1410" anti-foaming agent** — mentioned in comments at 2–3 ppm as part of an improved electrolyte formulation

## Techniques and Methods
- **Preparing the KOH solution** — dissolving KOH incrementally in deionized water (to avoid violent exothermic reaction), targeting 30% by weight
- **Diluting PVA** — watering down ~55% builders' PVA to ~20% concentration using deionized water
- **Dropwise mixing** — adding KOH solution to the PVA solution drop-by-drop while stirring continuously
- **Film casting** — pouring the milky mixed solution onto a flat plate surface
- **Drying / curing** — evaporating the film at 50–80 °C in an oven to form a solid gel film
- **Cutting to shape** — trimming the dried film into the desired battery separator geometry
- **Additive incorporation** — blending PVP or TiO₂ (~20%) into the mixture before drying to enhance properties

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed that a **3 molar** concentration is the normal/standard electrolyte strength.
- Advised that batteries using this electrolyte **need sealing** to prevent drying out; capacity fade can result from either drying or irreversible side reactions — drying is curable, irreversible reactions less so.
- Clarified that **oxidation itself is not the problem** in these batteries — what matters is whether the redox reactions are reversible.
- Recommended an improved aqueous electrolyte formulation: **7.5 M KOH solution containing 0.1 mol sodium stannate and 2–3 ppm Dowex 1410 anti-foaming agent** (or similar).
- Suggested checking the referenced patent for further details on battery construction.
