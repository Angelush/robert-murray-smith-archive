## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the history and practice of stone tool technology, tracing its origins back 3.3 million years to australopithecines — predating homo sapiens by over 3 million years. He demonstrates the Oldowan and Acheulian toolmaking techniques hands-on, including knapping a flint and using a bow-drill with a stone-tipped dowel to bore a hole in wood. His central takeaway is that we tend to overcomplicate engineering when history shows remarkably effective solutions achievable with the simplest possible materials.

## Key Topics
- The philosophy that engineering history informs modern practice
- The Oldowan tool kit: hammerstone and core stone flaking, originating ~3.3 million years ago
- Homo erectus and the development of napping over ~1 million years
- The Acheulian tool kit (named after Saint-Acheul, France, first described 1847)
- Hands-on flint knapping to produce sharp flakes
- Construction and use of a bow-drill with a stone tip to drill wood
- The longevity of stone tool designs (~2 million years for the hand axe form)
- Transition from pure stone tools to supplementary use of bone and antler

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Flint** — used as the core stone for knapping and producing sharp flakes
- **Deer antler (dia antler)** — historically used as a pressure-flaking tool; Robert substitutes beech wood
- **Beech wood** — used as a substitute for deer antler in pressure flaking
- **Deal (soft wood)** — used as the drilling target because of its softness
- **Dowel** — used as the shaft of the bow-drill

## Techniques and Methods
- **Oldowan flaking** — striking a core stone with a hammerstone to detach sharp flakes
- **Napping (pressure flaking)** — pressing a pointed tool (antler or wood) hard into a flake to remove smaller flakes and shape arrowheads, knife blades, or drill bits
- **Bow-drill construction and use** — wrapping a cord around a dowel tipped with a knapped stone point to rotate it at speed and bore a hole in soft wood
