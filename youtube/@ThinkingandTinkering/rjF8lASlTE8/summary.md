## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes two children's bicycle wheels to build a simple gravity battery/generator, connecting them vertically with a polypropylene belt and attaching a hand-crank drill generator to the upper wheel. Dropping weights drives the upper wheel to generate electricity. The first attempt partially works — the LED panel briefly lights up — but reveals clear failure points (belt slipping, weight hitting the floor stopping generation) that will guide improvements in a follow-up video.

## Key Topics
- Concept and construction of a gravity battery/generator from salvaged parts
- Using falling weights as a mechanical energy store (analogous to a weighted clock)
- First-attempt testing and honest failure analysis
- Brief historical/conceptual context: gravity batteries as a well-known energy storage method

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Polypropylene strapping (used as a fan belt, heat-sealed to form a loop)
- Steel square bracket (repurposed antenna connector, used as top wheel mount)
- Bicycle wheels (two children's bike wheels, one red/one blue)
- Weights with hook arrangement (dropped to drive the belt)
- Hand-crank drill generator (converted drill with drill chuck)
- LED lighting panel (used as the electrical load for testing)
- Alternatives mentioned but not used: marble, steel ball bearings, sand, water

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing bicycle tires and cutting forks to fit wheels into custom brackets
- Heat-sealing polypropylene strapping to form a continuous drive belt
- Mounting two wheels vertically in-line (one on a stair banister, one on the floor) to transmit rotation via belt
- Attaching a drill chuck to the upper wheel axle to couple the generator
- Dropping weights on the belt to drive wheel rotation and generate electricity
