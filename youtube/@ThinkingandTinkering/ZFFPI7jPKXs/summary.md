## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces his plan to build a large Van de Graaff generator using a stainless steel garden ornament ball as the dome. He explains the basic operating principles of a Van de Graaff generator using his existing small model as a reference, noting that voltage output scales with dome size. The video serves as a preview/introduction to a build series targeting 300,000–500,000 volts.

## Key Topics
- Comparison between a small existing Van de Graaff generator (~10,000V) and the planned large build (~300,000–500,000V)
- How dome size and roundness determine maximum voltage output
- Core components and operating principles of a Van de Graaff generator
- Triboelectric series and its role in charge separation between rollers
- Faraday's law applied to charge accumulation on the dome
- Parts sourcing plan for the larger build (belt sander motor, drain pipe, exercise band belt)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Stainless steel garden ornament ball (large dome)
- PTFE (Teflon) plumber's tape — used to wrap the top roller of the small generator
- Aluminium — used for the bottom roller
- Rubber exercise band (~1.2 m) — used as the belt; also planned for the large build
- 100 mm (4-inch) drain/sewer pipe — planned column for the large build
- 1.5V–9V DC motor with chain drive (from Maplin kit) — powers the small generator
- Old belt sander — planned donor for motor and rollers on the large build

## Techniques and Methods
- Roller material selection based on the triboelectric series to drive charge separation
- Wrapping PTFE tape around a roller to set its triboelectric position
- Charge collection via metal combs at top and bottom of the belt
- Exploiting Faraday's law (charge migrates to outer surface) to accumulate charge on the dome
- Reversing roller materials to swap polarity of dome charge
- Repurposing/cannibalising existing hardware (belt sander) for motor and rollers
