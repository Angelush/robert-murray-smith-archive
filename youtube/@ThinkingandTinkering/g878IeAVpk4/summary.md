## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a miniature Flynn motor using salvaged transformer parts, neodymium magnets, and soft iron laminations. He explains that scaling the motor down improves efficiency due to the more favorable volume-to-surface-area ratio at smaller sizes. The video walks through assembly step by step, with plans to test the device and measure current output.

## Key Topics
- Rationale for miniaturizing the Flynn motor (cost, efficiency, volume-to-surface-area relationship)
- Sourcing components from scrap/salvaged transformers
- Assembling the iron core from laminations
- Correct magnet orientation (North-North / South-South) for switchable flux
- Role of the keeper bar and alternate flux path
- Using a secondary coil to measure induced EMF/current output
- Trade-offs between wire gauge, coil turns, resistance, and power consumption

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Neodymium (Neo) magnets** — 0.5 cm × 2 cm rod magnets, used as the magnetic core
- **Soft iron laminations** — E and I shapes salvaged from an old metal transformer; 24 I-shapes (12 per core)
- **Super glue** — used to bond laminations and secure magnets in place
- **6 mm nail (mild steel)** — flattened and used as the keeper bar and alternate flux path bar
- **26–28 AWG magnet wire** — coils from a 1:1 isolation transformer (~100 turns per coil)
- **Plastic spacer** — cut from the transformer housing; used to hold and separate the magnet rods

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling old metal transformers to harvest laminations and coils
- Splitting glued laminations apart with a trimming knife blade
- Stacking and clamping laminations in a vice, bonding with super glue
- Drilling with a 6 mm drill and filing with a square needle file to create square holes in plastic spacer
- Threading coils onto iron cores by hand
- Setting magnet polarity orientation (N-N / S-S) before gluing
- Wiring coils in parallel to produce switchable electromagnetic flux
- Using a salvaged transformer coil as a secondary pickup to measure induced current with a meter
