## Overview
This video is a philosophical reflection on the nature of inventing, inspired by Robert's research into Starlite and its creator Maurice Ward. Drawing on a 1994 QED documentary ("Plastic Fantastic"), Robert argues that formal scientific training is far less important than curiosity, creativity, and the willingness to simply try things. The central takeaway is that trial and error — not academic theory — is the true engine of genuine innovation.

## Key Topics
- Maurice Ward (inventor of Starlite) as a model for the amateur inventor mindset
- The QED documentary "Plastic Fantastic" (1994) as a source of lessons on inventing
- The limited correlation between university education and inventorship
- An ICI study showing 5 of 6 top patent-holders had no university degree
- Professor Tony Chalice (Brunel University) on how formal training can produce "blinkered" thinking
- The Edison vs. Tesla debate: trial-and-error vs. theory-driven problem solving
- The liquid metal battery inventor who deliberately hired people with no battery experience
- Thermoelectric generation breakthroughs achieved by questioning established dogma
- The three core traits of inventors: creativity, stubbornness, and doing
- The risk of university training suppressing creative, lateral thinking

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comment replies are mostly brief affirmations, but several contain meaningful substance:

- **On safety as a driver:** Robert explicitly states that safety cannot be a driver of invention — "by its very nature safety is a brake." He clarifies he's not dismissing safety considerations, but it can never be an impulse to act.
- **On failure:** He affirms that "failure is as important, if not more so, as success."
- **On Edison and Tesla:** He agrees that both were flawed, saying their mistake "wasn't theirs, it was ours for putting them on too high a pedestal."
- **On science:** He offers a striking personal view — "science to me is a bit like being a priest mate" — suggesting a quasi-religious reverence for the process.
- **On the journey:** He reflects that "the journey is always the true reward mate," consistent with his broader philosophy of doing over outcome.
