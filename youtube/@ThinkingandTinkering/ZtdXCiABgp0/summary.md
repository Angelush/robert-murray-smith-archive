## Overview
A viewer submission to Robert Murray-Smith's "Made From Paper" competition, demonstrating a paper-based analogic rangefinder with a pinhole improvement. The creator addresses a key limitation of traditional analogic rangefinders — blurry edges — by adding a pinhole that sharpens the silhouette and improves distance estimation accuracy. A live test against a laser rangefinder confirms the paper device works, measuring a subject at exactly 6 meters.

## Key Topics
- Traditional analogic rangefinder design and how it works
- The blurry-edge problem with conventional rangefinders
- Pinhole optics as a solution for sharper edge definition
- Paper construction technique for the rangefinder
- Calibration method using known distances
- Comparison test against a laser rangefinder

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Paper (long strip, folded and layered for rigidity)
- Cellotape (for securing the slider to the body)
- Cord (threaded between the two body pieces)

## Techniques and Methods
- Folding a long paper strip in half to create double thickness
- Rolling and wrapping paper around the strip to form the body
- Cutting a precise triangle/window in the paper body
- Poking a pinhole through a spare folded piece of paper
- Calibrating the rangefinder at known distances (3 m, 6 m, 9 m) and marking scale positions
- Attaching the device to a phone camera for assisted measurement
- Validation by comparison with a laser rangefinder
