## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to synthesize graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) from cheap, readily available urea as a low-cost photocatalytic material for splitting water into hydrogen using sunlight. He then shows how to improve its photocatalytic performance by doping it with iron(III) chloride, producing a material that would cost ~£900 commercially for just a few pounds in materials and electricity.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of conventional water-splitting catalysts (high cost, rare metals)
- Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) as an abundant, inexpensive alternative photocatalyst
- Synthesis of g-C3N4 from urea via thermal decomposition
- Commercial cost vs. home synthesis cost comparison (~£900 vs. ~£3–4 per 10g)
- Additional applications: photocatalytic CO₂ reduction to methanol and ethanol
- Dopants that enhance photocatalytic activity
- Iron-doping procedure using iron(III) chloride in acetone
- Potential use in solar cells and semiconductors

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Urea (CH₄N₂O)** — primary precursor; ~£2/kg, the main component of urine and a common fertilizer
- **Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4)** — the target product; lemon yellow powder, p-type semiconductor
- **Iron(III) chloride (FeCl₃)** — used as dopant; 1–2g per 0.5–1g of g-C3N4
- **Acetone** — solvent for the iron-doping procedure (50mL)
- **Titanium dioxide (TiO₂)** — mentioned as a co-catalyst/dopant option
- **Platinum** — mentioned as a high-performance but expensive dopant
- **Zinc chloride (ZnCl₂)** — alternative dopant used in ethanol solution
- **Sulfur** — mentioned as a possible dopant
- **Nitrogen** — mentioned as a possible dopant (improves conductivity per comments)
- **Carbon dioxide** — substrate for photocatalytic conversion to methanol/ethanol

## Techniques and Methods
- **Thermal decomposition of urea** — heated to 550–700°C for 3 hours in a covered ceramic or pyrex vessel to form g-C3N4; produces strong ammonia smell
- **Microwave kiln synthesis** — cycling: 15 min on / 5 min off for 3 hours as an alternative to a potter's kiln
- **Iron-doping via solvent impregnation** — dissolving FeCl₃ in acetone, adding g-C3N4, stirring sealed for 5 hours at ~30°C on a magnetic stirrer
- **Centrifugation and washing** — separating the doped solid from the acetone supernatant, washing with acetone, then air-drying
- **Thin film deposition** (mentioned) — mixing g-C3N4 with solvent to create a paste for electrode coating and calcination
- **Photocatalytic water splitting** — placing the doped material in a cell exposed to sunlight to produce hydrogen gas

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On electrode construction:** Two electrodes required, at least one transparent; apply g-C3N4 paste to one, calcinate it, add an electrolyte as the charge carrier — "you are good to go."
- **On voltage and current output:** Voltage depends on materials and electrolyte; current depends on light intensity. Multiple cells can be wired in series or parallel to adjust voltage/amperage.
- **On heating losses:** Losses during synthesis are expected and intentional — you are burning off unwanted components. The container should not be fully sealed; he rested a saucer on top of the jar, which worked well.
- **On doping options:** Recommends looking into sulfur doping and heteroatomic doping in general. Nitrogen doping improves conductivity.
- **On further research:** Consistently directs commenters to Google Scholar and peer-reviewed papers rather than giving off-the-cuff answers, emphasizing that reading the literature is part of the skill set.
- **On motivation for the channel:** "There is hardly any real info out there — plenty of 'have a look at this isn't it wonderful' sort of stuff but next to none on how to go about doing it."
- **On CO₂ synthesis precursors:** Notes that flowing CO₂ and CO over the bed as precursor materials is needed, and promises to investigate further.
- **Future plans mentioned:** Intends to make a solar cell combining g-C3N4 with graphene, and will post a theory video; also interested in AZO (aluminium-doped zinc oxide) films and CS doping.
