## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to create a touch-activated light switch by painting conductive ink onto any surface — paper, card, or even a wall. The switch works by connecting the painted pad to a cheap capacitive touch sensor module, allowing a lamp to be toggled on and off by touch. The key insight is that the switch can be placed anywhere paintable, in any shape, and still works through a protective covering.

## Key Topics
- Using conductive ink as a touch-sensitive electrode
- How capacitive touch sensing works (body capacitance changes trigger the sensor)
- Wiring a capacitive touch sensor module into a mains lamp circuit (UK wiring conventions)
- Applying a protective membrane over the painted switch pad
- Practical applications: replacing conventional wall switches with painted equivalents
- Plans to sell the project as a kit

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — painted onto card, paper, or wall surface to form the touch electrode
- **White card / paper** — used as the substrate for the painted switch pad
- **Sticky-back plastic** — suggested as a protective membrane over the painted pad

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting a square (or any shape) of conductive ink onto a surface and allowing it to dry
- Running a painted line to the edge of the pad to provide a wiring connection point
- Wiring a mains capacitive touch sensor module using a terminal block (live, neutral, lamp output, and touch signal wire)
- Flipping the painted card face-down so the touch is detected through the paper (acts as a dielectric layer, maintaining capacitive sensitivity while protecting the ink)
- Optionally covering the pad with sticky-back plastic as a more permanent protective membrane

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Product compliance:** Noted that CE/safety testing only applies if the conductive ink is sold as a product coming off a production line.
- **Relay for high loads:** Suggested that if the touch switch is used to trigger a relay, it could handle any load whatsoever — removing the amperage limitation of the sensor module.
- **Replacing home switches:** Confirmed he intends to swap his own house light switches for painted conductive ink versions and will make a follow-up video.
- **Under-floor use:** Agreed that the touch sensor approach would work well under flooring too.
- **Capacitive mechanism:** Confirmed to a commenter that it works exactly like a touchscreen.
- **Playable wall:** Expressed enthusiasm for the concept of a "playable wall" using this technique.
- **Encouraged community:** Asked viewers who try the project to share videos of their results.
