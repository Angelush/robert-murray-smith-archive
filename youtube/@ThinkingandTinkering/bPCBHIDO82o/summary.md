## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a low-cost DIY centrifuge from acrylic discs and a hobby drill, motivated by the need to separate metal nanoparticles from solution without spending £200 on a commercial unit. The build uses heat-formed acrylic, a hole saw, and a bench-mounted hobby drill as the spin motor, achieving speeds up to 12,000 RPM. The total cost is around £10, making it accessible to cash-strapped independent researchers.

## Key Topics
- Budget constraints for independent researchers and DIY lab equipment
- Construction of a centrifuge rotor from acrylic sheet
- Using a hobby drill as a low-cost spin motor
- Speed control options (dimmer switch vs. PWM controller)
- Application to separating metal (zinc/silver) nanoparticles from solution

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **4mm acrylic sheet** — used to cut and form the centrifuge rotor discs
- **Superglue** — used to bond the rotor discs together
- **4mm screws** — used to fasten the three discs
- **8mm and 6mm rod/spindle** — central shaft of the rotor
- **25mm sample tubes** — the centrifuge sample holders; sized to fit the drilled rotor holes
- **Metal nanoparticles (zinc/silver implied)** — the target material to be separated
- **Ascorbic acid** — mentioned in comments as a capping agent to prevent rusting of particles
- **Copper salt / ammonia complex** — mentioned in comments in relation to copper nanoparticle synthesis
- **Oleic acid** — mentioned in comments as an anti-oxidant coating and phase transition agent for copper nanoparticles

## Techniques and Methods
- **Heat-forming acrylic** — heating acrylic sheet with a hairdryer until soft, then pressing it over a bowl mould to create a domed rotor disc
- **Hole saw drilling** — cutting 25mm holes in the rotor to accept sample tubes; drilling the central 8mm shaft hole
- **Bench-vice mounting** — clamping the hobby drill upright in a bench vice to serve as a stable motor base
- **Speed control via dimmer switch** — suggested as a simple variable speed method; PWM controller with brushless DC motor recommended in comments for more demanding applications

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On motor speed control**: Clarifies that a dimmer switch is a poor choice for high-demand applications; recommends a **PWM controller with a brushless DC motor** capable of up to 70,000 RPM in 48V+ configurations for more serious use.
- **On his lab vs. home builds**: Distinguishes between his home DIY projects (affordable, accessible) and his actual lab equipment (e.g., a homogeniser running at 13,000 RPM that cost more); notes he buys what works for the lab.
- **On centring the rotor**: Fixed an off-centre rotor by drilling it out, gluing and pinning it, then spinning it in a fixed drill to true it up.
- **On nanoparticle rust/compaction**: Advises capping particles with **ascorbic acid** to prevent rusting; notes impure chemicals cause inconsistent results.
- **On copper nanoparticle work** (top-level comment): Reports solving yield and size issues by complexing copper salt with ammonia; awaiting **oleic acid** delivery for anti-oxidant coating and phase transition; considering polymerisation to produce nanoparticle "hair" structures.
