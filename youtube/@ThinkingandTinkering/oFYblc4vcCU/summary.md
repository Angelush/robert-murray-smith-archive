## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a liquid-route method for incorporating graphene into plastic (ABS), solving the two core problems of loading and dispersion that plague conventional heat/extrusion approaches. By transferring graphene from a water suspension into acetone, then dissolving ABS directly into that acetone-graphene solution, he achieves thorough dispersion at higher loadings than melt-extrusion allows. The resulting graphene-filled ABS can be remelted and extruded into filament.

## Key Topics
- Why conventional heat/extrusion mixing fails above ~0.5% graphene loading (graphene acts as a lubricant)
- The two main challenges: loading percentage and thorough dispersion
- Using a liquid/solvent route as the solution to both problems
- Transferring graphene from aqueous suspension into acetone via filtration and solvent washing
- Dissolving ABS in the graphene-acetone solution to produce graphene-filled plastic
- Scalability considerations and acetone recovery for larger production runs
- Applicability of the method to other plastics by choosing an appropriate compatible solvent

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — in stable aqueous suspension; the filler material being incorporated into plastic
- **Water** — carrier medium for the graphene suspension
- **Acetone** — solvent used to transfer graphene off the filter paper and dissolve ABS; also the evaporative medium
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — the plastic being filled with graphene
- **Polycaprolactone** — mentioned as an alternative plastic requiring a different solvent
- **Chloromethane** — mentioned as the compatible solvent for polycaprolactone
- **HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — used as a non-stick cover sheet for casting the mixture

## Techniques and Methods
- **Filtration washing** — diluting graphene suspension with water (5:1 ratio) then vacuum/gravity filtering to collect graphene on filter paper
- **Solvent exchange (acetone wash)** — pouring acetone over filtered graphene to displace residual water and transfer graphene into an acetone-compatible suspension
- **Solution blending** — adding a known quantity of ABS directly to the graphene-acetone solution and shaking to mix
- **Solvent casting / evaporation** — pouring the mixture onto HDPE sheet and allowing acetone to evaporate, leaving graphene-filled ABS film
- **Solvent recovery** — described for bulk production: using a hood with cooling to condense and recapture evaporated acetone
- **Remelting and filament extrusion** — mentioned as the downstream step to convert the cast material into usable filament

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Safety of acetone**: Confirmed acetone is flammable and should be handled carefully — don't smoke over it — but noted that acetone washing of ABS is a well-known practice in 3D printing, so many people are already familiar with safe handling.
- **Properties of the resulting material**: Confirmed the graphene-filled plastic is both **stronger and conductive**, encouraging people to keep experimenting.
- **Filter paper**: Clarified that a simple **coffee filter paper** will work for the filtration step (some graphene will be lost, but most will remain).
