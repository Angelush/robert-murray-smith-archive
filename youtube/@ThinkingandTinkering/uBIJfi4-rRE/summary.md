## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith examines the hydrogen economy — the proposed replacement of fossil fuels with hydrogen — and argues that its "green" credentials are largely illusory under current production methods. He briefly surveys existing approaches (steam cracking, water electrolysis, ammonia as a hydrogen carrier) before advocating strongly for urea/urine electrolysis as an underexplored, energy-efficient, and ecologically beneficial alternative. His core takeaway is that the enormous daily volume of biological waste urine represents a largely ignored hydrogen feedstock that could simultaneously reduce pollution and produce fuel.

## Key Topics
- What the hydrogen economy is and why it appeals (burning hydrogen produces only water)
- Why current hydrogen production is not truly green (steam cracking of petroleum releases CO₂; grid electrolysis relies on coal/nuclear power)
- The circular hydrogen economy concept (electrolyse water → burn hydrogen → electrolyse again)
- Ammonia as a hydrogen carrier: the Haber–Bosch process powered by solar energy (Australian project), then ammonia electrolysis to recover hydrogen
- Energy advantage of electrolyzing ammonia vs. electrolyzing water
- Urea/urine electrolysis as a highly energy-efficient hydrogen source
- The vast scale of biological urine production (~240 million tons/day from humans alone, plus livestock)
- Environmental harm of current urine disposal (algal blooms, land and sea pollution)
- The social/psychological barrier to taking urine seriously as a fuel source

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Hydrogen (H₂)** — the proposed fuel; produced and burned in the hydrogen economy
- **Water (H₂O)** — feedstock for electrolysis; product of hydrogen combustion
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — byproduct of steam-cracking petroleum for hydrogen
- **Petroleum** — current primary feedstock for hydrogen via steam cracking
- **Ammonia (NH₃)** — proposed hydrogen carrier; produced via Haber–Bosch, then electrolysed to release H₂
- **Urea / Urine** — highlighted as a low-energy electrolysis feedstock for hydrogen production; also yields fertiliser as a byproduct

## Techniques and Methods
- **Steam cracking of petroleum** — current dominant industrial method for hydrogen production
- **Water electrolysis** — splitting water into H₂ and O₂ using electrical energy
- **Haber–Bosch process (solar-powered)** — synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen, cited in the context of an Australian renewable-energy project
- **Ammonia electrolysis** — electrolyzing ammonia to recover hydrogen; significantly more energy-efficient than water electrolysis
- **Urea/urine electrolysis** — electrolysing urine to produce hydrogen; demonstrated by four Nigerian schoolgirls who ran an engine for six hours on one litre of urine
