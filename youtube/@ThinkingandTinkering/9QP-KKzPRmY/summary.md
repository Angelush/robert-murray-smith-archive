## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a working wooden model of a piston-and-crank mechanism, sourcing inexpensive hardwood from kitchen chopping blocks rather than specialist suppliers. The video walks through cutting, assembling, and connecting all components — crankshaft discs, connecting rod, piston, and cylinder — to produce a functional model that converts rotary motion to linear motion. The main takeaway is that complex mechanical concepts can be illustrated beautifully and cheaply using basic woodworking techniques.

## Key Topics
- How piston-and-crank mechanisms work (linear ↔ rotary motion conversion)
- Sourcing cheap hardwood from kitchen chopping blocks as an alternative to specialist suppliers
- Cutting and assembling crankshaft discs, connecting rod, piston, and cylinder from wood
- The role of pivot points (small end at piston head, big end at crankshaft)
- How stroke distance is determined by the offset between the two crankshaft disc centres
- Adding a flywheel to complete the model

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Beech wood** — sourced from a £2.50 chopping block, used for structural parts
- **Redwood (probable)** — sourced from a £2.00 chopping block, used for contrasting colour
- **Dowels / cotter pins** — used as pivot pins and axles throughout the assembly

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hole saw cutting** — used to produce the circular disc components (crankshaft wheels, piston discs, cylinder blanks)
- **Drilling and dowelling** — joining piston layers and mounting pivot pins
- **Stacking discs** — three circles glued/stacked to form the piston body
- **Halving a disc** — half-circle piece attached to the piston base to create the small-end pivot
- **Sectional build-up** — crankshaft assembled in sections (rather than through-axle method) because the axle is fixed
- **Cutaway construction** — one side of the cylinder left open for visual demonstration of piston travel
