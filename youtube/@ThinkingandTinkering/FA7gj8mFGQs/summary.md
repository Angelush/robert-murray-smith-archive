## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the Turgo turbine as a simpler, cheaper alternative to the Pelton wheel, explaining that it is essentially a Pelton bucket cut in half with water entering at 20–30°. He demonstrates two DIY builds — one from stainless steel tablespoons welded onto a hub, and one from plastic teaspoons hot-glued onto plastic discs — showing that both can generate electricity when coupled to a stepper motor.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of the Pelton wheel (high head requirement, complex bucket geometry, wear, size/weight)
- How the Turgo turbine differs from the Pelton wheel and why it improves on it
- Optimal jet angle for Turgo turbines (20–30°)
- Efficiency of Turgo turbines (~90–93%)
- DIY suitability of Turgo vs. Pelton designs
- Build 1: stainless steel tablespoons welded onto a speaker-magnet hub
- Build 2: plastic teaspoons hot-glued onto layered plastic discs
- Pico-hydro power generation potential
- Reference to a peer-reviewed research paper on Pico Hydro Turgo turbine design parameters

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel tablespoons** — used as blades for the welded build; chosen for corrosion and pitting resistance
- **Polystyrene teaspoons** — used for the glued plastic build (noted as less durable)
- **Polycarbonate teaspoons** — recommended as a tougher, virtually unbreakable alternative for a more permanent build
- **Plastic discs** — used as the hub/mounting plate for the teaspoon build
- **Hot glue** — adhesive for the plastic teaspoon version
- **Speaker magnet/pole piece** — repurposed as the hub for the welded stainless steel build

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting spoon heads from handles at the correct profile
- Welding stainless steel spoons onto a metal hub at 30° twist angle
- Marking discs at 10° intervals for 36-blade spacing
- Using a card gauge to set the 30° blade twist during gluing
- Hot-gluing blades onto layered plastic discs
- Mounting the turbine on a stepper motor to demonstrate electricity generation
- Directing a water jet at 20–30° to the turbine plane

## Robert's Own Replies
- Acknowledged the turbine needed truing up after the spin test.
- Confirmed he plans to do a 3D-printed version of the Turgo turbine.
- Clarified that his builds are **demonstrations only** — for a long-term installation, engineering for durability is essential: waterproof bearings, grease, seals, and chain drives are all worth considering depending on the application.
- Noted that without an enclosure, water sprays everywhere, which will quickly rust unprotected components.
