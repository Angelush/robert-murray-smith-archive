## Overview
Robert assembles a hand-powered bellows generator from three previously built modular components: a set of bellows, a ping-pong ball check valve, and a vane motor. By pumping the bellows, air is directed through the valve in one continuous direction, spinning the vane motor to generate electricity. Robert demonstrates the completed system and notes it produces enough power to charge a phone.

- Ping-pong ball (used as a check valve)
- Bearing (added to improve smooth, true rotation)

## Techniques and Methods
- Bellows fabrication (manual air pump construction)
- Check valve construction using a ping-pong ball to ensure unidirectional airflow
- Vane/van motor construction for air-to-rotation conversion
- Modular component assembly
- Drilling holes in a motor cap to reduce airflow occlusion
- Press-fitting/adapting components with a 3D-printed adapter
- Rotation-to-electricity generation via a small motor run in reverse

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Output voltage:** Confirmed the generator output was **5V**.
- **Lever suggestion:** Recommended adding a lever to the bellows to make pumping easier and more efficient (mentioned multiple times).
- **Bellows construction:** Clarified that he had glued the bellows for demo purposes (to keep the top accessible), but acknowledged that **adding springs** inside would improve performance.
- **Two bellows:** Implied agreement that using two bellows in alternating opposition (like a Japanese box bellows) would produce a smoother, more continuous airflow.
- **Pedal power:** Pointed a commenter to his [pedal power playlist](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eZ0m09VPzBE&list=PLbQqm4rNo627yyPNE2MQXtvXNbrL-7vkJ) as a related project.
