## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the mechanical challenge of cornering on a delta trike (two driven rear wheels) and demonstrates a low-cost, DIY differential built entirely from repurposed bicycle freewheel cassettes. By orienting two freewheels on a shared rigid axle so both engage in the same direction, the assembly behaves like a locked axle going straight but allows the inner wheel to freewheel during cornering — achieving a differential effect without a traditional, expensive differential unit.

## Key Topics
- Delta vs. tadpole trike configurations and their traction trade-offs
- Why a solid driven axle causes wheel slip in corners
- How conventional differentials solve this and why they're impractical on trikes
- The freewheel-as-differential concept (limited-slip behaviour using pawl-and-ratchet mechanisms)
- Disassembling bicycle cassettes to expose the inner and outer freewheel sleeves
- Connecting the outer sleeve to the wheel and the inner sleeve to the axle
- Handling the mirrored orientation problem for the second freewheel
- Limitation: forward-only operation (no reverse drive)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Blank bicycle gear cassettes (used as freewheel units)
- Freewheel removal tools (drilled out to 16 mm to fit the axle, repurposed as splined drives)
- Rigid steel axle (16 mm)
- Locking ring (with external teeth)
- Bearings and original bearing cups
- Weld (used to join outer sleeve to wheel-mounting hardware)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembling cassette/freewheel clusters by striking the locking ring anti-clockwise with a punch or screwdriver
- Drilling out a freewheel removal tool to 16 mm to create a splined axle interface
- Separating inner and outer cassette sleeves to re-route the drive path (outer → wheel, inner → axle)
- Mirroring/reversing the second cassette orientation so both freewheels engage in the same rotational direction
- Cutting off the wheel-mounting flange from one cassette half and welding it to the reversed unit
- Bolting the assembly together to maintain axial tension
