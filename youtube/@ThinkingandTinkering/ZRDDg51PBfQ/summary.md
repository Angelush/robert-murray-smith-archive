## Overview
Robert provides a status update on his range of conductive inks, explaining the trade-offs he has discovered between waterproofing and electroconductive properties. He concludes that no single ink can serve all purposes, and announces a plan to maintain separate formulations for different tasks — standard conduction, "swagging," and electroplating — while also working toward a cost-effective electroluminescent ink.

## Key Topics
- Status of the main conductive ink (the "substantive" ink) and its properties
- Limitations of the standard ink — not strictly waterproof, problematic for electroplating
- Development of a waterproof conductive ink variant for electroplating plastics
- The "swag ink" and its unique electroconductive properties in salt water/seawater
- Trade-off discovered: improving waterproofing degrades electrogenerative conductivity
- Decision to keep the swag ink formulation separate and rename it for "swagging"
- Work-in-progress on a dedicated waterproof electroplating ink
- Development of an electroluminescent (EL) ink and the cost problem with commercial EL phosphors
- Home synthesis of zinc sulfide-based EL phosphors as a cost-saving approach

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink (standard/substantive)** — coats well, burnishes to good conductivity, not waterproof
- **Swag ink** — conductive in salt water and seawater solutions, has electrogenerative properties
- **Waterproof conductive ink** — experimental version intended for electroplating plastics; currently gel-like and grainy
- **Zinc sulfide (ZnS)** — base material for electroluminescent phosphors, ~$26/kg
- **Electroluminescent phosphors (doped zinc sulfide)** — commercial versions cost ~$500 for 500 g; Robert is attempting to make his own
- Organic substrates mentioned in use: **leaves, wood, leather**

## Techniques and Methods
- **Burnishing** — applied to the standard ink to achieve good conductivity
- **Electroplating plastics** — priming plastic, applying waterproof conductive paint, then plating over it
- **Swagging** — a distinct electroconductive process using the swag ink in salt water/seawater environments
- **Home synthesis of EL phosphors** — converting zinc sulfide into electroluminescent phosphor material to avoid commercial costs
- Stirring ink before use to re-suspend settled material

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Making EL phosphors at home is difficult** — properties are highly dependent on process conditions; homemade versions may be unstable.
- **EL powders do not contain phosphorus** — "phosphor" is just a legacy term; the materials are primarily doped zinc sulfate (zinc sulfide), which is what he is working with.
- **The grainy texture of the ink is real** — it can be improved or worsened depending on application technique.
- **60 ohms resistance is still usable** — though he acknowledges those extra few ohms are a frustration.
- **He is actively making his own EL phosphors** — confirming the cost problem with commercial sources and his DIY solution.
- **Expressed interest in seeing a user's leaf project** — called it "very clever," suggesting application of the ink to organic materials is yielding interesting community results.
