## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the global freshwater scarcity problem and reviews an MIT breakthrough in solar desalination. The MIT system uses thermohaline circulation to prevent salt clogging — a critical flaw in previous solar still designs — enabling a half-square-meter unit to produce 4–6 litres of fresh water per hour at a cost cheaper than tap water. Robert also demonstrates a DIY version using Oasis foam and activated carbon felt, showing a ~50% improvement in evaporation rate over a plain bowl of water.

## Key Topics
- Global freshwater scarcity: only ~1.2% of Earth's water is accessible fresh water
- The natural water cycle as the basis for desalination thinking
- History and limitations of simple solar stills
- The salt-clogging problem in solar evaporation systems
- MIT's thermohaline circulation-based solar still
- DIY replication using Oasis foam and activated carbon felt
- Cost comparison: MIT system vs. household tap water

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Seawater / salt water** — the input feed for desalination
- **Oasis foam** — a floral/craft foam used for its capillary wicking properties, described as mimicking plant cell structure
- **Activated carbon felt** — black felt used to absorb solar radiation and accelerate surface evaporation
- **Brine** — the concentrated salt byproduct discharged from the MIT system

## Techniques and Methods
- **Solar still (basic)** — bowl with plastic cover and central rock to condense and collect evaporated water
- **Surface-layer evaporation** — isolating a thin layer of water at the surface (via wicking material) rather than heating the bulk, dramatically improving efficiency
- **Thermohaline circulation** — exploiting density differences between hot fresh water (rises) and cold salty water (sinks) to continuously flush salt out of the evaporator, preventing clogging
- **Gravimetric comparison test** — Robert's DIY experiment weighing two 2-litre bowls (control vs. foam+felt) hourly to measure evaporation rate
