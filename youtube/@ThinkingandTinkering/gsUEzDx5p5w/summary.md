## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build an Ugrinsky VAWT (Vertical Axis Wind Turbine) using simple, inexpensive materials found around the home. He breaks down the geometry into two key ratios — S = 0.35D and L = D/2 — to make the construction accessible to anyone. The Ugrinsky design is claimed to be 54% more efficient than a standard Savonius turbine, and Robert shows it spinning successfully under a fan.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the Ugrinsky VAWT design and its geometric principles
- Simplifying the construction process for home builders
- Comparison with the Savonius turbine
- Demonstration of the completed rotor spinning in wind

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **200mm disc** — main rotor disc, cut with a hole saw
- **70mm PVC pipe** — used for the small semicircle blades (S)
- **Rainwater gutter (uPVC)** — cut into slices and heat-formed for the L-arc section
- **200mm diameter stainless steel kettle** — used as a forming mandrel for the uPVC gutter
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate / "crazy glue")** — bonding agent for assembly
- **Bicarbonate of soda** — used as a CA glue accelerator/filler for a hard, sandable joint

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting discs with a hole saw
- Cutting pipe into semicircles for blade sections
- Heat-forming uPVC gutter strip against a curved metal former (kettle) to achieve the correct arc radius
- Bonding with super glue and bicarbonate of soda to fill gaps and harden joints
- Geometric layout using a compass and diameter line to position blade arcs

## Robert's Own Replies
- **3D-printed versions exist**: Robert notes he has done videos on 3D-printed Ugrinsky rotors and has files on Thingiverse, but points out that with physical pipe-forming there is no size limit, unlike a small 3D printer.
- **Formula has leeway**: He clarifies that the formula he gave is optimised for best overall power, but different condition-specific optimisation sets of formulae exist. He suggests reading the referenced paper for deeper detail, deliberately keeping the video simple to get people started.
- **Water test done**: He mentions having done a water test and intends to post it.
- **Generator is separate**: He explains clearly that a turbine blade does not produce power by itself — it captures wind and converts it to rotation; an appropriate generator must then be attached. Efficiency in blade design is about wind-to-rotation conversion, not direct power output.
- **Blade count**: Two blades — he notes this is visible in the video and in reference images.
- **Starting point flexibility**: Any starting point in the geometry works as long as the S/L/D relationships are maintained.
- **Paint tins** floated as a possible forming material for the L section.
- **Thermal cutoff**: If used with a generator, a thermal cutoff would be essential to avoid melting plastic.
- **Related videos**: Directs viewers to videos 1749, 1753, and 1754 for related content.
