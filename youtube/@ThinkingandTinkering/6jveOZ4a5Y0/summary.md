## Overview
Robert demonstrates a simple water motor inspired by an 1901 design from *Model Engineering and Amateur Electrician*, updated using modern plastic and nylon materials. The motor works similarly to a Tesla turbine — a water jet strikes a textured disc to spin it — but is far easier to build. He runs it as a generator, powering an LED, and argues it represents a practical way to harvest energy from household water pressure.

## Key Topics
- Comparison to Tesla turbines and motivation for a simpler alternative
- Step-by-step construction of the water motor housing and rotor
- How the water jet interacts with the textured disc surface to produce rotation
- Using the motor as a generator (connected to a NEMA 17 stepper motor and LED)
- Historical context: water motors were used in 1901 homes (before electrical grids) to run sewing machines, fans, and lights
- Practical modern applications: inline with washing machines, storage tanks, or toilet cisterns

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Builders board / Sintra board** — main structural material for housing and rotor disc
- **Nylon netting (30 mesh)** — glued to rotor disc to create a rough surface for the water jet to grip
- **Super glue** — used to bond nylon netting to the disc
- **Skate bearings** — used in the front and back plates for the axle
- **NEMA 17 stepper motor** — used as a generator to demonstrate electrical output
- **LED** — used to demonstrate power generation

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting discs from builders board and laminating two layers for the housing ring
- Drilling front and back plates to accept skate bearings
- Assembling and gluing the motor housing with a cut outlet port
- Attaching 30-mesh nylon netting to the rotor disc with super glue
- Using a 1.5 mm orifice (jet nozzle) to produce a fine, high-pressure water jet
- Directing the jet at an acute angle toward the outer edge of the disc for maximum spin
- Running the assembly as a generator by connecting it to a stepper motor

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed the motor **is capable of meaningful power generation**, calling it a viable approach depending on build quality.
- Agreed that **hitting the disc at the edge** (rather than the face) would also work, though hitting the front is easier to build.
- Noted he only has up to **Volume 12** of the historical *Model Engineering* series, welcoming the tip about further volumes.
- Suggested a **multiple approach** (combining methods) is likely the best strategy for energy harvesting.
- Acknowledged uncertainty about **exact power output figures**, saying he was more focused on the build, but noted the motors "do have potential."
- Commented on the **limited availability of builders board / Sintra board**, noting Eurocell makes it in the UK but it's hard to find elsewhere — calling it "weird."
