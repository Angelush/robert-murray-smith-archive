## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a working supercapacitor using egg whisks as electrodes, inspired by a suggestion from community member "xyloscope." Activated carbon felt is stuffed inside stainless steel wool unwound from the whisk, submerged in a saltwater electrolyte, and charged at 1.2 V. The device successfully stores and releases energy — demonstrated by running a small motor — proving the concept works even if efficiency is limited.

## Key Topics
- Community-inspired DIY supercapacitor construction
- Using egg whisks (stainless steel) as electrode current collectors
- Activated carbon felt as the electrode active material
- Electric double-layer capacitor (EDLC) operating principle
- Voltage limits imposed by aqueous electrolytes
- Effective series resistance (ESR) and coulomb efficiency trade-offs
- Proposed improvements using graphite, plaster of paris, and steel wool composites

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Egg whisk** — stainless steel structure used as the electrode housing/current collector
- **Activated carbon felt** — primary electrode material providing high surface area
- **Steel wool (stainless steel)** — used as filler inside the whisk to pack the activated carbon
- **Sodium chloride (NaCl) / saltwater** — aqueous electrolyte; saturated solution (~1 L used)
- **Kitchen towel** — used as a separator between the two electrodes
- **Graphite** — mentioned as a proposed additive for improved composite electrodes
- **Plaster of paris** — mentioned as a binder/former for improved solid electrodes (referencing prior lead-acid battery video)

## Techniques and Methods
- Unwinding stainless steel wool from the whisk body to form a sock-like structure, stuffing with activated carbon felt, then rewinding
- Preparing a saturated NaCl electrolyte solution (stirring salt until dissolution ceases)
- Charging the cell at ≤ 1.2 V to stay below the electrolysis threshold for chloride/water (1.23 V) and avoid chlorine gas evolution
- Reducing charge voltage to 1.0 V to ensure only double-layer formation (no faradaic side reactions)
- Discharging through a small DC motor as a practical load test
- Proposed future method: mixing activated carbon + graphite + plaster of paris, packing into the whisk as a cast composite electrode
