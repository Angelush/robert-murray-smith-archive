## Overview
In this short video, Robert demonstrates destructive distillation of HDPE plastic bags, placing them in a homemade pyrolysis reactor (a modified paint can with a copper coil condenser) to convert the plastic entirely into a liquid fuel. He contextualises the experiment within his broader series on pyrolysis, distinguishing between dry distillation (collecting existing volatiles) and destructive distillation (breaking down a material until volatiles are produced). The resulting fuel resembles diesel and burns with a heavy, smoky flame.

## Key Topics
- Distinction between pyrolysis, dry distillation, and destructive distillation
- HDPE plastic (carrier bags, milk bottles, food packaging) as a feedstock for fuel production
- Conversion of plastic waste into a diesel-like liquid fuel
- Practical demonstration using a DIY paint-can reactor and copper coil condenser

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — the plastic used as feedstock; noted for leaving no residue after destructive distillation
- **Plastic carrier bags** — the specific HDPE source used in the experiment
- **Methanol** — referenced as the product of a prior experiment (dry distillation of oak wood, video 1710)
- **Copper microbore coil** — used as the condenser, wrapped around a sewage pipe
- Resulting fuel described as **diesel-like** (heavy, waxy when cooled, smoky flame)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pyrolysis** — heating material in the absence of oxygen (the underlying process)
- **Dry distillation** — pyrolysis used to collect pre-existing volatiles (prior videos)
- **Destructive distillation** — pyrolysis that chemically breaks down a material until distillable volatiles are produced; the focus of this video
- DIY reactor construction: paint can with plumbing fittings, sealed and heated
- Passive air-cooled condenser coil to condense vapours into liquid
- Thermal insulation with fire blanket and rock wool to maintain reactor temperature
