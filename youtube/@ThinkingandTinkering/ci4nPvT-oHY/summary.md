## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith completes and tests his electric trike built almost entirely from rolled-up newspaper tubes held together with glue and fiber. After assembling the final structural reinforcements, attaching wheels, motor, handlebars, and a saddle (opting against a recumbent seat), he takes the paper trike for its first run in a car park. The trike holds together under his weight plus the battery load, moves at a decent speed, and the homemade rear differential functions as intended.

## Key Topics
- Completion of a paper-tube trike frame (rolled newspaper construction)
- Structural reinforcements added to stress points (beams, stairs, spreaders)
- Sourcing axle and bearings from a commercial supplier (Fun Bikes)
- Chain drive layout for a long-wheelbase bike geometry
- Switching from a recumbent seat to a conventional saddle
- Electric motor installation and first test ride
- Performance of the homemade rear differential

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Rolled-up newspaper (primary structural material for the frame)
- Glue (bonding agent for paper tubes)
- Fiber (reinforcement alongside glue)
- Electric motor (rear-mounted drive)
- Batteries (powering the motor)
- Bike chain and cogs/cluster (drivetrain)
- Cranks and pedals (fitted as foot rests pending chain installation)
- Bearings and bearing holder (sourced from Fun Bikes)
- Rear differential axle (production axle from Fun Bikes)

## Techniques and Methods
- Space frame design using paper tubes (rolled newspaper as structural members)
- Reinforcing high-stress joints with diagonal beams ("stairs"), uprights, and spreaders
- Rear differential fabrication from standard bicycle parts (detailed in video #1175)
- Two-stage chain drive layout (common for long-wheelbase recumbent/trike geometry): pedal chainring → intermediate cluster → rear cog
- Bolt-on assembly of axle, wheels, forks, motor, handlebars, and saddle
- First-stage load testing: sitting on the frame before powered testing

## Robert's Own Replies
- The full build series is documented in his **"Making Things from Newspaper" playlist** — he directs multiple commenters there for construction details.
- He confirms the **production rear axle is likely fine structurally** unless someone is cornering at very high speed ("100 round a corner").
- He has **one more trike build planned** before moving on to motor/electric work.
- The frame was **left as bare paper deliberately** because a viewer asked him to, though he personally likes a painted finish.
- He considers a **trailer attachment** possible but leaves it as an exercise for the builder ("up to you on that one").
- Trikes used on public roads in the UK would need to be **speed-restricted to 3.7 mph** (referencing regulations for powered vehicles in that class).
- No plans to release **printed plans** for the build.
