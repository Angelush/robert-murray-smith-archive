## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a Fluidyne — a liquid piston Stirling engine — using only a few pieces of plastic tubing, angle fittings, and copper pipe. The engine is heated with a blowtorch (or optionally a Fresnel lens for solar power) and requires almost no machined parts. The key takeaway is that a functional heat engine can be built cheaply and simply, and can even be converted into a linear generator with a cork, a magnet, and a coil.

## Key Topics
- What a Fluidyne is (a liquid piston, free-piston type of Stirling engine)
- Minimal parts list and construction walkthrough
- Managing heat at junctions using a water-cooled tuna tin bath
- Demonstrating the engine running (liquid column oscillating)
- Converting the engine into a linear AC generator
- Alternative heat sources, including solar (Fresnel lens)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Clear plastic tubing — main loop body (clear chosen for visibility)
- Angle tube fittings (×4) and tee fitting (×1) — connecting the loop
- Copper pipe (×2, equal length) — hot and cold uprights
- Hose clips — securing joints
- Tank fitting — attaching the cooling reservoir
- Tuna fish tin — used as a water bath to cool the hot-side junction
- Water — working fluid (liquid piston) and coolant
- Cork with a magnet — floats in the tube to enable generator conversion
- Induction coil — wrapped around the tube to capture oscillating magnet output

## Techniques and Methods
- Tube-and-fitting assembly of a closed-loop Fluidyne circuit
- Water bath cooling to protect plastic fittings near the heat source
- Blowtorch heating of the copper hot-side pipe
- Solar heating via Fresnel lens (described as an alternative)
- Linear generator construction: floating magnet + external coil around oscillating column
