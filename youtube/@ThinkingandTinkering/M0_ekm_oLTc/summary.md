## Overview
Part 4 of Robert's budget wind turbine build series focuses on completing the structural body of the vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT). He transitions from a rough pallet-based test rig to a clean, collapsible frame built from aluminium honeycomb smartboard panels and threaded bars. The video closes with the generator body finished and ready for coil installation, with rooftop testing to follow.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the single-coil power test from the previous video
- Detailed look at the rotor's magnetic arrangement (72 ceramic magnets, alternating N-S polarity, potted in resin)
- Design philosophy: rim-drive (low torque, high speed) vs. axle-drive (high torque, low speed)
- Running cost tally and comparison against commercial 1 kW wind generators
- Building a new collapsible frame from donated aluminium honeycomb smartboard panels and aluminium angles
- Use of 12 mm threaded bars to tie the structure together
- Dual-orientation capability (horizontal or vertical mounting)
- Addressing viewer requests for formal build plans (none exist; the video series is the documentation)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ceramic magnets** — 4.5 cm × 2.5 cm × 1 cm, 3.5 kg pull, 72 total, arranged N-S-N-S around the rotor ring
- **Resin** — used to pot and fix the magnets in place
- **Aluminium honeycomb panels** (from donated smartboards/whiteboards) — used for top plate, base plate, and baffle plates
- **Aluminium angle sections** — recovered from a display stand, used on the frame diagonals to accept baffle plates
- **12 mm threaded bar** — 1 m lengths, 5 purchased for ~£15, used to bolt the structure together
- **Self-tapping screws** — used to fix baffle plates to the aluminium angles
- **Plasti-dip** — mentioned in comments as a suitable coating to protect the magnets

## Techniques and Methods
- Gluing magnets onto the rotor disk in alternating N-S polarity, then potting with resin
- Cutting aluminium honeycomb panels to 1 m × 1 m sections with a central axle socket and corner holes
- Assembling a collapsible frame by threading bolts through corner and centre holes
- Rim-drive generator design: placing the electromagnetic pickup at the wheel's rim to exploit low-torque, high-speed motion
- Using microwave oven transformer (MOT) coils as the planned stator windings
- Modular, break-down-and-reassemble construction for rooftop transport

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Magnet imbalance (repeated):** Potting the magnets in resin introduced a slight imbalance in the rotor — he acknowledges this needs correcting before serious testing.
- **Early performance data:** The wooden prototype produced 30 W with a single coil in a 12 mph wind — offered as a rough baseline for what to expect.
- **Magnet sourcing:** Magnets came from first4magnets; 20 were salvaged from exercise bikes. Without magnets and threaded bar the frame itself cost only £9.90.
- **Magnet orientation:** The ceramic magnets are magnetised through their large flat faces, not along their long axis like a bar magnet.
- **Baffle plates:** Primarily structural — they stiffen the box. Any wind-channelling effect is incidental.
- **Coil plans:** Intends to fit more coils but hasn't fixed the number or arrangement; describes the goal as "quite modest."
- **VAWT self-limiting behaviour:** Has heard that VAWTs are self-limiting in high winds and plans to test whether this holds true, as it would simplify safety considerations.
- **Braking:** Pondering a braking mechanism but interested to see if self-limiting behaviour makes it unnecessary.
- **Magnet protection:** Shrink wrap, plasti-dip, or even cling film are all adequate for protecting the magnets.
- **Frame material cost:** Aluminium honeycomb smartboard is ~£100 per 1.2 m × 2.4 m sheet if bought new — still cheaper than a commercial turbine.
- **Turbine dimensions:** The build is 1 m × 1 m × 80 cm; a commercial 1 kW turbine typically has a rotor diameter of ~1.8 m.
- **Blade geometry:** Angle of attack is 36° to the tangent; blades are 60° arc sections.
