## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to repurpose old smartphone batteries by pairing them with inexpensive USB charge/protection boards to make battery-powered household devices rechargeable. Building on his earlier videos (1272 and 1273) about testing and preparing used phone batteries, he shows that a single bare phone battery with an 83p charge board can replace disposable AA batteries in devices like fairy lights, radios, torches, and remote controls. The core takeaway is that this simple, cheap modification eliminates the need for disposable batteries across a wide range of everyday devices.

## Key Topics
- Background context from companion videos 1272 and 1273 (testing and preparing used phone batteries)
- Overview of use cases for repurposed phone batteries (fairy lights, radios, torches, remote controls, clocks)
- Introduction to cheap USB LiPo charge/protection boards (~83p each)
- Wiring a bare phone battery to a charge board (series vs. parallel configurations)
- Voltage compatibility: phone batteries (~3.85V nominal) vs. 2×AA devices (~3V)
- Current/capacity limits of basic charge boards (max ~1A)
- Connecting multiple batteries in parallel (amps add, voltage stays same) vs. series (voltage adds)
- Matching battery capacities when combining cells
- Making devices permanently rechargeable via micro-USB

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Used smartphone/phone batteries (e.g., flat Samsung batteries, 2.6 Ah)
- USB LiPo charge/protection board (micro-USB input, ~83p each, max ~1A output)
- AA batteries (as the replaced power source, 1.5V each)
- Sponge/foam tape (for mounting the board to batteries)
- Crocodile clip wires (red and black, for demonstration connections)
- Solder and solder wire

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing the original protection board from a phone battery to expose bare tabs (per video 1272)
- Tinning battery tabs with solder before attaching wires
- Soldering wires to charge board pads (battery+, battery−, out+, out−)
- Connecting batteries in parallel (same tabs joined) to increase amp-hours while keeping voltage constant
- Connecting batteries in series to increase voltage (noted as requiring a higher-rated board)
- Clipping output wires directly to battery contacts inside target devices (torch, radio, fairy lights)
- Using a micro-USB cable to charge from a wall socket or PC
