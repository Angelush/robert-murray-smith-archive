## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a Tesla turbine/pump from acrylic plastic sheet using basic tools. The video walks through the full fabrication process step by step, then concludes with a proof-of-concept demonstration showing the pump pulling enough vacuum to boil water at 40°C — part of an ongoing desalination project.

## Key Topics
- How a Tesla turbine works in both pump and motor configurations
- Step-by-step construction of the rotor (discs and spacers) from acrylic sheet
- Fabricating the turbine body and end plates
- Bearing mounting using resin casting
- Driving the pump with a Dremel tool via a pulley/belt system
- Proof-of-concept vacuum test for low-temperature water evaporation (desalination application)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Acrylic/perspex sheet** — primary construction material for discs, spacers, and body
- **Ethyl acetate** — solvent for welding acrylic (also sold as "Plast Weld" in hobby shops, or used as a type 10 cleaner)
- **Methyl ethyl ketone (MEK)** — alternative acrylic solvent
- **Acetone** — alternative acrylic solvent
- **Cyanoacrylate (CA) glue / acrylic glue** — used to bond components
- **Resin** — poured to create bearing recesses in end plates; also painted on exterior to seal air gaps
- **Aluminium tube (8 mm)** — used as the rotor axle
- **Copper pipe (22 mm)** — used as a mold for the resin bearing recess
- **Sheet rubber** — cut into a gasket for sealing the assembly
- **Leather** — improvised drive belt (proper belts were on order)
- **6 mm steel bolt and repair washers** — used to clamp discs for lathe-finishing

## Techniques and Methods
- Scoring and snapping acrylic sheet with a sharp knife (no power cutting)
- Center-punching with a nail and hammer to locate drill centers
- Hole-saw drilling of discs (100 mm cutter) and spacers (25 mm cutter) — done slowly to avoid melting
- Lathe-turning (or drill-as-lathe) to clean up disc edges after cutting
- Stepped drilling of exhaust/vent ports (starting at 4 mm, increasing in 2 mm increments)
- Stacking and aligning discs using a drill bit as an alignment pin through the vent holes
- Solvent welding / CA gluing of the rotor stack and body laminate under clamping pressure
- Resin casting with a copper pipe mold to create precision bearing seats
- Pulley/belt speed multiplication (~2:1 ratio) to drive the turbine from a Dremel (15,000–35,000 RPM input → ~70,000+ RPM rotor)
- Vacuum demonstration: drawing a partial vacuum to boil water at 40°C

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief social responses ("cheers mate", "lol"), but a few contain useful context:
- He confirmed the **squares cut from the sheet are used to make the turbine body**, not discarded.
- On **using CDs as disc material**: he noted it saves a little time but you lose that saving making a custom spindle and fitting bearings, so it's roughly a wash.
- On **bearing size**: he confirmed 3.8 mm is too small but **8.5 mm would work well** (in reply to a comment about bearing dimensions).
- He acknowledged he had to **pause the desalination project for a while** but intended to return to it.
- He shared his philosophy openly: *"if I am not going to use something I normally put it up on here so others can take it and run with it"* — he is not motivated by patents or commercialisation.
- He noted that **what viewers see is the finished version**; early failed attempts fill his trash can.
- He expressed interest in **making a Tesla motor version** of the device in a future video.
