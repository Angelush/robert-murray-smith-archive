## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a DIY oil burner heater based on the principles of the 18th-century Argand lamp, using only standard copper and brass plumbing fittings — no welding required. He traces the history of oil lamps from 10,000 BC through to the Argand design of 1780 to explain *why* the design works, then demonstrates that a chimney and central airflow dramatically improve combustion. The key takeaway is that the Argand-style burner is best suited to heavier oils (vegetable, used mineral), while the kerosene-stove style (covered in video 1677) suits lighter fuels.

## Key Topics
- Brief history of oil lamps from prehistoric times to 1850
- The Argand lamp principle: central air passage + chimney draft for improved combustion
- Construction of a homemade Argand-style burner from plumbing fittings
- Choosing burner style based on fuel type (heavy vs. light oils)
- Safety considerations for indoor use

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper compression tee fitting (22–15–15 mm / ¾–½ inch)** — the core burner head
- **22 mm copper pipe** — outer pipe of the burner
- **15 mm copper pipe** — inner pipe of the burner, creates the central air passage
- **Reducing fitting** — used as the oil feed inlet
- **Stainless steel table leg** — repurposed as the chimney
- **5-inch pan** (drilled for airflow) — used as the base/stand
- **Tank connector** — connects the fuel supply line
- **Bean can with gate valve** — fuel reservoir (from video 1677)
- **Cotton wool ball** — used as a wick (temporary)
- **Kevlar wick material** — mentioned as a reusable/everlasting alternative
- **Vegetable oil** — suitable fuel for this burner style
- **Used mineral oil / used car oil** — suitable fuel for this burner style
- **Paraffin / kerosene** — mentioned as better suited to the alternative (1677-style) burner

## Techniques and Methods
- Filing out the internal stop in a compression tee fitting to allow pipes to slide flush
- Assembling concentric pipe-in-pipe geometry to create a central air passage
- Wrapping wick material around the inner pipe assembly
- Gravity-fed fuel supply via gate valve and tank connector
- Fitting a chimney tube to induce forced draft and improve combustion

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Safety for indoor use:** Robert emphasises that anyone using this as an indoor room heater should place it on a 12 mm tile (for spillage/fire protection), keep it 400 mm from combustibles, vent via a chimney to the outside, and ensure adequate ventilation. He also explicitly tells one commenter to **get a carbon monoxide alarm**.
- **Fuel choice:** He notes that availability varies hugely by region — palm oil may be more accessible in some parts of the world than sunflower oil, and used oils vary widely. His general advice is to try it and see.
- **Wicks are not outdated:** In response to a commenter dismissing wicks, Robert defends them as centuries-proven technology and suggests checking how commercial heaters are made.
- **No catalyst needed:** He states a filter and preheat are sufficient; a catalyst is not necessary.
- **Venturi clarification:** He corrects a commenter — the reason for filing out the stop is simply to allow the pipes to slide level, not a Venturi effect.
- **Glass chimney:** He mentions he wanted a glass tube for the chimney and may order one; notes the original design was a lamp, so a glass chimney would make it a functional lantern.
- **Replication philosophy:** "I replicated a 1800s burner for you — where you take it is up to you."
- **Timeliness:** He encourages acting sooner rather than later, as heating supplies become scarce and expensive once cold weather arrives.
