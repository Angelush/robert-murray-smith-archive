## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains the concept of sheet resistance, a property used to characterize thin conductive films such as painted conductive inks. He derives the sheet resistance formula from bulk resistivity, showing why ohms per square is the appropriate unit for thin-film measurements. The key takeaway is that sheet resistance is geometry-independent for square regions, making it the practical standard quoted by conductive ink manufacturers.

## Key Topics
- Bulk electrical resistance and its dependence on resistivity, length, and cross-sectional area
- Why bulk resistance measurements become impractical for thin films
- Derivation of sheet resistance (Rs) from the standard resistivity equation
- The unit "ohms per square" and why square size is irrelevant
- Four-probe measurement method for accurate sheet resistance readings
- A practical hand-built square contact probe for measuring sheet resistance
- Calculating resistance of a painted line using the ohms-per-square figure

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Conductive ink / painted conductive films — the primary application context for sheet resistance
- Copper — used as the contact material in the hand-built probe
- Graphite — mentioned as an example material with a known resistivity

## Techniques and Methods
- Ohmmeter / two-point probe measurement (bulk resistance, noted as insufficient for thin films)
- Four-probe method — the accurate technique for measuring sheet resistance of thin films
- Hand-built square contact probe — copper contacts separated by a plastic block at equal width and length, pressed onto a film to read ohms per square directly

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Porous substrates**: If the substrate absorbs the conductive film, determining the true film thickness becomes difficult; a non-porous substrate removes that variable and simplifies the measurement.
- **Thick vs. thin film definitions**: There is no hard industry standard — thin films range from monoatomic layers to a few microns; thick films (typically inks and paint coatings) can be considerably thicker. The boundary is somewhat grey and not formally specified.
