## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the first step in a new method for making graphene ink: producing bromine water from accessible household chemicals. The approach intercalates bromine between graphite layers to weaken the van der Waals forces holding them together, allowing mild sonication to separate the layers into graphene. This video focuses solely on synthesising the bromine water safely and affordably, with the graphite intercalation and sonication steps deferred to a follow-up video.

## Key Topics
- Why graphene is needed and why existing production methods are problematic
- Graphite intercalation as a strategy for graphene production
- Van der Waals forces and how intercalation weakens interlayer bonding
- Why bromine is an effective intercalant for graphite
- Hazards of elemental bromine and motivation for making it in-situ
- Step-by-step synthesis of bromine water from household chemicals
- Safety precautions when working with bromine and chlorine gas

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — starting material to be intercalated and exfoliated into graphene
- **Bromine water (aqueous bromine)** — intercalant produced in this video; soaks graphite for ~6 hours
- **Deionised water** — solvent; 163 mL used in the 200 mL preparation
- **Sodium bromide (NaBr)** — bromide source; 4.4 g used; also potassium bromide mentioned as an alternative
- **Hydrochloric acid / Spirit of Salts (~35%)** — acid component; 4 mL used
- **Household bleach (sodium hypochlorite, ~5% solution)** — oxidant that displaces bromide to free bromine
- **Sodium thiosulfate** — neutralising agent kept on hand in case of spills
- **Sodium bisulfate** — alternative neutralising agent for bromine spills
- **Chlorine gas** — hazardous by-product released during the displacement reaction

## Techniques and Methods
- **Graphite intercalation** — soaking graphite in bromine water for ~6 hours to force layers apart
- **Sonication (mild)** — used after intercalation to complete exfoliation into graphene sheets
- **In-situ bromine generation via displacement reaction** — mixing NaBr, HCl, and sodium hypochlorite to produce bromine in aqueous solution without handling elemental bromine
- **Outdoor working / downwind positioning** — safety practice to avoid chlorine gas inhalation
- **Glove use and neutralising solution standby** — personal protective measures during synthesis
