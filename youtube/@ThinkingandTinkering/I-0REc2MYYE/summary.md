## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a zinc bromine flow battery from inexpensive and improvised components, including a candle holder, a carved fire brick, and homemade graphite electrodes. The video explains the electrochemistry behind the battery, how to calculate its energy capacity from electrolyte concentration, and covers key safety considerations around bromine and hydrogen gas.

## Key Topics
- Construction walkthrough of a DIY zinc bromine battery
- Role of the porous fire brick pot as a separator/container for the zinc anode
- Electrochemistry of zinc bromide: energy stored in the electrolyte, not the electrodes
- Calculating battery capacity using moles, Avogadro's number, and coulombs
- Charging regime: current density (20–50 mA/cm²) rather than fixed voltage
- Energy density vs. power density trade-offs; surface area of electrodes
- Safety: bromine (halogen) hazards, hydrogen off-gassing, ventilation requirements
- Option to gel the electrolyte with fumed silica to reduce spill risk

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zinc bromide (ZnBr₂)** — 2.5 mol/L electrolyte, the primary energy storage medium
- **Sodium bromide (NaBr)** — 0.5 mol, added to maintain conductivity as the cell charges
- **Zinc** — negative electrode material (scrap zinc foil, powder, or granules)
- **Bromine (Br₂)** — produced at the positive electrode during charging; settles to the bottom
- **Aluminium foil** — core of the positive electrode
- **Conductive high-density polyethylene (HDPE)** — encases the aluminium foil in the electrode
- **Graphite foil** — bonded (heat-glued) onto the conductive HDPE as the active positive surface
- **Non-conductive HDPE** — outer wrap of the positive electrode
- **Fire brick (alumino-silicate: Al₂O₃ + SiO₂)** — carved into a porous cup to hold the zinc; chemically inert in the electrolyte
- **Plasti-Dip** — rubber coating applied to the lid to prevent electrolyte absorption
- **Fumed silica (SiO₂)** — optional gelling agent to immobilise the liquid electrolyte

## Techniques and Methods
- Carving a fire brick into a cylindrical porous pot using a hole-cutting saw
- Heat-bonding graphite foil onto conductive HDPE to form the positive electrode
- Dip-coating the plastic lid in Plasti-Dip to seal it against electrolyte wicking
- Compacting zinc scrap/powder into the fire brick pot to ensure electrical continuity
- Charging by constant current density (20–50 mA/cm² of electrode surface area) with voltage adjusted to maintain the target current
- Monitoring charge completion by watching for voltage rise and onset of water-splitting (hydrogen gas bubbles)
- Optional gelation of electrolyte with fumed silica to prevent spills
