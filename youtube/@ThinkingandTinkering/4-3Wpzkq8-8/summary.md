## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a functional olive oil burner/lamp using recycled tin cans and carbon felt. He addresses the key challenge of olive oil's high viscosity preventing effective wicking, solving it by using carbon felt that transmits heat into the oil to reduce its viscosity and allow it to draw up to the flame. The device can serve as either a room heater or a decorative lamp, and burns the oil all the way down from the wick position.

## Key Topics
- Olive oil as a historical and practical substitute for lamp oil
- The viscosity problem: why standard wicks fail with olive oil
- Using carbon felt's heat-transfer properties to overcome viscosity
- Construction of a dual-can burner assembly from recycled food tins
- Using the burner as a room heater vs. a lamp
- Priming the burner with methylated spirits/ethanol to get it started

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Olive oil** — primary fuel; noted as cheaper than lamp oil, clean-burning, soot-less, and pleasant-smelling
- **Carbon felt (100% carbon)** — wicking and heat-transfer material lining the inner can
- **Methylated spirits / ethanol** — used as a primer to initially light and heat the burner; described as "ethanol with pink colour and something to make you vomit"
- **Vegetable oil** — briefly mentioned as a possible alternative fuel

## Techniques and Methods
- Drilling 9 mm holes around the top of a pea tin for the main burner body
- Drilling 6 mm holes around a cut ring from a bean tin to act as a carbon felt retaining clamp
- Lining the inner can with carbon felt and pressing it against the sides with the drilled ring
- Positioning the wick/felt 1–2 mm below the top surface
- Priming with ~2 ml of methylated spirits to initiate combustion and heat the oil
- Snuffing the flame with a lid (candle-snuffing method)
- Placing a toilet roll holder over the burner to create a radiant room heater
- Placing the burner inside a metal lantern for a decorative lamp effect
