## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith investigates the long-standing debate over whether wet cold (as experienced in England) feels worse than dry cold (as in the American Midwest), prompted by his wife's experience moving from Ohio to England. He reviews military experiments dating back to the 1950s and concludes that objectively, wet and dry cold make no meaningful difference to core body temperature — but acknowledges that perceived warmth is real and valid, and suggests calcium chloride dehumidification as a cheap way to improve comfort.

## Key Topics
- The subjective experience of wet cold vs. dry cold
- Personal anecdote: Robert's wife (from Ohio) finding English wet cold more uncomfortable
- Cheap home heating tip: using calcium chloride as a dehumidifier
- 1950s Canadian military experiments on cold exposure
- Follow-up experiments by French, British, and American forces
- More recent (~2020) Canadian military repetition of the same tests
- The role of wet clothing losing insulation vs. sunny dry-cold days providing infrared warmth
- Conclusion: cold is cold, but feelings matter

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Calcium chloride** — suggested as a cheap, passive dehumidifier; placing pots of it around a room to reduce humidity and make the air feel (and be cheaper to heat) warmer

## Techniques and Methods
- **Passive dehumidification** — using calcium chloride in open containers to absorb moisture from indoor air
- **Military cold-exposure trials** — subjects (clothed and unclothed) run through a parade yard while sprayed with icy water; core temperature measured orally and rectally
- **Comparative cohort testing** — splitting groups into wet-exposed vs. dry-exposed conditions to isolate the humidity variable
