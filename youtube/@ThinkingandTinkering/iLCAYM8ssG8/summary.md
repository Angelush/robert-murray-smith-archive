## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a "liquid diode" — a simple rectifier built from kitchen ingredients — after first explaining how diodes work using the analogy of a heart valve and covering the basics of AC rectification. He shows that a saturated sodium bicarbonate solution with one aluminum electrode and one lead electrode acts as a half-wave rectifier, capable of spinning a DC motor from an AC supply.

## Key Topics
- How diodes work: the heart valve analogy and one-way current flow
- AC waveforms and the concept of half-wave vs. full-wave rectification
- Brief history of diodes: mechanical, vibrating reed, selenium/copper oxide solid-state, and liquid variants
- The liquid diode as a practical rectifier built from household materials
- Live demonstration: rectifying 6V AC to spin a DC brushed motor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda)** — used as a saturated solution to form the electrolyte
- **Aluminum strip** — one electrode of the liquid diode
- **Lead strip** — the other electrode, completing the rectifying pair
- **6V AC supply** — power source for the demonstration
- **DC brushed motor** — load used to visually confirm rectification

## Techniques and Methods
- Half-wave rectification using a single liquid diode in series with a load
- Constructing an electrolytic-style liquid diode: saturated electrolyte + dissimilar metal electrodes
- Visual confirmation of rectification via motor spin direction (one-way rotation under DC, wobble under AC)
