## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a vintage gramophone clockwork mechanism (purchased for £20) into a working electrical generator. By mounting the mechanism to a board, attaching a disc with alternating-polarity magnets, and positioning a coil from a microwave oven transformer nearby, he successfully generates 9–12 volts — enough to light an LED. The video is framed as both a proof-of-concept and a template entry for a viewer competition.

## Key Topics
- Repurposing a vintage gramophone clockwork mechanism as a mechanical energy source
- Three-component generator framework: mechanical capture → conversion → storage/usage
- Adapting British imperial hardware to modern metric (M5) fittings
- Electromagnetic induction using permanent magnets and a salvaged coil
- Using rim speed (distance from center) to compensate for low RPM (78 RPM)
- Competition entry context and submission rules

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Gramophone clockwork mechanism (circa 1900s, British imperial sizing) — the mechanical energy source
- Breadboard — used as the mounting plate
- M5 screws — modern metric replacements for the original imperial fixings
- Brass bar — fabricated into the on/off switch lever
- Disc from a Wimshurst machine — repurposed as the rotating flywheel/magnet carrier
- Permanent magnets — arranged north-south alternating around the disc rim
- Coil from a microwave oven transformer — used as the pickup coil for induction
- LED — used to demonstrate and test power output

## Techniques and Methods
- Re-tapping holes from British imperial to M5 metric thread
- Mounting and aligning the clockwork mechanism to a breadboard base
- Gluing the Wimshurst disc onto the drive spindle
- Arranging magnets in alternating north-south polarity around the disc rim
- Positioning a salvaged transformer coil to intercept the rotating magnetic field
- Using rim-speed geometry to increase effective linear velocity at low RPM
