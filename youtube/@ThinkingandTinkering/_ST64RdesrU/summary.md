## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith gives a practical introduction to brushless DC motors, explaining how they work as both motors and generators. He covers the two wiring configurations (Y and delta), demonstrates running salvaged motors using a cheap electronic speed controller, and shows how to build a simple three-phase rectifier to use the same motors as generators. The key takeaway is that any brushless DC motor can be used either as a motor or generator with minimal, inexpensive components.

## Key Topics
- How brushless DC motors work (rotating magnetic field acting on a permanent magnet)
- Y (star) vs delta wiring configurations and how to identify them by solder points
- Using an electronic speed controller (ESC) and servo tester to run a brushless motor
- Salvaging brushless motors from printers, CD-ROM drives, and PC fans
- Building a three-phase rectifier bridge to use brushless motors as generators
- Dual-use principle: the same device functions as motor or generator depending on use

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Permanent ring magnets (internal to brushless motors)
- 1N4007 diodes (used to build the three-phase rectifier bridge)
- Solder / solder points (for connecting coil wires)

## Techniques and Methods
- Identifying motor wiring configuration (Y vs delta) by counting solder points (4 = Y, 3 = delta)
- Stripping position-sensing electronics from salvaged motors to expose the three coil wires
- Wiring an electronic speed controller (ESC) with a servo tester to drive a brushless motor
- Assembling a six-diode three-phase bridge rectifier: pairing diodes silver-to-black, grouping all silvers (positive DC out) and all blacks (negative DC out)
- Spinning the motor by hand and measuring DC output voltage with a voltmeter
