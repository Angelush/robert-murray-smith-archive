## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits Kew Gardens to introduce the field of biophotovoltaics — the conversion of light energy into electricity using photosynthesis and microbial electrochemistry. He explains how microorganisms at plant roots act like microbial fuel cells, harvesting electrons released during cellular respiration, and notes that the CO₂ produced is reabsorbed by the plants, making the process carbon-neutral. The video concludes with a hopeful outlook on this early-stage technology as a living, self-repairing energy source.

## Key Topics
- Kew Gardens as a centre for plant and fungal science
- Introduction to biophotovoltaics as a discipline
- How conventional batteries work (anode oxidation, cathode reduction)
- Microbial fuel cells: two-chamber design, anaerobic anode, aerobic cathode
- Exoelectrogens and how they transfer electrons (protein carriers, cell membrane export, mediator chemicals)
- Photosynthesis as the energy input for plant-based microbial fuel cells
- Carbon neutrality: CO₂ produced by microbes is consumed by the plant
- Moss walls and small-scale demonstrators (coffee table, clock)
- Biomimicry and the underappreciated complexity of plants
