## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a Frenette (friction) heater — a device where an inner drum rotates inside an outer drum with a narrow oil-filled gap, generating heat through fluid shear. He walks through his construction process using available pipe, perspex/acrylic discs, skate bearings, and standard hardware, then tests the finished device. The build is acknowledged as non-optimal but serves as a working proof-of-concept, achieving a temperature rise from ~13°C to ~33°C in roughly six minutes at 2,800 RPM.

## Key Topics
- What a Frenette (friction/fluid shear) heater is and how it works
- Design trade-offs: long-and-thin vs. short-and-broad drum configurations
- Step-by-step construction using improvised parts
- Bearing selection and thrust management for the rotating inner drum
- Sealing the oil chamber (silicone sealant, rubber gasket)
- Live temperature test and efficiency assessment
- Plans to revisit the design using interlacing discs for better efficiency

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel pipe** — outer drum housing
- **Smaller pipe** — inner rotating drum
- **Perspex / acrylic sheet (4 mm)** — disc end-caps and centering discs, cut with a hole cutter and lathe-turned
- **ABS glue** — sealing the end-cap assembly
- **Crazy glue (cyanoacrylate)** — bonding disc layers during alignment
- **6 mm steel rod** — central axle for the inner drum
- **8 mm steel bar** — through-bolts for the base plate assembly
- **Aluminium rod** — spacer bushing to adapt 8 mm bearing hole to 6 mm axle
- **Magnetech 10W40 oil** — working fluid (100 ml used in the test)
- **Fire-resistant silicone sealant** — sealing the outer pipe to the base plate
- **Rubber sheet** — top seal/gasket

## Techniques and Methods
- **Hole-saw cutting** — cutting acrylic discs from sheet stock
- **Lathe turning** — truing discs to a dead center and finishing a bevelled thrust nut
- **Drilling with alignment registration** — clamping two plates together and drilling simultaneously to keep hole patterns in registration; marking a reference notch
- **Bearing press-fitting** — using a vise (or hammer and wood block) to seat 22 mm skate bearings into drilled housings
- **Drift/press insertion** — driving the aluminium spacer bushing into the bearing centre hole
- **Silicone sealing** — applying sealant under the outer pipe before bolting down
- **Thermocouple temperature logging** — monitoring oil temperature rise during spin-up test
- **Hand drill as prime mover** — driving the inner drum at ~2,800 RPM for the test run

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Oil emulsification** is the main practical issue over time; additives can reduce it. He has only passing awareness of cavitation effects.
- He plans to **remake the device using interlacing discs** for better efficiency, helped by a newly purchased laser cutter.
- He describes a **series of seemingly unrelated videos** that will eventually connect into a complete system build, so viewers who aren't following the full arc can still take individual value from each video.
- He is planning an **"everyman oilpiggy"-style guide** — an ultra-cheap version, then a disc version, then a cavitation-type heater — and will publish it as a make-it guide if successful.
- He acknowledges awareness of **cavitation heaters** as a related but distinct device category, and has a block of aluminium arriving to build one.
- On efficiency: *"It's kind of an induction heater mate — the efficiency is rubbish lol"* — a self-deprecating note on the prototype's performance.
