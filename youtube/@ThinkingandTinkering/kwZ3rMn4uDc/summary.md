## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates his graphene-based conductive ink, recently repackaged into recyclable metal tins, by applying it to polyethylene — one of the most notoriously difficult plastics to coat. He walks through the full process of cleaning, priming, and painting the plastic with the ink, then electroplating the coated surface with copper using a universal electroplating solution. The key takeaway is that this low-cost, highly conductive graphene/graphite ink can render otherwise uncoatable plastics suitable for electroplating and EMF shielding.

## Key Topics
- Repackaging of the graphene ink into recyclable metal tins for environmental reasons
- Conductivity properties of the graphene ink (~0.8 ohms/square/mil)
- Cost considerations — pure graphene would be ~£1,000/litre, so graphite is blended in to reduce price
- Surface preparation (cleaning and priming) of polyethylene before ink application
- Electroplating a graphene-coated plastic surface with copper
- Potential applications: EMF shielding, conductive plastic casings, electroplating non-conductive substrates

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene conductive ink** — a graphene/graphite blend sold by Robert's company; ~0.8 ohms/square/mil conductivity
- **Polyethylene sheet** — chosen as the most difficult plastic to coat, used as the test substrate
- **Isopropanol (IPA)** — used to clean the polyethylene surface before coating
- **Universal electroplating solution** — proprietary solution capable of depositing copper, nickel, and zinc
- **Copper** — the metal electroplated onto the coated plastic in this demonstration
- **Graphite** — blended with graphene in the ink to keep costs down

## Techniques and Methods
- **Surface cleaning** — wiping polyethylene with isopropanol to remove grease and dirt
- **Mechanical priming** — rubbing a small amount of the ink directly into the polyethylene surface by hand to achieve adhesion (hypothesised to work via electrostatic attraction of graphene to the plastic)
- **Brush painting** — applying the conductive ink over the primed surface
- **Electroplating** — immersing the coated plastic in universal plating solution at 5V / 20mA for ~30–60 minutes to deposit a copper layer

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Adhesion mechanism**: Robert believes the graphene sticks to the polyethylene surface via **electrostatic attraction**, citing two Japanese papers on electrostatic attraction and graphene as the basis — he mentions this in multiple replies.
- **Layer count**: When asked, he states the ink contains approximately **100,000 layers**.
- **Shop link**: He confirms the ink is available for purchase and provides a link to his shop (workingink.co.uk).
- **Bullet resistance**: In a notably serious aside (also hinted at in the video), he clarifies he genuinely meant the suggestion that the material could be tested for bullet-stopping capability — "I wasn't being funny" — and invites someone to try it and make a video.
- **Carboxylic acids**: He confirms there is quite a lot of relevant literature on the topic (graphene adhesion), particularly involving carboxylic acids, and points people toward Google Scholar.
