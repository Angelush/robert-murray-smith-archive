## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to repurpose a scrap catalytic converter (donated by Rick Clement's scrapyard) into a flameless catalytic heater. He cuts a section from the ceramic honeycomb core, layers it with a wicking material, stainless steel mesh, and ethanol fuel, then ignites it to show that once the catalyst reaches operating temperature, it produces significant heat without any visible flame.

## Key Topics
- How catalytic converters work and why they can function as flameless heaters
- The precious metals (platinum, palladium, rhodium) embedded in the catalyst wash coat
- Flameless combustion: combining fuel, oxygen, and heat at the catalyst surface
- Comparison to commercial catalytic room heaters and ration-pack heaters
- Practical construction of a DIY catalytic burner from scrap parts
- Scaling up: the idea of stacking multiple catalyst blocks with an electric heating element for a proper room heater

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Catalytic converter (ceramic honeycomb core)** — the central catalytic element, sourced from a scrap yard for as little as £7
- **Platinum, Palladium, Rhodium** — precious metal catalysts embedded in the wash coat
- **Aluminium (in wash coat)** — used to create a rough surface to hold the catalyst salts
- **Ethanol (alcohol)** — fuel used to drive the catalytic combustion; noted as a cleaner burn than propane
- **Propane** — mentioned as an alternative fuel commonly used in commercial flameless heaters (no vaporisation needed)
- **Iron oxide / Magnesium and iron** — referenced as the reactive chemistry in ration-pack flameless heaters (for comparison)
- **Potassium dichromate** — mentioned as the hazardous chemical used in a previous homemade catalyst (video 1713), contrasted with the safer scrap-yard approach
- **Stainless steel mesh** — used to allow air (oxygen) to reach the catalyst base
- **Acrylic cup / felt wicking material** — used to distribute and vaporise the ethanol beneath the catalyst

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting open a catalytic converter with an angle grinder to extract the ceramic core
- Slicing a 2–2.5 cm thick section from the ceramic honeycomb block
- Layering a catalytic burner stack: wicking felt → stainless steel mesh → catalyst section
- Igniting ethanol to pre-heat the catalyst to operating temperature (~300–400 °C), then removing the flame and relying on flameless catalytic combustion
- Adding fuel incrementally to sustain the catalytic reaction
