## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates using a Gweike Cloud Pro laser cutter to reduce copper(I) oxide pottery glaze into conductive copper traces on plastic. By applying the oxide as a simple DIY ink and optimizing laser settings, he successfully prints a working electrical circuit that lights an LED. The video proves the concept is viable and points toward laser-printed PCBs, motor stators, and generators as future possibilities.

## Key Topics
- Laser reduction of copper(I) oxide to metallic copper
- Making a DIY conductive ink from pottery glaze, water, and matte medium
- Rod coating technique for applying uniform ink layers
- Optimizing Gweike Cloud Pro laser settings (speed, focal offset, power)
- Applications: printed circuit boards, motor stators/rotors, axial flux generators
- Comparison to traditional PCB etching with ferric chloride

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper(I) oxide (red copper oxide)** — pottery glaze used as the copper source; reduces to metallic copper under laser heat
- **Matte medium** — acrylic binder (~20% by weight) used to suspend the oxide powder into a paintable ink
- **Water** — diluent for the ink
- **Plastic sheet (from salvaged LCD TV)** — substrate for the coating
- **Ferric chloride** — mentioned as the conventional (wasteful) PCB etchant this method could replace

## Techniques and Methods
- **Rod coating (Mayer rod coating)** — spreading ink to a uniform thickness using a dowel and tape spacers
- **Laser sintering/reduction** — firing a CO₂ laser at copper oxide to thermally reduce it to metallic copper
- **Iterative parameter optimisation** — systematically varying laser speed, focal offset, and power to find the correct operating window
- **Repeat-coating passes** — applying and lasering multiple layers (×5) to build up a continuous conductive film
- **Continuity/conductivity testing** — using a multimeter and an LED circuit to verify the printed trace conducts electricity
