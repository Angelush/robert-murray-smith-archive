## Overview
In this short opinion piece, Robert Murray-Smith responds to a comment suggesting he has a duty of care as an influencer to model strict health and safety practices for young viewers. He argues that over-protection and instilling fear in children is a primary cause of the "snowflake generation," and that allowing kids to take risks, make mistakes, and earn "war wounds" is essential to developing confidence, pride, and resilience.

## Key Topics
- The responsibility (or lack thereof) of science communicators to enforce health and safety norms
- The social cost of over-protecting children and fostering fear
- The value of risk-taking and learning by doing
- His personal approach to supervising his son Luke with dangerous tools — giving space while quietly watching
- The difference between appropriate guidance and excessive restriction

## Robert's Own Replies
The author replies are largely brief acknowledgements ("cheers mate," "lol," "for sure mate"), but a few add meaningful clarification:

- **On balance:** "like everything mate - it has its place and only becomes a nuisance when it is over done" — he's not anti-safety, just anti-excess.
- **On human potential:** "People have no limitations mate - just the ones they put on themselves or the ones folks foster onto them."
- **On fear:** "fear brings out the worst in us not the best" and "it's not about scaring a lesson - it's about freedom to breathe and live."
- **On health and safety culture:** "Health and safety has nothing to do with health and safety or my neighbours - it's about control."
- **Clarifying his position:** "I am not saying we shouldn't protect mate - I am saying we shouldn't over protect."
