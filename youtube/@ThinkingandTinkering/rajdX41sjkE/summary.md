## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces rolling contact joints using the "Magic Wallet" as a fun starting point — a toy invented by French waiters that grips cards via elastic bands. He 3D-prints his own version in TinkerCAD, then extends the concept to curved-surface rolling joints using a design from Brigham Young University (BYU), demonstrating that these joints have very low friction, require no lubrication, and show strong potential for robotics and mechanical design.

## Key Topics
- The Magic Wallet as a practical example of a rolling contact joint
- How rolling joints work mechanically (bands/ligaments hold surfaces together as they roll)
- Biological analogues: knee and hip joints as natural rolling joints
- 3D-printed Magic Wallet design (TinkerCAD, using 360° print-in-place hinges)
- BYU's compliant mechanism / rolling contact joint design (curved surface variant)
- Applications of rolling contact joints in robotics and dirty/unlubricated environments
- Jacob's Ladder toy as a related mechanism (mentioned in comments)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Super glue — used to assemble the 3D-printed rolling joint components

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM implied) — printing the Magic Wallet and rolling joint parts
- Print-in-place hinges — 360° hinges designed to print as a single assembly
- Z-fold assembly — folding printed plates into the correct configuration before gluing
- Origami-style folding — squeezing flat-printed semicircles into curved/rounded surfaces
- Superglue bonding — securing tabs into slots after shaping
