## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a diaphragm (rolling/top-hat) piston using 3D-printed parts and a finger cut from a latex rubber glove, bypassing the tedious sanding normally required to achieve a good piston fit. The key insight is that the rolled rubber sleeve unrolls like taking off a sock, creating a near-frictionless seal with very low hysteresis. The result is a working piston that responds to even low pressures and can be incorporated into pumps, compressors, or engines.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of 3D-printed piston tolerances and the problem of tedious post-print sanding
- The diaphragm/rolling/top-hat piston concept and how the U-shaped rolling action works
- Designing the piston assembly in TinkerCAD (bullet-shaped piston, cylinder, top cap, sealing clips)
- Wet vs. dry lubrication for natural latex
- Step-by-step assembly using a latex glove finger
- Potential applications: standalone demonstration, pneumatic machines, pumps, compressors, engines

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Natural latex powder-free gloves** — used as the rolling diaphragm sleeve; the finger section is cut off and applied over the piston
- **Graphite powder** — used as a dry lubricant worked into the glove fingers; noted as commonly available as a locksmith's lock lubricant
- **Silicone sealant** — mentioned as an optional addition to improve the seal at the clipping joint
- **3D-printed PLA (implied)** — parts designed in TinkerCAD: piston (bullet shape), cylinder, top cap, sealing clips

## Techniques and Methods
- **TinkerCAD CAD design** — all components modelled before printing
- **FDM 3D printing** — printing the four-part assembly
- **Dry lubrication with graphite** — coating the latex finger inside and out before assembly to prevent self-adhesion and swelling
- **Latex finger preparation** — peeling glove fingers off, turning inside out, blowing to check for holes, then cutting to length
- **Rolling diaphragm assembly** — sliding the piston into the graphite-coated finger, pulling the latex over the cylinder lip to form the characteristic U-shaped roll
- **Mechanical clipping / optional silicone sealing** — securing the top cap and sealing clips to complete the assembly

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On applications:** He confirms it would make a compressor easily, but is uncertain about a vacuum pump; humorously suggests he could even make a mass spectrometer with it. He also confirms liquid pistons (water) have been used in Stirling engines, and notes this design would suit a Stirling engine well.
- **On seal integrity:** He advises gluing the top cap if desired, but notes the unlubricated side of the latex grips the piston quite well on its own — though he acknowledges more testing would be worthwhile.
- **On glove finger selection:** He clarifies that the thumb and little finger are noticeably smaller than the other fingers and are therefore less practical for this application.
- **On graphite quantity:** He suggests the amount of graphite released is probably less than what comes off when sharpening a pencil — implying it is not a significant concern.
- **On durability/repair:** He suggests trying a thicker or stronger latex material if the finger tears, noting it is an easy repair regardless.
- **On a related design:** He mentions he thought he had previously made something on a related topic, couldn't find it, but still has the model drawings and is open to sharing them.
