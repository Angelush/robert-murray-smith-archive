## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a touch-activated driftwood lamp using found objects collected from a local beach, inspired by the rocky island illustrations of Finnish artist Tove Jansson. The lamp uses a simple touch-sensor electronics kit combined with conductive ink and copper strip to allow the lamp to be switched on by touching anywhere on the driftwood base. The project emphasises upcycling and encourages viewers to repurpose scrap or found materials into functional objects.

## Key Topics
- Upcycling and found-object art/craft
- Building a touch-activated lamp from beach driftwood
- Inspiration from Tove Jansson's illustrations of the Bay of Finland (the finished piece ended up resembling Halong Bay, Vietnam)
- Assembling the lamp electronics: touch sensor, lamp holder, mains wiring
- Using conductive ink and copper strip to create a large touch surface
- Advice on supergluing lightweight/spongy driftwood

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Driftwood (collected from the beach)
- Edison screw filament bulb (vintage/steampunk style, warm reddish-orange tint)
- Copper strip (used to form the touch contact area)
- Conductive ink (painted around the base to extend the touch-sensitive surface)
- Superglue (high-viscosity, used to bond spongy driftwood pieces to a baseboard)
- Mains electrical cable (UK colour coding: brown = live, blue = neutral, green/yellow = earth)
- Varnish (mentioned in comments as an overcoat to protect the conductive ink)

## Techniques and Methods
- Collecting and selecting driftwood for aesthetic shape (rocks/islands silhouette)
- Cutting driftwood with a wire-cutter jig (a reused breadboard tool)
- Arranging and dry-fitting pieces before fixing
- Bonding lightweight porous wood with high-viscosity superglue
- Wiring a touch-sensor module to a mains lamp holder (live, switched-live, neutral, earth)
- Applying copper strip and painting conductive ink around the lamp base to create a large capacitive/resistive touch area
- Screwing in the bulb and testing the touch function

## Robert's Own Replies
- Confirmed that conductive ink **can be over-varnished** and still functions, though he notes he did over-varnish the ink in both lamp videos; he plans to make a dedicated video on over-painting/protecting the ink.
- Acknowledged the ink is **not waterproof on its own** without a varnish overcoat.
- Noted the early videos were lower quality but that practice and a better camera made a big difference.
- Described the project as "just a bit of fun and a space filler" while the main product/kit work continued.
- Expressed intent to take and post a photo when asked, and to demonstrate more use-case examples so viewers can see the range of possibilities with the lamp kit.
