## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith uses leftover bamboo poles from a previous project to build a bicycle frame, demonstrating that bamboo bikes are far simpler and cheaper to make than their commercial price tags suggest. He walks through cutting the bamboo poles to length using an existing bike as a template, fitting salvaged metal parts (head tube, axle bearings), and joining the frame using a peg-and-stitch lashing technique. The video ends with the bare frame assembled, with a follow-up planned to fit the remaining bike components.

## Key Topics
- Why bamboo bikes are expensive commercially despite being straightforward to build
- Using an existing bike as a measuring template instead of buying a kit/jig
- Cutting bamboo poles to length and shaping saddle-cut ends with a hole saw
- Bamboo's splitting tendency along the grain and how lashing prevents it
- Salvaging metal parts (head tube, axle/crank section, wheel dropouts, bearings) from scrap bikes
- Lashing/wrapping joint techniques for connecting bamboo poles
- Applying resin to joints after assembly to fix and firm everything in place

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bamboo poles** — primary structural material for the frame; leftover from a previous quad project
- **Jute twine** — chosen lashing material for its natural aesthetic
- **Polypropylene, carbon fiber, glass fiber, string, twine, duct tape, chicken wire** — mentioned as alternative lashing/wrapping materials
- **Resin** — applied after assembly to lock joints in place and create a firm bond
- **Salvaged steel parts** — head tube section, bottom bracket/crank shell, wheel dropouts, and bearings sourced from scrap bikes

## Techniques and Methods
- **Template measurement** — using a donor bike to derive all tube lengths and angles rather than purchasing a jig kit
- **Hole-saw saddle cutting** — using a hole-cutting saw matching the pole diameter to cut concave (saddle) ends so tubes nest cleanly against each other
- **Peg-and-stitch lashing** — drilling holes through the bamboo at joints, threading string with a bent-wire "upholstery needle," stitching through the holes, then wrapping the remainder tightly around the joint to cover the stitching
- **Circumferential wrapping** — winding lashing around the bamboo to resist longitudinal splitting
- **Resin impregnation** — saturating the finished lashed joints with resin to consolidate and rigidify the frame
