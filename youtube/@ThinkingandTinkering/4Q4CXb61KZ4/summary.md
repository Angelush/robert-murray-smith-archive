## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith takes his homemade Archimedes screw generator down to the sea to test it as a wave power device. Before the field test, he delivers a detailed conceptual argument that the screw mechanism itself — not the electrical generator attached to it — is the primary determinant of how much energy a system can produce, with the generator being merely an optimization problem. The sea trial shows promising results despite a mismatched angle and blade diameter, demonstrating that Archimedes screw devices have real potential for wave energy harvesting.

## Key Topics
- The Archimedes screw as an energy harvesting device for wave power
- Conceptual framework: the mechanical head (screw) determines maximum power output; the electrical generator determines efficiency
- Torque vs. rotational speed trade-offs in power generation
- Analogy between the screw generator and a rock rolling down a hill
- Historical comparison: British vs. Japanese motorcycle engine design philosophy (slow/high-torque vs. fast/low-torque)
- Faraday's and Ampere's laws as they apply to generator design
- Using a capacitor bank to decouple generator load from application load
- Design limitations observed during the sea trial (angle, blade diameter, length)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- NEMA motor (used as the electrical generator attached to the screw shaft)
- Capacitor bank (used to decouple generator load from output load)
- Diode bridge (used at the output between generator and capacitor bank)

## Techniques and Methods
- Direct field testing of the Archimedes screw generator in the sea (wave exposure)
- Capacitor bank buffering: storing energy from a slow/high-torque source and releasing it as usable electrical power
- Diode bridge rectification at the generator output
- Comparative mechanical analysis: treating the screw as a gravitational potential energy collector (analogous to a rock on a hill)
- Gear ratio reasoning applied to generator matching
