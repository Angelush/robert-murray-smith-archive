## Overview
Robert introduces vulcanized fiber — a material invented in 1859 by Thomas Taylor — made entirely from paper and zinc chloride. The process partially dissolves cellulose fibers to create a self-reinforcing, self-binding composite that is renewable, recyclable, and produces no waste. Robert presents it as a largely forgotten but highly practical bioplastic alternative well-suited to home lab experimentation.

## Key Topics

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Paper** — the raw substrate; source of cellulose fibers
- **Zinc chloride (ZnCl₂)** — used at 70% solution to partially dissolve cellulose; leached out progressively down to 0.05%; can be reused
- **Cellulose** — the structural polymer in paper; forms both fiber reinforcement and nano-fibrous "glue" after treatment
- **Nano-fibrous cellulose** — the dissolved fraction that acts as a binder within the material

## Techniques and Methods
- **Zinc chloride bath treatment** — immersing paper in a 70% ZnCl₂ solution to initiate surface dissolution of cellulose
- **Roll-to-roll pressing** — passing treated paper through a press immediately after the bath to consolidate layers
- **Progressive leaching** — sequentially reducing ZnCl₂ concentration (70% → 0.05%) to wash out all residual zinc chloride, leaving 100% cellulose
- **Lamination/layering** — building up multiple paper sheets to achieve desired thickness and mechanical properties
