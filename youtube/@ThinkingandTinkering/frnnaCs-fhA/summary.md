## Overview
Robert visits the Dova Transport Museum workshop as inspiration for thinking about flexible power transmission. He presents a 3D-printed hemispherical gear assembly — designed in Tinkercad and based on a patent he found — that can transmit rotational power across a variable angle, from 180° (in-line) down to nearly 0°. The key takeaway is that hemispherical gears maintain a consistent 1:1 gear ratio regardless of the angle between the shafts, making them particularly relevant for robotic joints.

## Key Topics
- Power transmission methods: belts, pulleys, chains, ropes, and gears as context
- Concept of flexible/angular power transmission using hemispherical gears
- 3D design in Tinkercad; model shared on Thingiverse
- Assembly walkthrough of the hemispherical gear mechanism (axle, clip, sliding housing)
- Gear tooth geometry: tapering teeth toward the top of the hemisphere vs. uniform-thickness teeth (from a second patent)
- Gear ratio properties: remains 1:1 regardless of shaft angle
- Robotics application: elbow, knee, hip, and shoulder joint analogues

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Gear ratio is always 1:1:** Robert clarifies that the gear ratio remains one-to-one regardless of the angle at which the hemispheres are positioned. His explanation: think of the hemisphere as a stack of gears all the same size — as you move them around, the size of the individual engaged section changes, but the relationship stays 1:1.
- **Magnets as an alternative retention idea:** In response to a suggestion, he notes you could just stick magnets in the end.
- **Open to follow-up:** He tells one commenter to do an animated mock-up and share it with him, suggesting he may be missing something in their argument.
