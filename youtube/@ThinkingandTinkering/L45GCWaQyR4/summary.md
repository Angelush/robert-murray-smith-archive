## Overview
Luke builds a flat panel heater by painting conductive ink onto a clear acrylic sheet using a ladder pattern, with copper tape electrodes and safety components including a thermocouple cutoff. Robert Murray-Smith appears in the video to explain the science behind using conductive ink as a heating element, discussing why the ink's high conductivity creates challenges at mains voltage and how manganese dioxide can stabilize heating behavior via its negative thermal coefficient.

## Key Topics
- Using conductive ink as a resistive heating element
- Ladder formation layout for even heat distribution
- Circuit design with rocker switch, thermocouple cutoff (40°C), and fused plug
- Ohm's law applied to a resistive AC element (no inductive component)
- The problem of conductivity increasing with temperature in conductive inks
- Manganese dioxide as a thermal stabilizing additive (negative thermal coefficient)
- Practical deployment options: low-voltage panels, battery-powered applications, large mains-connected panels, or transformer-stepped-down panels

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — the resistive heating element, painted in two coats onto the substrate
- **Clear acrylic sheet** — substrate for the ink, rated to ~220°C, well above the 40°C operating limit
- **Copper tape** (~1 inch wide) — electrodes applied along the two long edges
- **Masking tape** — used to define the ladder pattern during painting
- **IPA (isopropyl alcohol) wipes** — used to clean the acrylic surface before application
- **Manganese dioxide** — discussed by Robert as an additive to create a self-regulating heating ink; has a negative thermal coefficient of resistance (becomes more resistive as temperature rises, balancing the ink's tendency to become more conductive when heated)
- **Builder's board / Sintra board** — used to construct a freestanding base frame

## Techniques and Methods
- Surface preparation with IPA wipes to ensure clean adhesion
- Copper tape application with burnishing (using cardboard edge or fingernail) to eliminate wrinkles
- Masking tape ladder pattern: alternating masked/unmasked strips to create parallel ink rungs between the two copper bus bars
- Two-coat conductive ink application for target resistance (~77 Ω)
- Resistance measurement post-cure to verify amp draw via Ohm's law (230 V ÷ 77 Ω ≈ 2.9 A)
- Incremental voltage testing using a variac (20 V → 70 V) to characterize heating performance before connecting to mains
