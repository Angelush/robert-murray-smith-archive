## Overview
An introductory lecture from Robert Murray-Smith's practical graphene course, aimed at hobbyists and kitchen-table experimenters rather than lab scientists. Robert explains what graphene is, situates it among the carbon allotropes, and surveys the two main production philosophies (top-down and bottom-up), concluding that choice of method should always be driven by the intended end application — and that accepting material limitations is more productive than chasing perfection.

## Key Topics
- What graphene is: a single-layer 2D sheet of hexagonally arranged carbon atoms
- Carbon allotropes and the dimensional schema: graphite (3D), carbon nanotubes (1D), fullerenes (0D), graphene (2D)
- The original Scotch-tape discovery method (Andre Geim's work)
- Top-down vs. bottom-up production approaches
- Graphite oxide / graphene oxide route as the most accessible top-down method
- Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) on copper or nickel foil
- Bottom-up synthesis from polyaromatic hydrocarbon precursors (sugar, benzene, pitch, tar)
- How graphite powder particle size affects end-product properties (small powder → solution casting; large powder → gel/fiber spinning)
- Bridging grain boundaries with carbon nanotubes or metal nanowires
- The LightScribe laser method for making supercapacitors
- Applications: supercapacitors, batteries, transistors, solar cells, fuel cells, water filtration, transparent conductive screens, strong fibers
- Graphene modification: N-type doping (phosphorus) and P-type doping to create photovoltaic junctions
- 3D graphene foam/sponge structures and their surface-area advantages
- The principle of "know your materials" as a guiding philosophy throughout the course

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — bulk 3D precursor material; found in pencils
- **Graphene** — single-layer hexagonal carbon sheet; the target product
- **Graphite oxide / graphene oxide** — oxidised intermediate used in the top-down route; water-dispersible
- **Cellulose tape (Scotch/Sellotape)** — used in the original mechanical exfoliation discovery method
- **Copper foil** — catalyst substrate for CVD; absorbs carbon when hot, releases it on cooling
- **Nickel foil** — alternative catalyst substrate for CVD
- **Phosphorus** — used for N-type doping of graphene
- **Sugar (table sugar)** — carbon precursor for bottom-up synthesis
- **Benzene** — aromatic hydrocarbon precursor for bottom-up synthesis
- **Toluene** — mentioned as a hydrocarbon that can drive polyaromatic condensation
- **Pitch and tar** — carbon-rich precursors for bottom-up graphene synthesis
- **Sodium hydroxide** — used as a coagulation bath for wet-spinning graphene oxide gel into fibers
- **Carbon nanotubes** — used as conductive bridges across graphene grain boundaries
- **Copper metal nanowires** — used alongside graphene to bridge weak boundary points in transparent conductive films
- **Helium** — cited in the context of graphene's gas impermeability (graphene-coated balloons that don't deflate)
- **Water** — noted as the one substance graphene allows through, relevant to desalination

## Techniques and Methods
- **Mechanical exfoliation (Scotch-tape method)** — repeated peeling of graphite with adhesive tape; the original discovery technique
- **Top-down graphite oxide route** — oxidise graphite powder to graphite oxide, disperse in water, sonicate ~10–15 min to exfoliate into graphene oxide solution, then chemically reduce back to graphene
- **Solution casting / spin coating** — depositing graphene oxide solution onto a substrate and spinning to produce a thin film
- **Chemical vapor deposition (CVD)** — growing graphene on copper or nickel foil by thermal decomposition of a carbon source; dissolving away the metal foil to release the graphene sheet
- **Bottom-up synthesis from polyaromatic hydrocarbons** — condensing precursors (sugar, benzene, pitch, tar) by stripping non-carbon groups and fusing the resulting rings
- **Electrospinning / wet-spinning into sodium hydroxide** — spinning large-particle graphene oxide gel through a nozzle to produce graphene fibers and cables
- **LightScribe laser reduction** — coating a CD with graphene oxide then using a consumer LightScribe optical drive to locally reduce it to graphene, simultaneously creating a foam-like cup structure ideal for supercapacitors
- **Nanowire/nanotube bridging** — mixing graphene with carbon nanotubes or copper nanowires to create conductive paths across grain boundaries
- **N-type and P-type doping** — chemical modification of graphene (e.g. with phosphorus) to create semiconductor junctions for photovoltaic or electronic applications
