## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith disassembles a salvaged SMART Board — a touch-sensitive interactive whiteboard that was mass-deployed in UK schools during a government technology spending programme — to reveal the valuable materials hidden inside. He argues these discarded boards are a treasure trove of useful components for DIY electronics and construction projects, particularly for battery-making and structural applications.

## Key Topics
- Origin and history of SMART Boards in UK schools
- Why SMART Boards are now being discarded in large numbers
- Step-by-step disassembly of the board
- Identification and potential reuse of the internal materials
- Planned use of the salvaged materials in a wind turbine build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive plastic** — black carbon-filled plastic layer that forms the resistive touch sensor; noted as useful for battery pouches
- **Aluminium honeycomb** — rigid structural core of the board, described as very flat and strong; suggested as a construction material
- **White plastic layer** — top drawing/writing surface coating over the aluminium
- **Carbon-filled black plastic coating** — applied over the aluminium honeycomb; described as "amazingly conductive"
- **Contact/resistance strip** — the sensing strip that reads resistance across the conductive plastic layer

## Techniques and Methods
- Removing self-tapping screws to detach plastic trim
- Peeling back masking tape to expose wiring and connection points
- Using a carpet knife to slice along the connection point and separate the top plastic layer from the backing
- Delaminating the board by running the knife between the top plastic layer and the backing sheet
