## Overview
In this follow-up to video 1678, Robert compares his homemade oil heater against a commercially purchased greenhouse heater of similar size and capacity. He finds the commercial unit is a basic wick-driven device functionally identical to an Argand lamp, and concludes that his homemade version was cheaper, better made, and better performing. The deeper takeaway is a maker's philosophy: always drive for simplicity first, get it working, then iterate.

## Key Topics
- Unboxing and analysis of a commercial greenhouse oil heater
- Comparison of commercial vs. homemade (video 1678) oil heater design
- How the commercial burner works: wick-driven combustion with chimney draft
- Similarity to the Argand lamp design
- Engineering philosophy: simplicity reduces cost, failure points, and barriers to making
- Advice for makers: set a clear objective, build the simplest version first, then add features

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Oil (kerosene/paraffin implied)** — fuel for both heaters
- **Wick material** — drives fuel to the flame; Robert notes Kevlar and Amazon-sourced wicks as options in comments
- **Light-gauge sheet metal** — used in the commercial heater's reservoir (described as "essentially a biscuit tin") and chimney
- **Waste oil** — discussed in comments; too viscous to wick in the commercial unit but usable in the gravity-fed homemade version

## Techniques and Methods
- **Wick-driven combustion** — fuel drawn up by capillary action to the burner head
- **Chimney draft airflow** — chimney pulls air up past the wicks to sustain clean combustion
- **Dual-wick separation** — airflow channel between two wicks in the commercial burner head
- **Gravity-fed oil delivery** — the homemade 1678 design; does not rely on wicking, making it compatible with more viscous fuels
- **Iterative building** — start with the minimal working device, then add enhancements (e.g., a water-heating coil)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Waste oil compatibility:** The commercial heater cannot run on waste oil — it relies on wicking, and waste oil is too viscous. The homemade version from video 1678 works because it is gravity-fed, so viscosity is less of a problem.
- **Planned next steps:** Intends to add a copper coil for water heating; notes he has already done a sand battery project.
- **Safety reminder:** Like any open flame heater, it requires ventilation, fire protection, a chimney, and a carbon monoxide detector.
- **Clean burning:** Achieving clean combustion with oil requires forced air of some kind.
- **Thermal store progression:** For those wanting to add a thermal store, his recommended sequence is: (1) build and test the heater, (2) add a thermal store (rocks or sand), (3) add a heat exchanger (e.g., water coil).
- **Simplicity principle restated:** "The simplest thing that achieves your desired outcome — you can always improve it later. I think they call that optimisation."
- **Wick sourcing:** Wicks (including Kevlar) are readily available on Amazon.
