## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith documents the construction of a bamboo frame for an electric quad bike, reproducing the geometry of a previous metal quad built from welded children's bikes. He walks through his joinery method — socket-and-peg bamboo joints held with Gorilla Glue and reinforced with wrapped carbon fiber tow and resin — producing a remarkably lightweight frame.

## Key Topics
- Sourcing bamboo from UK Bamboo Supplies Limited (35–40 mm diameter)
- Planning and cutting bamboo sections for the back/chair section and rear axle subframe
- Socket-and-peg joint construction technique
- Using Gorilla Glue as a primary adhesive for assembly
- Reinforcing joints with carbon fiber tow wrapped through drilled holes and saturated with resin
- Comparison to and extension of a previous metal quad bike build (videos 1146, 1147, 1149)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Bamboo** (35–40 mm diameter) — primary structural material sourced from UK Bamboo Supplies Limited
- **Gorilla Glue** (polyurethane, waterproof) — adhesive for initial joint assembly; noted as same color as bamboo
- **Carbon fiber tow** — unidirectional fiber used to wrap and reinforce socket joints
- **Resin** — applied over carbon fiber wrapping to consolidate joints
- **Steel pipe** — previously welded in for the rear axle interface

## Techniques and Methods
- Dry-fitting sections before gluing to verify squareness and alignment
- Socket-and-peg joinery: drilling holes in bamboo so pieces slot into one another
- Sectional build approach: constructing the frame in discrete sections then assembling
- Carbon fiber wrapping: drilling a hole through the joint, threading tow through, wrapping around the joint, and painting with resin to create a structural carbon fiber collar
- Frame extended 100 mm over the reference metal quad bike design
