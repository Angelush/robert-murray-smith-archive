## Overview
In video #1475, Robert Murray-Smith explores water accumulators as a form of energy storage, demonstrating how pressurised water can function analogously to a battery. Using hands-on experiments with a Pelton wheel, a syringe, and commercial accumulator vessels, he shows that increasing water pressure dramatically reduces the volume of water needed to do useful work, making accumulators a potentially viable, cheap, and long-lasting alternative to conventional batteries.

## Key Topics
- Reaction turbines vs. impulse turbines and their relevance to energy storage
- The principle of the water accumulator: storing energy as pressurised water
- Historical accumulators (weight-on-piston) vs. modern gas-charged accumulators
- Demonstration comparing low-volume unpressurised water vs. pressurised water jet on a Pelton wheel
- Pressure vessels (CO₂ cylinders, scuba tanks) as readily available high-pressure containers
- Accumulators as industrial components (drilling rigs, nuclear cooling systems)
- Cost and longevity advantages of water accumulators over battery systems

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — the working fluid for energy storage and turbine driving
- **EPDM (ethylene propylene diene monomer)** — the rubber balloon/bladder material inside the domestic accumulator
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — pressurised gas in a scrap-yard aluminium cylinder used as an example pressure vessel (~2,000 PSI / 169 bar)
- **Nitrogen** — used for gas-charging accumulators in industrial settings
- **Compressed air** — used for charging domestic accumulators

## Techniques and Methods
- **Impulse jet demonstration** — 20 ml of water in a syringe forced through a needle to create a high-pressure jet, driving a small Pelton wheel
- **Weight-on-syringe demonstration** — applying a ~1 kg weight to a syringe to simulate piston-pressure charging
- **Gas charging (bladder accumulator)** — pre-charging an EPDM bladder to 50.7 PSI, then filling with water to reach 145 PSI operating pressure
- **Piston charging** — historical method using weighted pistons to pressurise water columns
- **Spring charging** — mentioned as another accumulator charging method
