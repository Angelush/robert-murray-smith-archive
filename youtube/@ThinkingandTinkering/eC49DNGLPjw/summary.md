## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a Mark II version of his hubless wheel, replacing the previous toothed timing belt drive with a bicycle chain wrapped around a steel ring welded inside an aluminium bicycle rim. The idea was contributed by channel member John McFadden, who suggested the chain as a more wear-resistant alternative. The result is a chain-driven hubless wheel demonstrated spinning under drill power, suitable for motorized bikes, wind turbines, and other drive applications.

## Key Topics
- Hubless wheel design (follow-up to video 1089)
- Replacing timing belt drive with bicycle chain
- Salvaging a steel ring from a discarded clock for welding
- Fitting the chain-bearing steel ring inside an aluminium bicycle rim
- Potential applications: motorized bicycle, wind turbines, general drive mechanisms
- Community-sourced ideas and the value of viewer contributions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Bicycle chain (used as the drive mechanism, wrapped around a steel ring)
- Aluminium bicycle rim (existing wheel, not weldable by Robert)
- Steel ring (salvaged from a clock found on the road)
- 4mm bolts and washers (used through spoke holes to secure the steel ring)
- Bicycle sprocket/cog (sourced from a children's bike, used on the drill for demonstration)

## Techniques and Methods
- Welding steel (attaching the bicycle chain to the steel ring)
- Metal cutting (extracting the steel circle from a salvaged clock)
- Bolting the steel ring inside the aluminium rim using existing spoke holes (4mm bolts through each hole)
- Demonstration rig using a cradle with rollers and a handheld drill to spin the wheel

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are brief and conversational, but a few points stand out:
- **Acknowledgement of John's credit**: Robert repeatedly emphasises that this was John McFadden's idea and that John deserves the credit, noting it grew from a comment John left on the previous video.
- **Historical parallel**: He agrees with a commenter that the chain-drive around a large ring is similar to the drive mechanism used in some steam engines.
- **Flat sprocket choice explained**: He clarifies he used a flat pressed steel sprocket specifically because it doesn't foul the bolt heads passing through the steel ring.
- **Community spirit**: He reflects warmly on the channel's collaborative nature — viewer feedback and suggestions being one of the things he values most.
- **Openness to alternatives**: He acknowledges the timing belt solution from video 1089 has its merits, but felt the chain idea deserved more visibility than just a comment suggestion, which is why he made the video.
- **Aluminium welding**: He admits he does not yet have the skill to weld aluminium, which is why a steel insert was needed.
