## Overview
This video is a progress update on an ongoing project to strengthen concrete using graphene, reporting results from over 400 experiments. The team demonstrates compressive strength measurements using a rebound (Schmidt) hammer, showing a graphene-enhanced block reaching 52 N/mm² — roughly double the standard 26–28 N/mm². A key highlight is a "real-world simulation" block made under deliberately poor, informal conditions that still matched or exceeded standard concrete performance.

## Key Topics
- Progress update after 400+ experiments across different formulations
- Rebound hammer testing methodology for measuring concrete compressive strength
- Comparison of graphene-enhanced concrete vs. standard concrete blocks
- "Real-world simulation" test: informal mixing by Tony, cured outdoors for one week
- Graphene oxide as a separate formulation, tested at 7 days
- Benchmarking against existing car park concrete on-site
- Planned next steps: flexural testing, elasticity testing, and independent verification

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene** — added to concrete mix; primary reinforcement agent, achieved 52 N/mm² in best block
- **Graphene oxide** — tested as an alternative additive; registered 33 N/mm² at 7 days; noted as potentially beneficial for flexibility
- **Cement / concrete** — base material mixed to BS 206 Part 1 standard (and informally)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Rebound (Schmidt) hammer testing** — industry-standard tool pressed onto concrete surface; bounce height gives reading in N/mm²
- **Standard BS 206 Part 1 curing** — controlled laboratory conditions, 7-day and 28-day cure cycles
- **Real-world simulation mixing** — intentionally informal: shovels, eyeballed quantities, outdoor curing for one week; designed to reflect typical DIY or construction-site practice
- **On-site benchmarking** — testing existing car park concrete to establish real-world baseline
- **Independent laboratory verification** — planned submission of samples for external validation
- **Flexural and elasticity testing** — announced as upcoming experiments
