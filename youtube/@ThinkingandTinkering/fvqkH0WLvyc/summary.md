## Overview
Robert demonstrates what he considers one of the easiest and cleanest methods for producing bulk graphene in solution: high-shear homogenization of graphite powder in a 75% acetone / 25% water co-solvent mixture. Unlike the blender-with-soap approach he covered previously, this method produces graphene in a solvent that simply evaporates, leaving clean, conductive graphene behind. The resulting dispersion achieves approximately 4 mg/mL concentration and remains stable for weeks.

## Key Topics
- Limitations of the previous soap-based blender method (low conductivity, difficult washing)
- Why conventional liquid phase exfoliation solvents (e.g. NMP) are problematic
- The co-solvent principle: why acetone/water mixture works where each alone does not
- Recipe and process parameters for the homogenization method
- Post-processing via centrifugation to remove large aggregates
- Stability and concentration of the resulting graphene dispersion

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite powder (325 mesh / 45 micrometers)** — feedstock, 40 g per liter of solvent
- **Acetone** — 750 ml (75% of total volume); one component of the co-solvent
- **Water** — 250 ml (25% of total volume); second component of the co-solvent
- **NMP (N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone)** — mentioned as the typical solvent used in conventional liquid phase exfoliation; noted as expensive, restricted in some countries, and unpleasant to handle
- **Soap** — used in his earlier blender method; causes low conductivity in dried films

## Techniques and Methods
- **High-shear homogenization** — using a lab homogenizer (£700 unit) at 13,000 RPM for 1 hour; noted that a kitchen blender in the 20,000–30,000 RPM range could substitute but will burn out quickly
- **Co-solvent liquid phase exfoliation** — exploiting the matched surface energy between graphite and the acetone/water mixture to keep graphene in stable suspension
- **Centrifugation at 1,000 RPM** — to sediment remaining large graphite aggregates and recover clean graphene dispersion
- **Solvent evaporation** — the acetone/water mixture evaporates cleanly, depositing graphene film without contamination
- **Raman spectroscopy** — mentioned in comments as the method Robert uses to verify graphene quality (sent to an external test lab at £150 per test)

## Robert's Own Replies
Several substantive clarifications emerge from Robert's comments:

- **Surfactant addition**: He adds a surfactant to the solution to prevent graphene restacking and agglomeration — an important detail not mentioned in the video itself.
- **Conversion rate**: Repeating the exfoliation process multiple times yields around 80% conversion of graphite to graphene.
- **Acetone recovery**: He recommends doing the process in a pressure cooker fitted with a copper coil condenser (like a still) to recover acetone vapour and avoid filling the room with fumes.
- **Graphene doesn't dissolve**: He clarifies that graphene does not dissolve — it only disperses, and with difficulty; the co-solvent achieves dispersion, not true dissolution.
- **Quality verification**: He sends samples to an external lab for Raman spectroscopy at £150 per test.
- **Drying caution**: He advises deciding what you want to do with the graphene *before* drying it, since redispersing dried graphene is difficult — the drying step may be unnecessary if you intend to apply it directly.
- **Mass production method**: He mentions he has developed a separate mass-production method but has not shared it publicly.
- **Monolayer vs. multilayer**: Monolayer graphene is not suitable for structural applications like supercars; multilayer wrinkled graphene (e.g. Dr. Kaner's LightScribe work) is better for such uses.
- **Conductive ink performance**: Graphene made by this approach gives an 8–10× improvement in conductivity over other conductive inks.
