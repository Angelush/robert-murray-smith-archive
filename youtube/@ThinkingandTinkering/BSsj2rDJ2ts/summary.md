## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits a traditional toy shop in Herne Bay to purchase a humming top, then dissassembles it to reveal the twisted-bar-and-slotted-washer spin mechanism at its core. He uses this mechanism — combined with a 3D-printed frame, a ratchet, thrust bearings, a serpentine coil, and a 2.5 kg barbell flywheel — to build a hand-pumped DIY generator capable of producing up to 6 volts, enough to charge a mobile phone.

## Key Topics
- How a humming top works (twisted bar + slotted washer mechanism)
- The same mechanism found in twist-and-pump drills
- Using TinkerCAD and Thingiverse to design and source 3D-printed parts
- Adding a flywheel to convert the toy mechanism into a generator
- Serpentine coil construction (referenced as a separate video)
- Magnet arrangement for power generation
- Voltage output and practical charging capability

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Humming top** — the donor toy providing the core spin mechanism
- **Steel bar and slotted washer** — the two-part mechanism inside the top
- **Thrust bearings (47 mm × 25 mm)** — used to support the rotating disk
- **2.5 kg barbell** — repurposed as the flywheel
- **20 mm × 4.5 mm disc magnets** — arranged N-S-N-S on the face of the inner spinning disk
- **Serpentine coil** — the pickup coil for the generator (construction detailed in a separate linked video)
- **3D-printed parts** — cage, pillars, feet, cap, and clutch plate (files on Thingiverse)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and reverse-engineering of a commercial toy
- 3D modelling in TinkerCAD (mix of original design, adapted parts, and direct Thingiverse grabs)
- Flywheel integration using a standard barbell weight
- Serpentine coil winding (detailed in a companion video)
- Alternating-polarity magnet arrangement on a rotating disk
- Hand-pump actuation via ratchet + clutch mechanism
