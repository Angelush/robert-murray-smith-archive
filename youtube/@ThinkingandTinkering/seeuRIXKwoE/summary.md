## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces geopolymers — a class of stone-like polymers formed from aluminium and silicon — and explains how mixing clay, sodium silicate (water glass), and magnesium oxide creates a mouldable paste that hardens rock-solid in a standard household oven at just 200°C. The central takeaway is that this geopolymer-based clay enables pottery, sculpture, and decorative objects without the need for a kiln, and he frames it as a viable DIY or commercial product idea.

## Key Topics
- Broad introduction to polymers: synthetic plastics, biological polymers (proteins, cellulose, starch), and geopolymers
- Geopolymers as a class: aluminium silicate formation chemistry
- Sodium silicate (water glass) — properties, safety, and diverse applications
- Industrial waste materials (fly ash, blast furnace slag, red mud) as aluminium sources for geopolymers
- Geopolymers as a low-CO₂ cement replacement
- Live demonstration of the aluminium sulfate + sodium silicate polymerisation reaction
- Recipe for a home geopolymer clay (local clay + magnesium oxide + sodium silicate)
- Curing behaviour: slow at room temperature, sets hard at 200°C
- Practical application: kiln-free pottery and sculpture

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sodium metasilicate / sodium silicate (water glass)** — 40% aqueous solution; binder and key reactant; alkaline, handle with gloves
- **Aluminium sulfate** — used in live demo to show instant aluminium silicate polymer formation
- **Local clay (aluminium silicate)** — dug from hillside; main body of the geopolymer mix
- **Magnesium oxide** — used as filler; reacts to form magnesium silicate (essentially talc); sourced from an equestrian supply centre
- **Blast furnace slag** — mentioned as an industrial aluminium source for geopolymers
- **Fly ash** — residual from coal burning; mentioned as an aluminium source
- **Red mud** — waste from aluminium smelting; mentioned as an aluminium source
- **Bentonite and zeolite** — noted as naturally occurring aluminium silicates

## Techniques and Methods
- **Pipette demonstration**: adding aluminium sulfate dropwise into sodium silicate solution to visually show rapid geopolymer formation (needle-like aluminium silicate crystals)
- **Clay extraction**: digging and processing raw clay from a local hillside (covered in a separate video)
- **Mixing geopolymer paste**: combining clay, magnesium oxide, and sodium silicate solution to a workable consistency
- **Oven curing at ~200°C**: heating the paste in a standard domestic oven to trigger geopolymer setting and produce a rock-hard, water-resistant result
- **Room-temperature ageing** (noted as slow — 2–3 years for full reaction without heat)
