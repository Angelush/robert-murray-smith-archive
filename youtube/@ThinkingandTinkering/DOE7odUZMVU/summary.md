## Overview
This is part 5c of Robert Murray-Smith's series on building a Van de Graaff generator from repurposed parts. In this episode, he demonstrates the modifications made to a belt sander to create the top and bottom rollers and motor assembly. He shows how the components have been adapted, fitted, and positioned in preparation for the next build steps.

## Key Topics
- Fabrication of the top roller from the sander's adjustable arm
- Why the original black carbon-filled plastic roller material was unsuitable for a Van de Graaff generator
- Wrapping the top roller in PTFE (Teflon) tape using super glue
- Adaptation of the bottom roller from the sander's rubber-coated aluminium wheel
- Maintaining the original drive belt/gear system from the sander
- Machining a U-groove into both pulleys to accept an O-ring as a drive belt
- Motor housing modification and wiring extension
- Physical positioning of all components on the generator base

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PTFE tape (Teflon/gasfitters tape)** — used to wrap the top roller to give it the correct triboelectric properties; thicker gasfitters variant preferred
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to bond PTFE tape to the top roller
- **Carbon-filled plastic** — original roller material, discarded as unsuitable for charge generation
- **Aluminium** — bottom roller material after rubber coating was removed
- **Car body filler** — used to seal the gap left after removing the sander handle from the motor body
- **O-ring (large)** — repurposed as the drive belt between motor pulley and bottom roller
- **Threaded bar (10 mm)** — used as the axle for the top roller
- **Thin lock nuts** — used with the threaded bar on the top roller
- **M6 bolts and large washers** — used to mount the motor on a slotted base plate for belt tensioning
- **Plastic (flat sheet)** — used to fabricate a bearing support plate
- **22 mm bearing** — original bearing from the sander, retained in the new plastic support

## Techniques and Methods
- Filing and sanding steel off the sander arm to repurpose it as a top roller mount
- Cutting slots into the top roller mount for easy belt removal/installation
- Skimming (turning) the original roller surface to remove ridges before wrapping
- Double-layer wrapping of PTFE tape with super glue adhesion
- Drilling a 22 mm hole in flat plastic sheet to house the original bearing
- Machining a U-groove into both pulleys to replace the original 5-slot belt track
- Slotting the motor base plate to allow sliding adjustment for belt tensioning
- Crimping wires and clipping the start capacitor for later reinstallation in control circuitry
- Sealing the motor body gap with car body filler and an electrician's seal plug
