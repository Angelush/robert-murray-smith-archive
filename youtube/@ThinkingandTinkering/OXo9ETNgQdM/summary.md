## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to repurpose a discarded exercise bike — one of three he found by the roadside in two weeks — into a small wind turbine. He modifies the bike's pressed-steel flywheel by bending its fins into fan blade shapes, then attaches neodymium magnets around the wheel and a salvaged microwave turntable motor coil to generate electricity. The video is an exploratory proof-of-concept, showing ~4–5 volts and ~20 mA output from a single coil in light, rainy wind conditions.

## Key Topics
- Salvaging and upcycling discarded exercise bikes found as roadside waste
- Exploratory disassembly and assessment of an unusual 1980s-era exercise bike with an air-resistance flywheel
- Modifying the pressed-steel flywheel into fan blades for wind capture
- Using neodymium magnets and a salvaged coil to build a simple axial-flux wind generator
- Comparison with previous exercise bike repurposing projects (hand-crank generators, test beds, water wheels)
- Encouragement to repair/reuse rather than discard consumer goods

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Pressed-steel flywheel** — the air-resistance wheel repurposed as turbine blades
- **Neodymium magnets** — arranged in alternating north-south polarity around the flywheel rim
- **Synchronous motor coil (microwave turntable motor)** — used as the single pickup coil for electricity generation
- **Chain and sprocket drivetrain** — noted as quality components salvaged from the bike
- **Tensioned friction belt** — the original resistance mechanism, noted as worn/slack

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and selective salvage of exercise bike components
- Mechanical modification: slicing and bending flywheel fins to create fan blade geometry
- Circumference-style generator arrangement: magnets mounted around the outer rim of a spinning wheel passing a stationary coil
- Open-circuit voltage and current measurement to characterise generator output
- Exploratory/iterative prototyping — assessing components before committing to a design
