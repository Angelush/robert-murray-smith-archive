## Overview
Robert demonstrates a template-and-tape method for making bellows of any size, using a printed card template and PVC-fabric tape. The resulting bellows are airtight, light-tight, springy, and durable — with a leatherette feel. The key advantage of this approach over his previous methods is that once a template is designed on a computer, it can be reprinted to produce identical bellows repeatedly, and the geometry can be adapted to virtually any shape.

## Key Topics
- Comparison to previous bellows-making methods (Venetian blind and large air-conditioning bellows)
- Designing and printing a template on card (tram lines, 45-degree angles, zigzag cutouts)
- Applying and layering PVC-fabric tape using the card template as registration
- Joining the taped sheet into a tube and folding it origami-style into bellows form
- Finishing with wooden end caps, stretchy PVC electrician's tape, decorative copper sheet, and furniture tacks
- Scalability: the same method works from very small (computer-printed) to very large (hand-drawn) templates

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Fabric tape (fabric covered in PVC)** — primary bellows material; springy, airtight, light-tight, durable
- **230 GSM card** — used as the printed template substrate
- **White PVC electrician's tape (stretch type)** — used to seal and bind end caps tightly
- **Copper sheet/strip** — decorative facing on the end caps
- **Furniture tacks (brass)** — used to fix end caps and decoration
- **Plastic-coated waterproof tape** — noted in comments as an alternative material he has used
- **PET tape** — noted in comments as another alternative
- **Sellotape** — noted in comments as another alternative
- **Gaffa tape** — noted in comments as a further alternative

## Techniques and Methods
- **Template design** — drawing tram lines 8 mm apart with 2 mm gaps, dividing the page into quadrants with 45-degree angles, all on a single sheet for registration
- **Scoring and cutting** — using a steel ruler and sharp knife to score the tram lines, then cutting out the zigzag strips
- **Tape layering** — applying PVC-fabric tape over the card template in sections, keeping zigzag cutouts clear, then taping over each cutout in sequence
- **Edge joining** — aligning the two edges on a block, using temporary holding tape before applying permanent fixing tape
- **Origami-style folding** — pushing in the shorter (trapezium) lines to form the bellows pleats; the structure folds itself once started
- **End cap finishing** — cutting wooden blocks to size (6.6 cm in this example), inserting into the bellows tube, wrapping with stretchy PVC tape, adding decorative copper and furniture tacks

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Alternative tape materials**: He has used plastic-coated waterproof tape, PET tape, sellotape, and gaffa tape in place of the primary PVC-fabric tape.
- **Template drawing is straightforward**: "It's just a lot of straight lines, copy and paste — done!" — the geometry is simple to replicate digitally.
- **Any geometry is possible**: This method is not limited to rectangular bellows — it can produce U-shaped, fully enclosed, and other complex geometries, including covers for CNC machines.
