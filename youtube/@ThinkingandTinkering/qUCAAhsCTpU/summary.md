## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates and explains how to build a low-temperature Stirling engine (referred to as a "metronome heat engine," originally designed by Attila Blade) intended to pump fluid for his SWAG generator project. The engine uses a steel wool displacer inside a stainless steel canister, a latex/rubber balloon diaphragm, and a threaded rod — and can also be adapted to generate electricity with a magnet and coil. The video walks through every construction step and ends with hard-won tuning lessons from running the finished device.

## Key Topics
- Introduction to the metronome heat engine and its intended role in the SWAG generator
- Overview of required materials and where to source them
- Step-by-step construction: canister body, steel wool displacer, diaphragm assembly
- Sealing with fiberglass body filler and black tape
- Tuning and troubleshooting the engine once running
- Future plans: adding an electric generator coil and a water jacket

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel food canister** (approx. 1 litre, with acrylic lid) — main engine body
- **Acrylic lid** — part of the canister; noted as a sealing concern
- **6 mm threaded bar** — displacer rod and structural element
- **Nuts and repair washers** — for securing the displacer
- **Neoprene rubber tap washers** — for sealing
- **Copper pipe fitting** (15 mm base) — outlet/inlet connection
- **10 mm OD / 8 mm ID tube** (5 metres) — fluid pumping tube
- **Steel wool (medium grade)** — used to form the displacer plug
- **Fiberglass body filler kit** (car body repair type, with hardener) — sealing the canister top
- **Latex glove** — first diaphragm attempt (proved too weak)
- **Rubber balloon** — replacement diaphragm (worked well)
- **Cyanoacrylate (crazy glue)** — bonding washers to diaphragm membrane
- **Rubber band** — used to hold the membrane flat during assembly
- **Black tape** — applied over locking rings to fix air leaks
- **Wire wool** — mentioned as winding material (same as steel wool in context)

## Techniques and Methods
- **Displacer fabrication**: unrolling medium-grade steel wool and winding it onto a threaded rod to form a cylindrical plug; trimming with scissors for a clean fit
- **Canister modification**: sawing the bottom off a plastic pot and using a hole saw to cut an opening for the diaphragm chimney; gluing the pot onto the canister
- **Fiberglass application**: mixing fiberglass body filler with hardener and trowelling it around the canister top to embed the copper fitting and improve the seal
- **Diaphragm assembly**: gluing two washers either side of a latex/rubber sheet with cyanoacrylate; burning the centre hole with a soldering iron; stretching the membrane flat using a rubber band before locking it with a threaded ring
- **Sealing with tape**: wrapping black tape around locking rings to eliminate air leaks
- **Engine starting**: placing the canister on a hot pan (~300 °C); manually initiating oscillation then letting it run

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Operating temperature**: confirms the hot plate runs at **300 °C**.
- **Kit availability**: the engine (or related kit) can be purchased from **www.fwgltd.co.uk**.
- **Quiet running**: making the engine "that way" ensures quiet operation; suggests experimenting with displacer volume and position in the cylinder for further tuning.
- **Heat resistance only**: in reply to a query, confirms the material just needs to withstand the heat — no other special property required.
- **Pigment vs. dye clarification** (tangential): notes that a pigment is a particle while a dye is a molecule — likely answering a question from another video but posted under this one; also mentions he is travelling to Australia for a couple of months.
