## Overview
Robert tests an upgraded version of his teaspoon wind turbine, adding a sheet aluminium cone to the centre hub — a suggestion from viewer Dave W. The cone directs wind into the blade sweep area more effectively, boosting measured efficiency from ~30% to ~55% of the Betz limit, which equates to approximately 93% of the theoretical maximum (Betz limit). He acknowledges the results are from bench testing and not rigorously controlled, but considers them striking enough to warrant further investigation.

## Key Topics
- The Betz limit and how turbine efficiency is calculated relative to it
- The concept of swept blade area vs. blade length
- Impact of adding a centre cone on a teaspoon-blade turbine
- Comparison of before/after efficiency measurements
- Suitability of the design for small-scale / home construction only
- Community-driven design iteration (credit to viewer Dave W)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Teaspoons (turbine blades, glued to a plastic disc)
- Plastic disc (blade mount)
- Sheet aluminium (cone fabrication)
- Glue (attaching cone and blades)
- Blinking LED (electrical load for testing)

## Techniques and Methods
- Bench-top wind tunnel testing with a portable anemometer (wind speed: 1.2 m/s per author comment)
- Separate voltage and current (milliamp) measurements to calculate power output
- Cone template calculation to generate a flat-sheet cutting pattern for the aluminium cone
- Controlled variable testing (same blade length, distance, wind speed, and load before/after cone addition)
- Efficiency calculated as percentage of the Betz limit: (measured % / 59%) × 100

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Clarification on the 93% figure:** Robert explicitly explains the maths — he achieved 55% efficiency of the available wind power; since the Betz limit is 59%, this gives 55/59 × 100 = 93% *of* the Betz limit. He was frustrated by commenters claiming he said the turbine exceeded the Betz limit, which he never did.
- **Wind speed:** Confirms the test was run at 1.2 m/s.
- **Potential upgrades:** He is open to bending the blades for improvement and likes the idea of increasing catchment area for more torque, but is cautious about magnetic bearings (too complex/expensive).
- **Cone modifications:** Interested in fixing the cone differently; considered cutting slots in the cone but is concerned this would add complexity and make replication harder — simplicity and replicability are key attributes of the design.
- **Air entrainment:** Acknowledges there is probably a degree of air entrainment contributing to the effect.
- **Scale limitation:** Reiterates the design is only practical at small/home scale; a large cone would be impractical.
