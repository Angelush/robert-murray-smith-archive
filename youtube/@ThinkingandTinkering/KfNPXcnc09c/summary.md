## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a trompe — a simple air compressor with no moving parts — using PVC pipe fittings. The device uses falling water to entrain and compress air, and while the prototype produces modest pressure at small scale, Robert sees significant potential in scaling it up or combining it with a ram pump to create a compressed air battery powered by rainwater.

## Key Topics
- What a trompe is and how it works (air compression via water flow, no moving parts)
- Historical and industrial use of trompes (blast furnaces in Spain, mining in Ragged Chute, Canada)
- Four main components: inlet pipe, expansion chamber, gas collection pipe, outlet pipe
- Construction walkthrough using PVC pipe fittings
- Air-water mixing mechanism using small polypropylene tubes ("alien dreadlocks")
- Pressure limitations at small scale (~15 PSI from mains supply at ~1 meter height)
- Potential for use as a compressed air battery fed by rainwater or household supply
- Combining a trompe with a ram pump (referencing "Mr. Thessalonian's trompe hammer") for higher pressure

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **70–90mm PVC pipe** (10 bar rated) — main structural pipe for inlet and expansion sections
- **PVC pipe fittings** — T-junctions, end caps, U-bend, adapters, connectors
- **5mm polypropylene tubing** — cut into ~half-metre strips to create the air-entraining bundle ("dreadlocks")
- **PVC cement** — for gluing pipe and polypropylene components
- **PTFE tape (gas type)** — for sealing threaded plumbing fittings
- **Quarter-inch NPT fittings** — barb, gas valve, T-junction, adapter for the pressure outlet
- **Pressure gauge** — to measure compressed air output

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting and gluing PVC pipe to form the four trompe sections
- Creating an air-entraining inlet using a bundle of 32 polypropylene tubes glued into an end cap (60–80% fill factor)
- Threading and sealing NPT plumbing fittings with gas-type PTFE tape
- Drilling a hole in the pipe wall and fitting a tank connector for the pressurised gas outlet
- Sizing the outlet pipe to no more than 70% of the inlet pipe length to avoid siphon effects
- Building an elongated/linear layout for clarity, with notes on how to make a compact version
- Suggested improvement: submerging the compression port and integrating with a ram pump accumulator

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are largely brief and conversational, but a few notable points emerge:

- **Rainwater enthusiasm:** He lives in England and notes they get more than enough rain to run a trompe from a rooftop collection system, reinforcing the rainwater-battery concept as practically viable for him personally.
- **Scale clarification:** His own build uses 3.6m of 70–90mm pipe; he notes he would use a different (more compact) design in a real application — the elongated layout was purely for illustration.
- **Ram pump distinction:** He clarifies that a ram pump uses accumulators and is a separate device from the trompe, and that he has discussed ram pumps previously as a potential turbine power source.
- **Design philosophy:** He consistently reiterates his approach of starting with a standard/simple version to understand the principles before iterating — *"start in one place and get hold of the principles then move on."*
- **Openness to suggestions:** He acknowledges community ideas about further pressure improvements and combining technologies as genuinely interesting, calling one suggestion "an idea of genius that needs exploring."
