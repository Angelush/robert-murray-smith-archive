## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the Banki Mitchell crossflow water turbine, tracing its history from its 1891 invention through to its modern use in microgeneration. He repurposes the rotor from a salvaged air conditioning unit to simulate the turbine and demonstrates it working with a bucket of water, highlighting its suitability for low-maintenance, variable-load small-scale hydro power.

## Key Topics
- History of the crossflow turbine (Paul Mortier 1891, Anthony Mitchell 1903, Donát Bánki 1922, Fritz Ossberger)
- How the Banki Mitchell / Ossberger turbine works — water flows *through* the rotor rather than around it
- Advantages: self-cleaning, low maintenance, good average efficiency under variable load
- Microgeneration scale — up to 2,000 kW, contrasted with the common "few kilowatts" assumption
- Comparison with other small hydro options: Pelton wheel, water wheel, Archimedes screw, Poncelet wheel
- Practical build: stripping an AC induction motor housing to extract rotor, shaft, bearings, and end plates
- Bearing support modification using extra-long M4 bolts drilled through the casing back plate
- Bucket-of-water demonstration showing the turbine spinning under no load
- Note that the air-vent holes needed drilling to prevent water choking/braking the rotor

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Salvaged air conditioning unit (lower section, large rotor) — used as the turbine body
- DC motor — intended as the generator, coupled via flexible coupler to the shaft
- M4 bolts (extra-long) — used to mount a rear bearing support
- Rubber bearing seat — existing part of the housing end plate

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of an AC induction motor (removing stator, squirrel-cage rotor, keeping shaft, bearings, end plates, and housing)
- Drilling holes in the rotor housing to allow water/air egress and prevent hydraulic braking
- Drilling the rear casing to accept extra-long M4 bolts for a second bearing mount
- Flexible coupler attachment to extend the shaft for DC motor connection
- Free-spin demonstration using a bucket of water poured over the rotor
