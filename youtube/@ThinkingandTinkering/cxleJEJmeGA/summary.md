## Overview
Robert unboxes and provides a first-look review of the Elegoo Centauri Carbon, a brand-new CoreXY 3D printer that Elegoo sent him for review. He compares it directly to the Bambu Lab X1 Carbon, evaluating build quality, setup experience, software, and hardware specifications, concluding that it is a strong and credible competitor to the Bambu at what he expects will be a significantly lower price point.

## Key Topics
- History and evolution of 3D printer movement systems: Cartesian, Delta, and CoreXY
- Robert's personal journey into 3D printing via Elegoo's earlier Cartesian machines
- Unboxing and physical inspection of the Elegoo Centauri Carbon
- Hardware comparison with the Bambu Lab X1 Carbon (build volume, display size, frame materials, heater wattage)
- Out-of-box setup process: attaching the display, removing transport screws, loading filament
- Wi-Fi connectivity setup and local network control (no mandatory cloud account required)
- Software compatibility (Prusaslicer, OrcaSlicer, bundled slicer)
- Scoring/verdict on arrival packaging, build quality, setup ease, and UI convenience
- Price speculation and competitive positioning vs. Bambu Lab X1C (~£1,000)
- Teaser for a head-to-head print performance comparison in a follow-up video

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA** — standard filament used as the primary printing material reference
- **TPU** — mentioned as an example filament type for temperature presets
- **Carbon fibre (filament)** — referenced in comments as a material the printer handles
- **Nanocrystalline mineral** — used as the air filtration medium inside the Centauri Carbon (contrasted with activated carbon used in the Bambu)
- **Activated carbon** — the filter material used in the Bambu Lab X1C for comparison
- **Hardened tool steel** — used in the Centauri Carbon's hot end/nozzle
- **Brass** — also part of the hot end assembly alongside hardened steel
- **Aluminium extrusion** — frame construction material of the Centauri Carbon
- **Die-cast magnesium** — used for the corner joints to add rigidity
- **Welded steel** — the frame construction method used by Bambu Lab (for comparison)

## Techniques and Methods
- CoreXY motion system (contrasted with Cartesian and Delta systems)
- Vibration compensation calibration (automated self-test on first boot, ~30 minutes)
- Extrusion compensation calibration (part of the same automated self-test)
- Filament loading/unloading via the touchscreen prepare menu
- Wi-Fi setup via on-machine network settings and local IP-based connection in the slicer software (no cloud account required)
- Camera monitoring via the bundled software and mobile app

## Robert's Own Replies
- **On price:** Reacted positively to a suggestion of under £500, saying "wow - under 500 - that is cool," implying that is in the right ballpark.
- **On fumes:** Clarified he primarily prints in PLA (no fume concern) and that fumes depend entirely on the filament used.
- **On the AMS multi-material system:** Confirmed he removed the AMS from his own X1C as he found it unnecessary, but acknowledged Elegoo are planning an equivalent system for the Centauri.
- **On P1S vs. Centauri comparison:** Argued the Centauri is far closer to the X1C than the P1S — the P1S has a poor display, is "just the P1P in a plastic box," and its hot end is not hardened, whereas the Centauri has a good display, all-metal build, and a hardened hot end.
- **On carbon fibre filament:** Confirmed the printer can handle it (replied "carbon fibre mate" in context of a comment about compatible materials).
- **On channel evolution:** Noted his channel has gone through four major theme changes (graphene → batteries → energy generation → 3D printing) and that pushback on change is predictable but wearing.
- **On the cloud:** Stated simply "i hate the cloud," consistent with his praise of the Centauri's local Wi-Fi setup vs. Bambu's cloud-dependent approach.
- **On filament extrusion:** Mentioned Filastruder and its copies, and a pellet-based extruder called Strooder (a Kickstarter project), in response to a comment about making your own filament.
