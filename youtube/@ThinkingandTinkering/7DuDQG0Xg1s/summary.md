## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates an upgraded version of his "exploding water" experiment, using five 2kV capacitors wired in parallel and a dedicated spark gap instead of a spark plug. The surprising finding is that the water ignites brilliantly on the polycarbonate board surrounding the spark gap, not inside the gap itself — a result he finds deeply puzzling and invites viewers to explain.

## Key Topics
- High-voltage capacitor discharge into water
- Redesigned spark gap replacing a previously used spark plug
- Unexpected location of ignition: water on the board, not in the gap
- Implications for building a spark-gap-erosion-free setup
- Potential future application to graphene reactor construction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Water (the subject of ignition/detonation)
- Polycarbonate board (high-voltage-grade, used as the surface on which water is placed)
- Microwave capacitors — 0.91 µF, 2000V, five units wired in parallel
- 2000V power supply

## Techniques and Methods
- Capacitor bank assembly: five 2kV capacitors in parallel to increase energy storage while maintaining voltage
- Spark gap discharge (replacing a spark plug to avoid electrode erosion)
- Direct deposition of water onto the board surface (not contained in an electrode cup)
- High-voltage pulse discharge through the water film

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Mechanism:** The sudden high-voltage discharge into the water detonates it — he offers this as the likely explanation for the phenomenon.
- **Circuit details:** The capacitors are microwave units, 0.91 µF at 2000V, connected in parallel and fed by a 2000V supply.
- **Board material:** The surface is polycarbonate rated for high-voltage installations.
- **HV path:** The high voltage is definitely passing through the water on the board.
- **Earlier negative result:** A prior (unfilmed) version used a cup-shaped electrode filled with water with an insulated central electrode — it only fizzed slightly, producing nothing like the current result.
- **Next steps:** Plans to remove the spark gap entirely and experiment with flat electrodes of large surface area to eliminate erosion problems.
- **Graphene reactor:** Intends to use this technique as the basis for a graphene reactor.
