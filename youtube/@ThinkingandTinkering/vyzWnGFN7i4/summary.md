## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the fundamentals of ink-making, demonstrating how to create a simple oil-based printing ink using recycled laser toner and boiled linseed oil. He then broadens the scope to explain "functional inks" — inks containing active materials like graphite, iron oxide, or cadmium sulfate that can serve purposes beyond decoration, enabling printed batteries, solar cells, supercapacitors, and thermoelectric generators.

## Key Topics
- The two main categories of ink: water-based (writing) and oil-based (printing)
- Functional inks and their applications in printed electronics
- The role of boiled linseed oil as a drying oil vehicle
- Using laser toner as a fine carbon pigment source
- Ink mixing techniques: palette knife, muller, and three-roll mill
- Ink additives: extenders, soap (release agent), rosin (tackifier), and drying agents
- Troubleshooting ink formulation (bleed-out, slow drying, adhesion)
- The tradition of closely guarded ink recipes

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Boiled linseed oil** — drying oil vehicle/binder; oxidises in air to form a hard, waterproof film
- **Laser toner** — fine carbon-based pigment; available in black and colours
- **Lamp black** — traditional carbon pigment made by soot deposition
- **Iron oxide** — additive for magnetic functional inks
- **Graphite** — additive for conductive functional inks
- **Cadmium sulfate** — additive for solar-active functional inks
- **Rosin** — tree sap powder; used as a tackifier and to improve hardness/scratch resistance
- **Soap** — release agent to aid ink transfer from printing block to substrate
- **Lead salts** — historically used as driers (noted as no longer usable)
- **Cobalt/manganese salts** — modern drying agents (Rustins turbine dryer with cobalt mentioned)
- **Gum arabic** — noted in a comment as the binder for newsprint ink (black + water + gum arabic)
- **SBR/CMC** — recommended in comments as a flexible binder alternative

## Techniques and Methods
- **Palette knife mixing** — manually folding and dragging toner into linseed oil on a glass surface
- **Muller grinding** — using a domed glass muller in circular motions to disperse pigment
- **Three-roll mill** — differential-speed steel rollers for efficient shear-mixing of ink
- **Block printing** — applying finished ink to a printing block and transferring to paper
- **Lamp black production** — holding glass near a candle flame in a reducing atmosphere to collect soot
- **Ink formulation troubleshooting** — adjusting oil ratio, adding dryers, extenders, or release agents

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Newsprint ink formula:** Confirmed it is simply black pigment, water, and gum arabic.
- **Binder advice:** Suggested SBR/CMC (styrene-butadiene rubber / carboxymethyl cellulose) as a flexible binder for someone having adhesion trouble.
- **Solar charging:** Noted he has done work on direct solar-charged printed devices himself; cautioned that energy applications are always "devil in the detail."
- **Drying mechanism:** Clarified that the oil oxidises naturally and black pigment does not interfere with this process — unlike some other materials, which is why dryers are sometimes necessary.
- **Conductive coloured inks:** Acknowledged that making a coloured *and* conductive alcohol-based ink is a genuine challenge and unlikely to be cheap.
- **Cancer concern (re: toner/materials):** Declined to take a position, stating he prefers to let people make up their own minds rather than engage in the argument either way.
