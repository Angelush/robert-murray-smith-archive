## Overview
In this short, spontaneous video, Robert Murray-Smith shares an unexpected discovery made late on a Sunday evening: that two pieces of carbon-coated paper (one graphite, one carbon), previously soaked in electrolyte, can generate over 430 millivolts when simply laid directly on top of each other — with no separator required. The key insight is that the waterproof matrix encapsulating the carbon and graphite may already act as a built-in separator, simplifying the construction of an all-carbon battery considerably.

## Key Topics
- Revisiting the all-carbon battery concept
- The standard battery construction model (anode, cathode, separator, electrolyte)
- Discovery that a separator may be unnecessary due to the waterproof matrix in the carbon-coated paper
- Spontaneous voltage generation from two carbon layers in direct contact
- Open questions about the underlying mechanism

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** — used as one electrode (graphite side of the paper)
- **Carbon** — used as the other electrode (carbon side of the paper)
- **Electrolyte** — papers were previously soaked in electrolyte; residual dampness remained at time of experiment
- **Carbon-coated paper** — the substrate carrying both active materials, described as encapsulated in a waterproof matrix

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting/coating paper with carbon and graphite inks to form electrode sheets
- Soaking coated paper in electrolyte
- Direct stacking of two electrode sheets (graphite-side up on one, carbon-side on the other) without a separator
- Voltage measurement with a multimeter
