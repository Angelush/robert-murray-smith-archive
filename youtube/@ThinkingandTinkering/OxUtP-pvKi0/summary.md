## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tears down a £30 mini ceramic PTC heater to examine its components and evaluate the marketing claims made about these products. He demonstrates that PTC heaters offer no efficiency advantage over any other electric resistance heater, debunks common advertising claims, and raises significant safety concerns about the specific unit examined — which lacked any fuse or thermal cutout.

## Key Topics
- What PTC (Positive Thermal Coefficient) means and how it works as a self-limiting heating mechanism
- Why all direct electric heaters are ~100% efficient, making "more efficient" claims meaningless
- Debunking the "heats up quickly" marketing claim via basic thermodynamics
- Internal component teardown: power board, control board, PTC ceramic elements, fan, LED
- Safety concerns: no fuse in plug, no fuse on board, no thermal cutout, loose crimp wiring found during disassembly
- Cost comparison: £30 for this unit vs ~£7 for a standard fan heater
- How to make DIY PTC rubber using silicone sealant (bath sealant/RTV) and graphite

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PTC ceramic** — the actual heating element; positive thermal coefficient material that self-limits temperature
- **Nichrome wire** — mentioned as comparison heating element used in standard heaters; prone to oxidation
- **Carbon/graphite** — mentioned as a component in DIY PTC rubber and as a general heating element material
- **Silicon carbide** — mentioned as another type of heating element, all ~100% efficient
- **Silicone sealant (RTV / bath sealant)** — base material for DIY PTC rubber; smells of vinegar (acetic acid cure type)
- **Graphite** — mixed into silicone sealant to create DIY PTC conductive rubber

## Techniques and Methods
- Teardown/disassembly of a consumer mini ceramic heater enclosure
- Inspection of PCB soldering and wiring loom crimps
- DIY PTC rubber fabrication: mixing graphite into silicone (RTV) bath sealant to create a self-regulating resistive heating material
- Basic thermodynamic analysis: calculating heating requirements by room volume and specific heat capacity

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Blunt verdict on the product:** "These are really just a bit of a scam mate" — you need more power or a bigger heater to actually warm a space.
- **On heating efficiency claims:** Directly confirmed to commenters that PTC heaters cannot be cheaper to run — the physics doesn't change regardless of how fast you heat. "It doesn't change the power required in any way if you heat slowly or quickly."
- **Safety concerns reinforced:** Described the unit as "a positive danger" — confirmed it was wired directly to 230V mains with no safety at all.
- **On the heating element itself:** Clarified that the PTC element is fine in principle and has legitimate uses, but this particular Chinese-made version was poorly made. "Don't buy a Chinese one."
- **On CE/Kite marks:** Noted these are self-certification schemes, implying they offer limited assurance of safety.
- **On DIY PTC rubber:** Confirmed the term "RTV" for the silicone sealant material and agreed it could work well as a floor heater or for warming clothes.
- **On the heating strip temperature:** Confirmed the PTC strip cannot exceed about 30°C surface temperature in normal operation — "so no you couldn't light a cigarette."
