## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to wind a bifilar pancake coil using a homemade acrylic jig, walking through the construction process step by step. He uses twisted-strand wire and explains the bifilar winding technique — where two wires are wound together and then connected so the current travels in one continuous loop. The video is a practical introduction before he plans to use the coils in a jewel thief circuit experiment.

## Key Topics
- What a bifilar pancake coil is and why a jig is necessary to make one
- Construction of the winding jig from acrylic sheet
- Wire selection and gauge considerations
- Step-by-step winding process and tension management
- Fixing and sealing the finished coil with super glue
- How to connect the wire ends to create the bifilar configuration
- Planned application: testing in a jewel thief circuit vs. a toroidal core

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 4 mm acrylic sheet — used to make the two jig discs
- Bolt, metal washer, nut — jig assembly hardware
- Plastic spacer (drilled from window frame material using a 25 mm hole cutter) — non-conductive separator in the jig
- 26 SWG twisted-strand plastic-coated wire (from Maplins) — primary winding wire
- Magnet wire — mentioned as an alternative; recommended no thinner than ~20 gauge
- Super glue (cyanoacrylate) — used to fix and seal the wound coil
- Lint-free cloth — used to wipe excess super glue over the coil surface

## Techniques and Methods
- Jig fabrication: cutting two acrylic discs, drilling a central hole and perimeter holes in one disc, and a small offset hole for threading wire
- Bifilar winding: feeding two wires simultaneously through the jig and winding them in parallel while maintaining tension and keeping wires parallel
- Tension management: adjusting nut tightness to control winding resistance and prevent acrylic disc bowing
- Glue fixation: applying super glue into the perimeter holes to wick onto the wire and lock it in place; a second full-surface coat applied after disassembly
- Bifilar connection: joining one centre lead to the opposite outer lead to create a single continuous winding loop
