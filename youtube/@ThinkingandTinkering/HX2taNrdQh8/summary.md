## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces carbon felt as a revolutionary wick material capable of wicking viscous oils like vegetable oil that standard cotton wicks cannot handle. He demonstrates it burning vegetable oil in a simple container and then shows it as the basis for an improved "plant pot heater," arguing its near-indestructibility makes it a practically permanent wick compared to cotton.

## Key Topics
- Wicking and capillary action explained using coffee filter paper
- Why traditional cotton wicks fail with heavy oils (viscosity limits)
- How cotton wicks are fireproofed (borax and table salt)
- Carbon felt as a superior wick material for heavy/viscous oils
- Soot and incomplete combustion — solved with a chimney
- Safety property: bulk vegetable oil won't ignite if knocked over
- Plant pot heater demonstration using the carbon felt wick

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Carbon felt** (activated) — the main wick material; sourced from cooker filters, odor-eating insoles, air filters
- **Vegetable oil** (specifically sunflower oil) — primary fuel demonstrated
- **Paraffin** — tested as a fuel
- **Biodiesel** — tested as a fuel
- **Kerosene** — referenced as a light, easily-wicked fuel for comparison
- **Cotton** — traditional wick material, burns at ~210°C
- **Borax** — used to fireproof cotton wicks
- **Table salt** — also used to fireproof cotton wicks
- **Methylated spirits / meths** — mentioned in comments to prime a reluctant wick
- **15 mm plumbing fitting** — used as a structural sleeve to hold the carbon felt wick

## Techniques and Methods
- Demonstrating capillary action with coffee filter paper dipped in liquid
- Rolling/fitting carbon felt into a 15 mm plumbing fitting to form a wick unit
- Submerging the wick in oil and waiting ~5 minutes for initial wicking
- Adding a glass chimney to improve oxygen supply and eliminate soot
- Constructing a plant pot heater: three bricks as a stand, terracotta pot inverted as a chimney/radiator

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was active in the comments and added several substantive clarifications:

- **Why carbon outlasts cotton as a wick:** Cotton burns at 210°C; carbon burns at 400°C+. Wicks are kept cool by vaporisation of the fuel, but the higher ignition threshold of carbon makes it far harder to destroy when used as a wick.
- **It's the capillary structure, not just the carbon:** Any material with suitable capillary action will work as a wick — carbon's longevity is a bonus, not the only factor.
- **Priming a reluctant wick:** Dose the carbon felt with a small amount of methylated spirits/meths to get it started if it won't light initially.
- **Fitting size is flexible:** The 15 mm plumbing fitting size isn't critical — use whatever fits; the carbon felt can be rolled to suit different diameters.
- **Soot is an incomplete burn:** He acknowledged soot as a real problem representing inefficiency and said he has ideas to address it in future videos.
- **Indoor use needs more work:** He agreed the plant pot version has too many issues for indoor use in its current form and described this video as proof-of-concept for the wick, not a finished heater design.
- **Camping stove potential:** He was enthusiastic that the vegetable-oil version would make an excellent tent/camping stove precisely because the oil won't burst into flames if knocked over — unlike lighter fuels.
- **Don't leave it unattended:** He explicitly said he wouldn't leave any fire unattended, as fire has "a bit of a life of its own."
- **Earlier videos on carbon felt:** He noted he has earlier (lower production quality) videos on making carbon felts and foams for those wanting to go deeper.
- **Hemp carbonised as a wick:** He agreed carbonised hemp would likely make an excellent wick material.
- **Tent heater design:** He mentioned he was actively thinking about designing a dedicated heater for tent use as a future video.
