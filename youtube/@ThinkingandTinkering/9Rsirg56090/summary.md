## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the engineering inside ballpoint pens — specifically the sub-millimeter ball bearing — and repurposes ballpoint pen nibs as a low-friction thrust bearing for a flywheel generator. He designs a 3D-printed tripod disc in Tinkercad that holds three pen nibs pointing inward to support a 1.2 cm ball bearing, demonstrating that precision engineering available for pennies can solve real problems like bearing friction in wind turbine/generator projects.

## Key Topics
- Brief history of writing instruments: reed pens (~3000 BC), quill and dip pens, first ballpoint pen (John Loud, 1888), and Laszlo Bíró's 1938 improvement
- How to make a dip pen and inkwell from an aluminium drinks can
- The chemistry and viscosity challenges of ballpoint pen ink
- The precision engineering inside a standard ballpoint pen (ball bearing < 0.5 mm)
- Designing a thrust bearing using ballpoint pen nibs and a 3D-printed tripod disc
- Integration of the bearing into a flywheel generator

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Aluminium drinks can (Coca-Cola) — used to make a dip pen nib and inkwell
- Ballpoint pen nibs — repurposed as bearing contact points; ball stated to be stainless steel (also available in ceramic or tungsten)
- 1.2 cm ball bearing — rides on the three pen nib tips
- Silicon lubricant — applied to reduce friction on the bearing
- Magnets — embedded in the flywheel for generator use
- Ink — newspaper ink cited as inspiration for Bíró's viscosity solution

## Techniques and Methods
- DIY dip pen fabrication from a cut and folded aluminium can, tape, and a stick
- Sanding the pen nib tip with wet-and-dry sandpaper
- 3D design in Tinkercad to create a tripod disc holder for pen nibs
- Gluing a cup/socket onto an 8 mm axle bar to seat the ball bearing
- Assembly of a cage with two bearings to prevent lateral axle movement
- Spin-testing the flywheel by pulling a string to demonstrate low-friction rotation
