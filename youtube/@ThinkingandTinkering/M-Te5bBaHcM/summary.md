## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the potential of cycloid drives as a low-cost, high-torque mechanism for solar tracking systems. He argues that small-to-mid-range solar farms lose 15–30% of potential energy output by using fixed frames instead of tracking mounts, and that existing tracking solutions are too expensive to justify. He proposes that a 3D-printed cycloid drive could provide the mechanical precision and strength needed to make solar tracking economically viable.

## Key Topics
- Energy losses in fixed-frame solar installations (15–30% without tracking)
- Overview of existing solar tracking solutions and their cost limitations
- How cycloid drives work: gear ratios, eccentric bearings, cycloidal discs, and output shafts
- Assembly walkthrough of the Hypercycloid 5 (by Ott Vinta, available on Thingiverse)
- Why cycloid drives are well-suited for solar panel actuation: high torque, low backlash, high gear reduction

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 3D-printed PLA/plastic components (implied by context of printing the cycloid drive)
- Eccentric bearing (used in the input shaft assembly)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing cycloid drive components from STL files
- Downloading and modifying STL files in Tinkercad
- Assembly of a dual-ring cycloid drive for balanced rotation
- Gear ratio calculation: (pins − lobes) / lobes, e.g. 12 pins, 11 lobes → 11:1 ratio
- Proposed use of a sensing system (light-based or positional) to drive the cycloid output shaft for solar tracking
