## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a DIY magnetic stirrer, a lab tool used to stir solutions for extended periods without manual effort. He opts for a more powerful drill motor over the commonly seen PC-fan approach, building a resin disc to hold magnets and enclosing everything in a builder's board box. The finished device includes a PWM speed controller and a homemade plastic-coated stir bar.

## Key Topics
- Why he needed a magnetic stirrer (hour-long solution stirring)
- Critique of the common PC fan-based approach (insufficient torque)
- Using a salvaged drill motor as a more powerful alternative
- Casting a resin disc to mount drive magnets
- Building the enclosure from builder's board
- Speed control via a PWM (pulse-width modulation) circuit
- Making a DIY stir bar from a magnet wrapped in sticky-back plastic

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Neodymium (neo) magnets** — used as drive magnets on the spinning disc
- **Hard drive magnet** — glued to the disc as the primary drive magnet; also used as the stir bar
- **Two-part epoxy resin filler powder** — cast into the spinning disc (fiberglass-reinforced version also mentioned as an option)
- **Red hardener** — catalyst for the two-part resin
- **Vaseline** — used as a release agent on the cog splines to prevent resin adhesion
- **Super glue** — bonding the hard drive magnet to the disc and assembling the builder's board box
- **Double-sided tape** — temporary fixturing during disc casting
- **Roofing lead** — beaten into nuggets and placed inside the box as ballast to reduce vibration
- **Sticky-back plastic** — used to coat/seal the stir bar magnet

## Techniques and Methods
- **Salvaging a drill motor** for higher torque than a PC fan
- **Casting a resin disc** using two-part epoxy filler powder around the motor's drive cog, using Vaseline as a mould release
- **Lathe-turning by hand** — spinning the disc against sandpaper using the motor itself to true it into a balanced circle
- **Drilling and bolting** a mounting plate using original drill fixings as guides
- **Box construction from builder's board** — cut with a craft/trimming knife, glued with super glue
- **PWM speed control circuit** — built from a MOSFET (salvaged from the drill), a 555 timer, and a variable resistor
- **Stir bar fabrication** — wrapping a small magnet in sticky-back plastic to seal it
