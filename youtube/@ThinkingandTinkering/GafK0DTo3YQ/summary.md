## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to 3D print and assemble custom waterproof, rust-free ball bearings using glass balls and printed plastic races. He argues that bearings are unsung heroes of mechanical design, and shows that by combining a printed four-part bearing assembly with cheap glass balls, you can produce corrosion-proof bearings ideal for outdoor applications like wind turbines and water wheels at a fraction of the commercial cost.

## Key Topics
- History of friction reduction: sliding vs. rolling friction, rollers, and ball bearings
- Bearings as critical but overlooked mechanical components
- Limitations of fully 3D-printed bearings (tendency to stick, skip, loose/tight fit)
- Design and assembly of a custom 4-part 3D-printed bearing using glass balls
- Cost comparison: DIY glass bearings vs. commercially purchased glass or ceramic bearings
- Applications: outdoor/weatherproof machinery (wind turbines, water wheels)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glass balls** — used as the rolling elements; rust-proof and waterproof
- **PLA or similar 3D print filament** — implied material for the printed races and cage
- **Super glue (cyanoacrylate)** — used to bond the two cage halves together
- **Steel balls** — mentioned as an alternative to glass
- **Stainless steel balls** — mentioned as a rust-resistant alternative
- **Ceramic balls** — noted as another alternative, slightly more expensive, available on eBay/Amazon
- **Duck grease** — referenced historically as lubrication for early rollers/wheel bearings

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing of bearing components (outer race, inner race, cage in two halves) — designed in TinkerCAD and published on Thingiverse
- Manual assembly: seating inner race inside outer race, inserting six glass balls, fitting cage halves around balls
- Spot-glueing cage halves with a minimal amount of super glue to lock the assembly
- Print-in-place bearing printing (mentioned as an alternative but noted as unreliable)
