## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith visits a coastal Site of Special Scientific Interest on the London clay belt to introduce a DIY energy device based on zeolite clay. When dry zeolite (X13/zeolite 13X) absorbs water, it releases significant heat through an exothermic adsorption reaction; Robert harnesses this temperature differential using a Peltier thermoelectric device to generate electricity and run a small motor. The system is presented as safe, rechargeable by simply drying the clay, and built entirely from cheap, abundant, non-caustic materials.

## Key Topics
- London clay belt geology and the SSSI coastal site as a source of clay
- Properties of zeolite clay and how it differs from ordinary clays
- The exothermic adsorption reaction when dry zeolite absorbs water
- Peltier (thermoelectric) devices and how a hot/cold differential generates electricity
- Live demonstration: zeolite + water → heat → Peltier device → motor running
- Rechargeability of the system by drying the spent clay
- Economic and practical viability of zeolite-based energy storage at scale

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zeolite X13 (zeolite 13X)** — synthetic aluminosilicate clay; the energy storage medium; also found in face masks and fish-pond filters; ~US$6/kg
- **Water** — triggers the exothermic adsorption reaction
- **Steel tin** — container for the clay/water reaction (hot side)
- **Graphite foil** — used as a thermal interface/contact material between tin and Peltier device
- **Marble slab** — acts as the cold-side thermal mass beneath the Peltier device
- **Peltier device** — thermoelectric module (also found in beer chillers) converting the heat differential to electrical current

## Techniques and Methods
- Exothermic water adsorption into zeolite galleries to generate heat
- Thermoelectric generation using a Peltier device bridging a hot and cold surface
- Graphite foil as a thermal contact layer to improve heat transfer
- Using a dense stone (marble) as a passive cold-side heat sink
- Drying the zeolite to remove absorbed water and "recharge" the energy store
- Insulating the hot side (e.g., thermos flask) to extend the duration of electricity generation

## Robert's Own Replies
- The specific zeolite used is **X13 (zeolite 13X)**, a synthetic aluminosilicate; cost is approximately **US$6 per kilogram**.
- Zeolites are aluminosilicates with both natural and synthetic sources; **Australia is a major source of natural zeolites**.
- This is **not free energy** — the clay must be dried out after use to restore its adsorptive capacity.
- The drying/regeneration phase (~200°C, which Robert notes is not a high temperature) can be powered by **solar energy**, making the full cycle renewable.
- Existing real-world systems have been developed with a **20-year lifespan** and a cost of **US$0.06 per kWh**.
- He cites a key academic paper: *"Developing a techno-economic model to evaluate the cost performance of a zeolite 13X-based space heating system"* — doi: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enconman.2021.114325
