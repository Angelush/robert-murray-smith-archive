## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a common shaded pole induction motor — typically found in microwave fans and similar appliances — into a switched reluctance generator with minimal modifications. By cutting the magnetic flux path, removing the rotor poles, reshaping the rotor into a bar, and adding permanent magnets, he achieves nearly 2 volts of output. He concludes that the two-pole design still works as a generator by varying flux intensity through the rotor's lower-reluctance path.

## Key Topics
- What shaded pole induction motors are and where they're commonly found
- The principle of switched reluctance generation
- Physical modifications needed to convert the motor to a generator
- How a two-pole configuration differs from a typical four-pole reluctance setup
- Demonstration of voltage output (~1.97V) from the converted device
- Trade-offs of the design (high coil resistance, milliamp-level current output)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Shaded pole induction motor (salvaged fan motor)
- Aluminium rotor bars and end plates (part of the induction circuit)
- Permanent magnets (added to the stator poles)
- Fine wire coil (potted, high resistance)
- Iron core (existing in the stator)
- Envelope case fan housing

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting the stator to break the magnetic flux path
- Removing existing shaded poles (pulled out with pliers)
- Reshaping the induction rotor into a bar geometry
- Inserting permanent magnets into the stator pole positions
- Reassembling the motor with the original fan (involute fan used to drive speed via blown air)
- Measuring AC output voltage with a meter

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Cheap construction is a feature, not a bug** — he acknowledges the notably low-cost build quality of these motors as characteristic.
- **Flux path is why the rotor shape matters** — if the rotor remained cylindrical (a drum), there would be no high and low point in the flux path, and reluctance generation would not work. The bar shape is essential.
- **Removing the poles is easy** — he confirms he did remove the shaded poles but forgot to highlight it on camera; they simply pull out.
- **Keeping it a reluctance generator** — when asked about an alternative approach (implied: using it differently), Robert notes that doing so would mean it is no longer functioning as a reluctance generator, which is the point of the experiment.
