## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith walks through his top 10 projects using salvaged microwave oven transformers (MOTs), dividing them into low-voltage and high-voltage categories. The video emphasises safety strongly — particularly around the high-voltage secondary winding — while demonstrating the remarkable versatility of these commonly discarded components. The key takeaway is that MOTs are powerful, multipurpose tools for the home experimenter, but the high-voltage applications have caused fatalities and must be approached with serious caution.

## Key Topics
- Structure of a microwave oven transformer (thick primary vs. thin secondary windings)
- Low-voltage projects: electromagnet, arc/spot welder, arc light, furnace, hot stapler, battery charger, generator, motor
- High-voltage projects: fractal wood burning, Jacob's Ladder
- Safety warnings around high-voltage use
- Re-winding the transformer core to produce custom output voltages (e.g. 12 V)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Salty water — used as the conducting medium painted onto wood for fractal burning
- Carbon rods — used as electrodes to produce an arc light
- Refractory brick — used to contain heat for a DIY metal-melting furnace
- Thick copper wire — wound onto the core for low-voltage / high-current secondary outputs
- Metal strip (fuse-like piece) — used in the hot-stapler application

## Techniques and Methods
- **Core disassembly** — cutting the weld line with an angle grinder or hacksaw to separate the E-core and remove windings
- **Re-winding** — adding a new secondary of a calculated number of turns to achieve a target output voltage (e.g. ~24 turns for 10 V out, adjusted by measurement to reach 12 V)
- **Rectification and smoothing** — converting the 12 V AC output to DC for use as a battery charger
- **Spot/arc welding** — wrapping a few turns of very thick wire to obtain high-current low-voltage output
- **Arc light construction** — connecting carbon rod electrodes to the high-current output
- **Furnace building** — enclosing the arc inside refractory brick to reach metal-melting temperatures
- **Hot stapling** — using a thin metal strip as a resistive heating element pressed into plastic to fuse parts
- **Generator/motor construction** — arranging six transformer cores in a ring with rotating magnets, or driving the coil with switched DC for a motor
- **Fractal wood burning (Lichtenberg figures)** — applying salty water to wood and connecting the high-voltage secondary across it
- **Jacob's Ladder** — connecting two diverging copper wires to the high-voltage output to produce a climbing arc
