## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the physics of candle-based plant pot heaters, calculates that a single candle outputs approximately 74 watts of heat, and identifies the core danger of traditional designs: terracotta plant pots crack under thermal stress. He then builds an improved, safer version using a stainless steel toilet brush holder on a broad-based metal frame with a homemade paraffin candle, demonstrating that the concept works well in small spaces when constructed from better materials.

## Key Topics
- Energy output of a candle: ~37,000 J/g of wax, burning ~8 g/hr ≈ 74 watts
- Why candles feel cool despite producing heat (convective heat loss rising to ceiling)
- How plant pot heaters work (thermal mass re-radiating heat)
- Danger of terracotta plant pots: poor quality control, thermal stress cracking, fire risk
- Why metal containers are a superior substitute
- Structural stability: broad-base pyramid geometry to prevent tipping
- Wick science: fuel-to-oxygen ratio, soot as a sign of incomplete combustion, wick sizing
- Paraffin vs. lamp oil as fuel
- Heating a person/small space vs. a large room

## Techniques and Methods
- **Energy calculation** — deriving watt output from burn rate (g/hr) × energy density (J/g)
- **Slot cutting by stitching** — drilling a row of holes in a line then filing between them to create a slot in sheet metal
- **Metal bending (gooseneck)** — bending strapping to position the wick holder over the center of the jar
- **Copper pipe crimping** — flaring and crimping a copper tube through a lid hole to form a secure wick guide
- **Wick sizing for clean combustion** — trimming wick length to balance fuel exposure with available oxygen, eliminating soot
- **Infrared temperature measurement** — using black tape to increase surface emissivity for accurate thermal camera/IR thermometer readings
- **Candle-making in a glass jar** — pouring paraffin into a jar with a fitted lid and copper-pipe wick holder
