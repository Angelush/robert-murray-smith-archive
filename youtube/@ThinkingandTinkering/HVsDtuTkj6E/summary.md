## Overview
In this third installment of his DIY multi-fuel home generator series, Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to convert a Vevor twin-cylinder air compressor into a steam/compressed air engine. The core modification involves replacing the original cylinder head with a custom-drilled engineered aluminum plate fitted with plumbing fittings as inlet and outlet ports, with a valve system to follow in a subsequent video.

## Key Topics
- The four requirements for converting a compressor into an engine: converting the cylinder, creating a valve, timing the valve, and providing a power supply (steam or compressed air)
- Fabricating a new cylinder head from an engineered aluminum block using the original valve chest as a drilling guide
- Installing 8mm brass plumbing fittings as inlet/outlet ports
- Gasket cutting for the new head
- Flywheel options: weighting the original aluminum pulley vs. using a salvaged cast iron flywheel from an exercise bike
- Finding top dead center (TDC) using a stainless steel straw
- Overview of valve approaches: slide valve, rotary valve, or electronically timed valve
- Applicability of the same conversion technique to four-stroke and motorcycle engines

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Engineered aluminum block** (20×20 cm, 2 cm thick, one flat face) — used as the new cylinder head blank
- **8mm brass plumbing fittings** — used as inlet and outlet ports drilled into the new head
- **Gasket material ("gruffle")** — cut to seal the new cylinder head
- **Cast iron flywheel** (4.5 kg, salvaged from an exercise bike) — used as a heavy flywheel
- **Lead / concrete** — suggested as options for adding mass to the original aluminum pulley flywheel
- **Stainless steel straw** — used as a simple TDC-finding tool
- **Wood** — used for the engine base and uprights (decorative/structural)
- **Steam / compressed air** — power supply options for the engine

## Techniques and Methods
- Supergluing the original valve chest to the aluminum plate as a drilling template for bolt holes
- Drilling inlet and outlet ports into the new cylinder head plate
- Cutting a custom gasket from sheet material
- Lathe work to cut a matching taper (Morse or Jacobs) on the flywheel boss
- Using a stainless steel straw dropped into the cylinder to locate top dead center visually
- Testing on compressed air before running on steam to check fit and valve requirements

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Compressor source and price:** The compressor is a Vevor twin-cylinder unit; UK price was £15. He provides a direct product link.
- **Valve timing angle:** Clarifies the valve timing is 180 degrees.
- **Why not a Stirling engine:** He considered it, but the two cylinders are cast in the same body, making hot/cold separation difficult. He considered sawing a gap but thought it too much work. If he finds a compressor with separate cylinders, he would make a Stirling.
- **Double expansion (compound steam):** Agrees it wouldn't work here because both cylinders have the same displacement.
- **Solenoid valves / longevity:** Not concerned about longevity — solenoids are used in car engines (e.g., Free Valve, diesel injectors) all the time.
- **Bump valves:** Confirms these were covered in a previous valves video.
- **Steam vs. petrol safety:** Notes that steam worries people, but he finds petrol far more dangerous.
- **Cylinder insulation:** Has seen cylinders insulated with wood, but suspects efficiency gains are negligible.
- **Running medium undecided:** May run on steam first, then switch to compressed air if steam causes issues.
- **Lathe work:** Has an adjustable cross slide on his lathe, which made the taper easy to cut.
- **Flash steam:** Expressed enthusiasm for flash steam as a concept.
