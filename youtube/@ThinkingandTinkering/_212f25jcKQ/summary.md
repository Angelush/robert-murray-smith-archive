## Overview
Robert designs a safer DIY alcohol burner after seeing a BBC News story about someone burning down their flat using plant pot heaters. The core safety improvement is keeping the liquid fuel reservoir sealed inside a tin, unexposed to the flame — with only wicks protruding through copper tube fittings in the lid. He emphasises that no burner is truly "safe," but this design is harder to knock over and reduces the risk of a large fuel spill catching fire.

## Key Topics
- Motivation: BBC News story of a flat fire caused by improvised plant pot heaters
- Two-part heater design: the burner unit vs. the enclosure it sits in
- Design principles for safer burners (wide/low profile, enclosed fuel reservoir)
- Step-by-step construction of the tin-and-copper-tube burner
- Wick material options
- The necessity of an enclosure for any open-flame burner

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Stainless steel tin with a closing lid** — used as the fuel reservoir body
- **Microbore copper tubing (8mm OD)** — cut into 2cm sections to form the burner heads; also available in 10, 15, and 22mm diameters
- **8mm compression olives** — used to create a sealed join between the copper tubes and the tin lid
- **Carbon felt** — Robert's preferred wick material, sourced from battery research supplies
- **Wick alternatives** — torn jeans, bandage strips, Kevlar wick
- **Methanol** — implied fuel (noted in author comment as producing fumes; replacement recommended)

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting copper tubing to length with a pipe/tube cutter
- Flaring tube ends using a lathe (or drill) with a screwdriver inserted into the spinning tube
- Flattening flanges against a steel block with an 8mm hole (used as a forming die)
- Drilling holes in the tin lid in two passes (4mm pilot, then 8mm) to avoid tearing the metal
- Seating copper tubes flange-down through the lid holes and crimping with olives by hammering
- Threading wick material up through each copper tube
