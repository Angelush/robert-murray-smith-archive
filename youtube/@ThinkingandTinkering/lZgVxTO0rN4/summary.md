## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith argues that the axle — inherited from ancient wooden rollers — has been an unnecessary constraint on electric motor and generator design. With brushless DC motors, electronic switching divorces timing from physical rotation, making the axle redundant. This opens the door to hubless (axle-less) motors and generators, particularly "generation at the rim," which Robert presents as one of the most exciting near-future developments in power-dense electrical machines.

## Key Topics
- The historical legacy of the axle, traced from wooden rollers to wheels to electric motors
- How brushed DC motors work (commutator, copper rings, mechanical switching)
- How brushless DC motors differ (electronic switching via power electronics)
- Why brushless motors are more efficient and higher-speed than brushed
- Why brushless design makes the axle physically unnecessary
- Hubless / axle-less motor and generator concepts
- "Generation at the rim" — voltage increases with distance from the axle (EMF = BLV sinθ)
- Benefits of rim-based generation: lighter, more efficient, higher power density, higher voltage output
- Robert's previously built hubless generator (gear-driven, thrust-bearing mounted)
- Teaser for an upcoming wind-based hubless generator build

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Steel (used in rotor and stator construction)
- Copper (used in rotor windings, stator windings, and commutator rings)

## Techniques and Methods
- Brushed DC motor commutation (mechanical switching via commutator and carbon brushes)
- Brushless DC motor commutation (electronic switching)
- Hubless / axle-less motor construction
- Rim-based generation (locating the generating coils at the outer rim to maximise velocity and thus EMF)
- Thrust bearing support for a rotating hubless assembly (no central axle)
- Gear-driven energy input at the centre with generation at the rim
- Tickler coil in stator windings to switch a transistor (mentioned in comments as a prior build)
- Reed switch commutation (referenced in comments)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Terminology**: Robert is relaxed about the various names (hubless, axle-less, rim-based) and says he quite likes "rim based motors or generators."
- **Prior builds**: He confirms he has already built related devices — one using a tickler coil in the stator coils to switch a transistor, and another referencing a reed switch for commutation.
- **Gravity generator**: He mentions he is making a model to demonstrate his idea of a "gravity generator" using this rim-based concept, and notes you don't necessarily need to raise the whole generator.
- **Hub motors in EVs**: He notes he recently looked at a production electric vehicle with four hub motors, showing the concept is entering mainstream use.
- **Thinking differently**: He appreciates commenters who approach problems from unexpected angles, and flags that people "thinking down a track" is one of the biggest obstacles to innovation.
- **Scepticism about hype**: He expresses scepticism toward whatever "latest reported magic" is capturing public imagination (likely a reference to over-hyped propulsion concepts), suggesting people act positively rather than wait for unproven technology.
- **Combining ideas**: He notes it is possible to combine hubless/rim-generation concepts with other approaches.
