## Overview
Robert salvages a discarded tower fan from a neighbour's rubbish, extracts its squirrel cage rotor, and mounts a 775 DC motor onto it to create a simple vertical axis wind generator. The build is completed in roughly an hour using minimal tools and off-the-shelf parts, demonstrating that useful wind generation hardware can be assembled from rescued consumer electronics. Robert's primary goal is to show that the concept is approachable and accessible — a proof-of-concept to inspire others to experiment.

## Key Topics
- "Skip magpie" philosophy: salvaging discarded appliances as parts sources
- Identifying the squirrel cage rotor as a ready-made vertical wind turbine blade assembly
- Why the original induction motor cannot be reused as a generator (no permanent magnets)
- Coupling a 775 DC motor to the squirrel cage rotor via a custom adapter bar
- Mounting the assembly on a builder's board structure
- Testing the turbine in light wind outdoors
- Potential improvements and deployment configurations (wall-mounted, pole-mounted)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Tower fan (discarded)** — the primary source of parts
- **Squirrel cage rotor** — extracted from the fan, used as the vertical turbine blade assembly
- **775 DC motor** — coupled to the rotor shaft to act as the generator
- **Metal bar (rod stock)** — machined into a coupling adapter between rotor and motor shafts
- **Bearings** — extracted from the original induction motor, reused on the adapter shaft
- **Builder's board (uPVC-coated foam / roofline board)** — used for the mounting structure; cut with a carpet knife or handsaw
- **Super glue / Krazy Glue** — used to bond builder's board components
- **Capacitor, synchronous motor, on/off switch, selector switch, clockwork timer** — other salvaged components noted for future projects

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly of consumer fan using standard and security screwdrivers
- Turning down a metal rod on a lathe to create a stepped coupling adapter
- Pressing/fitting salvaged bearings onto the adapter shaft
- Cutting and bonding builder's board with superglue to form a support frame
- Hand-testing the assembled turbine in ambient outdoor wind to confirm rotation and power generation

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert's comments are mostly brief acknowledgements, but several substantive points emerge:

- **Proof of concept, not a finished product:** He repeatedly emphasises these are proofs of concept intended to "stimulate creativity in people who do things rather than just comment on them." The goal is accessibility — showing it is easy enough that people will actually try it.
- **Motor spec:** Confirms it is a **775 motor**, the type found in portable drills, and notes he has been thinking about adding ducting to improve performance.
- **Alternative construction option:** Acknowledges that rather than bolting on an external DC motor, one could drill holes in the original rotor and epoxy in neodymium magnets to make it a direct generator — he chose the easier bolt-on route.
- **Slat idea:** Mentions he was "thinking slats" (as blade modifications) in response to improvement suggestions.
- **Builder's board clarification:** Identifies it as "roofline" board used for soffits and fascias, available from builders' supply stores in the UK.
- **Scale of output:** Honest that the lashed-together version could probably "light an LED or two" — the real value is as a fun project and educational starting point for kids or science fairs.
- **Company name:** Mentions his company is **First Working Generation Ltd**.
- **Bonus material from comments:** Notes that borax can be used to make a diode, and that porous carbon in a hydrogen system could provide hydrogen storage — tangential but characteristic of his wide-ranging thinking.
