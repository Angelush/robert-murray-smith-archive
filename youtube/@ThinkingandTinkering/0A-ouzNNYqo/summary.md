## Overview
Robert demonstrates two DIY approaches to generating electricity without moving parts: a magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) generator using ceramic magnets and copper electrodes fed with an ion-seeded flame, and an electrohydrodynamic (EHD) variant using an electrostatic high-voltage field in place of magnets. Both methods exploit the separation of ions and electrons in a conductive fluid (plasma) passing through a field, echoing Faraday's original experiments on the River Thames. The EHD version proves surprisingly effective, producing ~2 V and ~10 mA under load.

## Key Topics
- Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction and the motor/generator duality
- History of MHD: Faraday's 1832 Thames experiment and the 1851 measurement of current across the river
- The Japanese MHD ship *Yamato 1* (1990s) as a real-world application
- Construction of a DIY MHD generator from reclaimed ceramic magnets and copper wire
- Ion seeding of a plasma flame to boost generation
- Substituting a magnetic field with an electrostatic (high-voltage) field as an EHD generator
- Thermionic emission as a baseline effect vs. field-enhanced ion separation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Ceramic magnets** — reclaimed from hospital water-demineralisation pipe fittings (2 cm × 5 cm × 1 cm)
- **Steel plates** — 125 mm square, ~1 mm thick, used as magnetic yokes
- **Copper wire** — 18 SWG / 2.5 mm² cross-section, bent into triangular electrode shapes
- **Tungsten / Nichrome (Kanthal/kiln wire)** — mentioned as preferred electrode materials for high-temperature use
- **Potassium nitrate solution** — saturated solution used to seed the plasma with potassium ions
- **Super glue** — used to fix magnets to steel plates
- **Salty water** — mentioned as an alternative conductive fluid
- **Plasma** — produced by a blowtorch as the working conductive medium

## Techniques and Methods
- **MHD generator construction**: stacking ceramic magnets on opposing steel plates with unlike poles facing each other to create a transverse magnetic field; inserting bent copper electrodes at 90° to the field
- **Ion seeding**: atomising a saturated potassium nitrate solution through a glass tube and zipper wick into the flame to increase ion density and boost output voltage
- **Plasma generation**: using a blowtorch to create a low-grade plasma as the conductive working fluid
- **EHD (electrostatic) generation**: replacing the magnet assembly with two steel plates connected to a 20 kV DC high-voltage generator (12 V input), forming an electrostatic field through which the flame passes
- **Load testing**: inserting a 220 Ω resistor in series to measure real power output under load
