## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates wireless energy transfer between two homemade Tesla bifilar pancake coils, building on a previous "jewel thief" circuit experiment. By bringing the second coil (with an LED attached) into proximity with the powered first coil, he shows that inductive coupling causes the LED to light up without any direct electrical connection. He acknowledges the efficiency is likely poor but is captivated by the principle itself.

## Key Topics
- Wireless energy transfer via inductive coupling between two pancake coils
- Follow-up to a previous "jewel thief" circuit experiment
- Distance-dependent coupling behaviour (LED dims and extinguishes as coils separate)
- The practical relevance of this principle to wireless phone charging

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- None mentioned.

## Techniques and Methods
- Winding a bifilar pancake coil (two identical coils constructed)
- Jewel thief circuit configuration on the primary coil
- Twisting inner and outer wires of two coils together to form the secondary receiver
- Attaching an LED to the open ends of the secondary coil to visualise energy transfer
- Powered by a 1.5 V battery

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert clarifies in the comments that the effect is indeed inductive (Q-coupled) rather than anything mysterious, but pushes back on cynicism — framing curiosity and wonder as essential to experimentation. He notes the efficiency is probably terrible, yet points out that phone companies took a long time to commercialise this same simple principle for wireless charging. He also mentions prior related work (magnets on a Savonius rotor with static coils) and confirms he had been developing this wireless energy transfer approach for some time before posting the video.
