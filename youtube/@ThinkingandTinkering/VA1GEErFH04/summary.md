## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith traces the history of permanent magnets from iron and steel through ferrite, alnico, samarium-cobalt, and finally neodymium (NdFeB) magnets, explaining the three key properties any magnet must have. He then introduces iron nitride (Fe₁₆N₂) — a material first noted in 1951 but nearly forgotten — as a potentially revolutionary replacement: cheaper, rare-earth-free, easier to handle unmagnetized, and capable of delivering 2.5–3× more power per kilogram of magnet in motors and generators.

## Key Topics
- History of permanent magnets: iron, steel, ferrite, alnico, samarium-cobalt, neodymium
- The three critical magnet properties: magnetic saturation, coercivity, and Curie point (temperature stability)
- Problems with neodymium magnets: rare-earth cost, dysprosium dependency, Chinese supply dominance, difficulty handling pre-magnetized
- Iron nitride (Fe₁₆N₂) rediscovery and the University of Minnesota research program
- Niron Magnetics: commercializing iron nitride magnets (Gen 1 and Gen 2 releases)
- Performance advantages for DIY motors and generators

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Iron (Fe)** — base material for early magnets and iron nitride; 4th most abundant element, ~5% of Earth's crust
- **Steel** — replaced iron for magnets from ~1916
- **Ferrite** — first produced 1930; easily magnetized in situ
- **Alnico (aluminium-nickel-cobalt alloy)** — introduced 1931; high saturation but very low coercivity; used in switched-reluctance motors and magnetic chokes
- **Samarium-cobalt** — introduced 1965; rare-earth based
- **Neodymium (Nd)** — key rare-earth element in NdFeB magnets (~$120/kg of magnet)
- **Dysprosium (Dy)** — added to NdFeB for temperature stability; costs ~4× the neodymium content
- **Iron nitride / Fe₁₆N₂** — the proposed replacement; made from iron and nitrogen only
- **Ammonium nitrate** — used as nitrogen source in ball-milling synthesis of iron nitride
- **Ammonia** — used in the oxidized-iron-sheet synthesis route (reacts with iron oxide to implant nitrogen)
- **Nitrogen (N₂)** — 70% of the atmosphere; the nitrogen source in iron nitride

## Techniques and Methods
- **Magnetization by hammering in an electric field** — historical method for making iron/steel magnets
- **Electromagnet saturation** — standard method for magnetizing modern permanent magnets
- **Ion implantation (nitrogen ions)** — firing nitrogen ions at clean iron sheet at Los Alamos to produce Fe₁₆N₂ (proof-of-concept, not scalable)
- **Mechanochemical synthesis / ball milling** — tumbling iron powder with ammonium nitrate for ~2 days to produce iron nitride powder in bulk
- **Ammonia-reduction of oxidized iron** — oxidizing iron sheets then exposing to ammonia stream; nitrogen is implanted via acid-base reaction; produces sheets, wires, or sinterable powders
