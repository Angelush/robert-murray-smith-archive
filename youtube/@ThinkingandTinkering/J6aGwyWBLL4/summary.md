## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple, homemade memristor built using graphene oxide as the active layer sandwiched between two aluminium electrodes. By reversing the polarity of a 12V battery connected to the device, he shows a measurable and stable resistance change of ~30 ohms, illustrating the core memristive property of resistance memory. He presents this as a proof-of-concept "paintable" memristor ink system worth further exploration.

## Key Topics
- What a memristor is: a device whose resistance changes and is retained based on applied current/polarity
- Construction of a basic memristor using graphene oxide
- Demonstration of resistance switching (~193 ohms vs ~160 ohms) by reversing battery polarity
- The role of the aluminium electrode shorting configuration to prevent capacitive behaviour
- Graphene oxide as a candidate material for paintable/inkable memristors

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphene oxide** — used as the active resistive switching layer, painted onto aluminium and allowed to dry
- **Aluminium** — used as both electrode layers (top and bottom plates)
- **Polypropylene** — used as the substrate/insulating base for the device

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting/coating graphene oxide onto aluminium foil and allowing it to air dry to form the active layer
- Sandwiching the graphene oxide layer between two aluminium electrodes to form an Al/GO/Al stack
- Shorting the two aluminium plates together to eliminate capacitive charge storage effects
- Connecting a multimeter across the device to measure resistance in real time
- Reversing a 12V battery's polarity to switch the memristor between resistance states
