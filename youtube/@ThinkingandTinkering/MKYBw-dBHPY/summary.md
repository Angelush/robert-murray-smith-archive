## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explains how the quality of graphene produced is fundamentally determined by two factors: the quality of the graphite starting material and the production method chosen. He surveys the main methods available — from chemical vapor deposition to the Hummers method — comparing their accessibility, outputs, and suitability for different applications such as supercapacitors.

## Key Topics
- Relationship between graphite and graphene (graphene as single sheets stacked in graphite)
- How graphite flake size affects electrical conductivity of the final graphene product
- Sources of graphite: block, natural powder, synthetic powder, and graphite flake
- Cost and sourcing of high-quality graphite flake
- Top-down vs. bottom-up graphene production approaches
- Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD) — bottom-up method and its limitations (grain boundary faults)
- Hummers method — produces crumpled graphene oxide sheets
- Light scribe reduction of graphene oxide for supercapacitor electrodes
- Graphene Intercalation Compound (GIC) method — intercalating and then exploding chemicals between graphite layers
- Electrochemical exfoliation method
- Sonochemical (ultrasonic) exfoliation method
- Post-processing intercalation with ammonia and ferric chloride to improve conductivity

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite block** — art-shop grade, used as a demonstration of bulk graphite
- **Natural graphite powder** — ground rock form, various grades
- **Synthetic graphite powder** — fine powder form
- **Graphite flake** — preferred starting material; larger flakes yield larger graphene sheets and better conductivity
- **RS International graphite flake** — a mining company selling relatively affordable, high-quality graphite flake (website: `rightdowngraphite.com`)
- **Sigma-Aldrich graphite** — previously one of few suppliers; noted as very expensive
- **Carbon precursor gas** — used in CVD as the carbon source
- **Sulfuric acid (~30% concentration)** — used as electrolyte in the electrochemical method
- **Ammonia** — used for re-intercalation of graphene oxide to improve conductivity
- **Ferric chloride** — used for re-intercalation of graphene oxide to improve conductivity

## Techniques and Methods
- **Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)** — bottom-up method; carbon precursor gas passed over a heated catalyst; limited by grain boundary faults at domain joins
- **Hummers method** — top-down chemical oxidation of graphite to produce graphene oxide; results in crumpled rather than flat sheets
- **Light scribe reduction** — laser reduction of graphene oxide on disc substrates; produces porous, high-surface-area structures well-suited for supercapacitors
- **Graphene Intercalation Compound (GIC) method** — intercalating chemicals between graphite layers then rapidly expanding/exploding them to exfoliate graphene sheets
- **Electrochemical exfoliation** — graphite block in ~30% sulfuric acid electrolyte with +10V DC applied to peel off graphene flakes
- **Sonochemical (ultrasonic) exfoliation** — using a high-power sonicator (bath sonicator insufficient) to mechanically shake apart graphite into graphene sheets
- **Post-synthesis intercalation** — re-intercalating reduced graphene oxide with ammonia or ferric chloride to enhance conductivity

## Robert's Own Replies
Several substantive clarifications appear in the comments:

- **Graphite oxide vs. graphene oxide distinction**: Robert clarifies that the difference mirrors graphite vs. graphene — it is purely about layer count. Graphene oxide is one layer; graphite oxide is multilayer. If you obtain graphite oxide, you must dissolve and sonicate it down to graphene oxide before use.
- **GICs for supercapacitors**: He notes that GICs are not ideal for supercapacitors because they lack sufficient surface area compared even to activated carbon; supercaps require conductivity, large surface area, and pseudocapacitance together.
- **Graphene in composites**: A 3% addition of exfoliated graphite into resin yields approximately a 30% increase in strength.
- **Graphene composite fabrication technique**: He recommends impregnating a lightweight carrier layer with graphene oxide, baking it to reduce the oxide to graphene, and using this as an interlayer between sandwich composite layers; fibreglass substrate is suggested.
- **Reducing graphene oxide at home**: He simply leaves it on a radiator or in sunlight, and suggests UV light would also work.
- **Exfoliation method selection**: For users wanting to produce graphene for specific applications, he recommends searching Google Scholar for exfoliation methods, noting several good ones exist depending on intended use.
- **HTC / hydrothermal carbonisation**: He acknowledges having done work on HTC (specifically the Tang Lau method) and made a video on it.
- **"Green graphene"**: He references a video he made under that title.
