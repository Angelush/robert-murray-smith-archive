## Overview
Robert demonstrates a high-voltage discharge apparatus built from salvaged microwave oven components, using it to explosively vaporize small quantities of water, a tomato, and thin wire. The key takeaway is that a spark gap acting as a switch dumps capacitor energy near-instantaneously into a small brass chamber, causing anything inside to explode — and when submerged in water, the setup produces plasma electrolysis, a fundamentally different process from conventional battery-driven electrolysis.

## Key Topics
- Building a high-voltage discharge rig from microwave oven parts
- The role of the spark gap as a switch for instantaneous energy dump
- Exploding water, a tomato, and thin wire using stored capacitor energy
- Plasma electrolysis vs. conventional electrolysis
- Safety considerations (remote switch, containment can)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — the primary subject; exploded in the brass chamber and used as the medium for plasma electrolysis
- **Tomato** — used as a projectile/plug in a demonstration; blew the spark plug out of the chamber
- **Thin wire** — vaporized between two nails to demonstrate explosive wire discharge
- **Tin/cook can** — used as a containment shield after a prior experiment destroyed the setup

## Techniques and Methods
- **Capacitor bank discharge** — four microwave oven capacitors wired in parallel, charged via a MOT (Microwave Oven Transformer), then discharged through a spark gap
- **Spark gap switching** — spark plug used as a voltage-triggered switch to dump energy instantaneously into the chamber
- **Explosive wire vaporization** — thin wire bridged across two nails and connected to the spark gap circuit
- **Plasma electrolysis** — apparatus fully submerged in water, producing rapid localized plasma discharges rather than slow electrochemical decomposition
