## Overview
Robert demonstrates how to upcycle a glass bottle into a vase or pen pot by cutting it using a thermal stress technique involving ink and a microwave. The cut edge is then smoothed with wet/dry sandpaper, and optionally coated with plaster dip for decoration and safety. The process requires minimal materials and yields a clean, usable result in a couple of attempts.

## Key Topics
- Upcycling glass bottles/jars into functional containers
- Thermal stress glass cutting using ink and a microwave
- Using masking tape to achieve a straight cut line
- Completing a partial break with pliers
- Wet sanding cut glass edges smooth
- Optional plaster dipping for aesthetics and edge safety

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Glass bottle or glass jar** — the workpiece to be cut
- **Ink** — applied in a line around the bottle; absorbs microwave energy to create thermal stress and fracture the glass
- **Masking tape / painters tape** — used to guide a straight ink line and to mask the plaster dip boundary
- **Wet/dry sandpaper** — 80 grit, 180 grit, and 320 grit (P1200) used to smooth the cut edge
- **Plaster dip** — optional coating applied to the top rim for decoration and to cover any remaining sharp edges
- **Water** — used with the wet/dry sandpaper during sanding

## Techniques and Methods
- **Masking tape line guide** — wrapping tape around the bottle to mark a straight cut path
- **Ink thermal fracture** — painting a continuous ink line and microwaving on medium-to-low for 10–20 seconds to induce a stress fracture along the line
- **Pliers-assisted break** — lightly gripping any incompletely separated section and pulling to complete the clean break
- **Wet circular sanding** — sanding the cut rim in circular motions through progressively finer grits (starting at 320, finishing with 80) with water for approximately 15–20 minutes
- **Plaster dipping** — dipping the top of the bottle into plaster dip up to a tape-masked line, then removing the tape to leave a clean decorative lip
