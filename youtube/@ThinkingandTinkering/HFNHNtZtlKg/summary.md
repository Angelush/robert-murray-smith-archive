## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple refracting telescope using two water-filled lenses made from plastic sheeting (builders board / uPVC foam core), water, and acrylic gel tape — continuing from his earlier video 1834 on making water lenses. The finished telescope achieves approximately 2× magnification and is assembled inside sections of carpet tube for a total cost of around £2, proving the concept is viable.

## Key Topics
- Recap of water lens construction from video 1834
- Building two water-filled lenses with different focal lengths (100 mm and 200 mm)
- Calculating expected magnification from focal length ratio
- Assembling a sliding telescope tube from cardboard carpet tube
- Evaluating optical clarity of water lenses

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Water** — used as the optical medium inside the lenses
- **Builders board (uPVC foam core fascia/soffit)** — used as lens holders/mounts; UK equivalent is Eurocell product, US equivalent is Sintra board
- **Acrylic gel tape** — used to seal the edges of the lens sandwiches; commonly used for mobile phone repair
- **Black sticky plastic** — used as an aperture mask on the lens mount
- **Carpet tube (cardboard)** — used as the telescope barrel, with a narrower inner section for focusing

## Techniques and Methods
- **Water lens fabrication** — blowing a concave shape into plastic sheeting, sealing edges with acrylic gel tape to create a water-fillable cavity
- **Syringe filling** — injecting water into the lens via a straw/blunt needle syringe, relying on space around the straw to allow air to escape
- **Focal length measurement** — testing each filled lens to determine its focal length before assembly
- **Sliding tube focusing** — slitting and squeezing one carpet tube section to fit inside another, allowing the eyepiece to slide for focus adjustment
- **Proof-of-concept (POC) approach** — quick, cheap construction to test feasibility before refining

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Material clarification (repeated multiple times):** The board used is "uPVC foam core fascia and soffit" — the original product is from Eurocell; the US equivalent is Sintra board.
- **Filling method:** He filled the lenses using a syringe with a blunt needle; there is enough space around the straw for air to escape.
- **Hydrogel as an alternative:** Robert doubts hydrogel would work well optically, recalling it as "optically quite poor."
- **Laptop Fresnel lenses:** He clarifies these would not work as substitutes — they are cut to produce parallel lines of light with effectively infinite focus.
- **Concave vs. convex lens construction:** The same construction method works for concave lenses by flipping the surface; a thicker plastic sheet would be needed to resist water pressure.
- **POC philosophy:** He acknowledges a ¼ objective would have been better but explains he builds things quickly and cheaply first just to prove the concept, then refines afterward.
- **Ideal for kids:** He agrees with a commenter that this project would be great for children.
- **New camera:** Several comments about adjusting to a new camera affecting video quality.
