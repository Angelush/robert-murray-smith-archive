## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the concept of a swash plate-based continuously variable transmission (CVT) that operates without gears, belts, or friction drives. He designs, 3D prints, and demonstrates a working prototype inspired by a mechanism found in a public-domain engineering book, showing how the swash plate's angle can be varied to achieve a continuously variable gear ratio. The video concludes that while the prototype is a proof of concept with room for refinement, it demonstrates a legitimate non-friction CVT principle.

## Key Topics
- Robert's motivation for CVT research: optimising small wind turbines (e.g. the Harmony wind generator)
- Overview of common CVT types: disc CVT, belt CVT, ratchet/lever CVTs, and their shared reliance on friction
- How a swash plate works — converting rotary motion to linear motion via a cam-like push action, not friction
- Design and 3D printing of a swash plate CVT prototype in Tinkercad
- How gear ratio is varied by adjusting the effective position/angle of the swash plate
- Limitations of the prototype and potential improvements (replacing the roller with a sphere)

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (FDM) of prototype parts using Tinkercad designs, published to Thingiverse
- Swash plate mechanism: rotating angled disc drives a roller/pin downward (push-contact, not friction)
- Universal joint to capture and transmit the central rotation output
- Spring-loaded roller assembly with cradle, pin, clip, and gear
- Ratio adjustment via a sliding selector that changes the swash plate angle
- Proposed improvement: replacing cylindrical roller with a sphere to maintain contact through angle changes

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Clarifying the non-friction principle:** Robert repeatedly pushed back against commenters who claimed the device is friction-based, directing them to research swash plates. He clarified: *"it transfers power by pushing down — like your finger on a button"* and acknowledged there is inevitably some incidental friction, but that it is not a friction *drive*.
- **CVT ratio mechanism:** He explained *"think of it as a disk CVT — the distance of centre to roller definitely matters"* and noted the cone's role is mainly as a guide.
- **Neutral/zero output:** When asked how to achieve zero output (neutral), he suggested moving the roller to the centre, admitting it's something he'd need to work out.
- **Related video:** He repeatedly pointed commenters to another video (`https://youtu.be/0FwypgRLSZQ`) as further relevant context.
- **eCVT mention:** He noted there is an electric alternative called the eCVT.
- **NuVinci correction:** He explicitly stated *"the NuVinci is a friction drive"*, distinguishing his design from it.
- **Philosophy:** *"The joy is not the answer but the search for the answer."*
