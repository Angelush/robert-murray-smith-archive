## Overview
Robert visits the Centre for Alternative Technology (CAT) in Machynlleth, Wales — an alternative living demonstration site founded around 1973 and open to the public from 1976. He explores its three-pillar approach to sustainable living: food production, energy generation/storage, and eco-building. His main takeaway is that zero-impact living is broader than he had appreciated, encompassing food and building practices alongside the energy technologies he normally focuses on.

## Key Topics
- History and context of CAT — founded during the 1970s oil crisis era, alongside the self-sufficiency movement
- CAT's philosophy: existing technologies are sufficient for zero-impact living; the barrier is attitude and adoption, not invention
- Food production using upcycled containers (tyres, tin baths, sinks, buckets) and biological pest control
- The water-balance funicular railway as CAT's main entrance — a gravity-powered people mover
- Sustainable building materials and techniques (e.g. straw bale, hemp, pacters)
- Zeolite-based passive heating and cooling systems
- Robert's personal reflection on broadening his view of alternative technology beyond energy generation

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Zeolite clay** — used as a passive heating/cooling medium; absorbs moisture and releases heat, releases moisture and produces cooling; estimated that two washing-machine loads could heat/cool a house
- **Hemp** — mentioned as an alternative building material ("hemp barrels")
- **Straw** — referenced for straw-bale building construction
- **Pacters** — mentioned alongside hemp and straw as alternative building materials

## Techniques and Methods
- **Container/pot growing** — food crops grown in upcycled containers (stacked tyres, tin baths, waste bins, old sinks) to enable urban/small-space food production
- **Biological pest control** — used at CAT as an alternative to pesticides
- **Water-balance funicular railway** — gravity-powered cable car using differential water weight rather than a motor
- **Zeolite passive HVAC** — fins sunk into zeolite clay connected to the building envelope; solar heat in summer dries clay (cooling effect); autumn/winter humidity absorption releases stored heat
- **Straw bale, hemp, and pacter construction** — low-carbon alternative building methods highlighted at CAT
