## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a simple water-filled magnifying glass lens inspired by Josh Silver's self-adjusting eyeglasses, which use water pressure to change focal length. Using uPVC builders board (Cetraboard in the US), double-sided acrylic gel tape, and a drinking straw as a fill port, he assembles a biconvex lens chamber in just a minute or two. The result is a surprisingly effective, glass-like magnifying lens that can be made cheaply and quickly at home.

## Key Topics
- Josh Silver's water-filled self-adjusting eyeglasses as the original inspiration
- Making water lenses (referencing his earlier video #1384)
- Constructing a biconvex water lens magnifying glass from everyday materials
- The versatility of the design (varying size, curvature, and shape)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Builders board (uPVC with foam core)** — structural frame for the lens; sold as "Cetraboard" in the US
- **Double-sided acrylic gel tape** — used to seal the two lens halves together; sourced from mobile phone repair supplies
- **Super glue** — mentioned as compatible adhesive for builders board
- **Water** — the optical medium filling the lens cavity
- **Drinking straw** — used as the fill port to inject water into the sealed lens

## Techniques and Methods
- Cutting a circular lens hole in builders board
- Applying double-sided tape around the lens perimeter with a small gap left open
- Sandwiching two convex lens pieces together to form a biconvex chamber
- Using a drinking straw inserted in the gap to fill the chamber with water
- Sealing the finished lens once filled
