## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith tests an Archimedes screw water turbine he built from builders' plastic in a small stream near his home, after previously being unable to test it due to drought conditions. The device produces 5.1 volts, which he considers a clear success. He highlights its potential as an ultra-cheap emergency power supply, noting it is a replication of a Japanese invention.

## Key Topics
- Field test of a homemade Archimedes screw water turbine in a real stream
- Output voltage measurement (5.1V) and assessment of performance
- Low-cost manufacturing potential (estimated £1–2 for the casing)
- Use case as an emergency power supply
- The Japanese origin of the design concept
- Large-scale Archimedes screw installations (e.g., an ~80kW installation on the Thames)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Builders' plastic (used to construct the screw/casing)
- CD discs (mentioned in comments as material used for a related component)
- HDPE (suggested in comments as a preferred alternative plastic for vacuum-forming)

## Techniques and Methods
- Manual fabrication of the Archimedes screw from builders' plastic
- Vacuum moulding, injection moulding, and blow moulding discussed as scalable manufacturing alternatives
- Damming the stream with a pile of sticks to create a small reservoir/energy store
- Direct voltage measurement during stream test

- **Enclosure:** States his reading suggests there is no real improvement in performance whether the screw is enclosed or not.
