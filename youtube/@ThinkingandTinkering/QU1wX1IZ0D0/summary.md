## Overview
Robert assembles and tests a 3D-printed Darwin wind turbine, replicating and building on work by community member Max Kennedy. He confirms the turbine captures and channels airflow, then demonstrates it generating enough electricity to light an LED — crucially, the turbine works in all three orientations (upright, sideways, and upside down). This orientation-independence sparks a key new idea: placing a lightweight collection structure at height and piping the air down to a ground-level generator.

## Key Topics
- Assembly of a stacked Darwin wind turbine from 3D-printed PLA parts
- Airflow visualisation using coloured "dreadlock" streamers
- Power generation test using a PC fan converted to a generator and an LED indicator
- Discovery that the turbine works in all orientations (upright, on its side, inverted)
- Conceptual proposal: capturing wind at height via a lightweight pipe structure and generating at ground level
- Open collaborative exploration — inviting community members to test fin geometry, gap size, and Venturi additions

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **PLA (polylactic acid)** — 3D-printing filament used for all turbine parts; noted as unsuitable for a real-world build
- **Cyanoacrylate (crazy glue)** — used to bond PLA parts into a rigid tower
- **PC fan (converted to generator)** — repurposed as the electrical generator in the test
- **LED** — used as a load indicator to confirm electricity generation
- **Coloured streamers ("dreadlocks")** — purple yarn/string used to visualise airflow through the turbine

## Techniques and Methods
- **Fused deposition modelling (FDM) 3D printing** — printing individual turbine sections in PLA
- **Adhesive assembly** — gluing printed sections into a stacked tower with cyanoacrylate
- **Flow visualisation** — attaching flexible streamers around the turbine perimeter to observe airflow direction and velocity
- **Bench-top generation test** — connecting a fan-converted-generator to an LED to confirm electrical output
- **Multi-orientation testing** — repeating the generation test with the turbine upright, sideways, and inverted

## Robert's Own Replies
- **PLA is test-only:** Explicitly warns it is not suitable for a real installation ("don't use PLA for the real one lol").
- **Fins were left straight deliberately:** The 45° straight fins were uncured/uncurved on purpose for this test; he acknowledges curved fins would improve airflow and not impede it, but aren't essential for a positive result.
- **Fins likely need to extend outward more:** He believes the fins need larger edge projections — this is supported by the streamers still moving at the perimeter, suggesting incomplete capture.
- **Gap size is unknown:** He admits the current gap between sections may not be optimal and encourages experimentation.
- **Venturi effect is promising:** Multiple community suggestions about adding a Venturi were met with genuine interest ("I don't know — but I like the Venturi idea"; "maybe just a Venturi somewhere on the pipe mate").
- **Air wrapping around the pipe:** He pushed back on one commenter, noting that back-flap movement is caused by air wrapping around the pipe, not the fin angle — larger edge projections will clarify this without guessing.
- **Rain protection:** Acknowledged the need for some kind of rain trap for outdoor use but has no solution yet.
- **FEMM 4.2:** Plans to check whether this free electromagnetic simulation tool could be useful for the project.
- **No voltage/current measurements taken:** Confirms this was a qualitative existence-proof only; comparison figures would require careful setup and assumptions, which he leaves to community members.
