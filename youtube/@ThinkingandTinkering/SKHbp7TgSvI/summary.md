## Overview
In Part 5 of his DIY gasification and generator series, Robert Murray-Smith walks through the assembly of his gasifier unit, focusing on the first-stage cyclone filter (built from a repurposed fire extinguisher) and introduces his plans for a second-stage sawdust filter box, following the FEMA book's recommendations. The build is notably frugal — only £12 was spent on a length of pipe, with all other components sourced, found, or repurposed.

## Key Topics
- Assembly of the gasifier barrel unit into a steel cradle
- High-temperature sealant application at all gas-path joints
- First-stage cyclone filtration using a repurposed fire extinguisher
- How cyclone separation works (spiraling gas throws heavy particles to the walls)
- The condensate trap (glass jar screwed onto the bottom of the cyclone)
- Plans for the second-stage filter: a metal box with a baffle plate and sawdust bed
- FEMA book as the design reference for the overall system

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel barrel** — main gasifier vessel
- **High-temperature sealant** — applied at all panel joints and the door to prevent gas leaks
- **Fire extinguisher** — repurposed as the cyclone filter body
- **Aluminium** — used for the trap fitting, welded with aluminium welding rod
- **Glass jar** — used as the condensate trap below the cyclone
- **Sawdust** — second-stage filter medium inside the metal box; noted as recyclable back into the gasifier
- **309 welding rods** — used for welding stainless and mild steel (mentioned in comments)
- **Exhaust wrap** — considered for pipe joints (mentioned in comments)

## Techniques and Methods
- **MIG/stick welding** — used throughout for fabricating the frame, brackets, and fittings (Robert notes he only learned welding ~2 weeks prior)
- **Cyclone separation** — gas enters the fire extinguisher at an angle, spirals down, heavy particles are thrown to the walls and drip into the trap
- **Baffle plate filtration** — second-stage box design forces gas up one side and down the other through a sawdust bed
- **Repurposing/scrounging** — entire build philosophy; fire extinguisher fittings sawed off and re-welded, trampoline frame used as structural cradle
- **Paint and coating removal** — fire extinguisher and angle iron stripped before welding (noted in comments)

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Frame material:** The structural cradle is made from a dismantled trampoline.
- **Welding rods:** He used **309 rods** for welding a mix of stainless and mild steel, and found welding stainless not much different from mild steel. The rods were given to him for free by a friend.
- **New to welding:** He had only learned to weld about two weeks before this video and explicitly welcomed the practice the complex build gave him.
- **Aesthetic intentionality:** He noted other gasifier builds looked "scrappy" and deliberately aimed for a cleaner, well-fitted appearance — while acknowledging his is still made from scrap.
- **Waste heat recovery:** He was actively thinking about wrapping the unit in a copper coil to heat water, capturing otherwise wasted heat.
- **Pipe joint sealing:** Considering exhaust wrap on some pipe joints as an additional measure.
- **System purpose:** The gasifier is the backup power source for when wind doesn't blow, implying he has a wind energy system as primary.
- **Expert disagreement:** He philosophically noted that two experts will often give directly opposed advice, so he defaults to his own judgement — in this case following the FEMA book.
- **Project collaboration:** He politely but firmly declined to work on others' projects, citing time constraints from his own work, correspondence, and content creation.
