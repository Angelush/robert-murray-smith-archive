## Overview
In this short follow-up video, Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates running a Stirling engine using his conductive ink as a resistive heater, inspired by a viewer suggestion. The ink is painted directly onto the bottom plate of the engine, acting as a heating element when connected to a power supply, and proves more efficient than the Kanthal wire used in the previous video due to intimate thermal contact with the plate.

## Key Topics
- Using conductive ink as a resistive heater for a Stirling engine
- Efficiency improvement over Kanthal wire heater (due to direct contact with the heated plate)
- Thermal mass of the base plate sustaining engine operation after power is cut
- Demonstrating a practical application of Robert's own conductive ink product

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Conductive ink** — Robert's own product, used as a painted-on resistive heater on the engine's base plate
- **Copper strips** — used as electrodes at the bottom of the Stirling engine to connect the ink to the power supply
- **Kanthal wire** — mentioned as the heater used in the prior video, less efficient due to air gap from the plate

## Techniques and Methods
- Painting conductive ink directly onto a metal plate to form a resistive heating element
- Connecting the ink layer to a bench power supply via copper strip electrodes
- Observing residual thermal mass behaviour after cutting power to the heater

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Carnot efficiency limits**: Robert explains that most heat engines are only ~37% efficient due to Carnot constraints, making waste heat scavenging (e.g. from a laptop) impractical — only a few percent recoverable in reality.
- **Heater efficiency**: Any resistive heater (including the ink) is around 98–99% efficient at converting electricity to heat.
- **Power draw**: The setup drew around 10 watts; he notes it wasn't especially efficient but found the thermal mass of the base very interesting.
- **Graphene alternative**: Mentioned that graphene could also be used as a resistive heater in a similar setup.
- **Nitinol engines**: Suggested nitinol-based heat engines as a related avenue, noting a toy kit exists on eBay and pointing to his own video on fishing line as an artificial muscle.
- **Zeolite adsorption**: In response to an idea about a self-powering engine, Robert noted that water adsorption onto zeolite could achieve a similar effect.
