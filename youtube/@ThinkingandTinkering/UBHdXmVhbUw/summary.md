## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the surprisingly broad world of gear mechanisms, starting from first principles—three types of motion (rotation, translation, screw) and three spatial relationships between axles—before showcasing ten unusual and "bizarre" gear designs from various inventors and researchers. The video argues that gears are far more than the simple circular toothed wheels most people picture, and that the underlying principles open up a vast design space. Many of the examples are novelties or desk toys, but some have genuine practical applications in robotics, flow measurement, and more.

## Key Topics
- Fundamental principles of gears: three motion types (rotation, translation/sliding, screw) and three axle arrangements (parallel/in-plane, angled/bevel, skewed/worm)
- Geneva mechanism — used historically in film cameras for precise stop-and-go motion
- Bevel gears — used in car differentials; axles meet at an angle (commonly 90°)
- Worm gears — skewed axle arrangement; can provide speed differentials
- Mangle gear — a rack-and-pinion variant for back-and-forth translation
- Cube gear and Heart gear — by Emmert Lish; eight bevel gears with axles meeting at the cube's centre
- Brin gear — follows an octahedron geometry rather than a cube; bevel gears left untruncated
- Fibonacci/nautilus (non-circular) gears — one gear speeds up and slows down relative to the other during rotation
- Oval gears — used in mechanical flow meters; interlocking creates sealed pockets to measure liquid volume
- Half-sphere gears — by Oscar van Deventer; can fold 180° like a hinge while remaining interlocked
- Vennin gear (ACROBAT — Active Ball Joint Mechanism with Three Degrees of Freedom) — spherical gear developed at Yamagata University; used in robotics
- Mangle rack — a pin-based mechanism used by the akiyuki Brick channel in Lego models
- Borromean Rings gear mechanism — three interlocking identical gears in a rectangular arrangement that can still move together
- Henry Segerman's interlocking donut gear system — three inseparable torus shapes driven by a single corkscrew spindle

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing — STL files are available on Thingiverse for several of the gear designs shown (e.g., the Vennin gear)
- Mechanical flow metering — oval gears create sealed pockets whose volume, combined with rotation count, gives accurate liquid volume measurement
- Bevel gear geometry — truncated vs. untruncated forms explored in cube/Brin gear variants
