## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to copper-plate organic keepsakes — specifically leaves and flowers — using conductive ink and a basic electroplating setup. The central lesson is that patience and low current are the keys to success: rushing the process burns off the conductive coating, while running at a few milliamps per square centimetre over 12+ hours produces a beautiful, detailed copper finish.

## Key Topics
- Purpose and appeal of electroplating organic keepsakes (e.g. wedding flowers, pressed leaves)
- Setting up an electroplating tank using glass or plastic beakers
- Using a copper foil surround as the anode for even plating
- Preventing the item from touching the anode using a dishcloth liner
- Making a wire connection to delicate organic stems using stranded copper wire
- Handling greasy organics by pre-coating with varnish
- Applying graphene/graphite conductive ink by dip-coating
- Running the plating at low voltage (2 V) for an extended time (~12–24 hours)
- Teased future videos: plating clothes, baby shoes, leather, bone, and gothic jewellery

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Copper foil** — used as the surrounding anode in the plating tank
- **Stranded uncoated copper wire** — used to attach to and make electrical contact with the organic item
- **Graphene/graphite conductive ink** — applied by dip-coating to make the organic surface electrically conductive
- **Universal electroplating solution** — the electrolyte bath used for copper plating; described as producing a bright finish without additives
- **Varnish** — applied first to greasy organic surfaces to provide a clean base for the conductive ink
- **Plastic and glass beakers** — used as the plating vessel

## Techniques and Methods
- **Dip-coating in conductive ink** — submerging the leaf or flower entirely to achieve a conformal conductive coating without a brush (which could damage delicate structures)
- **Copper foil anode surround** — wrapping copper foil around the inside of the beaker so the item is evenly surrounded, giving more uniform plating than a single flat anode
- **Cloth liner in tank** — placing a dishcloth inside the tank to prevent the organic item from drifting and making contact with the anode
- **Low-voltage, low-current plating** — running at 2 V (a few milliamps per cm²) for ~12+ hours to avoid burning off the conductive ink layer
- **Varnish pre-treatment** — dipping greasy organics in varnish and letting it dry before applying conductive ink
