## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates a simple trick to restore optical clarity to transparent resin 3D prints after support removal leaves surface marks. The method involves scraping the surface clean with a blade (a cabinet-maker's technique), then wiping on a thin coat of the same uncured resin and UV-curing it to fill micro-scratches. He showcases the result by printing a Wollaston prism capable of functioning as a camera lucida drawing aid.

## Key Topics
- The problem of surface marks and loss of optical clarity after removing supports from transparent resin prints
- Blade/scraper technique for levelling surface blemishes (borrowed from cabinet making)
- Applying a thin coat of liquid resin to fill micro-scratches and restore transparency
- UV curing the resin coat to finalise the finish
- The Wollaston prism as a demonstration object and its use in camera lucida drawing aids

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Transparent/clear UV resin (used both for printing and as a surface filler coat)
- Tissue/cloth (used to apply the liquid resin coat)

## Techniques and Methods
- Blade/scraper surface finishing — holding a trimming knife or box-cutter blade flat and scraping along the surface to remove support marks and level the resin in a powdery form (analogous to cabinet-maker's scraper technique)
- Resin wet-coat filling — wiping uncured liquid resin onto the scraped, frosted surface with a tissue to fill micro-scratches
- UV curing — placing the coated part back under a UV cure lamp to harden the surface layer
- Sanding (noted as an alternative to scraping, but produces slightly more scarring; two coats recommended if sanding is used)
