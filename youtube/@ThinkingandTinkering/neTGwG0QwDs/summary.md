## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the challenge of insulating a J-type rocket stove to maintain the heat needed for efficient combustion and to run a Stirling engine. He identifies carbon foam as the best available insulation material and demonstrates how to make it at home using sugar, water, and nitric acid — based on a research paper on "sugar blobbing."

## Key Topics
- The fire triangle and why insulation is critical to rocket stove efficiency
- Carbon foam as the premier insulation material (originally developed by Ford in 1964)
- DIY carbon foam synthesis from sugar via a pyrolysis/carbonization process
- Reticulated vs. non-reticulated (closed-cell) carbon foam
- Using exhaust gas heat for secondary energy extraction (e.g., steam coil) without robbing combustion zone heat

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Sugar** — primary carbon source for foam synthesis
- **Water** — solvent for the sugar syrup
- **Nitric acid** (8 ml per 500g sugar / 500ml water) — foaming/catalytic agent; ammonium chloride or zinc nitrate cited as alternatives
- **Rockwool** — previously used as insulation, noted as non-ideal
- **Carbon foam** (phenolic-resin-derived commercially; sugar-derived DIY) — primary insulation material
- **Nickel salt** — mentioned as an additive to make carbon foam electrically conductive
- **Ammonium chloride / zinc nitrate** — alternative foaming/blowing agents

## Techniques and Methods
- **Sugar blobbing** — dissolving sugar in water, adding nitric acid, then oven-curing to produce carbon foam
- **Two-stage oven curing** — 120°C for ~2 days (caramelization + carbonization), then 220°C for 16 hours
- **Kiln reticulation** — optional 600°C / 3-hour kiln step to open cell structure (reticulated foam)
- **Silicone mold casting** — used because the carbonizing syrup adheres strongly to most surfaces
- **Oxygen injection** — secondary pipe added to the rocket stove combustion zone to complete the fire triangle
- **Exhaust heat extraction** — coil placed in exhaust gas stream to harvest heat without disrupting combustion

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Alternative foaming agents**: Nitric acid can be substituted with **ammonium chloride** or **zinc nitrate** for those who cannot obtain it; zinc nitrate can also act as a blowing agent.
- **Conductive carbon foam**: Adding a small amount of **nickel salt** to the sugar mix will make the resulting foam electrically conductive.
- **Bread as foaming agent**: Works but produces an inferior result compared to the recommended method — "horses for courses."
- **Comparison to Starlite**: Carbon foam is essentially the end-product that Starlite forms when it chars — making DIY carbon foam a more direct route ("cut out the middleman").
- **Easier alternatives**: Simpler routes exist but yield a less controlled pore size and lower-quality foam; he prefers the sugar method for its more controlled results.
- **Chamber geometry clarification**: If the stove body is formed directly from insulation, the combustion chamber is simply a cavity within it — there is no separate inner/outer chamber wall.
- **Smell during curing**: The slow carbonization process produces minimal odour, though oven use over multiple days is required.
