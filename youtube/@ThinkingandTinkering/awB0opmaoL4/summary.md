## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the concept of a variable stroke piston, inspired by the Miller engine used in the Mazda Millennia production car. He designs and 3D prints a working mechanical model that demonstrates how varying the eccentric position of a piston's internal slide changes the stroke length — from zero movement to maximum throw — with applications in variable compression engines, variable pumps, and CVTs.

## Key Topics
- The Mazda Millennia and its use of the Miller engine (invented by Ralph Miller, ~1957)
- How the Miller engine achieves efficiency by varying compression across the engine cycle
- The principle of variable stroke pistons and their mechanical implementation
- Applications of variable stroke pistons: engines, variable pumps, and CVTs (continuously variable transmissions)
- Step-by-step assembly of a 3D-printed variable stroke piston model
- How eccentricity determines stroke length (zero at center, maximum at full offset)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- PLA (polylactic acid) filament — used for all 3D-printed parts
- Cyanoacrylate (crazy glue/super glue) — used to bond PLA parts; noted as bonding stronger than the PLA itself
- Two-part accelerator spray — used with the cyanoacrylate for immediate setting

## Techniques and Methods
- CAD design in Tinkercad
- FDM 3D printing (PLA)
- Assembly of gear cage, eccentric, rack gear, flywheel, output cradle, and output rod
- Using a rack-and-pinion gear mechanism inside an eccentric to adjust the slide position
- Demonstrating variable throw by rotating the inner axle to shift the slide between center and offset positions
