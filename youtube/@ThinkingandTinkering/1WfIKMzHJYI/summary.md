## Overview
In Part 5 of his budget 1kW wind turbine series, Robert Murray-Smith spends a day experimenting with different coil configurations using microwave oven transformer (MOT) coils, testing how core presence, orientation, wire gauge, and magnet coverage affect power output. His main takeaway is that there is no single "correct" answer — coil design is a set of trade-offs between voltage, amperage, wind speed range, and efficiency.

## Key Topics
- Using microwave oven transformer (MOT) coils as generator stators
- Effect of open vs. closed core structure on output
- Optimal orientation of the E-core relative to passing magnets
- Primary vs. secondary coil comparison (voltage/amperage trade-offs)
- Effect of iron/ferrite cores on generator performance and drag
- Magnet-to-coil coverage and material efficiency
- Combining primary and secondary coils on the same core (spoiler: they cancel out)
- Coreless coil configurations as a viable alternative

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Microwave oven transformers (MOTs) — used as the coil/core source
- Laminated E-core iron — extracted from MOTs; tested with and without the closed top section
- Primary coil winding (thick wire, fewer turns) — from MOT
- Secondary coil winding (thin wire, many turns) — from MOT
- Steel bolt — used as an improvised core through a bobbin-wound coil
- Neodymium magnets (4.5 cm) — the permanent magnets on the turbine rotor

## Techniques and Methods
- Grinding or hacksawing MOT cores along weld lines to remove the top section and open the flux path
- Using a blower/fan to spin the turbine at a controlled ~10 RPM for consistent testing
- Testing coil orientation in two axes relative to magnet travel direction
- Measuring open-circuit and loaded voltage/current to calculate power (watts)
- Testing partial core insertion depth to find the effective range
- Connecting primary and secondary windings in series, parallel, and opposing phase configurations
- Improvised bobbin-and-bolt coil assembly as an alternative to MOT cores
