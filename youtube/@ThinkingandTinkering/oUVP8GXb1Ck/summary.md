## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith introduces the Italian folk art tradition of Carta Pesta (papier-mâché sculpture) from Lecce in southern Italy, where it is used to create figurines and large public sculptures. In this first part, he demonstrates how to create a resin face cast from a doll's head using plasticine, resin, and flour as a filler, then refine it with carving and sanding.

## Key Topics
- History and origin of Carta Pesta from Lecce, southern Italy
- Using a doll's head as a reference for face casting
- Making a plasticine impression mold of a face
- Casting a face using resin mixed with flour as a filler
- Cleaning, carving, and sanding the cast to refine features
- Filling air bubble holes with household/wood filler

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Plasticine** — used to make the impression mold of the doll's face
- **Alfred's Fast Glass resin** — casting resin used with a hardener to form the face
- **Resin hardener** (red-colored) — mixed with the resin; ratio affects set speed and brittleness
- **Plain flour** — added to the resin as a filler to make it carvable rather than glass-brittle
- **Wood filler / household filler** — used to fill air bubble holes in the cast surface
- **Sandpaper** — for smoothing lumps on the cast face

## Techniques and Methods
- **Impression molding** — pressing a doll's face into plasticine to create a negative mold
- **Resin casting with filler** — mixing resin and hardener with plain flour to produce a workable, carvable cast
- **Trimming and carving** — using tools to refine facial features (eyebrows, eyelids, mouth) after casting
- **Sanding** — smoothing surface imperfections on the cured resin cast
- **Hole filling** — packing air bubble voids with filler before painting
