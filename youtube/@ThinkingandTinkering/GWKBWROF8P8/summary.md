## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith repurposes a small rocket stove (built in video 1404) and a salvaged gas canister to create a highly efficient wood-fired water heater. By jacketing the rocket stove's hottest section inside the canister and connecting it to a waste-bin reservoir, he demonstrates a thermosiphon system that heats a significant volume of water to bathing temperature in roughly 15–20 minutes. The build is presented as stunningly simple and highly effective for off-grid or fuel-cost-conscious hot water production.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the original rocket stove design from video 1404 and how it works (air draw, combustion zone, afterburner section)
- Repurposing a salvaged propane/gas canister as a water jacket around the hottest part of the stove
- Thermosiphon water circulation between the heated canister and an external reservoir
- Live demonstration: heating a large volume of water to over 52°C (hot enough for a shower/wash)
- Brief discussion of alternative uses: mass heater (concrete, clay, or sand fill) and the concept of a sand battery

- **Concrete / mud / clay / sand** — mentioned as alternative fill materials for a mass heater or sand battery variant

## Techniques and Methods
- **Safe canister preparation**: emptying the gas canister, filling with water, then draining before cutting or working on it
- **Heat exchanger fabrication**: cutting a slot in the gas canister to seat the rocket stove's hottest section inside a water jacket
- **Thermosiphon circulation**: connecting the canister to an open reservoir so natural convection circulates cold-in/hot-out without a pump
- **Rocket stove combustion**: leveraging the afterburner section for maximum heat concentration at the jacket
- **Mass heater adaptation**: filling the same canister with concrete, clay, or sand instead of water for thermal mass storage

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Steam/pressure safety**: Repeatedly flags that a pressure gauge and blow-off/relief valve are essential if the system is enclosed or pressurised further. Notes that an open thermosiphon system is naturally safe since water can't exceed 100°C.
- **Welding leaks**: Acknowledges the tricky part of any upgrade is welding without leaks.
- **Afterburner efficiency**: Explains that the afterburner burns wood gas so completely that there would be little gas left to collect for other purposes.
- **Copper pipe flash-boiler variant**: Responds to suggestions about using copper pipe as a heat exchanger — notes that without micropore insulation the heat losses would be significant, and that steam bubbles in the pipe would be a technical challenge, calling his own design simpler and more effective for most people.
- **Sand vs. other thermal mass**: Confirms sand is better than alternatives for the sand battery/mass heater variant and says he plans to go deeper into this topic.
- **PCM (Phase Change Materials)**: Politely disagrees that PCM is the only storage option, noting there are alternatives.
- **Accessibility of the design**: Stresses that he is trying to keep his projects open to as many people as possible regardless of skill level or resources.
- **Gas canisters availability**: Reassures international viewers (particularly from the US) that salvaged gas canisters appear commonly in DIY project videos there too.
- **Energy output calculation**: Agrees the system is essentially a fire-tube boiler and that energy output could theoretically be calculated, though he hadn't measured it precisely himself.
