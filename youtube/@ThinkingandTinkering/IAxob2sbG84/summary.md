## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith scales up a feather-shaped 3D model (originally traced from a real feather in FreeCAD in video 2403) by 400% and prints six copies to use as wind turbine blades. He assembles them onto a nose cone and mounts the assembly on a serpentine coil generator (built in video 2070) for testing. A quick outdoor test confirms the turbine spins freely, and an indoor hair-dryer test with a multimeter yields an open-circuit voltage of approximately 6 volts at roughly 2 m/s wind speed — an encouraging initial result that warrants further investigation.

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Feather aerodynamics clarification:** Pushes back on a commenter who questioned feather lift properties — notes that "mid-wing that would be true but at the far end of the wing that would be wrong — check out a condor soaring."
- **Blade count vs. power:** Explains that adding more blades does not increase power output: "one will do just as well as 3 or 6 or 12 — it has very little to do with the number of blades but rather the swept area — more blades gives it 'more grip' so to speak but there is no increase in power."
- **AC vs. DC generators:** Gives a detailed reply to a commenter confused about DC output — all rotating-field generators are inherently AC; DC requires rectification (electrical) or a commutator/brush (mechanical). Directs the commenter to read up on motor/generator fundamentals and refers them to video 2403.
- **Sponsorship defence:** Acknowledges criticism about the FlashForge segment but notes he includes sponsored content far less frequently than most YouTube channels ("I do one video — maybe three a year and somebody complains about me selling out").
- **Manufacturing prospects:** States he is unlikely to try to get his inventions into manufacture — "that's a young man's game."
- **Files availability:** Confirms files are already online for community members to use themselves.
