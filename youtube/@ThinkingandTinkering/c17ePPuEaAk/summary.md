## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates the Tour Method for synthesising graphene oxide, a modification of the classic Hummer's method that is significantly safer due to the addition of phosphoric acid alongside sulfuric acid. The video covers the full procedure from ingredient selection through to filtration and washing, and emphasises that graphite source and particle size critically determine the quality and character of the final product.

## Key Topics
- Graphene oxide as a material in its own right, beyond being merely a precursor to graphene
- Comparison of the Tour method vs. the Hummer's method (safer, less dramatic reaction)
- Importance of graphite quality and particle size on final product properties
- Effect of graphite particle size on application (e.g. liquid crystal properties vs. inkjet printing)
- Step-by-step Tour method synthesis procedure
- Safety precautions with concentrated acids and temperature control
- Filtration, washing, and yield expectations

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Graphite** (3 g) — flake graphite powder recommended; 45 micron particle size used in this demo; quality and particle size directly determine end-product quality
- **Potassium permanganate** (18 g) — oxidising agent
- **Sulfuric acid** (360 mL, 96%) — concentrated; primary acid in the 9:1 mixture
- **Phosphoric acid** (40 mL, 75%) — key addition distinguishing the Tour method; reduces reaction hazard
- **Deionised water** (400 mL, as ice) — used to quench the reaction
- **Hydrogen peroxide** (3 mL, 35%) — added after water quench to terminate the oxidation reaction
- **Hydrochloric acid** (30%, ~200 mL) — used in the washing step
- **Ethanol** (~200 mL) — used in the washing step

## Techniques and Methods
- Dry mixing of graphite and potassium permanganate before acid addition
- Addition of 9:1 H₂SO₄/H₃PO₄ acid mixture to solid mixture (reaction self-heats to ~40–45°C)
- Heated magnetic stirring at 50°C for 12 hours (requires a hot plate/stirrer)
- Quenching by pouring reaction mixture over 400 mL deionised ice water
- Addition of hydrogen peroxide to stop the reaction (mixture turns bright yellow)
- Gravity filtration through a 45 micron mesh filter (graphene oxide passes through; residual graphite is retained)
- Sequential washing with deionised water, 30% hydrochloric acid, and ethanol (~200 mL each)

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was actively engaged in the comments with a mix of troubleshooting, safety guidance, and broader methodology advice. Key points:

- **Safety warnings to inexperienced users:** Explicitly cautioned commenters who asked basic questions to "think twice" before attempting this, recommending starting with a very small batch and building up experience first.
- **Troubleshooting failed batches:** Attributed most failures to poor temperature control or adding reagents too quickly. Advised starting again rather than trying to salvage a failed run.
- **Localised heating fix:** Suggested an alternative addition order to avoid hotspots — add acid and graphite first, cool it, then add KMnO₄ slowly while stirring, closer to the original Hummer's procedure.
- **Ice bath tip:** Recommended cooling beakers in the fridge/freezer before mixing if overheating is a problem.
- **Graphite quality:** Stated clearly that impure or low-grade graphite will ruin results — pure reagent-grade material is required.
- **Filter choice depends on intent:** Gave a detailed explanation that the choice between centrifuging and filtering depends entirely on particle size used and what the experimenter is trying to collect — emphasised thinking through the experiment design before choosing.
- **Purification:** Suggested dialysis as a possible purification route; also mentioned bath sonication or extended magnetic stirring to disperse product.
- **Alternative methods:** Referenced liquid phase exfoliation, electrochemical cleavage, GUITAR methods, and the Tang-Lau (bottom-up) method as alternatives — noted he finds the Tang-Lau disappointing.
- **The Tour paper:** Cited the original reference — *Marcano et al., ACS Nano, 2010, 4(8):4806–14*, "Improved synthesis of graphene oxide."
- **Patent note:** Advised that anyone intending to sell graphene oxide must use an unpatented synthesis method and cannot use the Tour method as patented without infringement.
- **Scale-up note (personal aside):** Mentioned that for graphite intercalation/expansion he typically processes 10–20 kg batches at a time.
