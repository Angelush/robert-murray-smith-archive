## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith builds a hand-powered generator inspired by a humming top's twisted-bar mechanism, first with a custom 3D-printed serpentine-coil design, then by rewinding and converting a common brushed DC motor into a brushless generator. The two systems are combined — the pump/flywheel drive from the humming top mechanism driving the rewound motor — producing 3–4 volts with very little effort, enough to charge a mobile phone.

## Key Topics
- The mechanics of a humming top (twisted bar + slotted washer) and its relation to twist-and-pump drills
- 3D printing a generator cage using TinkerCAD and Thingiverse components
- Using a 2.5 kg barbell as a flywheel
- Serpentine coil + neodymium magnet generator arrangement
- Rewinding a brushed DC motor rotor with thinner, more numerous wire turns
- Converting a brushed DC motor to a brushless generator by inverting the rotor/stator relationship using a larger bearing
- Gearing the pump mechanism to the rewound motor with a ~3:1 gear ratio

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **Steel** — humming top body components
- **Thrust bearings (47 mm × 25 mm)** — supports the rotating magnet carrier
- **Bearing (21 mm × 15 mm)** — fitted over commutator to invert rotor/stator in the motor conversion
- **2.5 kg barbell** — used as flywheel mass
- **20 mm × 4.5 mm neodymium magnets** — arranged N-S-N-S in the serpentine coil generator
- **Ferrite magnets** — found inside the original brushed DC motor (noted as weaker than neodymium)
- **0.6 mm gauge copper wire** — original motor winding (24 turns)
- **Thinner copper wire** — rewound rotor (150 turns, replacing the original 24)
- **3D-printed PLA parts** — cage, centering ring, gear, caps, feet, pillars (mix of original design and Thingiverse parts)

## Techniques and Methods
- Disassembly and mechanical analysis of a commercial humming top
- 3D modelling in TinkerCAD with parts sourced/adapted from Thingiverse
- Serpentine coil fabrication (referenced to a separate video)
- Motor disassembly and rotor rewinding (brushed DC → high-turn-count rewind)
- Brushed-to-brushless motor conversion by fixing a larger bearing over the commutator so the motor body becomes the rotor
- Gear ratio design (~3:1, 100-tooth drive gear to 30-tooth motor-body gear)
- Voltage testing with a multimeter during hand-pumping
