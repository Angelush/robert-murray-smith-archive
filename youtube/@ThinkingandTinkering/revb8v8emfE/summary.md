## Overview
Robert challenges the common term "generator," arguing these devices should be called **converters** because they do not create energy — they transform mechanical energy into electrical energy. Using a self-excited generator built from a washing machine motor (introduced in video 1363), he demonstrates that no electrical output is possible without a mechanical input, and that output power is always less than or equal to input power.

## Key Topics
- Why "generator" is a misleading term and "converter" is more accurate
- The principle that energy is converted, not created (conservation of energy)
- How voltage is determined by three variables: magnetic field strength, wire length, and rotational speed
- How amperage is a derived result of power input and voltage, not an independently generated quantity
- The importance of matching the input capture device (e.g. water wheel, wind turbine) to the converter's requirements
- The role of gearing in adapting slow, high-torque inputs to the speed requirements of a converter
- The need for an appropriate load on the output side to complete the circuit and draw current
- Wire sizing relative to expected current (amp) flow

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Copper wire (windings inside the converter)
- Steel (converter core)
- Permanent magnets (inside the converter)

## Techniques and Methods
- Self-excited generator / converter construction from a washing machine motor (universal motor)
- Hand-cranking a converter to demonstrate mechanical-to-electrical energy conversion
- Gearing as a method of matching input torque/speed characteristics to converter requirements
- Matching wire gauge to expected current load to prevent burnout
