## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith opens with a brief history of the 17th–18th century longitude problem — the difficulty of knowing one's east-west position at sea — and explains how the search for accurate timekeeping led to an obsession with pendulums. He then demonstrates a simple proof-of-concept pendulum generator built from a gym free weight, a 3D-printed bracket, bearings, and a salvaged coil from an old hamster-cage generator, showing that swinging a pendulum over a coil is enough to light an LED. The main takeaway is that the oscillating energy stored in a pendulum system can be harvested as electricity, and that multiple coils arranged along the arc of swing would increase output significantly.

## Key Topics
- The historical longitude problem and why accurate timekeeping was essential for navigation
- The British Navy's Longitude Prize as a driver of clock and pendulum innovation
- The isochronous property of pendulums (period independent of amplitude)
- Foucault's pendulum as proof of Earth's rotation
- The concept of a "near-perpetual" pendulum if friction and air resistance are eliminated
- Harvesting energy from a pendulum using electromagnetic induction
- Practical build: pendulum generator proof-of-concept lighting an LED

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Free weight (dumbbell) — used as the pendulum bob
- 8 mm threaded rod — pendulum arm
- 3D-printed bracket — printed in TinkerCAD to hold the weight and rod
- Bearings — pivot point at the top of the pendulum
- Ring from a salvaged hamster-cage generator — provides the swing frame and pivot
- Coil with integrated LED — placed on the floor beneath the pendulum to demonstrate induction

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D printing (TinkerCAD) — designing and printing a custom bracket with screw threads to mount the weight and rod
- Electromagnetic induction — coil placed beneath the swinging magnet/weight to generate current
- Bearing-mounted pivot — low-friction pivot to extend swing duration
- Proof-of-concept prototyping — repurposing salvaged parts (hamster-cage generator ring) combined with printed parts
