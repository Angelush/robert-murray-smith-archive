## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith explores the "Perpetual Wedge," a mechanical gear reduction mechanism invented by James White in 1786, demonstrating how a one-tooth difference between two meshing gears produces an extraordinary gear reduction ratio. Using 3D-printed demonstration models, he shows both the original spur-gear form and a more modern ring-gear (harmonic-drive-style) variant, explaining why such high-ratio speed reduction is practically valuable for converting fast motor output into high-torque, low-speed mechanical work.

## Key Topics
- The fundamental mismatch between motor/engine operating speeds and output shaft requirements
- History of European vs. Japanese motorcycle engine design philosophies as a case study in speed/torque trade-offs
- James White's Perpetual Wedge (patented 1786) and its operating principle
- How a one-tooth difference between two gears creates a 100:1 (or N:1) gear reduction in a single stage
- Demonstration of the original spur-gear version using a TinkerCAD-designed 3D-printed model
- Evolution of the concept into a compact ring-gear / pancake form analogous to a harmonic drive
- Practical applications: cranes, hoists, lifts, and compact high-torque drive systems

## Techniques and Methods
- TinkerCAD 3D CAD design for demonstration models
- 3D printing of gear components (spur and ring gear variants)
- Assembly of eccentric gear train: fixed ring gear + floating gear with N−1 teeth driven by a roller/planet carrier
- Arrangement of three planet-style rollers to distribute load (analogous to a planetary stage)
- Comparison of spur-gear form vs. flat "pancake" dual-ring-gear form for compactness
