## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith demonstrates how to build a small-scale Archimedes screw generator that can harvest energy from rainwater downpipes, gutters, or streams. He 3D prints the screw sections (sourced from Thingiverse), assembles them in a 110mm sewer pipe using copper plumbing fittings and super glue, then attaches a salvaged air conditioning motor as a generator. The device successfully lights an LED array when water is poured through, proving the concept as a low-cost emergency or supplementary power scavenger.

## Key Topics
- Archimedes screw as a hydroelectric generator concept (credited to a Japanese inventor)
- 3D printing the screw vs. the earlier hand-made version from old CDs and plumbing pipe
- Fitting the screw inside standard 110mm sewer/drain pipe
- Installation options: rainwater downpipe, gutter sweep-tee fitting, stream diversion
- Design variants: sealed rotating screw, rotating pipe with fixed screw, open half-pipe
- Trade-offs between efficiency, engineering complexity, and ease of cleaning
- Practical concerns: debris/leaf blockage, leaf guards, torque vs. RPM output

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- 110mm sewer/drain pipe (PVC)
- 3D-printed Archimedes screw sections (PLA filament implied)
- Copper plumbing pipe and fittings (15mm reducer to 8mm for bearing/axle)
- Super glue (adhesive for screw assembly)
- Tape (to achieve tight fit on copper guide pegs)
- UPVC foam/cellular sheet (used to build the test water-inlet box)
- Salvaged air conditioning motor (used as the generator)
- Old CDs and plumbing pipe (mentioned as the earlier hand-made screw method)

## Techniques and Methods
- Scaling a Thingiverse 3D model to fit a specific pipe diameter
- Removing print swarf (support/brim plastic) from printed parts
- Assembling multi-section screw with copper guide pegs and super glue
- Using copper plumbing reducers as end-bearing housings (15mm → 8mm axle)
- Drilling drain holes in a test pipe cap; centering the screw with a friction-fit cap
- Building a UPVC foam inlet box to simulate a water header for testing
- Open (half-pipe) variant to allow visual inspection and easier debris clearance
- Attaching a salvaged motor as a generator and connecting to a lighting bank for live demonstration

## Robert's Own Replies
- **Torque and RPM guidance:** Longer screws give more torque; the output characteristic is high-torque / low-RPM, so gearing will be needed for most generators. Amps depend on the specific generator used. The LED test array required ~20V turn-on voltage, confirming meaningful output.
- **"Six and two threes" philosophy:** Robert repeatedly reinforces that no single design variant is universally superior — sealed pipe, rotating pipe, or open half-pipe each suit different circumstances. He's comfortable with people disagreeing.
- **Home water battery concept:** He finds the idea of a rooftop tank + pump acting as a kind of battery genuinely interesting and approachable for home builders.
- **Debris and clogging:** He agrees debris is a real issue for fully enclosed pipe versions; a leaf guard at the top solves it for rainwater downpipes. Open half-pipe is easier to clean.
- **Legality note:** Installing such a device in public waterways/rivers would likely not be permitted in the UK; he's also unsure about drains from a regulatory standpoint.
- **3D printing accessibility:** This was only his third-ever print; he encourages beginners, noting that good results come quickly and printers are now affordable.
- **CD version reminder:** He repeatedly points viewers to his earlier video showing a hand-made version from old CDs — no 3D printer required.
- **Chimney/wind application:** A commenter suggested using it in a chimney flue; Robert loved the idea and said he wants to test it despite not having a chimney.
- **Centre for Alternative Technology:** He visited CAT in Wales and noted this device would fit very well there, especially alongside their water-lift system.
