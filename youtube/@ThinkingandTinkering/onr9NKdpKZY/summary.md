## Overview
Robert introduces the Prony brake as a simple, low-cost method for measuring the torque (and therefore power output) of a rotating shaft — specifically in the context of his wind turbine project. He explains both the classic wooden-block design invented in the 1800s and a band-type variant using a drum, rope or leather strap, and spring balances. The video concludes by teasing the construction of an actual Prony brake in a follow-up.

## Key Topics
- Why torque measurement is essential to calculating wind turbine power output
- The relationship between rotational speed, torque, and power (Power = torque × RPM)
- The concept of a dynamometer, specifically the absorption dynamometer type
- History and principle of the Prony brake (invented in the 1800s)
- Classic wooden-block Prony brake design and how it works
- Band/drum variant using spring balances and a leather strap
- The formula: Power = 2π × drum radius × RPM × force (in Newtons)

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Wooden blocks (clamping blocks for the brake)
- A lever arm with counterweight
- A nut and spring (to apply clamping pressure)
- A rotational drum (for the band-type variant)
- Fat rope, string, or leather strap (band material)
- Spring balances (two, for force measurement)

## Techniques and Methods
- Absorption dynamometer measurement (Prony brake method)
- Clamping a rotating shaft between wooden blocks and measuring the weight required to stall it
- Band-brake variant: wrapping a strap around a drum and measuring differential spring balance readings to determine torque
- Calculating power output: `Power = 2π × r × RPM × F(N)`
