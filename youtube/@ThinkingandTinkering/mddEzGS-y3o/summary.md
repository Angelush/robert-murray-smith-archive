## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith unboxes and reviews the Liberator rocket stove, a commercially made J-type rocket stove shipped to him from the US company Liberator. He explains the thermodynamic principles behind rocket stove combustion — particularly the distinction between the temperature at which gases ignite (~900°C) versus the temperature they can reach (1200°C+) — and praises the Liberator's ceramic-lined combustion pipe and targeted insulation strategy. He closes by soliciting community suggestions for potential improvements he might propose back to the Liberator team.

## Key Topics
- Unboxing and physical inspection of the Liberator rocket stove
- J-type rocket stove design with hopper feed
- Pyrolysis of wood and gas combustion temperature thresholds
- Flash point analogy (cooking oil at ~315°C) to explain ignition temperatures
- Ceramic insulation placement philosophy — why insulating only the combustion zone matters
- Claimed 99.5% combustion efficiency
- Proposed improvement: adding vent holes for increased oxygen at the combustion zone
- Proposed improvement: ash pan for easier cleaning access
- Trade-off between added features, complexity, and cost

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- **6mm steel** — body of the stove; noted as heavy-duty at ~120 kg
- **Stainless steel** — used for the ash grate
- **Ceramic insulation** — lined on the inside of the combustion pipe to protect the steel from corrosion and high-temperature combustion byproducts
- **Wood** — primary fuel; pyrolysis begins at ~300–400°C
- **Cooking oil** — used as a flash point analogy (~315°C flash point depending on type)

## Techniques and Methods
- **J-type rocket stove combustion** — wood pyrolysed in the feed section, gases ignited in the vertical burn tube
- **Ceramic lining of the combustion pipe** — ceramic applied to the interior to act as a sacrificial heat/corrosion barrier
- **Targeted insulation** — insulating only the combustion zone (not the full stove) to maintain ~900°C burn temperature without runaway heat buildup
- **Vent holes at the burn zone** — proposed modification to increase oxygen supply at the gas combustion point
- **Ash pan access** — proposed hinged panel beneath the burn zone for cleaning without disassembling the top

## Robert's Own Replies
Robert was highly engaged in the comments and added several substantive points:

- **Self-identifies as a research chemist** who also does a fair bit of teaching, and acknowledges he sometimes oversimplifies concepts, intentionally relying on the community to correct and add depth.
- **Confirmed the 99.5% efficiency figure** but agreed that throwing away the heat defeats the purpose.
- **Thermal mass as a battery**: Explained that thermal mass absorbs heat first (like charging), then releases it slowly after the fire dies — contrasting with direct radiant heating which is felt immediately.
- **Boiler/hot water coil**: Enthusiastic about the idea of integrating a copper coil for hot water; noted copper is good to ~1060°C and would be fine when water-filled. A boiler is "on his mind."
- **Heat exchanger and draw**: Acknowledged that a heat exchanger creates a tortuous path that could limit flue draw, suggesting fan-assisted airflow as a potential solution.
- **Plans to modify the stove** with an angle grinder — described with a "crazy scientist laugh."
- **Two holes at the top of the feed pipe** are already designed for outdoor air intake, per his understanding.
- **Metals4u** — mentioned as his UK steel supplier (online, with a cutting service), though he noted he pays a premium for it.
- **Sand battery / gasifier ideas**: Expressed interest in concepts like a combined rocket stove–sand battery and draw-down gasifier units raised by commenters.
- **Exhaust gas exchange**: Liked the idea of passing exhaust through a heat exchanger but noted the draw limitation concern.
