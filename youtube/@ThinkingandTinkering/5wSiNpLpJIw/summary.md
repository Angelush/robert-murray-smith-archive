## Overview
Robert demonstrates a reciprocating rack mechanism that converts back-and-forth slide motion into continuous unidirectional rotation, as part of his long-running project to develop a compact CVT (Continuously Variable Transmission) for a bicycle hub. He 3D-prints and assembles a physical prototype to understand the mechanism's behaviour, ultimately identifying a key limitation: it only functions correctly at full stroke length, which is incompatible with his need for a variable-stroke input.

## Key Topics
- Recap of the variable hypocycloid mechanism from video 2392 and its role in the CVT project
- Design constraints for a bicycle hub CVT: compact, circular (~100–150 mm wide), inexpensive, ideally press-steel construction
- Evaluation and rejection of a sliding gear mechanism (power loss during disengagement)
- Introduction and physical prototyping of a reciprocating rack mechanism
- How opposing rack teeth on a slider drive two gears that both rotate in the same direction via a central idler gear
- Mechanism #896 from Hitchcock's *Mechanical Movements and Devices and Appliances* and its appearance in *500 Mechanical Movements*
- The limitation of full-stroke dependency and the challenge of adapting it to variable stroke
- Robert's general research-and-prototype methodology for mechanical design

## Materials and Chemicals Mentioned
- Super glue — used to secure gears on axles if the fit is slightly loose during assembly
- 3D-printed parts (implied PLA or similar filament) — all prototype components

## Techniques and Methods
- 3D modelling in TinkerCAD to draft prototype parts
- FDM 3D printing of mechanism components
- Press-fit and tight-fit assembly of axles, slide guides, and gears
- Physical hand-testing of assembled prototype to verify motion behaviour
- Google image search (with "images" filter) used as a first-pass survey of available mechanisms
- Cross-referencing historical mechanical engineering reference books as a source of search terms and mechanism ideas
