## Overview
Robert Murray-Smith addresses the frequently asked question of how capacitors, supercapacitors, and batteries differ, arguing that these are not rigid, mutually exclusive categories but points on a continuum. He traces the structural and electrochemical transitions from simple parallel-plate capacitors through electrolytic capacitors and supercapacitors to lithium-ion batteries, showing how the boundaries blur at each step. His key takeaway is that how you define and distinguish these devices depends heavily on the functional context in which you need to use them.

## Key Topics
- Categories as aids to understanding versus fixed definitions (the "chair/table" analogy)
- Structure of a parallel-plate capacitor and the role of the dielectric
- The dielectric constant and how material choice affects capacitance
- Electrolytic capacitors: aluminium oxide as a dynamic dielectric layer
- Supercapacitors: high-surface-area carbon and the Helmholtz double layer
- The Debye length and the limit of ion-layer growth
- Pseudo-Faraday reactions in supercapacitors and the shift toward electrochemistry
- Lithium-ion batteries as "rocking-chair" batteries (ion intercalation mechanism)
- Energy density vs. power density as a practical axis for comparing devices
- Functionalist vs. strict-definitionist approaches to classifying energy storage

## Techniques and Methods
- **Parallel-plate capacitor model** — conceptual framework for understanding capacitance (plate area, separation distance, dielectric constant)
- **Electrolytic capacitor construction** — aluminium sheet with a grown aluminium oxide dielectric layer; electrolyte acts as the second plate
- **Double-layer (Helmholtz layer) formation** — ion adsorption onto high-surface-area carbon to create the capacitive layer in supercapacitors
- **Pseudo-Faraday reactions** — transient, surface-limited redox reactions introduced by electrolyte engineering in advanced supercapacitors
- **Ion intercalation ("rocking-chair" mechanism)** — shuttling of lithium ions between cathode and anode galleries in lithium-ion batteries
